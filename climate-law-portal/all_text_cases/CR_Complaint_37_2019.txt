This judgment pertains to the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), situated at Shahrah-e-Roomi, Lahore, alleging contraventions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The complaint, registered on 15th July 2019, seeks remedial action and imposition of penalties against the Respondent for alleged industrial pollution.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, an industrial enterprise engaged in the manufacturing of packaging materials, has been discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent and emitting noxious gases into the environment in excess of the prescribed NEQS. Specifically, the complaint detailed multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials. An initial inspection on 12th March 2019 revealed significant non-compliance. Subsequent sampling of wastewater discharge from the Respondent's facility on 18th March 2019 and air emissions from its stacks on 20th March 2019, followed by laboratory analysis, confirmed that parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH in the wastewater effluent, and Particulate Matter (PM), Sulfur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) in the air emissions, were consistently exceeding the NEQS. A show-cause notice was issued to the Respondent on 10th April 2019, demanding immediate corrective measures and submission of an Environmental Management Plan (EMP). The Complainant further alleged that despite the issuance of the notice and subsequent reminders, the Respondent failed to take satisfactory steps to mitigate the pollution, thereby continuing to pose a threat to public health and the environment, including contamination of groundwater and air quality degradation in the vicinity.
The Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports dated 12th March 2019 and 5th June 2019, clearly documenting the visible signs of pollution and non-functional or inadequately maintained effluent treatment plants (ETPs) and air pollution control devices. Laboratory analysis reports from the EPA-accredited laboratory, bearing reference numbers EPA/LAB/W-03/2019 and EPA/LAB/A-05/2019, dated 25th March 2019 and 28th March 2019 respectively, were submitted. These reports unequivocally showed COD levels in wastewater at 550 mg/L (NEQS: 150 mg/L), BOD5 at 280 mg/L (NEQS: 80 mg/L), TSS at 320 mg/L (NEQS: 200 mg/L), and pH ranging from 4.5 to 5.0 (NEQS: 6-9). Air emission analysis indicated PM levels at 650 mg/Nm3 (NEQS: 400 mg/Nm3 for industrial boilers), SO2 at 2200 mg/Nm3 (NEQS: 1700 mg/Nm3), and NOx at 1100 mg/Nm3 (NEQS: 600 mg/Nm3). The Complainant also submitted copies of the show-cause notice dated 10th April 2019 and subsequent follow-up letters, along with the testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, Assistant
Director of the Environmental Protection Agency, who reiterated the agency's findings and the Respondent's consistent non-compliance.
The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, represented by its legal counsel, Mr. Zahid Ali, vehemently denied the allegations of willful non-compliance. While acknowledging the initial inspection findings and the issuance of the show-cause notice, the Respondent contended that it had taken steps to address the identified issues. Mr. Ali argued that the facility is a large-scale industrial operation with complex processes, and achieving immediate full compliance with all NEQS parameters, especially for legacy infrastructure, presents significant technical and financial challenges. The Respondent submitted that it had initiated the procurement process for upgrading its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and installing advanced air pollution control equipment (APCE). They presented a conceptual plan for these upgrades and claimed that some preliminary works, such as internal audits and feasibility studies,
had been completed. They requested a reasonable timeframe to implement the proposed upgrades, citing their commitment to environmental responsibility and their significant economic contribution to the region. They further argued that the imposition of a heavy penalty would impede their ability to finance the necessary environmental improvements.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments presented by both parties and meticulously reviewed the documentary and testimonial evidence on record. The Complainant's evidence, particularly the detailed inspection reports and the accredited laboratory analysis reports (EPA/LAB/W-03/2019, EPA/LAB/A-05/2019), unequivocally establish that the Respondent's industrial effluent and air emissions have consistently exceeded the NEQS for various critical parameters. The significant exceedances in Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, Particulate Matter (PM), Sulfur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) are not isolated incidents but reflect a prolonged state of non-compliance, as evidenced by inspections spanning several months and the failure to adequately respond to the show-cause notice and subsequent reminders.
While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's claims regarding the complexity of industrial operations and the financial and technical challenges associated with upgrading legacy infrastructure, these challenges do not absolve an industrial entity of its fundamental responsibility to comply with environmental laws and standards. The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the NEQS are designed to protect public health and the environment, and their contravention, especially over an extended period, cannot be overlooked. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
