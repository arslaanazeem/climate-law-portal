**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent") concerning alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal, vested with jurisdiction under Section 20 and 21 of PEPA, 1997, has carefully considered the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, brought forth this complaint on 15.07.2020, asserting that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in textile manufacturing located in Lahore, was operating in contravention of environmental laws. Specifically, it was alleged that the Respondent was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/drain, which eventually merges into the River Ravi, thereby causing significant water pollution. Furthermore, the Complainant contended that the Respondent had failed to obtain a valid Environmental Approval/No Objection Certificate (NOC) for its current operational scale and expansions, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, and the Pakistan Environmental Protection Agency (Review of IEE/EIA) Regulations, 2000. Inspections conducted by EPA officials on multiple occasions revealed consistent non-compliance with the NEQS for industrial effluents, particularly concerning parameters such as Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a series of documents and witness testimonies. These included copies of the inspection reports dated 10.03.2020 and 25.05.2020, detailing observations of effluent discharge and the operational status of the Respondent's Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP). Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis
reports from accredited environmental laboratories, unequivocally demonstrating that the Respondent's discharged effluent consistently exceeded the prescribed NEQS for Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels. The Complainant also produced copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, dated 15.04.2020 and 05.06.2020, along with reminders and a final directive to cease non-compliant discharge, all of which remained unheeded by the Respondent, leading to the filing of the present complaint.
The Respondent, through its learned counsel, vehemently denied the allegations, asserting that it operates a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and is committed to environmental compliance. It was contended that any detected exceedances were either isolated incidents, attributable to sampling anomalies, or were within the process of being rectified through ongoing upgrades to their ETP. The Respondent submitted copies of maintenance records for its ETP and asserted that it had applied for, or was in the process of applying for, the requisite Environmental Approval/NOC, arguing that the delay was due to procedural complexities and not a deliberate defiance of law. They also emphasized their significant contribution to the national economy and employment.
The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties. The laboratory analysis reports submitted by the Complainant, conducted by independent and accredited laboratories, provide compelling and consistent evidence that the Respondent's industrial effluent repeatedly failed to meet the NEQS. The Respondent's explanation of 'isolated incidents' or 'sampling anomalies' is not substantiated by the consistent nature of the violations across multiple inspections. While the Respondent claims to have an ETP, the operational efficacy of said plant is clearly deficient, as evidenced by the effluent quality. Furthermore, the absence of a valid Environmental Approval/NOC, despite
repeated reminders and the clear mandate of Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, constitutes a distinct and serious violation. The Respondent's argument that it was 'in the process of applying' does not absolve it of the responsibility to operate with the necessary environmental approvals, particularly given the scale and nature of its industrial operations. The procedural complexities cited by the Respondent do not override statutory requirements. Furthermore, while the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's contribution to the national economy and employment, such contributions cannot be used as a justification for non-compliance with environmental laws. Environmental protection is a fundamental right and a collective responsibility, and no entity, regardless of its economic significance, is exempt from adhering to the prescribed standards designed to safeguard public health and the environment.
The Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof. The evidence, particularly the consistent laboratory analysis reports, unequivocally establishes that the Respondent has been and continues to discharge industrial effluent exceeding the NEQS, in clear violation of Section 16 of PEPA, 1997. Additionally, the Respondent's failure to obtain a valid Environmental Approval/NOC for its operations constitutes a breach of Section 12 of PEPA, 1997. The show-cause notices and directives issued by the EPA were not effectively complied with, demonstrating a persistent pattern of non-compliance.
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal concludes that M/s Nishat Mills Limited is guilty of violating Section 12 and Section 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. The continuous discharge of untreated or inadequately treated effluent poses a significant threat to the environment, particularly the River Ravi, and the health of the surrounding communities.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,000,000/- (Rupees Two Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 400,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
