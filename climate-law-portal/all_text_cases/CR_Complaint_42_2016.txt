This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), alleging violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal's jurisdiction is invoked under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, to adjudicate matters concerning environmental pollution and non-compliance with environmental laws. The complaint seeks appropriate legal action, including penalties and directives for environmental remediation, against the Respondent for its alleged polluting activities.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of glass products located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore, has been operating in a manner detrimental to the environment and public health. Specifically, the allegations centered on:
1.  **Discharge of Untreated or Partially Treated Industrial Effluents:** It was asserted that the Respondent's industrial processes generate significant quantities of wastewater, which is discharged into the municipal sewerage system and ultimately into surface water bodies, without adequate treatment, thereby exceeding the permissible NEQS for various parameters including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals.
2.  **Emission of Air Pollutants:** The Complainant further alleged that the Respondent's glass melting furnaces and other combustion sources emit particulate matter (PM), oxides of nitrogen (NOx), and sulfur dioxide (SO2) into the ambient air at levels significantly exceeding the NEQS for industrial air emissions, contributing to air pollution in the surrounding residential and industrial areas.
3.  **Improper Management of Hazardous Waste:** The complaint also highlighted concerns regarding the Respondent's handling and disposal of hazardous waste generated during its operations, stating a lack of proper segregation, storage, and disposal mechanisms in accordance with environmental regulations.
4.  **Failure to Obtain or Comply with Environmental Approval:** The EPA contended that the Respondent either lacked the requisite Environmental Approval (EIA/IEE) for its operations or was not adhering to the conditions stipulated therein, particularly concerning the operation and maintenance of its Environmental Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs).
5.  **Non-compliance with Notices:** The Complainant submitted that despite repeated inspections, issuance of environmental notices, and opportunities to rectify the violations, the Respondent had failed to take adequate measures to control pollution, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental laws.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 15.03.2015, 22.07.2015, and 01.12.2015, detailing observations of effluent discharge points, stack emissions, and waste management practices. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on 22.07.2015 and 01.12.2015, conducted by the EPA's accredited laboratory. These reports unequivocally indicated that the levels of COD, BOD, TSS, and certain heavy metals consistently exceeded the NEQS by significant margins. Similarly, stack emission monitoring reports from 01.12.2015 showed PM and NOx levels beyond permissible limits. The Complainant also produced copies of Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) and show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory responses or lack thereof. Photographs taken during inspections depicting visible pollution (e.g., discolored effluent, smoke plumes) were also presented.
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It contended that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental stewardship. The Respondent submitted that it had invested substantial resources in establishing and upgrading its ETP and APCDs. It presented its own internal monitoring reports, which, surprisingly, showed compliance with NEQS in some instances. The Respondent argued that the EPA's sampling methodology might have been flawed or unrepresentative, and that the EPA's laboratory results were not always consistent with international standards or its own internal checks. It further claimed that it was in the process of implementing a comprehensive environmental management plan, including further upgrades to its ETP and installation of bag filters for air emission control, which were expected to be fully operational by mid-2017. The Respondent also produced copies of its initial Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) approval, arguing that it had obtained necessary permissions and was striving for continuous improvement
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both parties and meticulously reviewed the voluminous evidence on record. While the Respondent's assertion of being a responsible corporate entity and its claims of investing in environmental infrastructure are acknowledged, the primary determinant for this Tribunal is objective compliance with the statutory environmental standards.
The Complainant's evidence, particularly the laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory,
unequivocally demonstrated consistent exceedances of the NEQS for various parameters in both industrial effluents and air emissions. These reports, generated by an official and accredited laboratory, carry significant probative value and were corroborated by multiple inspection reports detailing observed environmental degradation and non-compliance. The Respondent's contention regarding flawed sampling methodology or inconsistent laboratory results, while raised, was not substantiated with compelling counter-evidence that could effectively discredit the EPA's findings. The internal monitoring reports presented by the Respondent, showing compliance in "some instances," are insufficient to negate the consistent and documented violations established by the Complainant, particularly given the EPA's findings across different dates and parameters. Furthermore, the Respondent's claim of future upgrades and ongoing implementation of an environmental management plan, while indicative of potential future compliance, does not absolve it of its past and present liabilities for documented violations. The duty to comply with NEQS is continuous and immediate, not prospective.
Upon a thorough review of the entire record, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof. The evidence overwhelmingly establishes that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has been operating in violation of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards. Specifically, the Tribunal finds that the Respondent has:
1.  Discharged untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluents exceeding NEQS.
2.  Emitted air pollutants (particulate matter, NOx, SO2) from its operations at levels exceeding NEQS.
3.  Failed to adequately manage hazardous waste generated during its processes in accordance with environmental regulations.
4.  Failed to fully comply with the conditions of its environmental approval and environmental notices issued by the EPA.
These persistent violations have contributed to environmental pollution and posed a risk to public health, demonstrating a clear disregard for environmental protection laws.
Therefore, considering the gravity and persistence of the environmental violations, the documented non-compliance over an extended period despite repeated notices, and the potential adverse impact on the environment and public health, the Tribunal deems it necessary to impose appropriate penalties and issue directives for immediate and sustained compliance. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
