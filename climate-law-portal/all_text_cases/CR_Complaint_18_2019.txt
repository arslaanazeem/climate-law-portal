This judgment addresses Complaint No. 18/2019, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, situated in Kala Shah Kaku, District Sheikhupura. The complaint, lodged on 15th March 2019, alleges persistent violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) concerning the discharge of industrial effluent. The Complainant seeks the imposition of penalties and directions for the Respondent to cease the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated effluent into the environment.
The Complainant contended that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of various chemicals, has been consistently discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants in excess of the prescribed NEQS into the adjacent open drain, which ultimately flows into the River Ravi. It was specifically alleged that despite numerous inspections and warnings issued by the EPA over a period spanning several months prior to the filing of this complaint, the Respondent failed to take effective measures to control its pollution. The EPA’s inspection teams conducted visits to the Respondent's premises on multiple occasions, specifically on 12th December 2018, 28th January 2019, and 2nd March 2019. During these inspections, effluent samples were collected from the discharge points. Subsequent laboratory analyses conducted by the certified EPA laboratory revealed that key parameters such as Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels consistently exceeded the limits stipulated in the NEQS for industrial effluents. The Complainant submitted that show-cause notices dated 20th December 2018 and 5th February 2019 were issued to the Respondent, followed by a final warning on 10th March 2019, directing immediate compliance. However, these notices were allegedly disregarded, leading to the filing of the present complaint under Section 17 of the Act. The Complainant prayed for punitive action against the Respondent and an order compelling them to install or upgrade their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) to achieve compliance with NEQS.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. The Respondent asserted that it operates a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and is committed to environmental compliance. It was argued that any occasional exceedances of NEQS
were minor and attributable to operational fluctuations inherent in chemical manufacturing processes, rather than a deliberate disregard for environmental laws. The Respondent contended that its ETP is regularly maintained and operates efficiently, and that it has invested substantial resources in its pollution control infrastructure. It was further submitted that the EPA’s sampling methodology might not have been entirely representative, or that the samples might have been taken during unusual operational periods. The Respondent requested additional time to conduct its own independent analysis of its effluent and demonstrate its compliance, emphasizing its willingness to cooperate with the EPA to resolve any genuine concerns. They prayed for the dismissal of the complaint or, in the alternative, for a lenient view to be taken, allowing them an opportunity to rectify any proven deficiencies without punitive measures.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent, and has meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence placed on record. The core of the dispute revolves around the consistent discharge of industrial effluent exceeding NEQS limits. The Complainant, through the EPA, has produced detailed inspection reports, dates of visits (12th December 2018, 28th January 2019, 2nd March 2019), and certified laboratory analysis reports corresponding to effluent samples collected on these dates. These reports consistently indicate exceedances for BOD, COD, TSS, and pH levels, which are critical parameters for industrial effluent quality. Furthermore, the issuance of show-cause notices on 20th December 2018 and 5th February 2019, followed by a final warning on 10th March 2019, clearly demonstrates that the Respondent was duly informed of the violations and provided ample opportunity to take corrective action.
While the Respondent claims to operate a functional ETP and attributes exceedances to 'occasional operational fluctuations' or 'sampling methodology issues', no credible evidence was presented to substantiate these claims. No independent laboratory reports were submitted by the Respondent to counter the EPA's findings, nor was any detailed record of ETP maintenance or operational parameters provided to demonstrate its efficiency and regular functioning. The mere existence of an ETP does not absolve an industrial unit of its responsibility if the discharged effluent fails to meet the prescribed standards. The consistent nature of the violations, as evidenced by multiple inspections over several months
, clearly points towards a sustained pattern of non-compliance with the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards. The Tribunal finds the EPA’s evidence, including the certified laboratory reports and documentation of repeated warnings, to be compelling and uncontroverted by credible evidence from the Respondent.
It is well-established that the responsibility for ensuring compliance with environmental standards rests squarely with the industrial unit. While operational fluctuations can occur, the consistent nature of the exceedances over a significant period, despite multiple official warnings, indicates a systemic failure to adequately treat industrial effluent before discharge. The Respondent’s failure to provide independent corroborating evidence to support its claims of efficient ETP operation or to rebut the EPA’s findings leaves the Tribunal with no option but to accept the Complainant’s assertions as proven.
Therefore, based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal concludes that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited has contravened the provisions of Section 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, which prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste water in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards. Such persistent violations, despite clear directives from the regulatory authority, warrant punitive action as provided under the Act.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
For the reasons stated above, the Punjab Environmental Tribunal hereby finds the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, guilty of violating Section 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
Accordingly, it is hereby ordered as follows:
1.  **Penalty:** M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited is hereby directed to pay a monetary penalty of **PKR 750,000/- (Rupees Seven Hundred and Fifty Thousand only)** into the account of the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
2.  **Compliance Directive:** The Respondent shall immediately take all necessary steps to upgrade or modify its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and associated pollution control infrastructure to ensure that all discharged industrial effluent consistently meets the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
3.  **Compliance Plan Submission:** Within sixty (60) days from the date of this order, the Respondent shall submit a detailed compliance action plan, including a timeline for the upgrade/modification of its ETP and a strategy for achieving consistent NEQS compliance, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore. This plan shall be subject to review and approval by the EPA.
4.  **Regular Monitoring and Reporting:** For a period of one (1) year following the approval of the compliance plan, the Respondent shall conduct monthly independent laboratory analyses of its discharged effluent from a certified laboratory (other than the EPA's laboratory) and submit these reports to the EPA, Lahore, demonstrating continuous compliance with NEQS. The EPA shall also be at liberty to conduct its own unannounced inspections and sampling.
5.  **Further Action:** Failure to comply with any of the directives contained in this order, including the payment of the penalty or the submission and implementation of the compliance plan, shall expose the Respondent to further legal proceedings, including enhanced penalties, daily fines for continuing contravention, and other coercive measures as provided under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
This order shall come into effect immediately.
