This judgment addresses Complaint No. 2/2019, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), alleging violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and its allied rules and regulations. The complaint was formally lodged with this Tribunal on January 15, 2019, seeking an order for the cessation of polluting activities, imposition of penalties, and directives for environmental compliance. The Tribunal assumed jurisdiction under Section 25 of the PEPA, 1997, to adjudicate the matter.
The Complainant, through its Assistant Director, alleged that the Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, operating a glass manufacturing facility located in an industrial area within the jurisdiction of Lahore, was in continuous violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and other provisions of the PEPA, 1997. Specifically, the allegations included:
a) Emission of untreated or inadequately treated gaseous pollutants, including particulate matter, sulfur dioxide (SO2), and nitrogen oxides (NOx), into the ambient air, exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial emissions.
b) Discharge of industrial effluent containing high levels of suspended solids, heavy metals, and other toxic substances into the municipal drainage system without proper treatment, in contravention of NEQS for industrial effluents.
c) Improper storage and disposal of hazardous waste generated during the glass manufacturing process, posing a threat to soil and groundwater contamination.
d) Operating without a valid Environmental Approval (NOC) for its current scale of operations or failing to comply with the conditions stipulated in any previously granted approval.
e) Causing environmental degradation and nuisance to the surrounding communities and the general public due due to air and water pollution.
The Complainant asserted that despite repeated notices and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent had failed to implement adequate pollution control measures, thereby demonstrating persistent non-compliance.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on various dates between July 2018 and December 2018, detailing observations of visible smoke plumes, pungent odors, and improper waste handling practices at the Respondent's facility. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of air emission samples collected from the Respondent's stacks and effluent samples collected from its discharge points. These reports, prepared by an EPA-accredited laboratory, unequivocally demonstrated that the concentrations of particulate matter, SO2, and NOx in air emissions, and suspended solids, pH, and chemical oxygen demand (COD) in the effluent, significantly exceeded the NEQS. Photographic and videographic evidence depicting the discharge of dark smoke and untreated wastewater was also presented. Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, along with their unsatisfactory replies or lack thereof, were placed on record. The Complainant also produced an affidavit from an EPA official, Mr. Ahmed Khan, who participated in the inspections and sample collection, confirming the authenticity of the reports and observations.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel, denied the allegations. They contended that their facility was equipped with state-of-the-art pollution control devices, including electrostatic precipitators for air emissions and an effluent treatment plant (ETP) for wastewater. They presented their own internal monitoring reports, which, contrary to the EPA's findings, claimed compliance with NEQS. The Respondent'
's counsel argued that the EPA's sampling and testing methodologies might have been flawed or not representative of their continuous operations. They also submitted that any minor exceedances, if at all present, were temporary and attributable to unavoidable operational fluctuations, for which they were actively working on further mitigating measures. They emphasized their significant investment in environmental infrastructure and their commitment to sustainable practices. Furthermore, they produced copies of
environmental management plans, records of recent upgrades to their pollution control equipment, and invoices for chemicals used in the ETP, to demonstrate their ongoing efforts towards environmental stewardship. They further argued that the EPA had not consistently followed proper procedures for sampling and testing, or for issuing notices, thereby potentially rendering some of the evidence unreliable or inadmissible. They also highlighted their significant contribution to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to consider these factors when deliberating on any potential penalties.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
