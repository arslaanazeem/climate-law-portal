This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent") under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"). The complaint seeks remedial action and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of environmental laws and standards in the Respondent's industrial operations. The Tribunal, having duly considered the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties, proceeds to render its decision.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, operating an industrial unit involved in paper and packaging manufacturing, was found to be in persistent violation of various environmental regulations. Specifically, it was alleged that the Respondent was discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants far exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) into the public sewerage system/receiving waters without adequate treatment. Furthermore, the Complainant contended that the Respondent had failed to obtain a valid No Objection Certificate (NOC)/Environmental Approval for certain expansions or operational aspects, or had failed to comply with the conditions stipulated in any previously granted environmental approval. The complaint also highlighted the Respondent's alleged non-compliance with Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) and directives issued by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for the installation, upgrade, and effective operation of an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and adherence to NEQS. These actions, or inactions, were stated to constitute offences punishable under the relevant sections of PEPA, 1997.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of documents and testimony. Key among these was an inspection report dated 15.01.2024, conducted by an EPA team, which noted visible signs of pollution and inadequate treatment facilities at the Respondent's premises. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on 15.
01.2024, unequivocally demonstrated that the levels of various pollutants, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH, significantly exceeded the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Further evidence included copies of Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) issued to the Respondent on 10.03.2023 and 25.09.2023, along with subsequent show-cause notices, all indicating a pattern of non-compliance and a failure to implement the required corrective measures within stipulated timelines. The Complainant also submitted expert testimony from an environmental engineer, who detailed the potential adverse impacts of the Respondent's untreated effluent on aquatic life and public health, and highlighted the inadequacy of the existing treatment infrastructure.
In response, the Respondent, represented by its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations of persistent non-compliance. While acknowledging the presence of an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) at its facility, the Respondent contended that it had made substantial investments in its upgrade and maintenance. It was argued that any exceedances noted in the sample reports were either isolated incidents, attributable to unforeseen operational malfunctions, or a result of flawed sample collection and analysis methodologies. The Respondent further asserted that it was actively engaged in a comprehensive plan to modernize its ETP and was committed to achieving full compliance with NEQS. They presented internal compliance reports and invoices for ETP upgrades as evidence of their efforts, seeking leniency and
a pragmatic approach given their significant contribution to the national economy and employment. They requested additional time to fully implement their upgrade plans, suggesting that a punitive approach might hinder their ongoing efforts rather than facilitate compliance.
**Tribunal's Findings and Analysis:**
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence and testimonies presented by both parties. While acknowledging the Respondent's claims of ongoing efforts and investments in its ETP, the overwhelming evidence presented by the Complainant, particularly the independent laboratory analysis reports dated 15.01.2024, unequivocally demonstrated that the Respondent's industrial effluent consistently exceeded the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for various critical parameters. The argument that these were isolated incidents or attributable to unforeseen malfunctions does not hold weight against the backdrop of repeated Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) issued on 10.03.2023 and 25.09.2023, and subsequent show-cause notices. These documents clearly establish a pattern of persistent non-compliance and a failure to implement required corrective measures within stipulated timelines, indicating a serious disregard for environmental regulations.
The Respondent's contention regarding flawed sample collection or analysis methodologies was not substantiated with credible counter-evidence or expert testimony to challenge the integrity of the EPA's procedures. The expert testimony presented by the Complainant further elucidated the adverse environmental and public health impacts associated with the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent.
Therefore, the Tribunal finds that M/s Packages Limited has indeed violated Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Furthermore, the Respondent is found to be in violation of Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, for failing to comply

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal has carefully examined the inspection report, laboratory analysis results, and the photographic evidence produced by the Complainant. The measured pollutant concentrations significantly exceed the NEQS limits and the Respondent has failed to rebut this evidence with credible contrary data. The defense regarding ETP maintenance does not excuse ongoing pollution discharge and cannot serve as a justification for non-compliance with mandatory environmental standards.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
