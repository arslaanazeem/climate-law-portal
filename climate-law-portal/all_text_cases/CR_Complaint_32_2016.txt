This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent manufacturer of glass products, located in the industrial zone of Lahore. The complaint seeks redress for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), and its associated National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal has jurisdiction under Section 16 of the PEPA, 1997, to adjudicate upon complaints pertaining to contraventions of the Act and the rules and regulations framed thereunder.
The Complainant, through its legal counsel, Mr. Ahmed Ali, contended that the Respondent company has been operating its manufacturing facility in a manner detrimental to the environment and public health. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  **Discharge of Untreated Effluent**: The Respondent's industrial operations were alleged to be discharging inadequately treated industrial wastewater into the public sewerage system and subsequently into natural water bodies, with pollutant parameters significantly exceeding the NEQS. Specific parameters cited were Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and concentrations of heavy metals (Lead, Chromium).
2.  **Air Emissions Exceeding Standards**: The Respondent's furnace operations and other industrial processes were alleged to be emitting particulate matter (PM10), Nitrogen Oxides (NOx),
and Sulphur Oxides (SOx) from its stacks, often exceeding the permissible limits stipulated in the NEQS, contributing to air quality degradation in the surrounding residential and industrial areas.
3.  **Improper Management of Hazardous Waste**: The Respondent was also alleged to be improperly storing, handling, and disposing of hazardous waste generated from its manufacturing processes, including spent chemicals, broken glass contaminated with heavy metals, and sludge from its wastewater treatment plant, without adherence to the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003, or other relevant regulations, posing risks of soil and groundwater contamination.
The Complainant presented a series of inspection reports, laboratory analysis results from EPA-approved laboratories conducted on various dates between 2015 and 2016, and photographic evidence to substantiate these claims. These reports consistently indicated that the Respondent's effluent samples showed BOD levels ranging from 150-250 mg/L (NEQS: 80 mg/L), COD levels from 350-500 mg/L (NEQS: 150 mg/L), TSS levels from 200-300 mg/L (NEQS: 200 mg/L), and detectable concentrations of Lead and Chromium exceeding NEQS. Similarly, air emission
samples indicated PM10 levels frequently exceeding 150 µg/m³ (NEQS: 100 µg/m³), NOx levels above 200 ppm (NEQS: 170 ppm), and SOx levels occasionally surpassing 250 ppm (NEQS: 200 ppm). The photographic evidence depicted open dumping of waste and inadequate storage facilities for chemicals.
In response, the Respondent, represented by its legal counsel, Mr. Zahid Khan, vehemently denied the allegations. The Respondent contended that it is a responsible corporate entity with a strong commitment to environmental protection. It argued that:
1.  **Operational Challenges**: While acknowledging some historical challenges in meeting NEQS, the Respondent claimed that it had undertaken significant investments in upgrading its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices (APCDs) in late 2015 and early 2016. It asserted that the samples cited by the EPA were either outdated, not representative of current operations, or taken during periods of operational upset which have since been rectified.
2.  **Compliance Efforts**: The Respondent presented evidence of recent analytical reports (late 2016) from private EPA-certified laboratories, which, according to them, showed compliance with most NEQS parameters for both effluent and air emissions. They also submitted invoices and completion certificates for the installation of new filtration systems and scrubbers.
3.  **Waste Management Plan**: Regarding hazardous waste, the Respondent claimed to have an internal waste management plan and was in the process of contracting with an EPA-licensed waste management company for the proper disposal of specific hazardous materials, providing a draft agreement as proof of intent. They attributed any observed lapses to transitional phases in establishing a more robust system.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties, including the inspection reports, laboratory analyses, and photographic evidence submitted by the Complainant, as well as the Respondent's counter-reports, invoices, and explanations. It was observed that the EPA's reports, spanning from mid-2015 to early 2016, consistently demonstrated non-compliance across multiple parameters for both wastewater discharge and air emissions, as well as deficiencies in hazardous waste management. While the Respondent presented some recent reports indicating improved compliance, the Tribunal noted that these were limited in scope and frequency compared to the Complainant's extensive documentation and did not fully negate the earlier, consistent findings of violations.
The Tribunal further considered that the PEPA, 1997, places a strict liability on industries to operate in compliance with the NEQS and other environmental regulations. The argument of "operational challenges" or "transitional phases" does not absolve an entity from its statutory obligations, especially when the non-compliance has been persistent over a significant period, as evidenced by the Complainant. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent failed to adequately demonstrate consistent compliance with the NEQS for effluent discharge and air emissions, and its hazardous waste management practices were not in full adherence to the prescribed rules during the period under scrutiny. The environmental degradation caused by such non-compliance poses a direct threat to public health and the ecological balance of the surrounding areas.
Therefore, after careful consideration of the documentary evidence and arguments presented by both sides, the Tribunal concludes that the Complainant has successfully established that M/s Ghani Glass Limited was in violation of Section 11, 12, and 13 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, read with its associated National Environmental Quality Standards and Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
For the reasons stated above, the Tribunal hereby finds M/s Ghani Glass Limited, the Respondent, liable for contraventions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the rules and regulations framed thereunder.
It is hereby ordered as follows:
1.  **Penalty**: The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, is directed to pay a monetary penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** under Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, for the proven violations. This penalty shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
2.  **Compliance with NEQS**: The Respondent shall ensure immediate and continuous compliance with all National Environmental Quality Standards for industrial effluent discharge and gaseous emissions. The Respondent is directed to submit a comprehensive compliance report, including validated third-party laboratory analysis results demonstrating full compliance, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within **sixty (60) days** from the date of this order.
3.  **Hazardous Waste Management**: The Respondent shall forthwith implement a robust hazardous waste management system in strict accordance with the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003, and other relevant regulations. This includes proper segregation, storage, handling, transportation, and disposal of all hazardous waste. The Respondent shall provide proof of engagement with an EPA-licensed hazardous waste management facility and submit a detailed waste management plan to the EPA within **forty-five (45) days** from the date of this order.
4.  **Environmental Audit**: The Respondent is directed to commission an independent environmental audit by an EPA-certified environmental consultant within **ninety (90) days** to assess the overall environmental performance of its facility, identify any remaining non-compliance issues, and recommend corrective measures. The audit report shall be submitted to the EPA for review and necessary action.
5.  **Monitoring and Inspection**: The Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, is authorized and directed to conduct regular and unannounced inspections of the Respondent's facility to ensure strict compliance with this order and all environmental laws and regulations.
6.  **Costs**: The Respondent shall bear the costs of these proceedings, assessed at **PKR 500,000/- (Rupees Five Hundred Thousand only)**, to be deposited with the Tribunal within thirty (30) days.
Failure to comply with any part of this order may result in further legal action, including enhanced penalties and closure of operations, as provided under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
