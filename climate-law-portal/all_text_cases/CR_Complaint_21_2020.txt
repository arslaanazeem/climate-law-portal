This judgment addresses Complaint No. 21/2020 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a chemical manufacturing facility, for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent. The Tribunal, constituted under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), is seized of the matter to adjudicate upon the environmental transgressions and impose appropriate remedies and penalties as prescribed by law. The proceedings commenced with the filing of the complaint on 15.03.2020, following which notices were issued to the Respondent, who subsequently appeared through counsel and filed their reply. This Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties to arrive at a just and lawful decision.
The Complainant, through its Assistant Director, alleged that the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, operating a chemical manufacturing plant, has been habitually discharging industrial effluent directly into the open drainage system and ultimately into local water bodies without adequate treatment. This discharge, it was contended, contained hazardous pollutants exceeding the permissible limits set by the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Specifically, the complaint detailed multiple instances of non-compliance, citing elevated levels of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH imbalance, and traces of heavy metals in the effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points. The EPA further alleged that despite repeated warnings, inspection notices, and opportunities to rectify the situation, the Respondent failed to install and operate an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) or to sufficiently upgrade its existing treatment facilities to meet the prescribed NEQS. The Complainant asserted that the continuous discharge of such untreated or inadequately treated effluent posed a severe threat to public health, aquatic life, soil quality, and the overall ecological balance of the surrounding area, constituting a grave violation of the environmental protection laws of Pakistan.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 10.01.2020, 25.02.2020, and 05.03.2020, prepared by EPA field teams, detailing observations of effluent discharge and noting the absence or inadequacy of treatment facilities. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the EPA’s accredited laboratory, corresponding to the samples collected during the aforementioned inspections. These reports consistently demonstrated that key parameters such as COD (ranging from 450-600 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L), BOD (ranging from 200-300 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 80 mg/L), TSS (ranging from 350-500 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 200 mg/L), and pH (often outside the 6-9 range) significantly exceeded the NEQS. Furthermore, the Complainant produced copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 15.01.2020 and 01.03.2020, demanding compliance and outlining the potential legal consequences, which, according to the Complainant, were largely ignored. The testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, Assistant Director EPA, who led the inspection teams, corroborated the documentary evidence, detailing the sampling procedures and the Respondent's non-responsive attitude.
In its defense, the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its legal counsel, denied the allegations of deliberate non-compliance. They contended that they possessed an operational ETP and were making continuous efforts to improve its efficiency. The Respondent submitted a report from a private environmental consultant, dated 10.03.2020, which, while acknowledging some parameters were close to the NEQS limits, claimed overall compliance or significant progress towards it. They further argued that some fluctuations in effluent quality were inherent to chemical manufacturing processes and that they were in the process of procuring advanced equipment for ETP upgradation, citing delays due to import restrictions and the prevailing economic situation. The Respondent also presented their internal monitoring reports, which, contrary to the EPA's findings, showed parameters largely within acceptable limits. The testimony
of their Plant Manager, Mr. Iqbal Butt, further elaborated on the operational challenges and the steps taken to mitigate environmental impact, asserting that the company was a responsible corporate citizen and contributed significantly to the national economy through employment and industrial output. He also questioned the methodology of EPA's sampling, suggesting that the samples might not have been representative of the overall discharge quality or that the chain of custody for the samples was not adequately maintained, potentially affecting the integrity of the lab results. The Respondent urged the Tribunal to consider their ongoing efforts and not impose punitive measures that could jeopardize their business operations and the livelihoods of their employees.
**TRIBUNAL'S FINDINGS**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments, evidence, and testimonies presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent. While acknowledging the Respondent's claims of ongoing efforts and economic contributions, the overwhelming evidence presented by the Complainant, particularly the multiple, consistent, and accredited laboratory analysis reports from the EPA, clearly demonstrates a sustained pattern of non-compliance with the NEQS. The EPA's inspection reports, corroborated by the testimony of its Assistant Director, provide a credible account of the Respondent's failure to adequately treat its
effluent. The Tribunal finds the Respondent's defense, particularly the reliance on a private environmental consultant's report and internal monitoring data, to be less credible and insufficient to counter the robust and consistent evidence presented by the Complainant. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,600,000/- (Rupees One Million Six Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 320,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
