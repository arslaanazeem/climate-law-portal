This judgment addresses Complaint No. 25/2017, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a company engaged in the manufacturing of glass products. The complaint invokes the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 21 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleging various violations of environmental laws and regulations. The matter came before this Tribunal for adjudication of the environmental transgressions attributed to the Respondent's industrial operations located within the jurisdiction of Lahore.
The allegations against the Respondent stemmed from multiple inspections conducted by officials of the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) at their manufacturing facility. Primarily, the Complainant asserted that the Respondent was operating without a valid No Objection Certificate (NOC) for its industrial emissions and effluent discharge, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997. Furthermore, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial processes were generating gaseous emissions, particularly particulate matter, nitrogen oxides (NOx), and sulphur oxides (SOx), far exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial gaseous emissions. The complaint also highlighted the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/drains, with laboratory analyses indicating levels of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals (specifically lead and chromium) significantly above the NEQS for industrial liquid effluent. These actions, the Complainant contended, constituted a grave threat to public health and the environment, causing air pollution and water contamination, and were in direct contravention of the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and its allied rules and regulations.
In support of its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports dated 15.03.2016, 22.06.2016, and 05.10.2016, prepared by EPA field teams, which documented the visual observations of emissions and effluent discharge points. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory were submitted, bearing reference numbers EPA/Lab/AIR/2016/03-124, EPA/Lab/WATER/2016/06-345, and EPA/Lab/WATER/2016/10-678, which conclusively showed the exceedance of NEQS for both air emissions and liquid effluent on multiple occasions. Photographic and videographic evidence depicting dark smoke plumes from stacks and discolored effluent discharge was also presented. Additionally, copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 15.04.2016 and 01.07.2016, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were placed on record. The Complainant also produced an expert witness, Mr. Ahmed Khan, a Senior Environmental Analyst from EPA, who testified to the accuracy of the lab reports and the methodology used for sampling and analysis. The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, while not outright denying the presence of emissions and effluent, contended that they had undertaken significant steps towards environmental compliance. Their defense primarily focused on technical challenges inherent in glass manufacturing processes, the substantial investment already made in pollution control equipment, and ongoing efforts to upgrade their effluent treatment plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices. They submitted a timeline for the completion of these upgrades, a consultant's report outlining their proposed remedial measures, and receipts for the purchase of new filter bags and chemicals for the ETP. The Respondent argued that the EPA's sampling might have been conducted during periods of operational upset and did not represent their average performance
. The Respondent argued that the EPA's sampling might have been conducted during periods of operational upset and did not represent their average performance, and that a more holistic view of their environmental performance, including their proactive investments, should be considered. They requested additional time to fully implement their proposed upgrades and achieve complete compliance with the NEQS.
**FINDINGS OF THE TRIBUNAL:**
This Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the extensive documentary and testimonial evidence presented.
It is undeniable from the laboratory analysis reports, which are the cornerstone of the Complainant's case, that the Respondent's industrial emissions and effluent discharge consistently exceeded the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) on multiple occasions. The reports, supported by expert testimony, leave no reasonable doubt regarding the factual position of environmental non-compliance. The photographic and videographic evidence further corroborates the visual observations of pollution. The repeated issuance of show-cause notices and the Respondent's inability to demonstrate full compliance within the stipulated periods also weigh heavily against them.
While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's contention regarding technical challenges inherent in glass manufacturing and their purported investments in pollution control, these factors, however significant, do not absolve them of their statutory obligations under PEPA, 1997. Section 12 explicitly mandates the obtaining of an NOC, which the Respondent admittedly lacked for a significant period relevant to the complaint. More critically, Section 16 unequivocally prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration, or level in excess of the NEQS. The evidence on record clearly establishes a breach of both these fundamental provisions.
The Respondent's arguments about operational upsets, while potentially valid for isolated incidents, cannot justify a pattern of consistent exceedances documented over several months. Furthermore, the submission of a timeline for future upgrades and a consultant's report, while indicative of a future intent to comply, does not negate the past and ongoing violations proven by the Complainant. Environmental protection is a matter of strict liability, and the duty to comply with NEQS is continuous. The damage caused by pollution is often irreversible and poses a direct threat to public health and the ecological balance.
Therefore, after a thorough review of the entire record, this Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established the allegations of environmental violations against M/s Ghani Glass Limited. The Respondent has contravened Section 12 and Section 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by operating without a valid NOC and by discharging industrial emissions and effluent containing pollutants in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards.
**ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the findings above and exercising the powers vested in this Tribunal under Section 21 read with Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the following order is passed:
1.  **Declaration of Liability:** The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, is hereby found liable for violating Section 12 and Section 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
2.  **Financial Penalty:** The Respondent is directed to pay a monetary penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand only)** to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this judgment.
3.  **Remedial Directives:** The Respondent is forthwith directed to:
    a.  Immediately cease all industrial emissions and effluent discharges that are in excess of the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
    b.  Submit a comprehensive Environmental Management Plan (EMP) to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within forty-five (45) days, detailing the specific measures, timelines, and financial allocations for the complete upgrade and operationalization of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control (APC) devices to consistently meet all NEQS.
    c.  Ensure that all necessary pollution control equipment is fully functional and optimized to achieve compliance with NEQS within **ninety (90) days** from the date of this judgment.
    d.  Apply for and obtain a valid No Objection Certificate (NOC) for its industrial operations, including emissions and effluent discharge, from the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within **sixty (60) days** from the date of this judgment.
    e.  Submit monthly compliance reports, including third-party verified laboratory analysis reports of its emissions and effluent, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, for a period of one year, commencing from the date of achieving full compliance.
4.  **Monitoring and Enforcement:** The Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, is directed to rigorously monitor the Respondent's compliance with these directives and to take all necessary legal actions, including sealing of the facility or imposition of further daily penalties as per Section 17(2) of PEPA, 1997, in case of non-compliance with any part of this order.
5.  **Costs:** The Respondent shall also bear the costs of these proceedings, assessed at **PKR 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand only)**, payable to the Complainant within thirty (30) days.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,000,000/- (Rupees Two Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 400,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
