This judgment addresses Complaint No. 23/2016, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent industrial entity operating within the jurisdiction of Lahore. The complaint seeks redress for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and excessive air emissions, thereby causing environmental degradation and public nuisance. The Tribunal, duly constituted under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), is vested with the jurisdiction to hear and decide matters pertaining to environmental pollution and non-compliance with prescribed standards.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent's industrial facility, located at Shahrah-e-Roomi, Lahore, has been operating in contravention of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and Punjab Environmental Quality Standards (PEQS). Specifically, the allegations included: (a) persistent discharge of industrial wastewater containing pollutants such as high Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals, exceeding the permissible limits into the public sewerage system and open drains, without adequate treatment; (b) emission of smoke, particulate matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) from its boiler and generator stacks exceeding the prescribed air quality standards; and (c) failure to maintain an effective Environmental Management Plan (EMP) and submit regular environmental monitoring reports as required under the terms of its Environmental Approval. The Complainant contended that despite repeated warnings and notices issued by the EPA over the past two years, the Respondent had failed to take sufficient remedial measures, thus demonstrating a continuous disregard for environmental protection laws and posing a significant threat to public health and the local ecosystem.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 15.03.2015, 22.07.2015, and 09.02.2016, prepared by EPA field teams, detailing observations of effluent discharge and air emissions. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the certified EPA Laboratory, showing that samples of wastewater collected from the Respondent's discharge points on various dates consistently exceeded NEQS for BOD (averaging 350 mg/L against a limit of 80 mg/L), COD (averaging 800 mg/L against a limit of 200 mg/L), and TSS (averaging 450 mg/L against a limit of 200 mg/L). Similarly, air emission monitoring reports indicated exceedances for PM (averaging 750 mg/Nm3 against a limit of 600 mg/Nm3 for coal-fired boilers) and SO2. Photographs and video recordings depicting visible smoke plumes and dark-coloured effluent discharge were also presented. The Complainant also produced copies of the show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, along with the Respondent's responses, which were deemed unsatisfactory by the EPA. The Assistant Director, EPA, also appeared as a witness, reiterating the department's findings and the Respondent's non-compliance.
The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel and representatives, vehemently denied the allegations. While acknowledging its industrial operations, the Respondent contended that it operates with due regard for environmental protection and has invested significantly in pollution control technologies. It argued that the samples collected by the EPA were not representative, potentially contaminated, or taken without adherence to proper sampling protocols. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports, which, however, were not independently verified by a third-party accredited laboratory, claiming compliance with NEQS. It further submitted that it had initiated the process for upgrading its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and installing advanced air pollution control devices, presenting procurement documents and project timelines as evidence of its commitment to environmental compliance. The Respondent's Environment Manager testified that the facility was in a transitional phase of upgrading its systems and requested additional time to achieve full compliance
.
Having heard the arguments of both parties and meticulously reviewed the evidence presented, this Tribunal proceeds to render its findings.
Upon careful consideration of the comprehensive dossier presented by the Complainant, particularly the multiple inspection reports, the certified laboratory analysis reports from the EPA Laboratory, and the photographic/video evidence, it is unequivocally established that the Respondent’s industrial facility has been operating in persistent violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and Punjab Environmental Quality Standards (PEQS) for both wastewater discharge and air emissions. The consistency of the exceedances across various parameters and dates, as evidenced by the EPA’s certified laboratory reports, provides compelling and irrefutable proof of non-compliance. The average values for BOD, COD, TSS, PM, and SO2 significantly surpass the permissible limits, indicating a severe and sustained environmental impact. The repeated issuance of warnings and notices by the EPA, followed by the Respondent's failure to take adequate remedial actions over an extended period, further underscores a lack of diligence in adhering to environmental regulations.
Conversely, the Respondent’s defense primarily rested on questioning the sampling methodology and presenting internal monitoring reports, which, critically, lacked independent verification by an accredited third-party laboratory. While the Respondent’s assertion of having initiated upgrades to its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices demonstrates a belated acknowledgment of the problem and an intent towards future compliance, it does not absolve them of the established past and ongoing violations. The request for additional time, while noted, cannot override the immediate imperative to comply with environmental laws and protect public health and the ecosystem, especially given the prolonged period of non-compliance. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof, establishing beyond reasonable doubt that M/s Packages Limited has contravened the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and its associated regulations.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings and pursuant to the powers vested in this Tribunal under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, it is hereby ordered as follows:
1.  **Cessation of Violations:** The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, is directed to immediately cease the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and the emission of air pollutants exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and Punjab Environmental Quality Standards (PEQS) from its facility located at Shahrah-e-Roomi, Lahore.
2.  **Compliance Plan Submission:** The Respondent shall, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this order, submit a comprehensive and time-bound Environmental Compliance Plan to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore. This plan must detail the specific measures, technologies, and timelines for upgrading its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and installing advanced air pollution control devices to ensure full and continuous compliance with NEQS/PEQS for all parameters. The plan shall be subject to the review and approval of the EPA.
3.  **Regular Monitoring and Reporting:** The Respondent shall, commencing from the date of approval of the Compliance Plan, submit monthly environmental monitoring reports for both wastewater discharge and air emissions to the EPA. These reports must include laboratory analysis results from an EPA-certified independent third-party laboratory, demonstrating sustained compliance with NEQS/PEQS.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **02 February, 2023**.
