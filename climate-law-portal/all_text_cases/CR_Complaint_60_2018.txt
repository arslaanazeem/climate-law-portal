**JUDGMENT**
This complaint, bearing No. 60/2018, has been filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a company engaged in the manufacturing of various chemicals, having its industrial unit located in Kala Shah Kaku Industrial Estate, Sheikhupura. The complaint invokes the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 20 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), read with other enabling provisions of law, seeking appropriate legal action against the Respondent for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations. The Tribunal is mandated to adjudicate upon complaints pertaining to environmental pollution and non-compliance with National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
The Complainant's case, as articulated in the complaint and supporting documents, posits that the Respondent has been consistently discharging industrial effluent containing hazardous substances and emitting gaseous pollutants into the ambient air, both significantly exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Specifically, it is alleged that during routine inspections conducted by EPA officials on various dates, particularly on 15.03.2018 and 05.05.2018, the Respondent's effluent treatment plant (ETP) was found to be either non-operational or operating inefficiently, leading to the direct discharge of untreated or partially treated wastewater into the nearby sewerage system/drain, eventually contaminating surface and groundwater resources. Furthermore, allegations include the failure to adequately control air emissions from their boiler and process units, resulting in the release of particulate matter and noxious gases beyond permissible limits. The Complainant also asserted that the Respondent had failed to submit a comprehensive Environmental Management Plan (EMP) as required, and had not taken adequate measures to manage hazardous waste generated during its operations, thus posing a significant threat to public health and the environment. Despite repeated notices, warnings, and opportunities for rectification, the Respondent allegedly persisted in its non-compliant practices, necessitating recourse to this Tribunal.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant tendered a comprehensive set of evidence, including detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA field teams, photographs depicting the alleged non-compliant discharges and emissions, and crucially, laboratory analysis reports of effluent and air samples collected from the Respondent's premises. These laboratory reports, issued by the EPA's accredited laboratory, clearly indicated parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, heavy metals (e.g., Chromium, Lead), and various gaseous emissions (e.g., SO2, NOx, Particulate Matter) to be significantly higher than the NEQS. The Complainant also presented copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, along with the Respondent's replies, which were deemed unsatisfactory by the EPA. An expert witness, an environmental engineer from the EPA, also testified regarding the technical aspects of the violations and the inadequacy of the Respondent's environmental controls.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, categorically denied the allegations. Through its counsel and submitted written statement, the Respondent contended that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental stewardship. It claimed to have an operational Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices (APCDs), which are regularly maintained. The Respondent challenged the veracity and methodology of the EPA's sampling and analysis, suggesting that the samples might not have been taken in accordance with established protocols
and that the chain of custody of the samples was not properly maintained, potentially compromising their integrity. It was further submitted that the Respondent’s ETP and APCDs were, in fact, functional and regularly maintained, and any momentary exceedance of NEQS, if proven, was an isolated incident not reflective of their general operational standards. The Respondent also argued that it had invested significantly in environmental infrastructure and was continuously striving to improve its environmental performance. They presented their internal monitoring reports, albeit less comprehensive than those of the EPA, to demonstrate compliance. Furthermore, the Respondent's counsel emphasized that the company provides substantial employment and contributes to the national economy, urging a pragmatic approach and seeking an opportunity to address any deficiencies rather than facing punitive action.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the pleadings, documentary evidence, and oral testimonies presented by both parties. A thorough examination of the EPA's evidence, particularly the laboratory analysis reports from its accredited laboratory, leaves little room for doubt regarding the consistent exceedances of NEQS by the Respondent. The detailed inspection reports, corroborated by photographic evidence, clearly depict instances of inefficient ETP operation and uncontrolled emissions. The expert witness from the EPA provided a clear and convincing technical assessment, further strengthening the Complainant's case. While the Respondent challenged the sampling methodology and chain of custody, it failed to provide credible counter-evidence or expert testimony to substantiate these claims. The internal monitoring reports submitted by the Respondent were found to be insufficient in scope and frequency to counter the comprehensive data provided by the EPA. The Tribunal notes that environmental violations, particularly those involving hazardous substances and persistent non-compliance, pose a grave threat to public health and the ecosystem, and cannot be overlooked on the basis of
arguments related to economic contributions or unsubstantiated challenges to sampling protocols.
Having meticulously reviewed the entire record, this Tribunal finds the evidence presented by the Complainant to be overwhelming, credible, and sufficient to establish the Respondent’s persistent non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. The detailed laboratory reports, coupled with inspection findings and expert testimony, conclusively demonstrate that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited has been discharging industrial effluent and emitting gaseous pollutants significantly exceeding permissible limits, thereby causing environmental degradation and posing a risk to public health. The Respondent's generalized assertions regarding sampling integrity and chain of custody, unsupported by specific counter-evidence or expert analysis, fail to discredit the scientifically sound methodology employed by the EPA's accredited laboratory. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 3,200,000/- (Rupees Three Million Two Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 640,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
