This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), alleging contraventions of various provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The complaint, bearing No. 25/2024, was instituted before this Tribunal on March 12, 2024, seeking appropriate action, including penalties and remedial measures, against the Respondent for its alleged environmentally non-compliant operations. The Respondent, a textile manufacturing unit located within the jurisdiction of Lahore, was duly served and appeared through its authorized representative and legal counsel.
The Complainant’s primary allegations against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited are multifaceted. Firstly, it is contended that the Respondent has been operating its industrial facility without obtaining the requisite Environmental Approval (Initial Environmental Examination – IEE or Environmental Impact Assessment – EIA) under Section 12 of the Act, despite being an industrial activity falling within the purview of Schedule I and II of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Agency (Review of IEE/EIA Regulations), 2000. Secondly, and more critically, the Complainant alleges that the Respondent is engaged in the discharge of industrial effluent directly into the public sewerage system/drain without adequate treatment, thereby exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial liquid effluents, in blatant violation of Section 11 of the Act. Specific parameters cited as being in excess of NEQS include Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. Thirdly, the complaint asserts that the Respondent's industrial operations generate significant air emissions, specifically particulate matter (PM10), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), which are also reported to be exceeding NEQS, contributing to ambient air pollution, contrary to Section 15 of the Act. Lastly, the Complainant states that despite repeated inspections, issuance of Show Cause Notices, and Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) under Section 16 of the Act, the Respondent has consistently failed to implement
necessary corrective measures or demonstrate a genuine commitment to environmental compliance.
The Respondent, through its authorized representative and legal counsel, vehemently denied certain allegations and offered explanations for others. While acknowledging its operations involve processes that generate effluent and emissions, the Respondent contended that it is in the process of upgrading its existing Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and has initiated steps to improve its air pollution control mechanisms. It was
argued that the delays in achieving full compliance were due to technical challenges and significant capital investment required for state-of-the-art treatment facilities. The Respondent submitted that it had already engaged consultants and placed orders for new machinery to enhance its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air emission control systems, providing documentary evidence of purchase orders and project timelines. Furthermore, the Respondent questioned the methodology and frequency of EPA's sampling, suggesting that some readings might not be entirely representative of their continuous operational efforts. They also highlighted their contribution to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to consider a pragmatic approach that allows for a phased compliance plan rather than immediate punitive action.
During the proceedings, the Complainant presented detailed inspection reports, laboratory analysis reports of effluent and air emission samples collected on various dates, photographs depicting the alleged discharge points, copies of Show Cause Notices, and Environmental Protection Orders previously issued to the Respondent. These documents consistently indicated exceedances of NEQS for the parameters specified in the complaint. The Respondent, in turn, submitted its internal monitoring reports, project proposals for upgrades, and financial statements detailing investments made towards environmental improvements. The Tribunal carefully considered all submissions, including the Respondent's arguments regarding technical feasibility and economic impact.
Having heard both parties and meticulously reviewed the documentary and technical evidence presented, this Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established, through credible and consistent evidence, that the Respondent's industrial operations have been in contravention of key provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards. Specifically:
1.  **Violation of Section 12 (Environmental Approval):** The Respondent's admission of not possessing the requisite IEE/EIA approval for its current scale of operations, despite being a scheduled activity under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Agency (Review of IEE/EIA Regulations), 2000, constitutes a clear violation.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
