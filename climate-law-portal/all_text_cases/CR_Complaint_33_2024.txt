This Tribunal was seized of the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a company engaged in the manufacturing of various chemicals, having its industrial unit located in Kala Shah Kaku, Sheikhupura District. The complaint, registered on 15th March 2024, sought legal action against the Respondent for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS").
It is alleged by the Complainant that the Respondent has been operating its chemical manufacturing facility without adequately treating its industrial effluent and controlling its gaseous emissions, thereby discharging pollutants into the environment in excess of the prescribed NEQS. Specifically, the Complainant stated that multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials on various dates, including 10th January 2024, 28th February 2024, and 5th March 2024, revealed persistent non-compliance. The allegations detail that the effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points consistently showed levels of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals (specifically Chromium and Lead) significantly exceeding the NEQS. Furthermore, air emission monitoring indicated sulphur dioxide (SO2) and nitrogen oxides (NOx) concentrations above the permissible limits. The Complainant further alleged that despite repeated warning notices issued on 15th January 2024 and 1st March 2024, followed by an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) issued on 8th March 2024 under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, directing the Respondent to install and operationalize an effective effluent treatment plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices (APCDs), the Respondent failed to comply, demonstrating a disregard for environmental regulations and public health.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included certified copies of inspection reports detailing the observations made by EPA teams during their visits, photographic evidence depicting the discharge of untreated effluent into a nearby drain, and detailed laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory. These laboratory reports, bearing test dates corresponding to the inspection dates, consistently indicated pollutant concentrations far exceeding the NEQS for industrial effluents and air emissions. Furthermore, copies of the warning notices and the EPO, along with acknowledgements of their receipt by the Respondent, were submitted. The Complainant also produced an affidavit from Mr. Sarmad Khan, Environmental Officer, who was part of the inspection teams and attested to the findings. The Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its legal counsel, Mr. Zahid Ali, vehemently denied the allegations. The Respondent contended that it had an operational ETP and APCDs, and that any non-compliance was either sporadic or a result of temporary operational glitches, which were promptly addressed. The Respondent submitted its internal monitoring reports, although these were not certified by an independent accredited laboratory. It also presented maintenance logs for its ETP and APCDs. Counsel for the Respondent argued that the EPA's sampling methodology might have been flawed or that samples were not representative, and that the company was committed to environmental compliance, having invested significantly in pollution control infrastructure. However, no independent laboratory reports countering the EPA's findings were presented.
The relevant legal provisions governing this complaint are primarily found in the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, mandates that no person shall discharge or emit or allow the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the NEQS. Section 12 requires every proponent of a new project to file an Initial Environmental
Examination (IEE) or Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) for approval, which is not directly relevant to the present complaint concerning ongoing operational violations. However, Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, empowers the Agency to issue an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) to any person causing or about to cause pollution in contravention of the provisions of this Act. Failure to comply with an EPO is a serious offense. Furthermore, Section 17 of PEPA, 1997, prescribes penalties for contravention of the provisions of the Act, including fines and, in certain cases, imprisonment.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both parties and meticulously reviewed the evidence placed on record. The Complainant's case is built upon a solid foundation of documented inspections, verifiable laboratory analyses from an accredited EPA laboratory, photographic evidence, and the sworn affidavit of an Environmental Officer. The consistency of the pollutant levels exceeding NEQS across multiple inspection dates, coupled with the detailed laboratory reports specifying COD, BOD, TSS, heavy metals (Chromium and Lead), SO2, and NOx, leaves no room for doubt regarding the persistent nature of the Respondent's non-compliance. The issuance of warning notices and a subsequent EPO, with acknowledgements of receipt, further underscores the Respondent's failure to take timely and effective corrective measures despite repeated opportunities.
Conversely, the Respondent's defense, while asserting operational ETP and APCDs, lacked independent corroboration. The internal monitoring reports submitted
lacked the necessary independent verification to counter the robust evidence presented by the Complainant. The mere assertion of having operational ETP and APCDs, without presenting credible, independently certified data demonstrating compliance with NEQS, is insufficient to rebut the specific, scientific findings of the EPA. The Respondent's argument regarding potentially flawed sampling methodology or non-representative samples remained unsubstantiated by any expert testimony or counter-analysis reports. Furthermore, the maintenance logs, while indicative of some activity, did not prove the effectiveness of the pollution control systems in meeting environmental standards.
Based on the overwhelming and consistent evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established that the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, has persistently violated Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent and emitting air pollutants in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards. Moreover, the Respondent's deliberate failure to comply with the Environmental Protection Order issued on 8th March 2024 constitutes a clear contravention of Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, demonstrating a serious disregard for environmental regulations and the authority of the Environmental Protection Agency.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
Therefore, in light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal hereby declares M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited GUILTY of violating Sections 11 and 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
The Tribunal issues the following orders:
1.  The Respondent shall, within sixty (60) days from the date of this order, install, upgrade, and fully operationalize an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) capable of consistently treating its industrial effluent and controlling its gaseous emissions to meet the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for all parameters specified in the Complainant's reports, including COD, BOD, TSS, Chromium, Lead, SO2, and NOx. The Respondent shall submit a detailed compliance plan and timeline within fifteen (15) days to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for approval.
2.  The Respondent shall, immediately upon operationalization of the upgraded ETP and APCDs, commence regular independent third-party monitoring of its effluent discharge and air emissions, submitting monthly compliance reports, certified by an EPA-accredited laboratory, to the EPA for a period of one (1) year. The costs of such monitoring shall be borne entirely by the Respondent.
3.  The EPA is directed to conduct follow-up inspections and monitoring to ensure strict compliance with this order and to report back to the Tribunal on the Respondent's progress.
In addition to the above directives, and in exercise of the powers conferred under Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the Tribunal imposes a penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** on M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited for the persistent and deliberate violations of environmental laws and non-compliance with the Environmental Protection Order. This penalty shall be paid to the Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
It is further ordered that, should the Respondent fail to comply with the directives regarding the installation/upgradation and operationalization of the ETP and APCDs within the stipulated sixty (60) days, a further penalty of **PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand only)** per day shall be imposed for each day of continued non-compliance thereafter, until full compliance is achieved.
The costs of these proceedings shall also be borne by the Respondent.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,100,000/- (Rupees One Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 220,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **27 September, 2023**.
