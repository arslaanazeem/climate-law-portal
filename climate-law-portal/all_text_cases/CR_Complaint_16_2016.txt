This judgment addresses Complaint No. 16/2016, lodged by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited, a prominent glass manufacturing facility located in the industrial zone of Lahore. The complaint was filed under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"), alleging persistent environmental violations by the respondent. The Tribunal, presided over by Mr. Justice (R) Chaudhry Muhammad Iqbal, has carefully considered the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties to determine the culpability of the respondent and to ensure the enforcement of environmental laws for the protection of public health and the environment.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has been operating its industrial unit without adequately treating its industrial emissions and effluents, thereby causing significant air and water pollution. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  **Air Pollution**: Discharge of gaseous emissions, including but not limited to particulate matter, oxides of nitrogen (NOx), and sulfur dioxide (SOx), from its stacks, exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial gaseous emissions. This was observed to contribute to ambient air quality degradation in the surrounding residential and agricultural areas.
2.  **Water Pollution**: Discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial wastewater/effluent directly into the municipal sewerage system or nearby water bodies, with parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH values consistently exceeding the NEQS for industrial effluents.
3.  **Non-compliance with Environmental Protection Order (EPO)**: That despite numerous inspections, issuance of show-cause notices, and a formal Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of the Act, directing the respondent to install and operate appropriate pollution control devices for both air and water, the respondent failed to comply with the directives contained therein within the stipulated timeframe. This continued non-compliance constituted a direct contravention of the Act and the specific directions of the EPA.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated various intervals between 2014 and 2016, meticulously prepared by EPA field teams, detailing the observations of polluting discharges. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from EPA-accredited laboratories were submitted, which contained analytical data of air emission samples collected from the respondent's stacks and effluent samples collected from its discharge points. These reports consistently demonstrated that the emissions and effluents released by M/s Ghani Glass Limited significantly exceeded the prescribed NEQS, thus establishing a prima facie case of violation of Section 11 of the Act. Further evidence included copies of show-cause notices issued to the respondent, the formal Environmental Protection Order (EPO No. EPA/LH/2
015-001 dated October 20, 2015**, which explicitly directed the respondent to cease the discharge of untreated emissions and effluents and to install necessary pollution control equipment within a period of ninety (90) days. The Complainant further provided documentary evidence demonstrating the service of this EPO to the respondent and subsequent follow-up inspection reports confirming the respondent's
continued non-compliance with the directives of the EPO, thereby necessitating the filing of the present complaint before this Tribunal under Section 17 of the Act.
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its learned counsel, vehemently denied the allegations, asserting that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental stewardship. The Respondent contended that it has made significant investments in environmental management systems and pollution control technologies. While acknowledging certain operational challenges, the Respondent argued that the EPA's sampling methodologies and laboratory analyses might not have accurately reflected the overall operational compliance. Furthermore, the Respondent claimed that it was in the process of upgrading its existing pollution control infrastructure and had already initiated steps to procure advanced equipment for both air and water treatment. It was submitted that the timelines provided in the EPO were overly ambitious given the complexity and financial outlay required for such large-scale installations, and that the company was making good faith efforts towards achieving full compliance, albeit at a slower pace than stipulated. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
