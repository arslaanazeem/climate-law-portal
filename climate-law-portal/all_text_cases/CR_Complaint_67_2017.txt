Presided by: Justice (R) Ali Baqar Najafi
This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), regarding alleged violations of environmental laws and standards. The complaint was lodged under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), seeking remedial action and imposition of penalties for non-compliance with National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and other environmental regulations. The Tribunal is seized of the matter pursuant to its jurisdiction conferred by Section 22 of PEPA, 1997.
The EPA's allegations against M/s Packages Limited primarily revolve around the discharge of industrial effluent and emission of air pollutants exceeding the prescribed NEQS, as well as improper management of hazardous waste. Specifically, the complainant asserted that routine inspections conducted at the Respondent's industrial premises located in Lahore on multiple dates, including 15.03.2017 and 07.05.2017, revealed significant environmental non-compliance. It was alleged that the Respondent's effluent treatment plant (ETP), despite its existence, was either not operating efficiently or was bypassed, leading to the discharge of untreated or partially treated wastewater into the municipal sewerage system, eventually polluting surface waters. Laboratory analyses of samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on the aforementioned dates indicated concentrations of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and Oil & Grease substantially above the NEQS limits for industrial effluents. Furthermore, the complaint highlighted concerns regarding stack emissions from the Respondent's boiler house, with monitoring results showing particulate matter (PM) and oxides of nitrogen (NOx) exceeding the NEQS. The EPA also pointed to inadequate measures for the segregation, storage, and disposal of hazardous waste, particularly sludge from the ETP and chemical residues, in contravention of the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003. A show-cause notice dated 25.05.2017 was issued to the Respondent, to which the EPA found the reply unsatisfactory, prompting the filing of this complaint.
To substantiate its claims, the EPA presented a comprehensive set of documentary evidence, including detailed inspection reports prepared by environmental inspectors, chain of custody forms for samples, and official laboratory analysis reports from the certified environmental laboratory of the EPA, clearly indicating the exceedances of NEQS for various parameters. Copies of the show-cause notice issued to the Respondent, along with the Respondent's reply, were also submitted. Additionally, photographs depicting the alleged discharge points and waste storage areas formed part of the evidence. The Complainant's witnesses, including the Assistant Director and a laboratory analyst, testified to the authenticity of the reports and the procedures followed. The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel and representatives, vehemently denied the persistent non-compliance. They presented evidence of significant investments made in upgrading their ETP and air pollution control devices (APCDs) over the past several years. They submitted technical reports from independent consultants outlining the design and operational parameters of their environmental control infrastructure. It was contended that any observed exceedances were isolated incidents, possibly due to operational fluctuations or maintenance activities, and not indicative of habitual non-compliance. The Respondent also produced records of environmental monitoring conducted by third-party laboratories, which, in some instances, showed compliance with NEQS. Furthermore, they provided documentation of their hazardous waste management practices, including contracts with licensed waste handlers for disposal and records of internal training for staff on environmental protocols. The Respondent argued that they are an environmentally conscious corporate entity and are committed to sustainable operations.
The legal framework governing this matter is primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Specifically, Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount, concentration, or level which is in excess of the NEQS. Section 13 addresses the handling, storage, and disposal of hazardous substances. The power of the Tribunal to hear and decide complaints and impose penalties is derived from Section 16 and Section 17, respectively. The National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents (S.R.O. 549(I)/200
000, read with subsequent amendments, and the National Environmental Quality Standards for Air Emissions (S.R.O. 721(I)/2000), along with the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003, provide the specific benchmarks and regulatory requirements against which the Respondent's environmental performance is to be assessed.
Having meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence, heard the testimonies of witnesses, and considered the arguments presented by both parties, the Tribunal proceeds to analyze the merits of the complaint.
**ANALYSIS AND FINDINGS**
The core of the EPA's complaint rests on the laboratory analyses of samples collected from the Respondent's premises on 15.03.2017 and 07.05.2017, which consistently indicated exceedances of NEQS for various parameters in both effluent discharge and air emissions. The chain of custody forms and the certification of the EPA's laboratory lend substantial credibility to these reports. The inspection reports further corroborate the operational deficiencies observed.
While the Respondent has presented evidence of investments in environmental infrastructure and third-party monitoring reports showing compliance in some instances, these do not entirely refute the specific violations highlighted by the EPA on the aforementioned dates. The Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's commitment to environmental sustainability as a corporate entity and the efforts made to upgrade their facilities. However, the presence of an ETP or APCDs does not automatically guarantee compliance if these systems are not operated optimally, are bypassed, or are insufficient to treat the pollutant load to NEQS levels. The argument of "isolated incidents" becomes difficult to sustain when multiple parameters (BOD, COD, TSS, Oil & Grease, PM, NOx) were found to be in exceedance on two separate inspection dates, suggesting a more systemic issue in operational control or treatment efficiency rather than mere fluctuations.
Specifically, the Tribunal finds that the EPA has successfully discharged its burden of proof regarding the discharge of industrial effluent exceeding NEQS limits for BOD, COD, TSS, and Oil & Grease on 15.03.2017 and 07.05.2017, in contravention of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997. Similarly, the evidence regarding stack emissions exceeding NEQS for PM and NOx on the same dates also stands proven, constituting a violation of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997. Furthermore, the observations regarding inadequate segregation, storage, and disposal practices for hazardous waste, including ETP sludge and chemical residues, as
observed during the inspections and supported by photographic evidence, indicates a failure to comply with Section 14 of PEPA, 1997, read with the hazardous Substances Rules, 2003. While the Respondent presented contracts with licensed waste handlers, the on-site management and storage practices observed by the EPA inspectors at the time of inspection were clearly deficient and posed a potential risk to the environment. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent failed to demonstrate robust and consistent adherence to the prescribed standards for hazardous waste management.
The Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's overall corporate commitment and past investments in environmental infrastructure. However, these factors do not absolve the Respondent from liability for the specific violations proven by the EPA's evidence on the dates in question. The consistent exceedances across multiple parameters and on different dates point towards a need for more rigorous operational control, maintenance, and potentially further upgrades to ensure sustained compliance with NEQS and environmental regulations.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing analysis and findings, the Tribunal holds M/s Packages Limited, the Respondent, liable for violations of Section 11 and Section 13 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the rules framed thereunder.
Therefore, the Tribunal issues the following order:
1.  **Immediate Remedial Actions:** The Respondent shall, within **ninety (90) days** from the date of this order, undertake and complete the necessary upgrades and operational adjustments to its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) to ensure consistent and continuous compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents and air emissions.
2.  **Hazardous Waste Management:** The Respondent shall, within **sixty (60) days** from the date of this order, implement and demonstrate a comprehensive and compliant system for the segregation, secure storage, and proper disposal of all hazardous waste generated at its premises, strictly in accordance with Section 14 of PEPA, 1997, and the hazardous Substances Rules, 2003. This includes ensuring proper labeling, secure containment, and regular disposal through licensed waste handlers, with complete record-keeping and manifest systems.
3.  **Submission of Compliance Reports:** The Respondent shall submit quarterly compliance reports to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, for a period of **one (1) year** following the completion of the remedial actions specified in paras 1 and 2 above. These reports shall detail the operational efficiency of its ETP and APCDs, and include third-party laboratory analysis reports confirming continuous compliance with NEQS. These reports shall also include detailed records of hazardous waste generation and disposal.
4.  **Environmental Penalty:** Pursuant to Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, for the proven violations of Section 11 and Section 13 of the Act, a penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand Only)** is hereby imposed upon M/s Packages Limited.
5.  **Payment of Penalty:** The imposed penalty shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this order. Failure to pay the penalty within the stipulated time may result in further legal action, including recovery as arrears of land revenue, as provided under Section 17(2) of PEPA, 1997.
6.  **Continuing Non-Compliance:** The Tribunal warns the Respondent that any future or continuing non-compliance with environmental laws and the directives of this order shall attract more severe penalties, including daily fines as provided under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, and other appropriate enforcement actions.
There shall be no order as to costs.
