This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent manufacturing entity located in Lahore. The complaint, lodged under Section 19 of the Punjab Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), seeks the imposition of penalties and remedial measures for alleged violations of environmental standards and regulations. The Tribunal convened to ascertain the veracity of the allegations, review the evidence presented by both parties, and render a decision in accordance with the law.
The Complainant, through its legal counsel, Mr. Ahmed Jamal, Senior Legal Advisor, EPA, alleged that the Respondent's industrial facility was operating in contravention of several environmental protection laws and regulations. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  Discharge of industrial effluent directly into the public sewerage system and ultimately into surface water bodies without adequate treatment, resulting in pollutant concentrations exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial and municipal effluents, as notified under the PEPA, 1997.
2.  Emission of air pollutants from its manufacturing processes, particularly particulate matter and gaseous pollutants (e.g., NOx, SOx), exceeding the NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions.
3.  Improper management and disposal of hazardous and non-hazardous solid waste generated by its operations, leading to environmental degradation and potential health risks.
4.  Failure to submit an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) or Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) report, or to obtain the requisite Environmental Approval (NOC) for significant expansions or modifications to its plant operations, as mandated by Section 12 of the PEPA, 1997, and its associated regulations.
5.  Non-compliance with previous directives and warning notices issued by the EPA regarding the aforementioned violations.
The Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence to substantiate its claims. This included:
1.  **Inspection Reports**: Multiple inspection reports dated 15.03.2022, 28.04.2022, and 10.06.2022, prepared by EPA field teams, detailing observations of environmental non-compliance at the Respondent's facility, including visible discharge of coloured effluent and open dumping of waste.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports**: Official laboratory reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, confirming that samples of industrial effluent collected from the Respondent's discharge points on 15.03.2022 and 10.06.2022 showed concentrations of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals (e.g., Chromium, Lead) significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports from 28.04.2022 indicated elevated levels of particulate matter (PM2.5, PM10) and Sulphur Dioxide (SO2) near the Respondent's chimney stacks.
3.  **Photographic and Videographic Evidence**: Visual documentation depicting the alleged violations, such as untreated effluent discharge, waste accumulation, and emissions from stacks.
4.  **Correspondence**: Copies of warning notices and show-cause notices issued to the Respondent by the EPA, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies or lack thereof.
5.  **Witness Testimony**: The Assistant Director, EPA, and two environmental inspectors testified before the Tribunal, reiterating the findings of their reports and inspections.
The Respondent, represented by its legal counsel, Mr. Zahid Hussain, Advocate, contested the allegations. Its defence primarily rested on the following points:
1.  The Respondent claimed to have an operational effluent treatment plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices (APCDs), asserting that any exceedances were temporary or due to unforeseen operational upsets, and not systemic non-compliance.
2.  It argued that the sampling methods employed by the EPA were not always representative or conducted in its presence, and challenged the accuracy of some laboratory results.
3.  Regarding waste management, the Respondent claimed to have agreements with third-party contractors for waste disposal and asserted that any open dumping was incidental or by unauthorized individuals.
4.  The Respondent maintained that its operations were largely in compliance and that it was actively investing in upgrading its environmental infrastructure. It presented some internal monitoring reports, which, however, lacked independent verification and comprehensive parameters.
5.  It also argued that it had initiated the process for obtaining necessary environmental approvals for any recent expansions.
The relevant legal provisions invoked in this complaint are primarily from the Punjab Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and its subsidiary rules and regulations:
1.  **Section 11 (Pro
hibition of discharge or emission of pollutants exceeding established standards), Section 12 (Initial Environmental Examination and Environmental Impact Assessment), Section 13 (Prohibition of import of hazardous waste), Section 14 (Handling of hazardous substances), and Section 19 (Penalties for contravention of the Act). Additionally, the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial and municipal effluents and industrial gaseous emissions, promulgated under Section 11 of the PEPA, 1997, are central to determining compliance.
**TRIBUNAL'S FINDINGS AND ANALYSIS:**
The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent.
The Complainant's evidence, particularly the multiple inspection reports, independently verified laboratory analysis reports, and corroborating photographic/videographic evidence, provides a consistent and compelling narrative of environmental non-compliance by the Respondent. The laboratory reports unequivocally demonstrate that the Respondent's industrial effluent and air emissions consistently exceeded the prescribed NEQS for various critical parameters. The visible discharge of coloured effluent and open dumping of waste, as documented and testified, further substantiates the allegations of improper waste management. The issuance of multiple warning and show-cause notices by the EPA, followed by continued violations, indicates a pattern of disregard for environmental regulations and previous directives.
The Respondent's defence, while asserting the presence of environmental control infrastructure, largely failed to rebut the specific and detailed evidence presented by the Complainant.
1.  The claim of an operational ETP and APCDs is directly contradicted by the consistent exceedances of NEQS in the EPA's laboratory reports. Temporary operational upsets cannot account for the persistent nature of the violations observed over several months. The Respondent's internal monitoring reports lacked independent verification and comprehensive parameters, thus holding limited evidentiary value against the robust findings of the EPA.
2.  Challenges to the EPA's sampling methods were generic and not supported by concrete evidence
...concrete evidence or alternative valid analytical results. The EPA's sampling protocols are established under environmental regulations and their laboratory is accredited, lending significant weight to their findings. The Respondent's general assertions, without specific technical rebuttal or independently verified contradictory data, are insufficient to discredit the EPA's systematic evidence collection.
Regarding waste management, while the Respondent claimed agreements with third-party contractors, the ultimate responsibility for ensuring proper management and disposal of waste generated by its operations lies with the Respondent. The photographic and videographic evidence, coupled with inspection reports, clearly depicted improper onsite storage and open dumping, indicating a failure in the Respondent's internal waste management practices, irrespective of external contractor arrangements. This constitutes a direct violation of environmental standards designed to prevent soil and water contamination and public health hazards.
Finally, the Respondent's argument that it had "initiated the process" for obtaining necessary environmental approvals for expansions under Section 12 is inadequate. The law mandates obtaining the requisite Environmental Approval (NOC) *before* commencing significant expansions or modifications. Mere initiation of the process does not absolve the Respondent of the violation of operating without the legally required approval, which is a foundational requirement for ensuring sustainable development and preventing adverse environmental impacts.
The Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof. The evidence presented overwhelmingly demonstrates that M/s Packages Limited has been operating in persistent violation of Section 11 (Prohibition of discharge or emission of pollutants exceeding established standards), Section 12 (Initial Environmental Examination and Environmental Impact Assessment), and Section 14 (Handling of hazardous substances) of the Punjab Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) promulgated thereunder. The pattern of non-compliance, despite repeated warnings, indicates a serious dereliction of environmental responsibility.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal hereby issues the following final order and penalty under Section 19 of the Punjab Environmental Protection Act, 1997:
1.  **Declaration of Guilt:** M/s Packages Limited (the Respondent) is found guilty of contravening Sections 11, 12, and 14 of the Punjab Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial and municipal effluents and industrial gaseous emissions.
2.  **Monetary Penalty:** The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, is hereby directed to pay a monetary penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million Only)** to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
3.  **Remedial Measures and Compliance Directives:**
    a.  **Effluent Treatment:** The Respondent shall immediately take all necessary steps to upgrade, optimize, and ensure the efficient operation of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) to consistently meet the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial and municipal effluents. A detailed action plan for ETP upgrade and optimization, including timelines, shall be submitted to the EPA within **sixty (60) days**.
    b.  **Air Pollution Control:** The Respondent shall immediately implement measures to upgrade, optimize, and ensure the efficient operation of its Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) to consistently meet the prescribed NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions. A detailed action plan for APCD upgrade and optimization, including timelines, shall be submitted to the EPA within **sixty (60) days**.
    c.  **Waste Management:** The Respondent shall develop and implement a comprehensive Solid and Hazardous Waste Management Plan, ensuring proper segregation, storage, treatment, and disposal of all waste generated from its operations through EPA-approved facilities and contractors. All forms of open dumping of waste within or outside the premises shall cease immediately. The Waste Management Plan shall be submitted to the EPA within **ninety (90) days**.
    d.  **Environmental Approvals:** The Respondent shall complete the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) or Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) process, as applicable, and obtain the requisite Environmental Approval (NOC) from the EPA for all significant expansions or modifications to its plant operations that have been undertaken or are planned, within **one hundred and eighty (180) days**.
    e.  **Monitoring and Reporting:** The Respondent shall establish a robust internal environmental monitoring system and submit quarterly compliance reports to the EPA for a period of two years, detailing progress on all remedial measures and demonstrating continuous compliance with NEQS. The first such report shall be due three months from the date of this order.
4.  **Timeline for Full Compliance:** The Respondent shall achieve full compliance with all prescribed NEQS for effluent discharge and air emissions, and fully implement the approved waste management plan, within **six (6) months** from the date of this order.
5.  **Default Clause:** Failure to comply with any part of this order, including the payment of the monetary penalty or the implementation of the remedial measures within the specified timelines, shall result in the imposition of an additional daily fine of **PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand Only)** for each day of continuing contravention, as per Section 19(2) of the PEPA, 1997. **FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,100,000/- (Rupees Two Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 420,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
