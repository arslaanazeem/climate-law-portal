This judgment addresses the environmental complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited, a prominent textile manufacturing unit located in Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The complaint seeks enforcement of environmental laws and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal convened several hearings, heard arguments from both parties, and meticulously reviewed the evidence presented to arrive at a just and equitable decision in the interest of environmental protection and public health.
The Complainant, through its legal representative, alleged that M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent") was operating its textile dyeing and finishing processes without adequately treating its industrial effluent. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  Discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into the municipal sewerage system, which eventually flows into the River Ravi, thereby causing water pollution in contravention of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997.
2.  Analysis of effluent samples collected on multiple occasions, specifically on May 15, 2018, and August 02, 2018, revealed parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH values significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial effluents.
3.  Failure to install and operate a fully functional and efficient Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) despite repeated directives and notices from the EPA.
4.  Non-compliance with the conditions stipulated in the Environmental Approval (NOC) granted earlier, particularly regarding the regular monitoring and reporting of environmental performance.
5.  Absence of a proper mechanism for the management and disposal of hazardous waste generated from the dyeing processes, leading to environmental contamination.
6.  Failure to respond satisfactorily to the show-cause notice issued by the EPA on September 10, 2018, and subsequent final notice dated October 25, 2018.
The Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence to substantiate its claims. This included:
1.  **Inspection Reports:** Detailed reports from EPA field teams dated May 15, 2018, and August 02, 2018, documenting observations of dark-colored effluent being discharged from the Respondent's premises. The reports also noted the apparent lack of a fully operational ETP at the time of inspection or its inadequacy.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Certified laboratory reports from the EPA Environmental Monitoring Laboratory, Lahore, corresponding to the samples collected on the aforementioned dates. These reports clearly indicated that the effluent samples exceeded NEQS limits. For instance, the May 15, 2018, sample showed COD at 450 mg/L (NEQS: 150 mg/L), BOD5 at 280 mg/L (NEQS: 80 mg/L), and TSS at 250 mg/L (NEQS: 200 mg/L). Similar exceedances were recorded for the August 02, 2018, sample.
3.  **Correspondence:** Copies of the show-cause notice (No. AD/EPA/LHR/2018/543 dated September 10, 2018) and the final notice (No. AD/EPA/LHR/2018/698 dated October 25, 2018), along with proof of their delivery to the Respondent.
4.  **Witness Testimony:** The Assistant Director, EPA, Lahore, appeared before the Tribunal and testified under oath, reiterating the findings of the inspection teams and the non-compliance observed.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. Their defense primarily rested on the
following grounds:
1.  **Existence of an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP):** The Respondent asserted that it indeed possessed an Effluent Treatment Plant and had made substantial investments in its installation and maintenance. It was contended that any observed exceedances were either due to temporary operational malfunctions, routine maintenance, or an upgrade process that was underway at the time of the EPA inspections. They claimed to be committed to environmental compliance and had taken steps to improve their ETP's efficiency.
2.  **Challenging Sample Validity:** The Respondent questioned the methodology of sample collection and preservation, suggesting that the samples might not be truly representative of their routine discharge or that their integrity could have been compromised, leading to inaccurate laboratory results. They requested an independent analysis or re-sampling under joint supervision.
3.  **Contribution to Municipal Sewerage System:** It was argued that the Respondent's effluent constituted only a fraction of the overall discharge into the municipal sewerage system, which itself was not adequately equipped to treat industrial waste to stringent standards. They implied that holding them solely responsible for the ultimate pollution of River Ravi was disproportionate.
4.  **Active Compliance Efforts:** The Respondent submitted internal records and plans indicating their intention to further upgrade their ETP and implement better waste management practices, demonstrating a proactive approach to environmental stewardship, albeit with some operational delays. They also claimed to have responded to the EPA notices, albeit perhaps not to the complete satisfaction of the Complainant.
5.  **Economic Impact:** The Respondent highlighted its significant contribution to the national economy, employment generation, and commitment to sustainable business practices, urging the Tribunal to consider these factors while adjudicating.
**Tribunal's Deliberation and Findings:**
The Tribunal carefully considered the arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the documentary and testimonial evidence.
Firstly, regarding the Respondent's claim of possessing an ETP and its temporary malfunction or upgrade, the Tribunal noted that while an ETP might exist, the critical issue is its *operational efficiency* and *consistent compliance* with NEQS. The laboratory analysis reports presented by the Complainant, certified by an accredited EPA laboratory, consistently showed significant exceedances of key parameters (COD, BOD5, TSS, pH) on multiple dates. These reports serve as strong prima facie evidence of non-compliance. The Respondent failed to provide concrete, independently verifiable evidence (such as maintenance logs, third-party audit reports confirming the upgrade status and efficiency, or self-monitoring reports demonstrating compliance post-upgrade) to effectively counter the EPA's findings. A mere assertion of an ETP's existence or ongoing upgrade, without demonstrable results, does not absolve an industrial unit from its legal obligation to meet environmental standards.
Secondly, the challenge to sample validity raised by the Respondent was found to be unsubstantiated. The EPA's sampling procedures are standardized, and the samples were analyzed by its own certified laboratory. No credible evidence was presented to suggest any deviation from standard protocols or compromise of sample integrity. If the Respondent had doubts, it had the opportunity to conduct its own independent sampling and analysis, which it failed to do effectively at the relevant times.
Thirdly, the argument that the Respondent's effluent is a small part of the municipal sewerage system, and that the system itself is inadequate, does not provide a legal defense. Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, explicitly prohibits the discharge of any effluent or waste that causes or is likely to cause pollution in excess of the NEQS. The responsibility to treat industrial effluent to NEQS before discharge, irrespective of the ultimate receiving body or municipal infrastructure, rests squarely with the industrial unit. The cumulative impact of multiple industries discharging inadequately treated effluent can severely damage the environment, as evidenced by the pollution of the River Ravi.
Fourthly, while the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's stated commitment to environmental protection and its economic contributions, these factors do not permit deviation from mandatory environmental laws and standards. Compliance with NEQS is a non-negotiable legal requirement designed to protect public health and the environment. The repeated directives and notices from the EPA, coupled with the persistent exceedances, indicate a pattern of non-compliance that required the Tribunal's intervention. The Respondent's responses to the show-cause notices were not deemed satisfactory by the EPA, leading to this complaint.
Based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established that M/s Nishat Mills Limited was indeed discharging industrial effluent in contravention of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and that its effluent parameters consistently exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards. The Respondent failed to operate a fully functional and efficient Effluent Treatment Plant capable of achieving the prescribed standards, and also failed to adequately manage its hazardous waste, thereby causing environmental pollution.
**Order and Penalty:**
In light of the foregoing findings, and exercising the powers vested under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the Punjab
Environmental Tribunal, Lahore, hereby issues the following order:
1.  **Declaration of Contravention:** M/s Nishat Mills Limited (the Respondent) is hereby found to be in contravention of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, for discharging industrial effluent exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and for failing to adequately manage its hazardous waste,

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,900,000/- (Rupees One Million Nine Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 380,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
