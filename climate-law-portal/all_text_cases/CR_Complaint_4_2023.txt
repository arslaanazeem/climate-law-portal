**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 4/2023, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located in the industrial zone of Sheikhupura Road, Lahore. The complaint seeks redress for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter "PEPA, 1997"), specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent into the environment, causing significant pollution and posing a threat to public health and ecological balance. The Tribunal is seized of this matter under its mandate to adjudicate environmental disputes and enforce environmental laws within its jurisdiction.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent has been consistently operating its industrial facility without an adequately functioning Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) or, alternatively, failing to operate its existing ETP effectively. It was specifically averred that despite multiple warnings, notices, and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent continued to discharge industrial waste containing hazardous chemicals, dyes, and other pollutants directly into the adjacent irrigation canal and surrounding agricultural lands. The Complainant submitted that routine monitoring conducted by EPA officials on various dates, specifically on 15.01.2023, 03.02.2023, and 28.02.2023, revealed that the discharged effluent consistently exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for several critical parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels. These discharges, it was contended, are in direct contravention of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, which prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the NEQS. The Complainant further asserted that the Respondent's persistent non-compliance demonstrates a wilful disregard for environmental regulations and public welfare, necessitating the imposition of penalties and remedial orders under Section 17 of PEPA, 1997.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier comprising several key pieces of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA field teams, clearly documenting the visible pollution, the point of discharge, and photographic evidence depicting the discolored and foul-smelling effluent entering the canal. Crucially, three separate laboratory analysis reports from EPA-accredited laboratories were submitted, corresponding to the samples collected on the dates mentioned in the allegations. These reports unequivocally indicated that the effluent parameters were significantly above the prescribed NEQS, with COD levels consistently found to be 4-5 times higher than the permissible limit, and TSS levels exceeding limits by a factor of 3. Copies of the show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 20.01.2023 and 07.03.2023, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were also tendered. An expert witness, Dr. Ali Raza, an environmental chemist from the University of the Punjab, testified on the adverse impacts of such untreated discharge on aquatic life, soil fertility, and human health, corroborating the EPA's assessment of environmental degradation.
The Respondent, represented by its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. While acknowledging its industrial operations and the presence of an ETP, the Respondent contended that it was making sincere efforts to comply with environmental standards. It was submitted that the ETP was undergoing an upgrade and that any exceedance of NEQS was temporary and inadvertent, attributable to operational glitches or external factors beyond its immediate control. The Respondent claimed to have invested substantial capital in environmental management and presented purchase orders for new ETP components as evidence of its commitment. It also submitted a self-monitoring report for March 2023, claiming improved compliance, though this report lacked independent verification. The Respondent further argued that the sampling methodology employed by the EPA might have been flawed, leading to unrepresentative results, and that the impact on the environment was exaggerated. However, no independent laboratory reports challenging the EPA's findings were presented by the Respondent.
The legal framework governing this matter is primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Specifically, Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, is central to the complaint, prohibiting the discharge or emission of any
effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the NEQS. Furthermore, Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, grants the Agency the power to issue Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) to direct persons to take measures for the prevention or abatement of pollution. Crucially, Section 17 of PEPA, 1
997, provides for penalties for contravention of the provisions of the Act, including imprisonment and fines, and also empowers the Tribunal to issue appropriate remedial orders. Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, further grants the Tribunal the power to hear and decide complaints and appeals under the Act.

The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties and the arguments advanced by their respective counsels. **V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
