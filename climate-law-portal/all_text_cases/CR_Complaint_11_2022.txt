**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 11/2022, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a company engaged in the manufacturing of various chemicals, including caustic soda, chlorine, and allied products. The complaint seeks enforcement of environmental laws and regulations, imposition of penalties, and remedial measures against the Respondent for alleged violations of environmental standards.
The Complainant alleges that the Respondent has been consistently discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent and emitting noxious gases into the environment, causing significant pollution to water bodies and ambient air quality in violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA 1997). Specifically, it is contended that multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials revealed the Respondent's effluent discharge parameters, particularly for pH, Total Suspended Solids (TSS), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), and phenols, to be far in excess of the prescribed NEQS. Furthermore, the Complainant asserts that the Respondent's air emissions from its industrial processes, specifically concerning chlorine and particulate matter, have also exceeded permissible limits, impacting the health and well-being of the surrounding communities and the environment. The complaint details that despite numerous warning letters, show-cause notices, and final notices issued by the EPA on various dates, including a final notice dated 15.09.2022, the Respondent failed to take adequate corrective measures, thereby necessitating the filing of the present complaint before this Tribunal. The Complainant seeks stringent action, including the imposition of maximum penalties as prescribed under the law, and directions for the immediate installation and operationalization of effective pollution control devices.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included certified copies of inspection reports dated 12.04.2022, 05.07.2022, and 01.09.2022, conducted by EPA teams at the Respondent's facility. Crucially, these reports were accompanied by laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, which clearly indicated that samples of industrial effluent collected from the Respondent's discharge points on these dates showed pH levels ranging from 4.5 to 5.0 (NEQS limit: 6.5-8.5), TSS values between 350 mg/L and 420 mg/L (NEQS limit: 200 mg/L), COD levels from 500 mg/L to 650 mg/L (NEQS limit: 400 mg/L), and detectable levels of phenols where none should be present. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports from the same inspection dates revealed chlorine concentrations exceeding 3 ppm (NEQS limit: 1 ppm) and particulate matter (PM10) levels above 200 µg/m³ (NEQS limit: 150 µg/m³). The Complainant also submitted copies of the show-cause notices
, final notices, and the Respondent's replies (if any) which were deemed unsatisfactory by the EPA. These documents collectively demonstrated a persistent pattern of non-compliance and a failure on the part of the Respondent to address the environmental concerns despite repeated official communications and opportunities to rectify the situation.
The Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its learned counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It was contended that the company is a responsible corporate entity with a long-standing commitment to environmental protection. The Respondent submitted that it had already invested substantially in an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices, which were operational and regularly maintained. It was argued that the alleged exceedances in NEQS parameters might be due to sampling errors, calibration issues with EPA's equipment, or intermittent operational anomalies rather than a consistent failure. The Respondent also presented its own internal monitoring reports, although these were not from an EPA-accredited laboratory, claiming they showed compliance with most parameters. Furthermore, the Respondent pleaded
that it was in the process of upgrading its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and installing advanced air pollution control devices, and had already incurred significant expenses in this regard. The Respondent further submitted that it had requested the EPA for a reasonable timeframe to complete these upgrades and achieve full compliance, arguing that a sudden shutdown or excessive punitive measures would severely impact its operations, employment, and contribution to the national economy. It was also argued that the company was committed to sustainable practices and was actively exploring cleaner production technologies. The Respondent urged the Tribunal to take a holistic view, considering their ongoing efforts and the potential socio-economic ramifications of a harsh penalty.
This Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent, and meticulously reviewed the entire record, including the documentary evidence. The evidence presented by the Complainant, particularly the certified laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, stands as strong and credible proof. These reports, generated from samples collected on multiple dates, consistently indicate that the Respondent's industrial effluent and air emissions have repeatedly exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The deviations in pH, TSS, COD, phenols, chlorine, and particulate matter are not minor fluctuations but significant exceedances, suggesting a systemic failure in the Respondent's existing pollution control mechanisms or their inadequate operation.
While the Respondent's claims of investing in upgrades and their internal monitoring reports are noted, the Tribunal finds that these do not sufficiently counter the consistent and objective data provided by the EPA. Internal monitoring, while useful for self-assessment, lacks the independent verification required to establish compliance, especially when contradicted by statutory regulatory body's findings. The argument regarding sampling errors or intermittent operational anomalies also fails to hold ground given the recurring nature of the violations across different inspection dates. The responsibility for ensuring continuous compliance with NEQS lies squarely with the Respondent, as mandated by the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA 1997).
Section 16 of PEPA 1997 unequivocally prohibits any person from discharging or emitting any effluent, waste, air pollutant, or noise in an amount, concentration, or level that exceeds the NEQS. The persistent failure of the Respondent to adhere to these standards, despite repeated warnings and notices from the EPA, constitutes a clear violation of this fundamental provision. Section 26 of PEPA 1997 provides for penalties for contravention of the Act, including fines and directions for remedial measures. The 'polluter pays' principle, a cornerstone of environmental jurisprudence, dictates that the party responsible for pollution should bear the costs of managing it to prevent damage to human health or the environment.
In light of the overwhelming evidence establishing persistent non-compliance, this Tribunal finds that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited has failed to operate its industrial facility in accordance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and has thereby violated the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. The Respondent's ongoing efforts to upgrade its facilities, while commendable, do not absolve it of past and continuing violations. However, the Tribunal also takes into consideration the Respondent's stated commitment to environmental protection and its ongoing investment in upgrades. A balanced approach is necessary to ensure environmental protection while allowing industries to operate responsibly.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **27 March, 2022**.
