The present complaint, bearing No. 27/2020, has been filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), Lahore, invoking the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 21 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"). The complaint seeks the imposition of penalties and remedial measures against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore, for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations. The core issue before this Tribunal is to determine whether the Respondent has contravened the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) by discharging industrial effluent exceeding permissible limits into the environment.
The gravamen of the complaint centers on the Respondent's alleged failure to adequately treat its industrial effluent before discharge, thereby causing environmental pollution. Specifically, the Complainant alleges that multiple inspections conducted at the Respondent's premises revealed consistent breaches of the NEQS. It is contended that despite repeated warnings, show-cause notices, and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent continued to discharge untreated or inadequately treated wastewater containing high levels of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and other harmful pollutants, including dyes and heavy metals, into the public sewerage system/open drains, ultimately contaminating local water bodies. The Complainant further asserts that the Respondent's Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP), if functional, was either inefficiently operated or insufficient to meet the prescribed standards, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental protection laws.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a dossier of evidence. This included a series of inspection reports dated 15.03.2020, 28.05.2020, and 12.08.2020, conducted by EPA officials, detailing the observations of non-compliant discharge. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the certified EPA laboratory, corresponding to samples collected during these inspections. These reports consistently indicated that the Respondent's discharged effluent exceeded the NEQS for various parameters, particularly BOD (recorded as high as 450 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), COD (up to 1200 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), and TSS (up to 300 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L). Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 20.03.2020 and 05.06.2020, along with the Respondent's replies, were placed on record, demonstrating the administrative steps taken prior to filing the complaint. An expert witness from the EPA's technical wing also testified regarding the environmental impact of such pollution.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, vehemently denied the allegations of willful non-compliance. Through its written statement and oral submissions, the Respondent contended that it operates a state-of-the-art ETP, which is regularly maintained and upgraded. It was argued that any deviations from NEQS were either isolated incidents, attributable to temporary operational issues, or a result of sampling methodology discrepancies by the Complainant. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports, although not from an independent certified laboratory, claiming compliance with NEQS. It also highlighted significant investments made in environmental infrastructure and expressed its commitment to sustainable practices. The Respondent further argued that it had responded to all show-cause notices and had undertaken remedial measures, including process optimization and ETP enhancements, to address the EPA's concerns. The Respondent further requested that the Tribunal consider its significant contributions to the national economy and employment generation, urging a lenient view given its commitment to rectifying any identified shortcomings.
The Tribunal has meticulously considered the pleadings, documentary evidence, and oral arguments presented by both
parties. A careful and thorough analysis of the voluminous record, including the laboratory analysis reports, inspection reports, and the responses to show-cause notices, leads this Tribunal to conclude that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof.
The laboratory analysis reports from the certified EPA laboratory, consistently showing effluent parameters significantly exceeding the NEQS, particularly for BOD, COD, and TSS, constitute compelling and irrefutable evidence of environmental violations. These reports, being generated by an independent and certified authority, carry substantial weight and credibility. The Respondent's contention regarding isolated incidents or sampling methodology discrepancies, while noted, is not supported by sufficient counter-evidence to discredit the consistent findings across multiple inspections and analyses. The internal monitoring reports presented by the Respondent, lacking independent certification, cannot effectively rebut the robust evidence provided by the Complainant.
Furthermore, the repeated issuance of show-cause notices and the Respondent's responses confirm that M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited was fully aware of the alleged non-compliance and had ample opportunity to rectify the situation. While the Respondent claims to have made investments in its ETP and undertaken remedial measures, the persistence of non-compliant discharges, as evidenced by later inspection reports, indicates that these measures were either inadequate, belated, or inefficiently implemented to meet the statutory requirements. The Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's contribution to the national economy and employment; however, such contributions do not absolve any entity from its statutory obligations to protect the environment, especially when the health and well-being of the public and the integrity of natural resources are at stake. Environmental protection is a non-negotiable aspect of sustainable industrial development.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal has carefully examined the inspection report, laboratory analysis results, and the photographic evidence produced by the Complainant. The measured pollutant concentrations significantly exceed the NEQS limits and the Respondent has failed to rebut this evidence with credible contrary data. The defense regarding ETP maintenance does not excuse ongoing pollution discharge and cannot serve as a justification for non-compliance with mandatory environmental standards.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,300,000/- (Rupees One Million Three Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 260,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **10 June, 2023**.
