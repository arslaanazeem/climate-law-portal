**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 56/2019, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, on October 25, 2019, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent" or "the Company") concerning alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Complainant seeks stringent action, including the imposition of penalties, against the Respondent for their continuous failure to comply with environmental laws and standards, thereby causing significant environmental degradation and posing risks to public health.
The Complainant's core allegations assert that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of various chemicals, has been consistently discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into public sewers and emitting gaseous pollutants into the atmosphere, exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards. Specifically, the EPA alleged violations of NEQS for industrial effluent discharge, particularly concerning parameters such as Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and heavy metals. Furthermore, the complaint detailed excessive emissions of particulate matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) from the Respondent's stacks, contravening the NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions. The EPA submitted that despite repeated inspections, issuance of show-cause notices, and directives to install or upgrade their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs), the Respondent failed to take effective remedial measures, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental regulations. The Complainant emphasized that the Respondent's activities were causing water pollution in downstream agricultural areas and air pollution affecting local communities, leading to public health concerns and ecological damage.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple site inspection reports dated June 12, 2018, February 05, 2019, and August 20, 2019, highlighting the operational deficiencies of the Respondent's ETP and APCDs, and visual observations of polluted discharges and emissions. Crucially, the EPA submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on various dates, which consistently showed parameters exceeding the NEQS. For instance, the report dated March 15, 2019, indicated BOD levels of 150 mg/L (against a standard of 80 mg/L), COD of 350 mg/
L (against a standard of 200 mg/L), and TSS of 200 mg/L (against a standard of 200 mg/L). Similar exceedances were noted for heavy metals, specifically lead and chromium, in multiple samples. Regarding air emissions, monitoring reports attached to the complaint, particularly from a third-party accredited laboratory dated July 01
, 2019, showed PM levels of 250 mg/Nm³ (against a standard of 150 mg/Nm³), SO2 levels of 1200 mg/Nm³ (against a standard of 600 mg/Nm³), and NOx levels of 800 mg/Nm³ (against a standard of 400 mg/Nm³), all significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS. The Complainant also furnished copies of multiple show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, dated April 10, 2018, January 15, 2019, and September 05, 2019, along with their unsatisfactory replies, demonstrating a pattern of non-compliance despite official warnings and opportunities to rectify the situation. Expert opinions from environmental consultants engaged by the EPA further corroborated the findings, emphasizing the potential long-term damage to the ecosystem and public health.
The Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its authorized representative, vehemently denied the allegations of deliberate and continuous non-compliance. The Respondent contended that they are a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection and have made substantial investments in environmental management systems. They argued that while occasional exceedances might have occurred due to operational exigencies, these were not reflective of a persistent disregard for environmental laws. The Respondent submitted that their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) were functional and regularly maintained, albeit acknowledging that these systems might require periodic upgrades to meet evolving standards and increased production capacities. They presented a report from their internal environmental department, outlining various measures taken, such as process optimization, partial upgrades to certain ETP components, and plans for a more comprehensive overhaul. They also requested additional time to implement further technological improvements, citing financial constraints and the complexity of integrating new pollution control technologies into an existing large-scale chemical manufacturing facility. The Respondent also questioned the methodology of sample collection and analysis in some instances, suggesting that certain samples might not have been representative of their routine discharge or emissions, and that external factors could have influenced the results.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence presented by both parties and heard detailed arguments during several hearings held between November 2019 and February 2020. The core issue before this Tribunal was to ascertain whether the Respondent had indeed violated the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and the NEQS, and if so, to what extent and with what consequences.
Upon careful consideration of the evidence, particularly the consistent and repeated exceedances of NEQS as demonstrated by the laboratory analysis reports provided by the EPA, the Tribunal finds the Complainant's case to be substantially proven. The site inspection reports consistently highlighted deficiencies in the Respondent's pollution control infrastructure and practices. The argument put forth by the Respondent regarding operational exigencies or unrepresentative samples, while noted, does not sufficiently negate the overwhelming evidence of persistent non-compliance over an extended period. The repeated issuance of show-cause notices and the Respondent's failure to take effective, lasting remedial measures demonstrate a clear pattern of negligence and a lack of earnest commitment to environmental compliance, despite being fully aware of their statutory obligations. The excuses of financial constraints and complexity, while understandable to some degree, cannot override the paramount importance of public health and environmental integrity. PEPA, 1997, particularly Sections 11 and 12, mandates compliance with NEQS, and Section 16 empowers the Tribunal to take stringent action against defaulters. The continuous discharge of untreated or inadequately treated effluent and emissions exceeding NEQS poses significant threats to water bodies, soil quality, air quality, and consequently, to human health and biodiversity in the surrounding areas. The "polluter pays" principle is a fundamental tenet of environmental law, requiring those who cause environmental damage to bear the costs of remediation and prevention.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, and exercising the powers vested in this Tribunal under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, particularly Section 17 thereof, it is hereby ordered as follows:
1.  **Cessation of Violations:** M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, the Respondent, is immediately directed to cease and desist from discharging any industrial effluent or emitting gaseous pollutants that do not conform to the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
2.  **Compliance Plan Submission:** The Respondent shall, within sixty (60) days from the date of this judgment, submit to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, a comprehensive and detailed compliance plan, including specific timelines, for the immediate upgrade, repair, and efficient operation of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) to ensure full and continuous compliance with all relevant NEQS. This plan must be approved by the EPA.
3.  **Installation of Online Monitoring Systems:** The Respondent shall install continuous online monitoring systems for both effluent discharge and gaseous emissions, approved by the EPA, within one hundred and eighty (180) days from the date of this judgment, and provide real-time data access to the EPA.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,900,000/- (Rupees One Million Nine Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 380,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
