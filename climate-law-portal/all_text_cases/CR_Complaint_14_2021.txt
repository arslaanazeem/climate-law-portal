This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent") under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"). The complaint pertains to alleged violations of environmental standards and regulations concerning the discharge of industrial effluent from the Respondent's textile manufacturing facility located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The Tribunal was seized of the matter following repeated failures by the Respondent to comply with notices issued by the EPA regarding their environmental performance.
The Complainant alleged that the Respondent's textile manufacturing unit was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into the municipal sewerage system, which eventually leads to the River Ravi, thereby causing significant water pollution. Specifically, the EPA contended that multiple inspections and subsequent laboratory analyses of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points consistently revealed concentrations of various pollutants, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and heavy metals (specifically Chromium and Lead), exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Complainant further asserted that the Respondent had failed to install and/or effectively operate an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) commensurate with the scale of their operations and the nature of their waste, despite having been issued multiple warnings, show-cause notices, and directives for compliance under Sections 8, 11, and 16 of PEPA, 1997. The allegations also included a failure to submit regular self-monitoring reports as required by law and the conditions of their Environmental Approval.
The Complainant presented a robust body of evidence to substantiate its claims. This included:
1.  **Inspection Reports:** Detailed inspection reports dated 15.03.2020, 22.07.2020, and 10.11.2020, prepared by EPA field teams, documenting the observations of direct discharge, the absence of a fully functional ETP, and the visual characteristics of the effluent.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Three separate accredited laboratory analysis reports (Report No. EPA/LAB/2020/03-112, EPA/LAB/2020/07-215, and EPA/LAB/
2020/11-301) corresponding to the inspection dates, clearly indicating the exceedance of NEQS for COD, BOD, TSS, pH, Chromium, and Lead.
3.  **Correspondence:** Copies of warning notices (dated 01.04.2020, 05.08.2020), show-cause notices (dated 15.11.2020), and final directives (dated 01.12.2020) issued to the Respondent, outlining the violations and demanding corrective actions, along with acknowledgements of receipt.
4.  **Expert Witness Testimony:** The testimony of the EPA's Lead Environmental Scientist, who elaborated on the analytical findings, the environmental impact of the pollutants, and the inadequacy of the Respondent's existing pollution control measures.
The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, appeared through their legal counsel and submitted a written reply, denying the allegations in part. They contended that they are a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental compliance and claimed to have an existing Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) which, they asserted, was operational, albeit perhaps undergoing upgrades or maintenance at the time of some inspections. They argued that some exceedances might be attributable to factors beyond their direct control, such as fluctuations in raw material quality or variations in production processes. The Respondent also questioned the methodology of sample collection and the chain of custody for the laboratory analyses, suggesting potential irregularities. They requested more time to fully implement advanced treatment technologies and bring their operations into complete compliance.
The Tribunal carefully considered the evidence presented by both parties and the arguments advanced. The inspection reports clearly documented the visual observations of effluent discharge and the reported state of the ETP. The laboratory analysis reports, originating from an accredited EPA laboratory, provided objective scientific data on pollutant concentrations, consistently demonstrating significant exceedances of the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for multiple parameters. The Respondent's objections regarding sample collection methodology and chain of custody were not substantiated with concrete evidence sufficient to invalidate the credibility or accuracy of the reports. Furthermore, the extensive correspondence submitted by the Complainant clearly demonstrated that the Respondent was afforded ample opportunity and repeated warnings to rectify the situation, yet failed to do so effectively or within the stipulated timelines. The Respondent's claim of an operational ETP was contradicted by the persistent inspection findings and the consistently high levels of pollutants in the effluent samples, indicating that even if an ETP existed, its effectiveness was demonstrably insufficient to meet environmental standards.
The Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof. The evidence overwhelmingly establishes that the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, has been discharging industrial effluent that consistently exceeds the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS), thereby violating Section 11 of PEPA, 1997. Furthermore, the Respondent's failure to comply with the directives issued by the Environmental Protection Agency constitutes a clear violation of Section 16 of PEPA, 1997. The environmental harm caused by the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the municipal sewerage system and ultimately the River Ravi is a serious matter, impacting public health, aquatic ecosystems, and the overall environmental integrity of the region. The Tribunal also notes the Respondent's failure to submit regular self-monitoring reports, which is a breach of their environmental approval conditions and the provisions of
the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, as well as the Pakistan Environmental Protection Agency (Review of IEE/EIA) Regulations, 2000.
Having carefully considered all the evidence and arguments presented by both parties, the Tribunal hereby finds M/s Nishat Mills Limited guilty of violating Section 11 and Section 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act,

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
