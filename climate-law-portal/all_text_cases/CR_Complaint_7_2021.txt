### JUDGMENT
This judgment pertains to the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), alleging contravention of environmental laws and regulations. The Tribunal has carefully considered the pleadings, evidence, and arguments presented by both parties.
The Complainant, representing the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, initiated these proceedings under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), and the rules and regulations framed thereunder. The complaint, registered as Complaint No. 7/2021, was filed on **March 15, 2021**, following a series of inspections and monitoring activities conducted at the Respondent's industrial facility located in Shahrah-e-Roomi, Lahore. The Respondent, a prominent industrial entity involved in manufacturing various paper and packaging products, is alleged to have repeatedly violated the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents and air emissions, thereby causing environmental pollution detrimental to public health and the ecosystem.
The core of the allegations against M/s Packages Limited revolves around persistent non-compliance with the prescribed environmental standards. Specifically, the Complainant asserted that:
1.  The Respondent's industrial effluent discharged into the municipal sewerage system and ultimately into open drains consistently exceeded the NEQS for various parameters, including Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. Multiple sampling events conducted by EPA officials and analyzed by an accredited laboratory demonstrated these exceedances.
2.  The Respondent's industrial stacks and vents emitted smoke and particulate matter exceeding the NEQS for air emissions. Observations during site visits indicated visible smoke plumes, and subsequent sampling revealed higher than permissible levels of particulate matter (PM10 and PM2.5) and Sulphur Dioxide (SO2) from specific operational units, particularly the boiler house and certain processing sections.
3.  The Respondent had failed to adequately operate and maintain its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs), leading to the aforementioned violations. Despite repeated notices and warnings issued by the EPA, corrective measures were either insufficient or not implemented effectively.
4.  The Respondent was operating certain processes without valid environmental approvals or had made significant modifications to its operations without updating its Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) or obtaining a No Objection Certificate (NOC) for expansion, as required under Section 12 of PEPA, 1997.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included:
1.  **Inspection Reports:** A series of dated inspection reports from EPA officials, detailing observations of the Respondent's facility, operational status of pollution control equipment, and visible signs of pollution. Key reports included those dated **January 12, 2021, February 8, 2021, and March 1, 2021**.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Certified laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited environmental laboratory, corresponding to the samples collected during the aforementioned inspections. These reports graphically illustrated the exceedance of NEQS for effluent parameters (BOD ranging from 150-250 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L; COD from 400-650 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L; TSS from 200-350 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L) and air emissions (PM10 up to 250 µg/m³ against a standard of 150 µg/m³).
3.  **Show Cause Notices and Correspondence:** Copies of multiple show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, dated **February 15, 2021, and March 5, 2021**, outlining the violations and demanding corrective action, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies.
4.  **Photographic and Video Evidence:** Visual documentation depicting the discharge of discolored effluent and visible smoke plumes emanating from the Respondent's premises.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, vehemently denied the allegations, asserting its commitment to environmental stewardship. Through its legal counsel, the Respondent submitted:
1.  **Environmental Management Plan (EMP):** A copy of its internal EMP, highlighting ongoing efforts to upgrade its ETP and install advanced APCDs. They claimed significant investments had been made in pollution control infrastructure.
2.  **Internal Monitoring Reports:** Select internal monitoring reports, which, while sometimes showing marginal exceedances, generally presented a picture of compliance or near-compliance, arguing that the EPA's sampling might have been unrepresentative or conducted during atypical operational cycles.
3.  **Permits and Licenses:** Copies
of existing environmental permits and licenses, asserting that they were operating within the ambit of previously granted approvals and had submitted applications for renewals or updates where necessary.
3.  **Challenge to Sampling Methodology:** The Respondent contended that the EPA's sampling methodology was flawed. They argued that grab samples, as opposed to composite samples, might not accurately represent the average discharge quality over a period. They also questioned the
representativeness of the sampling locations chosen by the EPA, suggesting that samples might have been taken from points not indicative of the final treated discharge or emissions. They further asserted that their internal monitoring, conducted by qualified personnel, often showed compliance, implying a discrepancy that warranted further investigation and collaborative monitoring. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal has carefully examined the inspection report, laboratory analysis results, and the photographic evidence produced by the Complainant. The measured pollutant concentrations significantly exceed the NEQS limits and the Respondent has failed to rebut this evidence with credible contrary data. The defense regarding ETP maintenance does not excuse ongoing pollution discharge and cannot serve as a justification for non-compliance with mandatory environmental standards.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,000,000/- (Rupees Two Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 400,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **18 May, 2023**.
