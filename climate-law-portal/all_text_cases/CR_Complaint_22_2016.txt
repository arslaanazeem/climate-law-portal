**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"). The Tribunal, seized of the matter, has jurisdiction to adjudicate upon environmental complaints and impose penalties as stipulated by law.
The Complainant initiated these proceedings alleging that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of packaging materials, was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/environment, thereby causing water pollution in violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and the provisions of PEPA, 1997. Specifically, it was alleged that during routine inspections conducted by EPA officials, the Respondent's effluent discharge was found to contain pollutants exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial effluents. The Complainant sought the imposition of penalties and directives for compliance with environmental laws.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a series of documents and witness testimonies. Key among these were:
1.  **Inspection Report dated 15.03.2016:** This report documented an unannounced inspection of the Respondent's premises, noting observations regarding the operation of their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and the discharge point.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Report No. EPA/LAB/123/2016 dated 20.03.2016:** This report detailed the analysis of an effluent sample collected by EPA officials on 15.03.2016 from the Respondent's discharge point. The report indicated that parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), and Total Suspended Solids (TSS) significantly exceeded the permissible limits set by the NEQS for industrial effluents. For instance, COD was reported at 450 mg/L (NEQS: 150 mg/L), BOD5 at 280 mg/L (NEQS: 80 mg/L), and TSS at 300 mg/L (NEQS: 200 mg/L).
3.  **Show Cause Notice No. EPA/LHR/SCN/345/2016 dated 25.03.2016:** Issued to the Respondent based on the inspection and laboratory findings, requiring an explanation for the non-compliance.
4.  **Final Direction Order No. EPA/LHR/FDO/567/2016 dated 20.04.2016:** Issued after the Respondent's unsatisfactory reply to the show cause notice, directing the Respondent to rectify the non-compliance within a stipulated period and warning of legal action.
5.  **Testimony of Mr. Adnan Khan, Assistant Director, EPA:** He affirmed the contents of the inspection reports and the procedural steps taken by the EPA, including the collection of samples and issuance of notices.
6.  **Testimony of Ms. Sadia Rehman, Senior Chemist, EPA Laboratory:** She confirmed the methodology and accuracy of the laboratory analysis, asserting that the sample was properly collected, preserved, and analyzed according to standard protocols.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel, contested the allegations. They argued that:
1.  The Respondent operates a fully functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and is committed to environmental compliance.
2.  The reported non-compliance might have been an isolated incident due to a temporary operational glitch in the ETP, which was promptly rectified.
3.  They presented their own internal monitoring reports (undated but claiming to be from
the same period as the EPA's inspection) which, according to them, consistently demonstrated compliance with the NEQS. They further contended that the EPA's sample collection methodology or chain of custody might have been flawed, leading to inaccurate results, though no concrete evidence was presented to substantiate this claim. The Respondent highlighted its long-standing corporate reputation and significant investments in environmental protection infrastructure, asserting that any deviation from compliance would be an aberration rather than a systemic failure. They requested the Tribunal to dismiss the complaint or, in the alternative, impose a lenient penalty and provide ample opportunity to demonstrate ongoing compliance.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the documentary and testimonial evidence placed on record. The core of the complaint rests on the scientific evidence of effluent analysis provided by the EPA. The Complainant's witnesses, Mr. Adnan Khan and Ms. Sadia Rehman, presented a coherent and credible account of the inspection process, sample collection, and laboratory analysis. The detailed Laboratory Analysis Report No. EPA/LAB/123/2016 dated 20.03.2016 clearly indicates significant exceedances of critical parameters (COD, BOD5, TSS) beyond the permissible NEQS limits. The methodology and accuracy of the analysis were affirmed under oath, and no substantial challenge was mounted by the Respondent to discredit the scientific validity of these reports.
While the Respondent claimed to operate a functional ETP and presented undated internal monitoring reports, these reports lacked the necessary authentication and procedural rigor to counter the official, dated, and verified laboratory results presented by the EPA. The assertion of an "isolated incident due to a temporary operational glitch" remained an unsubstantiated claim, as no evidence was provided regarding the immediate rectification, subsequent monitoring results confirming compliance, or proactive communication with the EPA regarding such a glitch. The burden of demonstrating compliance, especially after a formal notice of non-compliance, rests heavily on the industrial unit. The Respondent failed to provide convincing evidence to rebut the specific findings of the EPA.
Therefore, based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant, Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, has successfully discharged its burden of proof. The evidence clearly establishes that M/s Packages Limited was discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards, thereby contravening the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, particularly Section 11 thereof, which prohibits the discharge of effluent or waste in excess of the NEQS. This act of pollution poses a threat to public health and the environment.
**ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, and exercising the powers vested in this Tribunal under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, it is hereby ordered as follows:
1.  **M/s Packages Limited is found guilty** of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
2.  **A penalty of PKR 750,000/- (Rupees Seven Hundred Fifty Thousand only)** is hereby imposed on M/s Packages Limited for the aforementioned contravention. This amount shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Government of Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this judgment.
3.  **The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, is directed** to immediately take all necessary measures to ensure that its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) operates at optimal efficiency, and that all discharged industrial effluent consistently meets the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents.
4.  **The Respondent shall submit quarterly self-monitoring reports** of its effluent quality, duly verified by an EPA-approved third-party laboratory, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, for a period of two (2) years, commencing from the date of this judgment.
5.  **The Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, is authorized** to conduct unannounced inspections and collect samples from the Respondent's premises at any time to verify compliance with this order and the PEPA, 1997.
6.  **Failure to comply with any part of this order** shall constitute a continuing contravention and may result in further legal action, including the imposition of additional fines as provided under Section 17(1) of the PEPA, 1997, and other applicable laws.
7.  There shall be no order as to costs.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
