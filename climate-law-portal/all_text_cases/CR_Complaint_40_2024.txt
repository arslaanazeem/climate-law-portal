Presided by: Justice (R) Chaudhry Muhammad Iqbal
This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 40/2024, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent manufacturing entity located within the jurisdiction of Lahore. The complaint alleges significant breaches of environmental laws and regulations, particularly concerning the discharge of industrial effluent and air emissions, thereby posing a threat to public health and the environment. The Tribunal has taken cognizance of the matter under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), and has meticulously reviewed the submissions and evidence presented by both parties.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that M/s Packages Limited has been operating its manufacturing units without adequately treating its industrial wastewater and controlling its air emissions, resulting in the discharge of pollutants exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Specifically, the allegations included: (a) persistent discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/drain, containing parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals, significantly above the prescribed NEQS; (b) emission of smoke and particulate matter from the factory's stacks exceeding the NEQS for industrial air emissions, particularly concerning Sulphur Dioxide (SO2) and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx); (c) failure to maintain and operate the installed Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control (APC) devices effectively and consistently; and (d) operating certain processes without obtaining the requisite Environmental Approval (NOC) or failing to comply with the conditions stipulated in previously granted NOCs, in violation of Section 12 of PEPA, 1997. The Complainant further asserted that repeated notices and warnings issued to the Respondent regarding these violations had largely gone unheeded or resulted in only superficial and temporary compliance measures.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 15.01.2024, 28.02.2024, and 10.04.2024, conducted by EPA teams, detailing observations of dark, pungent-smelling effluent discharge and visible smoke plumes. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from EPA's accredited environmental laboratory, confirming that samples of industrial effluent collected from the Respondent's discharge points on 15.01.2024 and 10.04.2024 showed COD levels of 550 mg/L and 620 mg/L respectively (against NEQS of 200 mg/L), BOD levels of 280 mg/L and 310 mg/L (against NEQS of 80 mg/L), and TSS levels of 350 mg/L and 410 mg/L (against NEQS of 200 mg/L). Similarly, air emission monitoring reports from the stack on 28.02.2024 indicated particulate matter at 700 mg/Nm3 (against NEQS of 600 mg/Nm3 for existing industries) and SO2 at 1800 mg/Nm3 (against NEQS of 1700 mg/Nm3). Photographic and video evidence depicting the aforementioned environmental transgressions was also presented. Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued to M/s Packages Limited on 20.01.2024 and 05.03.2024, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were placed on record.
The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations, contending that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental stewardship. The Respondent's defense primarily rested on the following points: (a) that it has an operational Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control (APC) systems in place, which are regularly maintained; (b) that any exceedances noted by the EPA were isolated incidents, possibly due to temporary operational malfunctions or peak production loads, which have since been rectified; (c) that the EPA's sampling methodology might have been flawed or unrepresentative of typical operations; and (d) that it has invested substantial capital in upgrading its environmental infrastructure. As evidence, the Respondent submitted its own internal monitoring reports for March and April 2024, purporting to show compliance with NEQS, and a certificate from an independent consultant attesting to
the satisfactory operation and maintenance of its environmental control systems. The Respondent also produced copies of its Environmental Management Plan (EMP) and Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) reports to demonstrate its commitment to sustainable practices. They argued that the EPA's sampling was potentially flawed, and the single instances of exceedances, if any, were not indicative of a systemic failure, but rather temporary anomalies that had been promptly addressed.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the documentary and scientific evidence placed on record. While the Respondent presented internal reports and a consultant's certificate, these submissions lack the independent verification and comprehensive nature of the evidence provided by the Complainant. The EPA's laboratory analysis reports, conducted by an accredited laboratory, consistently indicate significant exceedances of NEQS for both effluent discharge and air emissions on multiple dates. The photographic and video evidence further corroborates the visual observations of environmental degradation. The Tribunal notes that the Respondent's explanations of "isolated incidents" or "temporary malfunctions" are not supported by evidence demonstrating immediate and sustained rectification following the repeated notices from the EPA. The persistent nature of the violations, as evidenced by the multiple inspection reports spanning several months, indicates a systemic failure rather than isolated events. Furthermore, the Respondent's failure to provide convincing counter-evidence regarding the alleged flaws in EPA's sampling methodology weakens its defense significantly. The Tribunal finds the Complainant's evidence to be credible, consistent, and overwhelmingly supportive of the allegations made.
On the basis of the comprehensive evidence presented and the arguments heard, the Tribunal finds that M/s Packages Limited has indeed contravened the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, particularly Section 12 and Section 17 thereof, and has failed to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluent and air emissions. The Respondent's repeated failure to adequately treat its industrial wastewater and control its air emissions, despite warnings and show-cause notices from the EPA, constitutes a serious breach of environmental laws,
and a blatant disregard for the well-being of the surrounding community and the ecosystem. Such persistent non-compliance by a prominent industrial entity like M/s Packages Limited undermines the very objectives of environmental protection legislation and sets a dangerous precedent. The Tribunal emphasizes that environmental compliance is not merely an optional corporate social responsibility but a mandatory legal obligation. The evidence clearly demonstrates that the Respondent has failed in its duty to operate its facility in an environmentally sound manner, thereby contributing to pollution and potential harm.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Punjab Environmental Tribunal, Lahore, hereby issues the following final order and penalty:
1.  **Declaration of Guilt:** M/s Packages Limited is hereby found guilty of violating Section 12 and Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and failing to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluent and air emissions.
2.  **Immediate Compliance:** The Respondent is directed to immediately cease all discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent and to bring its air emissions within the prescribed NEQS.
3.  **Environmental Compliance Plan (ECP):** The Respondent shall, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this order, submit a comprehensive Environmental Compliance Plan (ECP) to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,300,000/- (Rupees One Million Three Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 260,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **05 December, 2022**.
