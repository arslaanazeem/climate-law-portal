**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 48/2021 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent manufacturing facility located in Lahore. The complaint seeks redress for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of industrial effluent and emission of air pollutants exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal, having heard arguments from both sides and reviewed the submitted evidence, proceeds to deliver its findings and final order.
The Complainant, through its inspection teams, alleged that M/s Packages Limited was operating its manufacturing processes in contravention of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and the rules and regulations framed thereunder. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  **Discharge of Untreated/Inadequately Treated Industrial Effluent:** It was alleged that the Respondent's industrial effluent, discharged into the municipal sewerage system, consistently exceeded the NEQS for various parameters, including Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH.
2.  **Emission of Air Pollutants Exceeding NEQS:** The Complainant further alleged that emissions from the Respondent's industrial stacks, particularly particulate matter and oxides of nitrogen (NOx), were found to be above the permissible limits set by the NEQS for industrial air emissions.
3.  **Failure to Comply with Show Cause Notices:** The EPA claimed that despite multiple inspection reports, issuance of show cause notices dated 15.03.2021 and 20.05.2021, and subsequent follow-up inspections, the Respondent failed to take adequate corrective measures to bring its operations into compliance with the environmental standards.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented the following evidence:
1.  **Inspection Reports:** Detailed reports from EPA inspection teams dated 01.03.2021, 10.05.2021, and 05.07.2021, documenting observations of the Respondent's effluent discharge points and stack emissions.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Certified laboratory reports from the EPA Environmental Monitoring Laboratory, Lahore, analyzing samples of industrial effluent collected on 01.03.2021 and 10.05.2021, showing BOD levels of 150 mg/L and 180 mg/L (against NEQS limit of 80 mg/L), COD levels of 450 mg/L and 520 mg/L (against NEQS limit of 150 mg/L), TSS levels of 200 mg/L and 250 mg/L (against NEQS limit of 200 mg/L), and pH values outside the permissible range of 6-9. Air emission reports indicated particulate matter exceeding 200 mg/Nm³ (against NEQS limit of 150 mg/Nm³) and NOx levels of 700 mg/Nm³ (against NEQS limit of 600 mg/Nm³).
3.  **Show Cause Notices and Correspondence:** Copies of the show cause notices issued to the Respondent, along with records of subsequent correspondence, indicating the Respondent's acknowledgement but alleged lack of effective action.
The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, vehemently denied the persistent non-compliance. Their defense included:
1.  **Existence of Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices:** The Respondent asserted that they have a functional ETP and air pollution control equipment installed and regularly maintained. They submitted maintenance logs and operational records of these facilities.
2.  **Temporary Malfunctions:** They contended that any exceedances observed during the EPA's inspections were due to temporary operational malfunctions or power fluctuations, which were promptly addressed. They provided repair records for their ETP and air scrubbers.
3.  **Commitment to Environmental Compliance:** The Respondent emphasized its long-standing commitment to environmental responsibility, highlighting past investments in green technologies and corporate social responsibility initiatives. They presented a plan for upgrading their existing ETP and installing advanced continuous emission monitoring systems (CEMS) by the end of 2022.
4.  **Dispute over Sampling Methodology:** While not outright rejecting the EPA's findings, the Respondent raised concerns regarding the consistency and representativeness of the sampling methodology employed by the EPA during certain inspections, suggesting that isolated samples might not reflect the overall operational efficiency.
The Tribunal considered the following legal provisions while adjudicating the matter:
1.  **Section 11 of PEPA, 1997:** Prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards, or where no such standards have been prescribed, in excess of the standards established by the Agency.
2.  **Section 12 of PEPA, 1997:** Pertains to the handling of hazardous substances, which may be relevant if the effluent contains such materials.
3.  **Section 14 of PEPA, 1997:** Deals with prohibitions against discharges of certain pollutants.
4.  **Section 16 of PEPA, 199
7: Pertains to the powers of the Tribunal to issue Environmental Protection Orders.
5.  **Section 17 of PEPA, 1997:** Outlines the penalties for contravention of Section 11, including fines and imprisonment, and empowers the Tribunal to impose such penalties.
6.  **National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS):** The specific standards for industrial effluent and air emissions, as notified by the Government of Pakistan under PEPA, 1997, are directly applicable and form the benchmark for compliance.
**Findings of the Tribunal:**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments, evidence, and legal provisions. While acknowledging the Respondent’s assertion of having an existing Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices, and their stated commitment to environmental compliance, the overwhelming evidence presented by the Complainant, particularly the certified laboratory analysis reports from multiple dates, unequivocally demonstrates consistent exceedances of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for both industrial effluent and air emissions.
The Respondent's defense of "temporary malfunctions" does not adequately explain or excuse the repeated violations across different inspection dates and for various parameters. Environmental compliance is an ongoing obligation, not merely a state to be achieved intermittently. A functional ETP and air pollution control system must operate effectively and consistently to meet the prescribed standards. The provided repair records, while showing efforts, do not negate the fact that during the periods of EPA inspection, the Respondent's discharges and emissions were found to be non-compliant.
Regarding the Respondent's concerns about sampling methodology, the Tribunal notes that the samples were collected and analyzed by the official EPA Environmental Monitoring Laboratory, which is mandated to perform such functions. The methodology employed is presumed to be standard and reliable unless specific, credible evidence to the contrary is presented, which has not been done in this case. The consistent pattern of exceedances across different parameters and dates further strengthens the reliability of the EPA’s findings.
The issuance of multiple show cause notices and the subsequent failure of the Respondent to bring its operations into compliance within a reasonable timeframe, as evidenced by later inspection reports, indicates a persistent lack of effective corrective action despite being duly informed of the violations. While the Respondent’s plan for upgrading its ETP and installing CEMS by the end of 2022 is a positive step, it is a future commitment and does not absolve them of past and present non-compliance.
Based on the totality of the evidence, the Tribunal finds that M/s Packages Limited has violated Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent and emitting air pollutants in amounts and concentrations exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards. Such violations pose a risk to public health and the environment and cannot be overlooked.
**Final Order and Penalty:**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Punjab Environmental Tribunal, exercising its powers under Sections 16 and 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, hereby issues the following Environmental Protection Order and imposes the subsequent penalties:
1.  **Immediate Compliance:** M/s Packages Limited is hereby directed to immediately bring its industrial effluent discharge and air emissions into full and consistent compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). This includes ensuring the optimal functioning and, where necessary, immediate upgrade of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices.
2.  **Submission of Compliance Plan:** The Respondent shall submit a detailed, time-bound compliance action plan to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, within **fifteen (15) days** from the date of this judgment. This plan must outline specific measures, timelines, and responsible personnel for achieving and maintaining NEQS compliance for both effluent and air emissions. The plan must also include details of the proposed ETP upgrade and CEMS installation.
3.  **Installation of Continuous Emission Monitoring System (CEMS):** The Respondent shall install a fully functional and calibrated Continuous Emission Monitoring System (CEMS) for its major emission stacks and a Continuous Effluent Monitoring System (CEMS) for its main effluent discharge point, linked to the EPA's central monitoring system, within **ninety (90) days** from the date of this judgment. The data from these systems shall be made accessible to the EPA in real-time.
4.  **Regular Reporting:** Until the CEMS are fully operational, the Respondent shall submit monthly self-monitoring reports, duly verified by an EPA-approved third-party laboratory, confirming compliance with NEQS, for a period of six months.
5.  **Monetary Penalty:** For the established violations of Section 11 of the PEPA, 1997, M/s Packages Limited is hereby ordered to pay a penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** to the Environmental Protection Agency, Government of Punjab, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this judgment.
6.  **Daily Fine for Non-Compliance:** Should the Respondent fail to comply with the directives set forth in paragraphs 1, 2, and 3 of this Order within their respective stipulated timelines, a further daily fine of **PKR 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand only)** shall be imposed for each day of continued non-compliance until full compliance is achieved, without prejudice to other legal actions available to the EPA.
7.  **Right of Inspection:** The Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, retains the right to conduct unannounced inspections and collect samples at any time to verify compliance with this Order and the NEQS.
The Complaint No. 48/2021 is accordingly disposed of.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,900,000/- (Rupees One Million Nine Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 380,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **03 October, 2022**.
