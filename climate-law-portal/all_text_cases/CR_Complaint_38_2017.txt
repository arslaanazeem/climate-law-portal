This judgment pertains to a complaint lodged by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, alleging persistent violations of environmental laws and standards. The complainant, acting under the mandate of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"), brought forth this matter before the Tribunal seeking redress and enforcement of environmental compliance. M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, a prominent industrial entity engaged in the manufacturing of various chemical products, is situated in Kala Shah Kaku, District Sheikhupura, and is consequently subject to environmental regulations concerning industrial emissions and effluents.
The core of the allegations leveled against the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, revolves around the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the surrounding environment, specifically into the local drainage system which eventually merges with natural water bodies. The Complainant asserted that regular monitoring and inspection visits conducted by EPA officials between late 2016 and mid-2017 revealed consistent non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents, as prescribed under the Act. Specifically, it was alleged that the discharged effluent contained parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels far exceeding the permissible limits. The complaint further detailed that multiple Show Cause Notices and Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) had been issued to the Respondent, directing them to rectify the non-compliance and install/upgrade their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) to meet the NEQS. Despite these notices and directives, the Respondent allegedly failed to implement effective measures, thereby continuing to pollute the environment and posing a significant threat to public health and aquatic ecosystems.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports, dating from October 2016 to July 2017, meticulously prepared by EPA field teams. Crucially, these reports were accompanied by comprehensive laboratory analysis certificates issued by the EPA's accredited laboratory. These certificates provided scientific evidence, detailing the specific exceedances of NEQS for various parameters in the effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on multiple occasions. Photographs depicting the discharge of discolored effluent were also submitted as visual evidence. Furthermore, copies of the Show Cause Notices and Environmental Protection Orders issued to the Respondent were placed on record, demonstrating the EPA's efforts to secure voluntary compliance prior to approaching the Tribunal. On their part, the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through their legal counsel and technical representatives, contested the allegations. They presented their defense by claiming that they had an operational ETP, albeit acknowledging that it might require upgrades. They submitted internal monitoring reports, which, however, did not fully align with the EPA's findings. They also presented a phased plan for the modernization of their ETP and highlighted their substantial investment in environmental management systems. The Respondent argued that temporary operational issues or external factors might have contributed to occasional non-compliance, rather than a deliberate disregard for environmental laws. They also questioned the methodology of sample collection on certain occasions.
The legal framework governing this complaint primarily stems from the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Section 11 of the Act strictly prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount
or concentration that is in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS), or where no NEQS have been prescribed, in excess of the standards established by the Federal Agency. Section 16 of the Act empowers the Environmental Tribunal to impose penalties for violations of the Act and its rules, including fines and imprisonment. Furthermore, Section 17 grants the Tribunal the authority to issue Environmental Protection Orders,
including directing the person to take such measures as specified in the order, prohibiting the discharge or emission of any pollutant, requiring the installation of pollution control equipment, or even directing the closure of an industrial unit if necessary to prevent or abate pollution. **V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
