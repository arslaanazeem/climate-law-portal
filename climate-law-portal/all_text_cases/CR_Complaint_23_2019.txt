This judgment addresses Complaint No. 23/2019, lodged by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA" or "Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent manufacturing entity located in Lahore. The complaint invokes the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleging various violations of environmental laws and standards. The Tribunal, established to adjudicate matters related to environmental protection and pollution control, has carefully considered the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties to arrive at a just and equitable decision.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around persistent non-compliance by the Respondent with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and other environmental regulations. Specifically, the EPA alleged that the Respondent's industrial facility was:
1.  Discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the municipal sewerage system, which eventually flows into the River Ravi, thereby exceeding the NEQS for various parameters including Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH.
2.  Emitting air pollutants, particularly particulate matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), from its boiler stacks and power generation units, which consistently surpassed the prescribed NEQS for industrial emissions.
3.  Improperly managing hazardous waste generated from its manufacturing processes, leading to environmental contamination risks and non-adherence to the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003.
4.  Failing to install, operate, and maintain an effective and fully functional Environmental Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) despite repeated notices and warnings issued by the EPA since 2017.
5.  Operating without valid No Objection Certificates (NOCs) or failing to renew them for certain operational aspects as required by law.
The Complainant contended that these violations posed a significant threat to public health and the environment, causing pollution of water bodies, air quality degradation, and potential harm to ecosystems and human populations in the vicinity.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 15.03.2018, 22.08.2018, and 05.02.2019, which documented the observations of EPA field teams regarding the Respondent's operational practices and discharge points. Crucially, the EPA submitted laboratory analysis reports from accredited laboratories (e.g., PCSIR Laboratories Complex, Lahore) for effluent and air emission samples collected during these inspections. These reports consistently showed exceedances of NEQS for BOD (up to 350 mg/L against a limit of 80 mg/L), COD (up to 800 mg/L against a limit of 200 mg/L), TSS (up to 280 mg/L against a limit of 200 mg/L), and PM (up to 800 mg/Nm3 against a limit of 600 mg/Nm3 for boilers using oil/gas). Photographic and videographic evidence depicting the colour, foam, and visible particulate matter from discharge points and stacks was also presented. Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 10.04.2018 and 18.09.2018, along with the Respondent's inadequate replies, were placed on record, demonstrating a history of non-compliance and the EPA's consistent efforts to
enforce environmental regulations. The EPA also highlighted the Respondent's failure to provide satisfactory explanations or concrete action plans in response to these notices, which ultimately led to the filing of the present complaint.
The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its learned counsel, vehemently denied the allegations, characterizing them as exaggerated and, in some instances, factually incorrect. The Respondent contended that it is a responsible corporate
citizen. The Respondent maintained that it has consistently endeavoured to comply with environmental regulations and has made substantial investments in its environmental management systems. It was asserted that the Respondent operates an existing Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs), which, though perhaps requiring periodic upgrades, are generally functional and designed to meet NEQS. The Respondent's counsel argued that the EPA's sampling methodology might have been flawed or unrepresentative, and that the laboratory analysis reports, while showing some exceedances, did not accurately reflect the Respondent's overall compliance efforts or the operational efficiency of its treatment facilities. It was further submitted that any observed exceedances were temporary or minor in nature, often attributable to operational upsets or maintenance activities, rather than a deliberate disregard for environmental standards. The Respondent also presented evidence of its corporate social responsibility initiatives, including tree plantation drives and community engagement, to underscore its commitment to environmental stewardship. Regarding the alleged hazardous waste mismanagement, the Respondent contended that it follows established procedures for segregation, storage, and disposal through licensed third-party contractors, and that any isolated incidents of non-compliance were promptly rectified. The Respondent sought dismissal of the complaint, asserting that it was a responsible corporate entity striving for sustainable operations and that the punitive measures sought by the EPA were disproportionate to the actual environmental impact, if any.
**Tribunal's Analysis and Findings:**
This Tribunal has thoroughly reviewed the pleadings, documentary evidence, and oral submissions of both the Complainant and the Respondent. The core issue before this Tribunal is to determine whether the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, has violated the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and its associated rules and regulations, particularly the NEQS and Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003.
The evidence presented by the EPA, including multiple inspection reports and independent laboratory analysis reports from accredited institutions, paints a clear picture of persistent non-compliance. The consistent exceedances of NEQS for critical parameters in both effluent discharge (BOD, COD, TSS) and air emissions (PM) cannot be dismissed as minor or temporary operational upsets. The laboratory reports, specifically indicating BOD levels up to 350 mg/L against a limit of 80 mg/L and COD up to 800 mg/L against a limit of 200 mg/L, demonstrate a significant failure to adequately treat industrial waste. Similarly, air emission exceedances, such as PM up to 800 mg/Nm3 against a limit of 600 mg/Nm3, indicate a deficiency in air pollution control. The photographic and videographic evidence further corroborates the visual observations of pollution, lending weight to the EPA's claims.
While the Respondent's counsel argued about the potential flaws in sampling methodology or the representativeness of samples, no concrete evidence or expert testimony was adduced to convincingly challenge the scientific validity or procedural integrity of the EPA's collection and analysis processes. The mere assertion of flaws, without substantiation, cannot override the consistent findings from multiple independent analyses.
The history of repeated show-cause notices issued by the EPA since 2017, coupled with the Respondent's inadequate responses and continued violations, underscores a pattern of non-compliance rather than isolated incidents. While the Respondent claims to have invested in ETPs and APCDs, the operational effectiveness of these systems is clearly compromised, as evidenced by the persistent exceedances. Mere installation is insufficient; the systems must be fully functional, properly maintained, and consistently operated to meet the prescribed standards.
Regarding hazardous waste management, the Respondent's claim of adherence to established procedures is undermined by the EPA's allegations of contamination risks. Without robust documentation of proper segregation, storage, and disposal manifests, and in the face of EPA's findings, the Tribunal finds the Respondent's assertions unconvincing. The failure to obtain or renew necessary NOCs for certain operational aspects further points to a casual approach towards regulatory compliance.
The Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's status as a prominent manufacturing entity and its contributions to the economy. However, economic considerations cannot take precedence over the fundamental right to a clean and healthy environment, which is enshrined in the Constitution and protected by PEPA, 1997. The discharge of untreated effluent into the municipal sewerage system and ultimately into the River Ravi poses a grave threat to aquatic life, public health, and the overall ecological balance. Similarly, excessive air emissions contribute to air quality degradation, impacting the health of surrounding communities.
Based on the overwhelming evidence presented by the Complainant, this Tribunal finds that the Environmental Protection Agency has successfully proven the violations alleged against M/s Packages Limited. The Respondent has failed to operate its industrial facility in compliance with the NEQS for effluent and air emissions, has not adequately managed hazardous waste, and has demonstrated a consistent failure to respond effectively to regulatory notices. These actions constitute a clear breach of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
**Final Order and Penalty:**
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Declaration of Violation:** M/s Packages Limited is found to be in violation of Sections 11, 12, 13, and 14 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluent and air emissions, as well as the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003.
2.  **Monetary Penalty:** M/s Packages Limited is hereby directed to pay a monetary penalty of **PKR 10,000,000/- (Rupees Ten Million Only)** to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order. This penalty is imposed under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, considering the gravity and persistent nature of the violations and the potential harm to public health and the environment.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,600,000/- (Rupees One Million Six Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 320,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **23 March, 2023**.
