**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 28/2018, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent" or "ICL"), regarding alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal, established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, possesses the requisite jurisdiction to adjudicate matters concerning environmental pollution and non-compliance with environmental laws and standards. The proceedings commenced upon the filing of the complaint on 15th May 2018, following which notices were issued to the Respondent, who subsequently appeared through their legal counsel to contest the allegations.
The Complainant, through its authorized officer, alleged that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of various chemical products, was operating in a manner detrimental to the environment and public health. Specifically, it was alleged that on multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials, particularly on 1st March 2018 and 20th April 2018, the Respondent's industrial operations were found to be in gross violation of environmental regulations. The primary allegations included:
1.  Discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/drainage channels, with laboratory analysis revealing parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and heavy metals (e.g., Chromium, Lead) significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS.
2.  Emission of hazardous air pollutants, including particulate matter (PM10), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), from their stacks without adequate pollution control measures, resulting in levels far above the NEQS for industrial emissions.
3.  Improper management and disposal of hazardous solid waste generated during the manufacturing process, leading to open dumping and potential soil and groundwater contamination.
4.  Failure to obtain or renew the requisite Environmental Approval/No-Objection Certificate (NOC) for certain operational expansions or modifications, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, and the Pakistan Environmental Protection Agency (Review of IEE/EIA) Regulations, 2000.
5.  Non-compliance with previous Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) issued by the EPA, particularly EPO No. EPA/LHR/123/2017 dated 15th November 2017, which had directed the Respondent to upgrade its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and install appropriate Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs).
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive set of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA field teams on 1st March 2018 and 20th April 2018, which documented the visual observations of effluent discharge points, stack emissions, and waste disposal practices. Crucially, the Complainant submitted authenticated laboratory analysis reports
confirming the exceedance of NEQS for various parameters in both effluent and emissions. Photographic and videographic evidence depicting the alleged pollution incidents, including discharge of colored effluent and visible stack emissions, were also tendered. Furthermore, copies of the earlier Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) and notices issued to the Respondent, along with records of their non-compliance, were placed on record. The Complainant emphasized the
persistent and blatant disregard for environmental laws and public health, necessitating stringent enforcement actions to protect the environment and ensure compliance.
The Respondent, through their learned counsel, vehemently denied the allegations, asserting that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited is a responsible industrial entity committed to environmental stewardship. They contended that significant investments had been made in upgrading their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and installing air pollution control devices. It was argued that the alleged exceedances were either isolated incidents, attributable to unforeseen operational upsets, or based on flawed sampling and analysis methodologies employed by the EPA. The Respondent further submitted that they were in the process of applying for the necessary environmental approvals for their operational modifications and that the previous EPOs were being addressed diligently, albeit with some delays due to technical complexities and procurement challenges. They requested the Tribunal to consider their ongoing efforts and grant them additional time to achieve full compliance, emphasizing the economic impact of any punitive measures on their operations and workforce.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties. Upon careful consideration of the inspection reports, laboratory analysis results, photographic and videographic evidence, and the past record of Environmental Protection Orders, the Tribunal finds the Complainant's case to be substantially proven. The scientific data from multiple independent analyses consistently demonstrates that the Respondent's effluent and emissions significantly exceeded the prescribed NEQS on several occasions, indicating a systemic rather than an incidental failure to comply. The visual evidence corroborates the observations of untreated discharges and visible emissions. Furthermore, the Respondent's failure to adequately address the directives contained in previous EPOs, particularly EPO No. EPA/LHR/123/2017, underscores a pattern of non-compliance that cannot be overlooked. While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's claims of ongoing efforts, these efforts have demonstrably been insufficient to bring the operations into compliance with national environmental standards within a reasonable timeframe. The argument regarding flawed sampling methodology was not substantiated with credible counter-evidence. The obligation to protect the environment and public health takes precedence over commercial interests, especially when persistent violations are established.
**FINDINGS:**
Based on the evidence and arguments, the Tribunal finds the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, guilty of the following violations:
1.  Discharging industrial effluent in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS), in violation of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997).
2.  Emitting hazardous air pollutants exceeding the NEQS, in violation of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997.
3.  Improper management and disposal of hazardous solid waste, constituting a threat to the environment and public health, in violation of Section 14 of PEPA, 1997.
4.  Failure to obtain or renew requisite Environmental Approval/NOC for operational expansions/modifications, in violation of Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, and the Pakistan Environmental Protection Agency (Review of IEE/EIA) Regulations, 2000.
5.  Non-compliance with Environmental Protection Order No. EPA/LHR/123/2017 dated 15th November 2017, in violation of Section 16 of PEPA, 1997.
**ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the above findings and exercising the powers vested under Sections 17 and 22 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the Tribunal hereby issues the following order:
1.  **Monetary Penalty:** The Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, is hereby directed to pay a monetary penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million Only)** for the established violations of the PEPA, 1997, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this judgment. In addition to this, a further daily penalty of **PKR 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand Only)** shall be imposed for each day the violations specified in points 1, 2, and 3 of the Findings continue beyond **ninety (90) days** from the date of this judgment.
2.  **Compliance Directives:** The Respondent is directed to immediately undertake and complete the following measures within the specified timelines:
    a.  **Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) Upgrade:** Within **ninety (90) days**, the Respondent shall upgrade its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) to consistently meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for all parameters. A detailed action plan for this upgrade, including technical specifications and timelines, must be submitted to the EPA within **fifteen (15) days**.
    b.  **Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs):** Within **one hundred and twenty (120) days**, the Respondent shall install and commission appropriate Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) for all emission sources to ensure compliance with the NEQS for industrial emissions. A detailed action plan for this installation, including technical specifications and timelines, must be submitted to the EPA within **fifteen (15) days**.
    c.  **Hazardous Waste Management:** Within **sixty (60) days**, the Respondent shall develop and implement a comprehensive Hazardous Waste Management Plan, approved by the EPA, ensuring proper collection, storage, treatment, and disposal of all hazardous waste generated, strictly adhering to the national regulations. Open dumping or unscientific disposal is strictly prohibited.
    d.  **Environmental Approvals:** Within **forty-five (45) days**, the Respondent shall submit complete applications for all outstanding or renewed Environmental Approvals/No-Objection Certificates (NOCs) to the EPA, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, and the Pakistan Environmental Protection Agency (Review of IEE/EIA) Regulations,

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 4,400,000/- (Rupees Four Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 880,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
