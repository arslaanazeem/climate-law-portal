### **JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 62/2020 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited, a prominent glass manufacturing facility located in the industrial zone of Lahore. The complaint seeks to enforce compliance with environmental laws and standards, alleging significant environmental pollution caused by the respondent's operations, thereby posing a threat to public health and the environment. The Tribunal, having heard both parties, examined the evidence presented, and considered the relevant legal provisions, proceeds to deliver its findings and final order.
The complainant, representing the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency, initiated these proceedings under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), following a series of inspections and monitoring activities at the respondent's industrial premises. The core of the allegations revolves around the respondent's alleged failure to adhere to the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and other statutory requirements designed to protect the environment.
The allegations leveled against M/s Ghani Glass Limited are manifold. Firstly, the complainant asserts that the respondent's manufacturing processes, particularly the operation of its glass melting furnaces, consistently emit air pollutants, including particulate matter (PM), oxides of sulfur (SOx), and oxides of nitrogen (NOx), far exceeding the permissible limits set by the NEQS. This leads to visible smoke plumes and contributes significantly to ambient air pollution in the surrounding areas. Secondly, it is contended that the respondent has failed to implement an adequate and environmentally sound solid waste management system. Specifically, large quantities of industrial solid waste, including broken glass cullet, refractory waste, and other process residues, are allegedly being improperly stored and disposed of, leading to land pollution and potential contamination of groundwater. Thirdly, the complainant points out the respondent's non-compliance with the conditions stipulated in its Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) approval or No Objection Certificate (NOC), particularly concerning the operation and maintenance of pollution control equipment. Finally, the complaint alleges a consistent failure on the part of the respondent to submit timely and accurate environmental monitoring reports to the EPA, hindering regulatory oversight and assessment of compliance status.
To substantiate its claims, the complainant presented a comprehensive array of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on various dates between January 2019 and June 2020, detailing observations of excessive emissions, inadequate waste storage, and non-functional or poorly maintained pollution control devices. Crucially, the complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of air emission samples collected from the respondent's stacks, which unequivocally demonstrated exceedances of NEQS for PM, SOx, and NOx on several occasions. Photographic
evidence was also submitted, illustrating visible emissions from stacks and improper waste storage practices. Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued to the respondent, along with their unsatisfactory replies or lack thereof, were presented to demonstrate the respondent's persistent non-compliance and disregard for regulatory directives.
In response, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. The respondent contended that it is a responsible corporate entity with a strong commitment to environmental stewardship. They argued that their operations are conducted in accordance with best available techniques and that they have invested heavily in state-of-the-art pollution control equipment. Specifically, regarding air emissions, the respondent claimed that the EPA's sampling methodologies might have been flawed or that the reported exceedances were isolated incidents, not reflective of their routine operations. They also asserted that their solid waste management system, while continuously being improved, is adequate and that all waste is either recycled or disposed of through approved channels. The respondent further submitted that they regularly engage with environmental consultants to ensure compliance and that any delays in submitting reports were administrative oversights, not deliberate attempts to evade oversight. They requested the Tribunal to dismiss the complaint, arguing that the EPA had failed to present conclusive evidence of persistent and deliberate non-compliance that would warrant punitive action.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties. It is evident from the inspection reports and, more critically, the independent laboratory analysis reports of air emissions, that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has indeed been operating in violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards for air pollutants. The multiple instances of exceedance for PM, SOx, and NOx cannot be dismissed as
isolated incidents or sampling flaws. The cumulative weight of the evidence, including consistent exceedances across multiple samples and repeated observations by EPA officials detailed in their inspection reports, paints a clear picture of persistent non-compliance. The Tribunal finds the respondent's arguments regarding flawed sampling methodologies unsubstantiated, as no credible counter-evidence or independent expert analysis was presented to challenge the EPA's laboratory reports. Similarly, while the respondent claims to have an adequate solid waste management system, the photographic evidence and inspection reports clearly indicate improper storage and handling of industrial waste, which poses a significant risk of land and groundwater contamination. The assertion of 'administrative oversights' for non-submission of reports is also unconvincing, as timely reporting is a statutory obligation crucial for effective environmental governance.
Therefore, based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal concludes that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has indeed violated various provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards. Specifically, the respondent has contravened Section 11 (Prohibition of certain discharges or emissions), Section 12 (Initial environmental examination and environmental impact assessment), and Section 16 (Powers to make rules) read with the NEQS, as well as failing in its duty under Section 13 (Monitoring and enforcement) by not providing accurate reports. Such persistent non-compliance undermines the very spirit of environmental protection legislation and poses an unacceptable risk to public health and the ecological integrity of the area.
### **FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal hereby issues the following final order and penalty:
1.  **Declaration of Violation:** M/s Ghani Glass Limited is found to be in violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for air emissions (Particulate Matter, Oxides of Sulfur, Oxides of Nitrogen) and has failed to implement adequate solid waste management practices and comply with reporting requirements under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
2.  **Remedial Directives:** The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, is hereby directed to:
    *   **Air Pollution Control:** Immediately upgrade, repair, and ensure the efficient operation of all air pollution control devices (e.g., bag filters, electrostatic precipitators, scrubbers) attached to its glass melting furnaces and other emission sources, to consistently meet the prescribed NEQS. The Respondent shall submit a detailed action plan for achieving this compliance within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this judgment.
    *   **Solid Waste Management:** Develop and implement a comprehensive and environmentally sound solid waste management plan within **sixty (60) days** from the date of this judgment. This plan shall include proper segregation, storage, recycling, and disposal of all industrial solid waste, including broken glass cullet and refractory waste, through approved and licensed facilities, ensuring no land or groundwater contamination.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,300,000/- (Rupees One Million Three Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 260,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **13 November, 2022**.
