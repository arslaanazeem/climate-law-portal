This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 28/2017 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), situated at 17-KM, Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The complaint seeks redress for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS").
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, a prominent manufacturer of glass products, has been operating its industrial facility in a manner detrimental to the environment and public health. Specifically, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial processes, particularly the operation of its glass melting furnaces, were emitting particulate matter, nitrogen oxides (NOx), and sulfur dioxide (SOx) into the atmosphere at levels significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial emissions. Furthermore, the complaint detailed that the Respondent had failed to install and effectively operate adequate air pollution control devices, despite repeated notices and directives from the EPA. It was also contended that the Respondent had not obtained the necessary Environmental Approval (Environmental Impact Assessment – EIA or Initial Environmental Examination – IEE) or its subsequent renewal, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, for its operational expansion and continued functioning. The Complainant emphasized that despite several warning letters and show-cause notices issued under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, dating from late 2016 to early 2017, the Respondent had failed to take satisfactory corrective measures or provide a credible compliance plan, thereby necessitating the filing of the present complaint before this Tribunal.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports prepared by EPA officials on various dates in 2016 and 2017, detailing observations of visible smoke plumes and inadequate pollution control infrastructure at the Respondent's facility. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from samples collected from the Respondent's stacks on 15.11.2016 and 02.02.2017 were submitted, which unequivocally demonstrated that the concentrations of particulate matter (PM), NOx, and SOx consistently exceeded the NEQS by factors ranging from 1.5 to 3 times the permissible limits. also tendered. The Complainant further submitted copies of the warning letters, show-cause notices, and the Respondent's evasive or unsatisfactory replies, demonstrating a consistent pattern of non-compliance and disregard for environmental regulations.
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It was contended that the Respondent is a responsible corporate entity with a
responsible corporate entity with a strong commitment to environmental stewardship. They asserted that they had undertaken significant investments in upgrading their production facilities and were actively exploring advanced pollution control technologies. It was further argued that the reported exceedances of NEQS, if any, were either isolated incidents or attributable to transient operational fluctuations, rather than a systemic failure to comply. The Respondent's counsel also questioned the methodology and calibration of the EPA's sampling equipment, suggesting that the laboratory reports might not accurately reflect the average operational emissions. They contended that obtaining and implementing sophisticated air pollution control devices was a time-consuming and capital-intensive process, and they were in various stages of procurement and installation. Regarding the Environmental Approval, the Respondent claimed that applications for renewal were pending with the EPA, and any delays were procedural and not indicative of a deliberate disregard for regulatory requirements. They emphasized their economic contribution to the region and the potential adverse impact of any harsh punitive measures on their workforce and operations.
The Tribunal meticulously considered the arguments and evidence presented by both sides. It is undisputed that the Respondent operates a large industrial facility involved in glass manufacturing, a process inherently associated with significant atmospheric emissions. The core of the Complainant's case rests on the consistent findings of NEQS exceedances, the alleged failure to install adequate pollution control devices, and the absence of a valid Environmental Approval.
Upon review of the evidence, the Tribunal finds the Complainant's submissions to be robust and compelling. The inspection reports, detailing visible smoke plumes and inadequate infrastructure, were corroborated by photographic and videographic evidence. More critically, the laboratory analysis reports, generated from samples collected on two distinct occasions (15.11.2016 and 02.02.2017), unequivocally demonstrated that the emissions of particulate matter, NOx, and SOx from the Respondent's facility consistently exceeded the prescribed NEQS. The Respondent's general denials and unsubstantiated claims regarding faulty sampling methodology were not supported by any credible counter-evidence, such as independent expert reports or their own concurrent monitoring data. The consistent pattern of exceedances, ranging from 1.5 to 3 times the permissible limits, negates the argument of "isolated incidents" or "transient operational fluctuations."
Furthermore, the repeated issuance of warning letters and show-cause notices by the EPA, coupled with the Respondent's evasive or unsatisfactory replies, clearly indicates a prolonged period of non-compliance and a failure to take timely and effective corrective actions. The absence of a valid Environmental Approval or its renewal, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, constitutes a clear procedural violation that undermines the regulatory framework designed to prevent and control pollution. While the Tribunal acknowledges the economic contributions of industrial entities, compliance with environmental laws is paramount and cannot be sacrificed for commercial considerations. The health and environmental impacts of sustained pollution far outweigh the perceived inconveniences of compliance.
Based on the overwhelming weight of evidence, the Tribunal is satisfied that the Complainant has proven beyond reasonable doubt that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has been operating its industrial facility in violation of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, particularly Section 12 and Section 16 thereof, and has consistently failed to meet the National Environmental Quality Standards for industrial emissions.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
