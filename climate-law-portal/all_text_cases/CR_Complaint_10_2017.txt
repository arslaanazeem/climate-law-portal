This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), alleging violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and its allied rules and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and air emissions from its textile manufacturing facility. The Tribunal was seized of the matter upon receiving the formal complaint, seeking an Environmental Protection Order, imposition of penalties, and remedial measures against the Respondent for its alleged non-compliance with environmental standards.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's failure to adequately treat its industrial wastewater before discharge and to control its air emissions, thereby causing pollution to the environment. Specifically, it was alleged that during an inspection conducted on 15.03.2017 by an EPA team, the Respondent's facility located at Raiwind Road, Lahore, was found to be discharging industrial effluent directly into a nearby drain without proper treatment. Samples collected from the discharge point and the Respondent's premises, upon laboratory analysis, revealed parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH values significantly exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Furthermore, it was alleged that the Respondent's boiler stack emissions were not equipped with adequate air pollution control devices, and monitoring indicated particulate matter (PM) levels above the prescribed NEQS for industrial emissions. The Complainant had issued multiple show-cause notices and an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, on 10.04.2017, directing the Respondent to install and operationalize a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and appropriate air pollution control equipment within a specified timeframe, and to submit regular compliance reports. The Respondent, despite receiving these notices and the EPO, allegedly failed to comply with the directives, leading to the initiation of this complaint before the Tribunal under Section 17 of PEPA, 1997.
In support of its complaint, the EPA presented a comprehensive set of documents and testimonies. Key evidence included:
1.  **Inspection Report dated 15.03.2017:** Detailing the observations made by the inspection team, including the direct discharge of effluent, lack of a functional ETP, and visible air emissions.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports (No. EPA/Lab/2017/045 dated 25.03.2017):** Issued by the EPA's accredited laboratory, confirming that the collected wastewater samples exceeded NEQS for COD (e.g., 500 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), BOD (e.g., 300 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), TSS (e.g., 250 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), and pH (e.g., 4.5 against a standard range of 6-9). Air emission analysis indicated PM levels of 600 mg/Nm³ against a standard of 300 mg/Nm³.
3.  **Copies of Show-Cause Notices (dated 20.03.2017 and 05.04.2017):** Documenting the formal communication to the Respondent regarding the violations and demanding explanations.
4.  **Environmental Protection Order (EPO No. EPA/EPO/10/2017 dated 10.04.2017):** Issued under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, specifying remedial actions and timelines.
5.  **Correspondence:** Including the Respondent's replies to the show-cause notices, which the Complainant deemed unsatisfactory, as they did not demonstrate concrete steps towards compliance.

The Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, through its legal counsel, appeared before the Tribunal and submitted a written statement in response to the complaint. In its defense, the Respondent primarily contended that it was fully aware of its environmental obligations and had initiated steps towards compliance, but faced unforeseen technical and financial challenges in installing the required advanced Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices. It was argued that the textile industry is capital-intensive, and the sudden imposition of stringent standards, coupled with the economic climate, made immediate compliance difficult. The Respondent claimed to have engaged environmental consultants to assess the best available technologies and was in the process of procuring necessary equipment. They also argued that some initial measures were taken to mitigate pollution, such as rudimentary screening of effluent, and that the EPA's sampling might not fully represent their overall operational practices, though no counter-analytical reports were presented. They requested more time to achieve full compliance and emphasized their significant contribution to the national economy and employment generation.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by the Complainant and considered the arguments advanced by both parties. It is an established principle that environmental protection is a fundamental right, and industries have a non-negotiable responsibility to operate within the prescribed environmental standards. The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) are not mere guidelines but legally binding requirements designed to prevent environmental degradation and protect public health.
The Complainant's evidence, particularly the Inspection Report dated 15.03.2017 and the Laboratory Analysis Reports dated 25.03.2017, unequivocally demonstrated that the Respondent's facility was discharging industrial effluent with parameters significantly exceeding the NEQS for COD, BOD, TSS, and pH. Similarly, the air emission analysis showed particulate matter levels well above the permissible limits. These findings were corroborated by the visual observations recorded during the inspection. The Tribunal noted that the Complainant had followed due process by issuing multiple show-cause notices and subsequently an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) dated 10.04.2017, providing the Respondent with ample opportunity to rectify the violations. The Respondent's replies to these notices and the EPO, as per the record, did not demonstrate concrete, verifiable steps towards achieving compliance within the stipulated timeframes. While the Tribunal acknowledges the operational and financial challenges that industries may face, such challenges cannot serve as an excuse for persistent non-compliance with environmental laws, especially when the violations are as egregious and long-standing as evidenced in this case. The Respondent's claims of initiating steps were not substantiated with concrete proof of substantial progress, such as contracts for ETP/APCD installation, detailed project timelines, or interim compliance measures that significantly reduced pollution levels. The absence of counter-evidence challenging the EPA's laboratory analysis further strengthens the Complainant's case.
The Tribunal finds that the Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, has demonstrably failed to comply with the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, particularly Section 12 (requiring approval of environmental impact assessment for new projects/expansion) and Section 17 (prohibiting discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS), and has also failed to comply with the lawful directives contained in the Environmental Protection Order issued under Section 16 of the Act. The persistent discharge of untreated effluent and uncontrolled air emissions constitutes a clear and present danger to the environment and the health of the surrounding communities.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal hereby declares M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited guilty of violating the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and its allied rules and regulations, specifically the National Environmental Quality Standards.
Therefore, it is hereby ordered as follows:
1.  **Environmental Protection Order:** M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited is directed to immediately cease the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and uncontrolled air emissions.
2.  **Installation and Operationalization of Treatment Facilities:** The Respondent shall, within a strict period of **six (6) months** from the date of this judgment, install and fully operationalize a state-of-the-art Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and appropriate Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) that ensure all discharges and emissions consistently meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
3.  **Submission of Compliance Plan:** Within **thirty (30) days** of this judgment, the Respondent shall submit a detailed compliance plan, including engineering designs, procurement schedules, and implementation timelines for the ETP and APCDs, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, for approval.
4.  **Regular Monitoring and Reporting:** Upon operationalization of the ETP and APCDs, the Respondent shall conduct regular self-monitoring of its effluent and emissions, submit monthly compliance reports to the EPA, Lahore, and arrange for quarterly third-party verified environmental audits for a period of two years.
5.  **Monetary Penalty:** In exercise of the powers conferred by Section 17(2) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, a penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand Only)** is imposed on M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited for the violations committed. This amount shall be paid to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this judgment.
6.  **Continuing Contravention Penalty:** Should the Respondent fail to comply with the directives contained in clauses 1, 2, 3, and 4 of this order within the specified timelines, an additional daily penalty of **PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand Only)** shall be imposed for each day of continuing contravention, until full compliance is achieved.
7.  **Costs:** The Respondent shall bear the costs of these proceedings.
Failure to comply with any part of this order may result in further legal action, including but not limited to, the sealing of the industrial unit under the relevant provisions of PEPA, 1997.
