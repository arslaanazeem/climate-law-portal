This judgment pertains to a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent industrial enterprise located in Lahore. The complaint invokes the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleging contraventions of various provisions of the said Act and its allied National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal, being duly constituted under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, is empowered to adjudicate upon such matters and impose penalties as prescribed by law.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around persistent non-compliance by the Respondent with environmental standards. Specifically, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial facility was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into public sewers and ultimately into natural water bodies, with parameters exceeding the NEQS for industrial effluents. Furthermore, the complaint detailed concerns regarding air emissions from the Respondent's operations, particularly from its boiler stacks and other process units, which were reported to be emitting particulate matter and gaseous pollutants (Sulphur Dioxide, Nitrogen Oxides) above the prescribed NEQS. The EPA also raised concerns about the improper storage and disposal of hazardous waste generated during the manufacturing processes, including chemical sludge and residues, which were not being managed in accordance with the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003, leading to potential soil and groundwater contamination. The Complainant asserted that despite repeated notices and warnings issued by the EPA, the Respondent had failed to take adequate remedial measures to ensure full compliance.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports (Exhibit C-1 to C-4), dated 15th March 2017, 22nd May 2017, and 10th July 2017, respectively, documenting the observations of EPA field teams regarding the Respondent's operations. These reports included photographic evidence of dark-colored effluent discharge and visible smoke emissions. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports (Exhibit C-5 to C-8) from samples collected on various dates, analyzed by the EPA's accredited laboratory. These reports indicated that the biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), chemical oxygen demand (COD), total suspended solids (TSS), and pH levels of the Respondent's effluent consistently exceeded the NEQS. Similarly, the air quality monitoring reports demonstrated exceedances of particulate matter (PM10), SO2, and NOx limits. The Assistant Director, EPA, Mr. Ahmed Khan (CW-1), testified, detailing the history of non-compliance, the issuance of environmental protection orders (EPOs), and the Respondent's failure to adhere to them. He also presented correspondence exchanged between the EPA and the Respondent, showing the latter's acknowledgment of issues but insufficient corrective action.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations of willful non-compliance. It was argued that the Respondent is a responsible corporate entity with a long-standing commitment to environmental stewardship and had invested significantly in pollution control equipment. The Respondent's Environment Manager, Mr. Raza Ali (RW-1), testified that the company had an operational effluent treatment plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices (APCDs), which were regularly maintained. He presented internal monitoring reports (Exhibit R-1 to R-4) which, while occasionally showing minor fluctuations, generally indicated compliance or near-compliance. The Respondent also submitted invoices and service records for upgrades to its ETP and APCDs, asserting that it was in the process of implementing further improvements to meet stringent environmental standards. An independent environmental consultant, Dr. Sara Khan (RW-2), testified on behalf of the Respondent, explaining the complex nature of the industrial processes involved and the challenges of achieving absolute compliance at all times, suggesting that the EPA's sampling might have been unrepresentative or conducted during atypical operational cycles. The Respondent further contended that some of the alleged exceedances
were minor, temporary deviations, or could be attributed to the inherent variability of industrial processes, rather than a deliberate failure to comply. They also highlighted their significant contribution to the national economy, employment generation, and corporate social responsibility initiatives, requesting a lenient view given their overall commitment to environmental protection and ongoing efforts to upgrade their facilities.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 300,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
