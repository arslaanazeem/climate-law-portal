**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses the environmental complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent" or "Nishat Mills"), a textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore. The complaint, lodged under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleges significant violations of national environmental quality standards and non-compliance with environmental regulations, thereby posing a threat to public health and the environment. The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's alleged failure to adequately treat its industrial effluent and control its air emissions. Specifically, the EPA contended that multiple inspections conducted at Nishat Mills' premises revealed consistent discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial wastewater into the municipal sewerage system, eventually leading to local water bodies, in contravention of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for Municipal and Industrial Effluents. Furthermore, the complaint highlighted the absence of effective air pollution control measures for emissions from the Respondent's boiler house and dyeing processes, resulting in the release of pollutants exceeding the NEQS for Industrial Gaseous Emissions. The EPA also alleged that the Respondent had failed to respond effectively to prior warning notices and Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) issued under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental compliance.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 15.03.2022, 28.05.2022, and 05.08.2022, detailing observations of effluent discharge points and emission sources. Crucially, the EPA submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on these dates, which indicated levels of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH values significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS. Similarly, air emission samples showed elevated levels of particulate matter (PM), sulfur dioxide (SO2), and nitrogen oxides (NOx) beyond permissible limits. Photographic and videographic evidence depicting the visible discharge of colored effluent and black smoke plumes from the Respondent's premises were also tendered. The Assistant Director, EPA, also appeared as a witness, affirming the contents of the inspection reports and the non-compliance observed. Copies of show-cause notices and an EPO issued to M/s Nishat Mills Limited on 10.06.2022, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were also part of the record.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its counsel, denied the persistent non-compliance. The Respondent argued that it had invested significantly
...in environmental management systems and effluent treatment plants (ETPs) and air pollution control devices. The Respondent asserted that it was fully committed to environmental protection and had made substantial efforts to comply with NEQS. Its counsel argued that any instances of non-compliance, if they occurred, were isolated operational glitches, temporary breakdowns, or attributable to external factors beyond their immediate control, such as fluctuations in raw material
...quality or production demands. They contended that the EPA's sampling might not be representative of their overall operations and that the laboratory analysis reports lacked sufficient detail regarding chain of custody and calibration of equipment to be considered irrefutable. The Respondent also submitted some internal monitoring reports and certificates of partial upgrades to their ETP and air emission control systems, asserting that these demonstrated ongoing efforts towards compliance. Furthermore, it was argued that the textile industry is a significant contributor to the national economy, and overly stringent enforcement without considering practical challenges could have detrimental impacts on industrial growth and employment.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent. While acknowledging the Respondent's claims of investment in environmental infrastructure and its economic contribution, the primary mandate of this Tribunal is to uphold the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and ensure compliance with environmental standards to protect public health and the environment. The evidence presented by the Complainant, particularly the multiple, consistent laboratory analysis reports from different dates, showing significant exceedances of NEQS for both effluent and air emissions, carries substantial weight. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,200,000/- (Rupees One Million Two Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 240,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
