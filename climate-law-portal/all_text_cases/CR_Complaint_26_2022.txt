This judgment addresses Complaint No. 26/2022, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), situated at Kala Shah Kaku, District Sheikhupura. The complaint seeks enforcement of environmental laws and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA 1997) and its allied rules and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and air emissions exceeding prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of various chemicals, has been consistently discharging industrial effluent containing hazardous substances directly into the nearby water bodies without adequate treatment, thereby polluting the environment and posing significant threats to public health and aquatic life. Furthermore, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations result in air emissions, particularly from its boiler and process stacks, which exceed the NEQS for various parameters, including Particulate Matter (PM), Sulfur Oxides (SOx), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx). The Complainant submitted that despite repeated warnings, issuance of environmental improvement notices under Section 16 of PEPA 1997, and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent has failed to take effective measures to control pollution from its premises. Specifically, the allegations include:
a) Discharge of industrial effluent with parameters (Biological Oxygen Demand - BOD, Chemical Oxygen Demand - COD, Total Suspended Solids - TSS, pH, etc.) exceeding the NEQS, in violation of Section 11 of PEPA 1997.
b) Emission of air pollutants from stacks exceeding the NEQS, in violation of Section 11 of PEPA 1997.
c) Failure to install and effectively operate an appropriate Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs).
d) Non-compliance with the conditions stipulated in the Environmental Approval/No Objection Certificate (NOC) granted to the Respondent.
e) Failure to submit regular self-monitoring reports as required under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2014.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports, dating from January 2021 to September 2022, conducted by EPA teams at the Respondent's facility. These reports consistently highlighted deficiencies in the Respondent's environmental management systems. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent and air samples collected from the Respondent's premises on multiple occasions by EPA-accredited laboratories. These reports unequivocally demonstrated that the discharged effluent parameters and stack emissions were significantly above the permissible NEQS. For instance, an effluent sample collected on 15.06.2022 showed BOD at 350 mg/L (NEQS: 80 mg/L), COD at 800 mg/L (NEQS: 150 mg/L), and TSS at 280 mg/L (NEQS: 200 mg/L). Similarly, air emission reports from 21.07.2022 indicated PM levels of 600 mg/Nm³ (NEQS: 200 mg/Nm³) and SOx levels of 2500 mg/Nm³ (NEQS: 1700 mg/Nm³). The Complainant also produced copies of Environmental Improvement Notices (EINs) issued to the Respondent on 10.03.2021, 05.08.2021, and 01.04.2022, along with reminders, which remained largely unheeded. Photographic evidence depicting the discharge of dark-colored effluent into a drain was also presented.
The Respondent, represented by its legal counsel, denied the allegations, contending that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection. It was argued that the Respondent has an operational Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and has invested substantially
in environmental protection measures, including the installation of an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and various air pollution control devices. They contended that while minor operational glitches might occasionally occur, the plant is generally compliant with environmental standards. The Respondent's counsel further argued that the EPA's sampling methodology and laboratory reports were not entirely reliable, suggesting that samples might have been collected under unusual operational conditions or that
the analytical results were flawed due to improper handling or calibration issues. They further asserted that they had initiated a project to upgrade their existing ETP and install more efficient APCDs, and that significant progress had been made, requesting more time to achieve full compliance. The Respondent also submitted some internal monitoring reports, which, however, lacked independent verification and showed occasional non-compliance. They emphasized their contribution to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to consider a lenient view given their commitment to future compliance.
This Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both parties and meticulously reviewed the documentary and photographic evidence placed on record. The core of the Complainant's case rests on a consistent pattern of violations evidenced by multiple inspection reports and, more critically, by laboratory analysis reports from EPA-accredited laboratories. The samples were collected on different dates, under varying conditions, and consistently showed parameters exceeding the NEQS by significant margins. The Respondent's contention regarding the unreliability of EPA's sampling methodology and laboratory reports lacks specific, credible evidence to counter the detailed and certified reports presented by the Complainant. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,100,000/- (Rupees One Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 220,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
