This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA" or "Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "Respondent"), a prominent glass manufacturing facility located at 18-Km Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The complaint alleges various violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"), concerning the Respondent's industrial operations and their environmental impact. The Tribunal assumed jurisdiction over the matter pursuant to Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, to adjudicate upon the environmental offences and impose appropriate penalties and directions.
The Complainant's allegations, as detailed in the complaint and subsequent submissions, primarily revolve around the Respondent's failure to comply with environmental standards and regulatory requirements. Specifically, it was alleged that:
i. The Respondent's industrial processes, particularly its furnace stack emissions, were consistently exceeding the prescribed NEQS for Particulate Matter (PM), Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx), and Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), thereby contributing to air pollution in the vicinity.
ii. The Respondent was operating its facility without a valid Environmental Approval (formerly EIA approval) for certain expansions and modifications undertaken subsequent to its initial establishment, in violation of Section 12 of PEPA, 1997 and the Environmental Impact Assessment and Initial Environmental Examination Regulations, 2000.
iii. The Complainant further contended that the Respondent had failed to install and/or maintain adequate pollution control equipment, or that the existing equipment was inefficient, leading to the aforementioned exceedances.
iv. Repeated notices of violation issued by the EPA had not resulted in satisfactory compliance or demonstrable efforts towards achieving the prescribed standards within the stipulated timelines.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive array of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 15.03.2021, 28.04.2021, and 10.06.2021, detailing observations of visible emissions and operational deficiencies. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited environmental laboratory, confirming the exceedance of NEQS for stack emissions. The report dated 20.03.2021 showed PM at 350 mg/Nm³ (NEQS: 200 mg/Nm³), NOx at 1200 mg/Nm³ (NEQS: 800 mg/Nm³), and SO2 at 1800 mg/Nm³ (NEQS: 1700 mg/Nm³ for existing industries). Subsequent reports indicated marginal improvements but still above permissible limits. Photographic evidence depicting dark plumes emanating from the Respondent's stacks was also presented. Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices and final notices issued to the Respondent, along with their unsatisfactory replies, were placed on record.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, vehemently denied the allegations. Through its counsel and submitted written statements, the Respondent argued that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection. It contended that:
i. The sampling methodologies employed by the Complainant's team were flawed and did not accurately represent the typical operational conditions of the plant.
ii. The Respondent had invested significantly in state-of-the-art pollution control technologies, including Electrostatic Precipitators (ESPs) and bag filters, and was actively working on upgrading its systems.
iii. The Respondent furnished its own internal monitoring reports, which, while showing some fluctuations, generally claimed compliance or near-compliance with NEQS. However, these reports were not from an independent accredited laboratory.
iv. Regarding the environmental approval, the Respondent argued that its operations were established prior to the full implementation of the EIA regulations for certain categories, and any subsequent modifications were minor and did not warrant a fresh EIA, or that applications for necessary approvals were pending with the EPA.
v. The Respondent also presented evidence of ongoing efforts to procure and install advanced monitoring equipment and sought additional time to achieve full compliance, citing technical complexities and supply chain challenges.
The pertinent legal framework governing this matter is primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Specifically, the following provisions are relevant:

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
