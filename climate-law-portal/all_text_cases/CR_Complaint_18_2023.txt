This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore, for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The complaint, lodged on May 15, 2023, seeks the imposition of penalties and remedial measures against the Respondent for causing environmental pollution.
The Complainant's primary allegations against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited are multifaceted, stemming from multiple inspections and monitoring activities conducted by the EPA. Firstly, it is alleged that the Respondent has been continuously discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into the municipal sewerage system, which eventually flows into natural water bodies, thereby exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial and municipal effluents. Secondly, the Complainant asserts that the Respondent has failed to install and/or effectively operate a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) despite repeated directives and warnings from the EPA. Thirdly, the Respondent is accused of operating without the requisite Environmental Approval (Initial Environmental Examination or Environmental Impact Assessment approval, as applicable) for its current operations, or failing to comply with the conditions stipulated in any previously granted approval. Lastly, the Complainant highlights the Respondent's consistent non-compliance with show-cause notices, final warnings, and Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) issued by the EPA under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental regulations.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated January 10, 2023, March 02, 2023, and April 20, 2023, detailing observations of direct effluent discharge, the apparent non-functionality or inadequacy of the Respondent's purported ETP, and the absence of proper waste management practices. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on February 15, 2023, and April 25, 2023. These reports, prepared by the EPA's accredited laboratory, unequivocally demonstrated that key parameters such as Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels significantly exceeded the NEQS. Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued on February 20, 2023, and a final warning dated March 15, 2023, along with a formal Environmental Protection Order (EPO) issued on April 05, 2023, were presented, clearly outlining the violations and demanding corrective action, none of which, according to the Complainant, were satisfactorily addressed. The Assistant Director, EPA, and a field inspector also testified, corroborating the documentary evidence and detailing their observations during site visits.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, through its legal counsel and factory manager, vehemently denied the extent of the alleged violations. They acknowledged having an Effluent Treatment Plant but contended that it was undergoing upgrades and maintenance, which temporarily affected its optimal performance. They presented some internal maintenance records and invoices for chemical purchases, attempting to demonstrate ongoing efforts towards environmental compliance. The Respondent's counsel argued that the samples collected by the EPA might not have been representative due to the plant's operational fluctuations and that their facility was a significant contributor to the local economy, employing a large workforce. They requested leniency and additional time
to bring their operations into full compliance. They further submitted that the economic impact of any stringent measures by the Tribunal would be detrimental to their business and the livelihoods of their employees.
The Tribunal carefully considered the arguments advanced by both parties and meticulously reviewed the documentary and oral evidence presented. While the Respondent acknowledged the existence of an Effluent Treatment Plant and claimed ongoing upgrades, they failed to produce any credible, independently verifiable evidence to counter the EPA's laboratory analysis reports, which unequivocally established the exceedance of NEQS parameters. The internal maintenance records and invoices for chemical purchases, while indicative of some activity, did not demonstrate that the ETP was functioning effectively to meet the prescribed
NEQS. The Respondent's assertion that the samples might not have been representative due to operational fluctuations was not supported by any credible counter-evidence or independent analysis. The consistent exceedance of critical parameters across multiple samples taken on different dates strongly indicates a systemic failure in effluent treatment. Furthermore, the claim of "upgrades and maintenance" affecting optimal performance does not absolve the Respondent of its statutory obligation to comply with NEQS at all times, especially given the repeated notices and an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) from the EPA. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent's arguments regarding economic impact, while noted, cannot take precedence over the fundamental right to a clean and healthy environment as enshrined in the Constitution of Pakistan and protected by PEPA, 1997. Continuous environmental degradation poses a far greater long-term cost to society and public health.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
