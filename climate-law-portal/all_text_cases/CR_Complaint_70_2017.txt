This judgment addresses the environmental complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent textile manufacturing unit, for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal was seized with the matter to determine whether the Respondent has contravened environmental laws by discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the environment, thereby causing pollution.
The Complainant alleged that the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, operating a large textile processing unit, has been consistently discharging industrial effluent containing hazardous chemicals and pollutants directly into the municipal sewerage system, which eventually flows into natural water bodies, without adequate treatment. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  Failure to install and/or properly operate an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) capable of treating industrial wastewater to meet the prescribed NEQS for industrial effluents.
2.  Repeated violations of NEQS, particularly concerning parameters such as Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and various heavy metals, as evidenced by multiple inspections and laboratory analyses conducted by the EPA.
3.  Non-compliance with environmental improvement notices and directions issued by the EPA on various dates, including a notice dated 15.05.2016 and a final warning dated 10.03.2017, requiring the Respondent to take corrective measures and achieve compliance with NEQS.
4.  Causing environmental degradation and posing a significant threat to public health and aquatic life due to the discharge of untreated wastewater.
5.  Operating without a valid No Objection Certificate (NOC) for effluent discharge, or failing to adhere to the conditions of any existing NOC.
The Complainant presented the following evidence to substantiate its allegations:
1.  **Inspection Reports**: Detailed inspection reports dated 02.04.2016, 18.11.2016, and 05.04.2017, prepared by EPA field teams, documenting observations of effluent discharge points and the apparent lack of functional treatment facilities or inadequate treatment.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports**: Three separate laboratory analysis reports from the EPA Environmental Monitoring Laboratory, corresponding to samples collected on 02.04.2016, 18.11.2016, and 05.04.2017. These reports consistently showed that the discharged effluent parameters (e.g., BOD ranging from 450-580 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L; COD from 1200-1550 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L; TSS from 350-480 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L) significantly exceeded the prescribed NEQS.
3.  **Correspondence Record**: Copies of environmental improvement notices, show-cause notices, and final warnings issued to the Respondent, demonstrating a history of non-compliance and the EPA'
s persistent efforts to bring the Respondent into compliance.
The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It was contended that the Respondent operates an existing Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and is committed to environmental stewardship. The Respondent argued that any exceedances in the NEQS parameters might be attributable to temporary operational issues, maintenance activities, or potential anomalies
in sampling or testing procedures. The Respondent further asserted that it has invested substantial resources in its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and is in the process of further upgrading its facilities to ensure full compliance with NEQS. It was also argued that the EPA had not adequately considered the complexities involved in treating industrial textile effluent and that the timelines provided for compliance were often unrealistic. The Respondent requested the Tribunal to dismiss the complaint or, in the alternative, provide a reasonable timeframe for achieving compliance without imposing harsh penalties, highlighting its significant contribution to the national economy and employment.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties. The Complainant's evidence, particularly the consistent findings in the laboratory analysis reports from multiple sampling dates over a period of more than a year, paints a clear and compelling picture of persistent non-compliance. The argument of "temporary operational issues" or "potential anomalies" put forth by the Respondent holds little weight against the backdrop of three separate, independently conducted analyses all showing significant and repeated exceedances of critical NEQS parameters. The consistent high levels of BOD, COD, and TSS, far exceeding the permissible limits, indicate a systemic failure in the Respondent's effluent treatment process, rather than isolated incidents.
Furthermore, the record of correspondence, including environmental improvement notices, show-cause notices, and a final warning, clearly demonstrates that the EPA had provided ample opportunities and time to the Respondent to rectify the situation. The Respondent's failure to achieve compliance despite these repeated notices undermines its claim of commitment to environmental stewardship in practice. While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's economic contributions, such contributions cannot be a justification for violating environmental laws that are designed to protect public health and the environment. The PEPA, 1997, particularly Section 12, mandates that no person shall discharge or emit any effluent or waste in an amount, concentration, or level that is in excess of the NEQS. Section 14 prohibits the discharge of any untreated effluent

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,300,000/- (Rupees One Million Three Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 260,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
