**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 31/2018 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), seeking action under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"), for alleged violations of environmental laws and National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal, vested with jurisdiction under Section 20 of the Act, is tasked with adjudicating disputes arising from environmental pollution and enforcing compliance with environmental regulations.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of various chemicals, including caustic soda and chlorine, located in Kala Shah Kaku, District Sheikhupura, was repeatedly found to be discharging industrial effluent into the environment without adequate treatment. Specifically, it was asserted that the Respondent's effluent discharge consistently exceeded the prescribed NEQS for several critical parameters, posing a significant threat to public health, aquatic life, and the general environment. The complaint detailed a history of non-compliance, including multiple inspections, issuance of warning notices, and show-cause notices by the EPA, all of which allegedly failed to bring the Respondent into full compliance with environmental standards. The Complainant sought a declaration that the Respondent was in violation of the Act and NEQS, an order for immediate compliance, and the imposition of penalties as stipulated by law.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant submitted a comprehensive array of evidence. This included certified copies of inspection reports dated 15.03.2017, 22.08.2017, and 05.02.2018, meticulously documenting the observations of EPA officials regarding the Respondent's effluent treatment plant (ETP) operations and discharge points. Crucially, the Complainant presented laboratory analysis reports from the certified EPA Laboratory, bearing reference numbers EPA/LAB/2017/1123, EPA/LAB/2017/1789, and EPA/LAB/2018/0215, corresponding to effluent samples collected during the aforementioned inspections. These reports consistently showed that parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels in the Respondent's discharged effluent were significantly above the permissible NEQS limits. For instance, the report dated 05.02.2018 indicated a COD level of 350 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L, and a BOD5 level of 180 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 80 mg/L. Furthermore, the Complainant tendered copies of the Show Cause Notice No. AD(L)/EPA/2017/567 dated 10.04.2017 and Environmental Protection Order (EPO) No. AD(L)/EPA/2017/987 dated 05.09.2017, issued to the Respondent, detailing the violations and requiring remedial actions, which were allegedly not fully complied with. An affidavit from the Assistant Director, EPA, Lahore, Mr. Asif Mehmood, affirmed the veracity of the facts and documents presented.
The Tribunal considered the relevant legal framework, primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Key provisions applicable to this case include:
*   **Section 11 (Prohibition of discharge of effluent, etc., in excess of standards):** This section prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the NEQS.
*   **Section 16 (Environmental Protection Order):** This section empowers the Agency to issue an EPO requiring
a person to take such measures as may be specified in the order to prevent or abate pollution, or to mitigate the adverse effects of pollution, or to restore the environment.
*   **Section 17 (Penalties):** This section prescribes penalties for contravention or failure to comply with any provision of the Act, an order, or direction issued thereunder. It stipulates a fine which may extend to
one million rupees, and in case of a continuing contravention, with an additional fine which may extend to one hundred thousand rupees for every day during which such contravention continues.
The Respondent, through its learned counsel, vehemently contested the allegations, asserting that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection. While acknowledging certain past challenges in meeting NEQS, the Respondent contended that significant investments had been made in upgrading its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and implementing cleaner production technologies. It was argued that the reported exceedances were either isolated incidents, attributable to unforeseen operational upsets, or that the EPA's sampling and analysis methodology might not have accurately reflected the overall compliance status. The Respondent presented internal monitoring reports and invoices for ETP upgrades, claiming substantial progress towards achieving full compliance. It was further submitted that the company employed a dedicated environmental management team and had a robust internal environmental monitoring program. The Respondent prayed for the dismissal of the complaint or, in the alternative, for a lenient view given its proactive measures and economic contributions.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence and heard arguments from both sides. The core of the Complainant's case rested on the certified laboratory analysis reports from the EPA Laboratory, which consistently indicated exceedances of NEQS for key parameters. These reports, being from a statutory body's accredited laboratory, carry significant evidentiary weight, especially when corroborated by detailed inspection reports. The Respondent's assertion of ongoing upgrades and isolated incidents, while noted, did not sufficiently rebut the direct evidence of non-compliance presented for the specific dates in question. The repeated nature of the violations, as evidenced by the series of inspection reports, warning notices, and the Environmental Protection Order, undermines the claim of isolated incidents or unforeseen upsets. The Tribunal observes that while proactive measures are commendable, they do not absolve the Respondent of liability for past and ongoing non-compliance with the prescribed standards. The purpose of the Act is to prevent and control pollution, and persistent violations, regardless of mitigation efforts, require decisive action. **V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
