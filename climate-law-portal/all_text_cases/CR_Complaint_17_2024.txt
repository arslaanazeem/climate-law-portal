This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 17/2024, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), with its registered office located at [Fictional Address], Lahore. The complaint was instituted under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the rules and regulations framed thereunder, seeking remedial action and imposition of penalties for alleged environmental violations. The proceedings were conducted under the esteemed Chairmanship of Mr. Justice (R) Ali Baqar Najafi, duly appointed to preside over this Tribunal.
It is alleged by the Complainant that the Respondent, an industrial chemical manufacturing unit, has been operating in violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for effluent discharge and gaseous emissions, thereby causing significant environmental pollution. Specifically, the complaint details that on multiple occasions, routine inspections conducted by EPA officials revealed non-compliance. The primary allegations include: (a) Discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/water bodies, with pollutant levels exceeding the prescribed NEQS for parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals. (b) Emissions of hazardous air pollutants from its stacks, particularly particulate matter and oxides of nitrogen (NOx), exceeding the NEQS, without adequate pollution control measures. (c) Failure to maintain proper records of waste generation, treatment, and disposal as required by environmental regulations. (d) Operating without a valid Environmental Management Plan (EMP) approval or failing to adhere to the conditions of its previously approved EMP, despite repeated warnings and notices issued by the EPA. The Complainant asserts that these violations pose a direct threat to public health and the environment, including local water resources and air quality.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a robust body of evidence. This included: (i) Inspection reports dated 15.01.2024, 28.01.2024, and 12.02.2024, signed by EPA officials, detailing observations of dark-colored effluent discharge and visible smoke plumes. (ii) Laboratory analysis reports from EPA-accredited laboratories, bearing reference numbers LAH/ENV/2024/005, LAH/ENV/2024/018, and LAH/ENV/2024/032, corresponding to samples collected from the Respondent's effluent discharge points and stack emissions on the aforementioned dates. These reports unequivocally indicated levels of COD (e.g., 450 mg/L against NEQS of 200 mg/L), BOD (e.g., 280 mg/L against NEQS of 80 mg/L), TSS (e.g., 300 mg/L against NEQS of 200 mg/L), and particulate matter (e.g., 200 mg/Nm³ against NEQS of 100 mg/Nm³) significantly exceeding the NEQS. (iii) Photographs and video footage depicting the alleged violations, particularly the color and turbidity of the discharged effluent and the density of stack emissions. (iv) Copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 20.01.2024 and 05.02.2024, and the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies thereto, demonstrating a pattern of non-compliance and inadequate corrective measures. (v) Witness testimony from Mr. Ahmed Khan, Assistant Director (Technical), EPA, who personally supervised the inspections and sample collection, confirming the findings of the reports. The Respondent, in its defense, primarily argued that it possesses a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control (APC) devices, attributing any exceedances to temporary operational glitches or power fluctuations beyond its control. It further contended that it has made substantial investments in environmental compliance and is committed to sustainable practices. However
, the Respondent failed to provide any credible, independent corroborating evidence to support its claims of 'temporary operational glitches' or 'power fluctuations' explaining the consistent and significant exceedances across multiple inspection dates. The laboratory reports, supported by photographic and video evidence, consistently demonstrated pollutant levels far beyond the permissible NEQS, suggesting a systemic failure in their pollution control infrastructure rather than isolated incidents. The Tribunal notes that while the Respondent claimed 'substantial investments in environmental compliance,' these investments clearly have not translated into effective compliance with the NEQS, as evidenced by the repeated violations. The Respondent also failed to produce updated operational records for its ETP and APC devices that would demonstrate proper maintenance or efficiency, nor did it offer any independent audit reports to counter the EPA's findings. The Tribunal finds the Respondent's defense to be unsubstantiated and insufficient to rebut the overwhelming evidence
presented by the Complainant.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
