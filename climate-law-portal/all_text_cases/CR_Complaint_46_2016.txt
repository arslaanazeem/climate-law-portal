This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent") for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The complaint, registered as No. 46/2016, was lodged on October 12, 2016, seeking the Tribunal's intervention to enforce environmental compliance and impose penalties for the Respondent's persistent failure to manage industrial effluents in accordance with prescribed standards, thereby causing environmental degradation. The Tribunal’s jurisdiction is invoked to ensure the protection, conservation, rehabilitation, and improvement of the environment, and for the prevention and control of pollution.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, a textile manufacturing unit located in an industrial area within the jurisdiction of Lahore, has been continuously discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system, which eventually contaminates agricultural lands and groundwater resources, posing significant threats to public health and the ecosystem. Specifically, it was alleged that multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials revealed consistent exceedance of NEQS for key parameters such as Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels in the effluent discharged from the Respondent's premises. The Complainant further asserted that despite repeated warnings, issuance of show-cause notices, and directions to install or upgrade an operational Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP), the Respondent failed to take effective measures to mitigate pollution, demonstrating a blatant disregard for environmental regulations. The show-cause notice dated June 15, 2016, and the final warning notice dated September 1, 2016, specifically highlighted non-compliance and warned of legal action under PEPA, 1997.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive body of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA field teams on various dates, notably April 20, 2016, July 5, 20
16, and August 25, 2016. These reports were accompanied by certified laboratory analysis reports from EPA-approved laboratories, which consistently showed effluent parameters exceeding the NEQS. Furthermore, copies of the aforementioned show-cause notice dated June 15, 2016, and the final warning notice dated September 1, 2016, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were also submitted. The Complainant emphasized that the Respondent's responses consistently failed to provide a concrete action plan for achieving compliance or to demonstrate any tangible progress in addressing the pollution issues.
The Respondent, through its legal counsel, contested the allegations, primarily arguing that it had initiated steps towards upgrading its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and was actively working to comply with environmental standards. They claimed that the process of ETP upgrade was complex and time-consuming, requiring significant capital investment and technical expertise, and that they had faced unforeseen delays. The
Respondent, through its legal counsel, contested the allegations, primarily arguing that it had initiated steps towards upgrading its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and was actively working to comply with environmental standards. They claimed that the process of ETP upgrade was complex and time-consuming, requiring significant capital investment and technical expertise, and that they had faced unforeseen delays. The Respondent further requested additional time to complete the upgrade and bring their operations into full compliance, suggesting that punitive action at this stage would impede their efforts rather than facilitate environmental improvement. However, the Respondent failed to provide concrete evidence of significant tangible progress, such as detailed project timelines, expenditure receipts for ETP components, or interim measures taken to mitigate pollution during the upgrade period, that would substantiate their claims of active compliance efforts beyond mere intent.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments put forth by both the Complainant and the Respondent, and meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence presented. It is evident from the numerous inspection reports and certified laboratory analyses submitted by the EPA that M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited has consistently discharged industrial effluent exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for key parameters. These violations were observed over an extended period, dating back to at least April 2016, and persisted despite multiple warnings and show-cause notices issued by the environmental regulatory authority.
While the Tribunal acknowledges the complexities and financial implications involved in upgrading an Effluent Treatment Plant, the continuous and unchecked discharge of pollutants poses an undeniable threat to public health and the environment, which cannot be overlooked or indefinitely excused. The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, specifically Section 11, places a clear legal obligation on all industrial units to ensure that their discharges conform to the NEQS. Section 16 of the Act empowers this Tribunal to take necessary measures to prevent and control pollution, including the issuance of orders for compliance and the imposition of penalties. The Respondent’s failure to take prompt and effective action, despite repeated opportunities, demonstrates a clear breach of its environmental responsibilities and a disregard for the statutory framework designed to protect the environment. The excuses of complexity and delays, while noted, do not absolve the Respondent of its primary duty to comply with environmental standards, especially when no robust interim measures or an approved, verifiable compliance schedule was presented to the EPA or this Tribunal. The evidence overwhelmingly establishes a persistent pattern of non-compliance.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
