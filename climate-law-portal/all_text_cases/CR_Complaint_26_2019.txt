**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 26/2019, instituted by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), an industrial unit engaged in textile manufacturing, for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS") promulgated thereunder. The proceedings commenced following the filing of the complaint, wherein the Complainant sought appropriate legal action, including imposition of penalties and directions for compliance, against the Respondent for environmental degradation.
The Complainant alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations, specifically its dyeing and finishing processes, were discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into the public sewer system and subsequently into a natural drain, thereby causing significant water pollution. It was further alleged that repeated inspections by EPA officials revealed consistent exceedance of the prescribed NEQS for various parameters, including but not limited to Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. The Complainant asserted that despite multiple warning notices and environmental protection orders issued under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, the Respondent failed to install a fully functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) or to adequately maintain its existing treatment facilities to meet the statutory standards. The EPA contended that such non-compliance constituted a direct violation of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, which prohibits the discharge of effluent or waste into the environment in an amount, concentration, or manner that causes or is likely to cause pollution, and also a breach of Section 12, as the Respondent was operating without a valid No Objection Certificate (NOC) for its updated operations or had failed to adhere to the conditions of any prior NOC.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports dated 15.03.2019, 02.05.2019, and 18.06.2019, conducted by EPA teams at the Respondent's premises. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on these dates, duly certified by the EPA's accredited laboratory. These reports consistently indicated that the discharged effluent contained pollutants far exceeding the permissible limits set by the NEQS. For instance, the report dated 02.05.2019 showed COD levels of 550 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L, and TSS levels of 300 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 200 mg/L. The Complainant also produced copies of Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) issued to the Respondent on 25.04.2019 and 20.06.2019, directing immediate remedial measures, which, according to the Complainant, were largely ignored. The EPA officials who conducted the inspections and collected samples appeared as witnesses, affirming the contents of their reports and the procedure followed. In response, the Respondent, through its authorized representative, denied the allegations, claiming that it had an operational ETP and was making efforts to
comply with the NEQS. The Respondent contended that its ETP was functional, albeit facing occasional operational challenges due to fluctuations in raw material input and power supply issues, which sometimes affected its optimal performance. They further submitted that they had invested significantly in environmental management and were committed to achieving full compliance. The Respondent also questioned the accuracy of some laboratory reports, suggesting potential discrepancies in sampling or analysis, though
failed to provide concrete evidence to support these claims, such as independent laboratory reports or challenges to the EPA's sampling methodology at the time of collection. They emphasized their substantial investment in the existing ETP and reiterated their commitment to environmental stewardship, requesting a pragmatic approach that considered the operational complexities of a large-scale textile unit and the economic implications of severe penalties.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the pleadings, the documentary evidence, and the testimonies of the witnesses presented by both parties. The Complainant's consistent submission of inspection reports and certified laboratory analysis reports, spanning several months, provides strong and compelling evidence of the Respondent's effluent discharge continuously exceeding the prescribed NEQS for critical parameters such. The procedural integrity of the EPA’s sampling and analysis was not effectively challenged or disproven by the Respondent. While the Respondent claimed to have an operational ETP and to be making efforts, these assertions were largely unsupported by specific, verifiable data, such as detailed ETP operational logs, maintenance records, or independent audit reports demonstrating consistent compliance or genuine efforts towards it. The Tribunal noted that general claims of "operational challenges" or "fluctuations" cannot serve as a perpetual excuse for persistent non-compliance with statutory environmental standards, especially after repeated warnings and the issuance of Environmental Protection Orders. The failure to effectively comply with the EPOs dated 25.04.2019 and 20.06.2019 further underscores the Respondent’s inadequate response to the environmental directives.
Based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established that the Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, has been discharging industrial effluent into the environment in an amount and concentration that causes pollution, thereby violating Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Furthermore, the Respondent has failed to operate its facility in adherence to the conditions of a valid No Objection Certificate or obtain one for its updated operations, constituting a contravention of Section 12 of PEPA, 1997. The environmental degradation caused by the Respondent's operations is a serious matter, impacting public health and the ecosystem, and warrants immediate and decisive action.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal hereby issues the following order:
1.  **FINDING OF GUILT:** The Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, is found guilty of contravening Sections 11 and 12 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
2.  **PENALTY:** A fine of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million Only)** is imposed on the Respondent for the contraventions established. Additionally, a daily fine of **PKR 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand Only)** shall be imposed for every day the contraventions continue beyond **sixty (60) days** from the date of this judgment, until full compliance is achieved as verified by the Environmental Protection Agency.
3.  **DIRECTIONS FOR COMPLIANCE:** The Respondent is directed to:
    a.  Immediately undertake all necessary measures

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,100,000/- (Rupees One Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 220,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
