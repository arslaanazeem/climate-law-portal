This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), concerning alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations. The complaint, registered on 15.03.2022, seeks the imposition of penalties and remedial measures against the Respondent for persistent non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and other provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997").
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's textile manufacturing facility located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore. It is contended that the Respondent has been consistently discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants far exceeding the permissible limits set by the NEQS, without adequate treatment. Specifically, the EPA alleged that despite numerous inspections, issuance of show-cause notices, and opportunities for rectification, the Respondent failed to install and operate an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) capable of treating its industrial wastewater to the prescribed standards. The complaint details instances where samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points revealed concentrations of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels significantly higher than the NEQS. Furthermore, the Complainant asserted that the Respondent failed to submit regular self-monitoring reports as required under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2000, and demonstrated a general disregard for environmental compliance.
In support of its complaint, the EPA presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included a series of inspection reports dating from July 2021 to February 2022, prepared by EPA officials, documenting the state of the Respondent's wastewater management system. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the Punjab Environmental Quality Testing Laboratory, an accredited facility, confirming the exceedance of NEQS for various parameters in samples collected on 12.08.2021, 01.11.2021, and 20.01.2022. Photographic evidence depicting the discharge of discolored effluent into a nearby drain was also placed on record. Copies of show-cause notices issued on 25.08.2021 and 05.12.2021, along with the Respondent's responses (which the Complainant argued were unsatisfactory and lacked concrete action plans), were also presented. The Complainant further relied on the testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, Environmental Inspector, who conducted several inspections and personally collected samples, attesting to the persistent nature of the violations.
The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its legal counsel, contested the allegations. While acknowledging its industrial operations and the generation of wastewater, the Respondent argued that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to
environmental stewardship. They contended that they have initiated steps towards upgrading their existing Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and have invested significant resources in modernizing their wastewater management infrastructure. Their counsel submitted that the process of installing a state-of-the-art ETP is complex and time-consuming, requiring specialized equipment and technical expertise, and that they are making good faith efforts to achieve full compliance. They further argued that some of the samples cited by the Complainant might not be fully representative of their overall discharge, or that operational glitches, rather than systemic failures, were responsible for temporary exceedances. They also highlighted their contribution to the national economy and employment, implicitly seeking a more lenient approach. They requested the Tribunal to consider their ongoing
efforts for environmental compliance, the substantial investment already made, and to grant them a reasonable timeframe to complete the ETP upgrade without imposing harsh penalties that could adversely impact their operations and workforce.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the pleadings, the documentary evidence presented by both parties, and the testimony adduced. The Complainant, through a series of inspection reports and crucially, laboratory analysis reports from an accredited facility, has established a consistent pattern of non-compliance with the NEQS by the Respondent. The samples collected on 12.08.2021, 01.11.2021, and 20.01.2022, unequivocally demonstrate that the industrial effluent discharged by M/s Nishat Mills Limited exceeded permissible limits for BOD, COD, TSS, and pH. The photographic evidence further corroborated the visual aspect of the alleged discharge. The testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, Environmental Inspector, provided a direct account of the persistent nature of these violations despite repeated warnings and show-cause notices.
While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's claim of being a responsible corporate entity and their stated commitment to environmental stewardship, the evidence on record clearly indicates a failure to meet statutory obligations over a significant period. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 300,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
