This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), an industrial entity engaged in manufacturing and processing activities, located within the territorial jurisdiction of this Tribunal. The complaint, lodged under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleges serious contraventions of environmental laws and standards, posing a threat to public health and the environment. The Tribunal has carefully considered the submissions of both parties and the evidence on record to arrive at a reasoned decision.
The Complainant's primary contention is that the Respondent has consistently failed to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents and air emissions, as prescribed under the PEPA, 1997. Specifically, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial unit was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial wastewater directly into the municipal sewerage system, which eventually flows into natural water bodies, thereby contaminating water sources and harming aquatic life. Furthermore, the Complainant asserted that the Respondent's boiler stacks were emitting air pollutants, including particulate matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), in concentrations significantly exceeding the permissible NEQS limits, contributing to ambient air pollution in the surrounding residential and industrial areas. The complaint also highlighted the Respondent's alleged failure to maintain and operate an efficient Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs), despite repeated warnings and notices issued by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). It was further contended that the Respondent had not submitted regular environmental monitoring reports as required by law, nor had it obtained the necessary environmental approvals for certain operational expansions, indicating a disregard for environmental regulatory
framework.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, vehemently denied the allegations, asserting that it operates its industrial facility in an environmentally responsible manner and is committed to complying with all applicable environmental laws and standards. The Respondent contended that it has invested significantly in environmental protection infrastructure, including a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs), which
are regularly maintained and upgraded to ensure optimal performance. They presented internal monitoring data and some external audit reports, arguing that these reports generally indicated compliance or minor deviations that were promptly addressed. The Respondent further contended that the EPA's sampling methodology and analysis might have been flawed or not representative of their continuous operational practices. They also submitted that they had applied for environmental approvals for operational expansions and were awaiting the necessary clearances, implying that any delay was not due to their negligence. Furthermore, the Respondent emphasized its significant contribution to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to consider a balanced approach that promotes industrial growth alongside environmental protection. They sought dismissal of the complaint, or alternatively, a directive for collaborative efforts to achieve full compliance rather than punitive action.
**Tribunal's Findings and Analysis:**
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties, including the inspection reports, laboratory analysis results from the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), the Respondent's internal records, and the expert testimonies.
Upon examination of the laboratory reports submitted by the Complainant, it is evident that multiple samples of industrial effluent collected from the Respondent's discharge points on various dates between 2017 and 2018 consistently showed levels of Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and other parameters significantly exceeding the NEQS limits. Specifically, reports dated 15.03.2017, 28.08.2017, and 10.05.2018, bearing EPA-approved laboratory stamps, confirmed exceedances ranging from 50% to 200% above the prescribed NEQS for various pollutants. Similarly, air emission monitoring reports from boiler stacks, particularly those from 01.04.2017 and 20.09.2018, indicated concentrations of Particulate Matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) that were 30-70% higher than the permissible NEQS.
The Respondent's assertion regarding the functionality of its ETP and APCDs, while acknowledged, is not sufficiently supported by evidence demonstrating *consistent* compliance with NEQS. The internal monitoring data provided by the Respondent, while showing some periods of compliance, also contained gaps and inconsistencies, and lacked independent third-party verification for continuous periods. The Tribunal notes that while investment in environmental infrastructure is commendable, the ultimate measure of compliance is the actual quality of discharges and emissions. The argument regarding potential flaws in EPA's sampling methodology was not substantiated with concrete evidence or independent expert analysis that could discredit the EPA's findings. The EPA's procedures, as outlined in the Pakistan Environmental Protection (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2001, and subsequent guidelines, appear to have been followed.
Regarding the failure to submit regular environmental monitoring reports and obtain necessary environmental approvals for operational expansions, the Tribunal finds the Respondent's explanations inadequate. The requirement for regular reporting is a statutory obligation under Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, read with Rule 12 of the IEE/EIA Regulations, 2000, and is crucial for regulatory oversight. Similarly, Section 12 mandates obtaining Environmental Approval (EIA/IEE) for new projects or significant expansions, which the Respondent failed to demonstrate it possessed for the alleged expansions at the relevant times.
The cumulative evidence presented by the Complainant, including multiple inspection reports, photographic evidence of discharge points, and consistent laboratory analyses, overwhelmingly demonstrates that the Respondent has, on numerous occasions, contravened the provisions of PEPA, 1997, specifically Section 11 (prohibition of discharge of untreated effluent) and Section 15 (prohibition of discharge of air pollutants exceeding NEQS), and has also failed to comply with Section 12 (environmental approval) and other related rules concerning self-monitoring and reporting. The environmental degradation caused by such persistent non-compliance poses a verifiable threat to public health and the ecological balance of the surrounding areas.
Therefore, after careful deliberation and evaluation of all material facts and legal arguments, this Tribunal finds the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, liable for contraventions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards.
**Final Order and Penalty:**
In light of the findings above, and exercising the powers vested in this Tribunal under Section 22 and Section 23 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the Tribunal hereby issues the following order:
1.  **Declaration of Violation:** M/s Packages Limited is declared to have violated Section 11, Section 12, and Section 15 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards, by discharging untreated/inadequately treated industrial effluents and emitting air pollutants exceeding permissible limits, and by failing to obtain necessary environmental approvals and submit regular monitoring reports.
2.  **Cessation and Remediation:** The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, is hereby directed to:
    a.  Immediately cease the discharge of any industrial effluent that does not meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and ensure that all air emissions comply with the NEQS.
    b.  Submit to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) within **sixty (60) days** from the date of this order, a comprehensive Environmental Management Plan (EMP) detailing specific measures, timelines, and financial commitments for upgrading and optimizing its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) to consistently meet all NEQS. This plan must also address the establishment of a robust internal monitoring system and the regular submission of environmental monitoring reports.
    c.  Obtain all necessary environmental approvals (EIA/IEE) for any existing unapproved operational expansions and for any future projects, in accordance with Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, within **one hundred and twenty (120) days** of this order.
    d.  Engage an EPA-approved independent third-party environmental auditor to conduct quarterly environmental performance audits for a period of two years, with reports to be submitted directly to the EPA and this Tribunal.
3.  **Monetary Penalty:** In accordance with Section 22(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and considering the gravity and persistence of the violations, a penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** is hereby imposed on M/s Packages Limited. This penalty shall be paid to the Environmental Protection Agency within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this order.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 300,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **28 May, 2023**.
