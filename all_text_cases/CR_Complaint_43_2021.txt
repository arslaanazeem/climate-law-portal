This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 43/2021, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA" or "Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"). The complaint seeks the imposition of penalties and directions for compliance due to alleged violations of environmental standards and regulations by the Respondent's manufacturing facility located in Lahore. The Tribunal has jurisdiction to adjudicate upon such matters as conferred by Section 21 of PEPA, 1997.
The Complainant's primary allegations are that the Respondent's industrial operations consistently failed to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial gaseous emissions and industrial effluents, as prescribed under PEPA, 1997. Specifically, the EPA alleged that during multiple inspections conducted on 15.03.2021, 28.05.2021, and 12.08.2021, significant exceedances were observed and recorded in the Respondent's stack emissions for Particulate Matter (PM), Sulfur Dioxide (SO2), and Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx). Furthermore, the effluent discharged from the Respondent's facility into the public drainage system was found to contain levels of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), and Total Suspended Solids (TSS) far exceeding the permissible NEQS limits. The Complainant further contended that despite repeated warnings, issuance of show-cause notices (dated 05.04.2021 and 20.06.2021), and opportunities to rectify the deficiencies, the Respondent failed to install or adequately operate an effective Air Pollution Control System (APCS) and an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) capable of bringing their emissions and discharges within NEQS
limits. The Complainant further emphasized that despite repeated warnings, issuance of show-cause notices (dated 05.04.2021 and 20.06.2021), and opportunities to rectify the deficiencies, the Respondent failed to install or adequately operate an effective Air Pollution Control System (APCS) and an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) capable of bringing their emissions and discharges within NEQS limits. The Complainant underscored that these persistent violations constituted a grave threat to public health and the environment, necessitating the imposition of maximum penalties and stringent compliance directives under the Act.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its learned counsel, vehemently contested the allegations of persistent non-compliance. While acknowledging certain initial operational challenges at their facility, the Respondent contended that significant remedial measures had been undertaken subsequent to the EPA's initial inspections and notices. They presented documentary evidence, including procurement orders for new equipment, installation progress reports, and expenditures incurred for upgrading their pollution control infrastructure. The Respondent argued that the EPA's sampling dates did not reflect the current improved status of their facility and that a more recent assessment would demonstrate substantial
compliance with NEQS. They further requested that the Tribunal order fresh sampling and analysis to reflect their current operational status and argued that imposing penalties based on old data would be unjust and disproportionate, especially given their genuine efforts and significant financial investment in environmental upgrades. The Respondent also highlighted their commitment to sustainable practices and their contribution to the national economy, pleading for a lenient view given their proactive steps towards compliance.
The Tribunal carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent. Evidence presented by the EPA, including detailed inspection reports and laboratory analyses from 15.03.2021, 28.05.2021, and 12.08.2021, unequivocally established that the Respondent's facility was operating in clear violation of the NEQS for both gaseous emissions and industrial effluents on those specific dates. The repeated nature of these violations, despite prior warnings and show-cause notices, indicated a pattern of non-compliance over a significant period. While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's submission of procurement orders and installation progress reports indicating efforts towards upgrading their pollution control infrastructure, these documents primarily demonstrated *intent* and *ongoing efforts* rather than conclusive *achievement* of NEQS compliance at the time the complaint was filed or during the Tribunal's initial proceedings. The Respondent's request for fresh sampling was considered, but the Tribunal found that the onus was on the Respondent to demonstrate actual compliance, not merely ongoing efforts, especially when facing a complaint based on proven past violations. Furthermore, the Tribunal noted that the alleged violations posed a significant environmental risk and potential harm to public health, which cannot be overlooked solely on the basis of remedial steps taken *after* the complaint was initiated. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,100,000/- (Rupees One Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 220,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
