**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 44/2022, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA" or "Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located at [Hypothetical Address, e.g., Ferozepur Road, Lahore]. The complaint, lodged under Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"), pertains to alleged violations of environmental standards and non-compliance with statutory obligations regarding the discharge of industrial effluent. The proceedings commenced on 15th July 2022, following the issuance of a show-cause notice by the EPA which remained unaddressed to the satisfaction of the regulatory body.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's consistent discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system, which ultimately converges into the Ravi River, without adhering to the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial and municipal effluents, as specified in S.R.O. 742(I)/93 dated 24th August 1993, and subsequent amendments. Specifically, it was alleged that during routine inspections conducted on multiple dates, including 10th May 2022 and 2nd June 2022, the EPA collected samples of effluent discharged from the Respondent's premises. Laboratory analyses of these samples revealed concentrations of various parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH, significantly exceeding the permissible NEQS limits. For instance, the analysis reports indicated COD levels often ranging between 350-450 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L, and BOD5 levels between 180-250 mg/L against a limit of 80 mg/L. Furthermore, the pH levels were found to be outside the prescribed range of 6-9. The Complainant asserted that these violations demonstrated a blatant disregard for environmental protection laws and posed a serious threat to aquatic life, public health, and the ecological balance of the receiving water body. The Complainant also highlighted the Respondent's failure to install and operate an efficient Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) or to adequately maintain existing facilities, despite repeated verbal and written warnings, including an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) issued on 20th June 2022 under Section 16 of the Act, which the Respondent allegedly failed to comply with within the stipulated timeframe.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA field teams, dated 10th May 2022 and 2nd June 2022, which documented observations of effluent discharge points and the general environmental conditions within and around the Respondent's factory premises. Crucially, the Complainant submitted certified copies of laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, confirming the exceedance of NEQS for the collected effluent samples. Photographic evidence depicting the color and apparent quality of the discharged effluent was also presented. Furthermore, copies of the show-cause notice dated 15th June 2022, the Environmental Protection Order dated 20th June 2022, and the subsequent non-compliance notice were tendered, establishing the procedural steps taken by the EPA. Mr. Tariq Mehmood, Assistant Director (Technical) EPA, appeared as a witness, providing testimony regarding the inspection procedures, sample collection methodology, and the findings of the laboratory analyses. He affirmed that standard operating procedures were strictly followed in all aspects. The Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, through its legal counsel and Production Manager, Mr. Asif Khan, largely denied the allegations, contending that they possessed a functional ETP which was regularly maintained. The Respondent argued that any momentary exceedance of NEQS might have been due to unforeseen operational fluctuations or maintenance activities, and not a systemic failure. They submitted internal monitoring reports for selected periods, which, however, did not consistently align with the EPA's findings. The Respondent also pleaded for leniency, citing their significant contribution to the national
economy and employment generation, and requested that the Tribunal take a lenient view of the matter.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties, carefully considering the arguments advanced by the Complainant and the Respondent. The certified laboratory analysis reports, submitted by the Complainant, unequivocally demonstrated that the effluent discharged from the Respondent's premises consistently exceeded the prescribed NEQS limits for critical parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. These reports, originating from an accredited laboratory and corroborated by the detailed testimony of Mr. Tariq Mehmood, carried significant probative value. The comprehensive inspection reports further substantiated the Complainant's assertions regarding the observed state of the Respondent's effluent management system and the apparent quality of the discharged effluent.
While the Respondent claimed to possess a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and cited unforeseen operational
fluctuations or maintenance activities," the evidence presented by the EPA, particularly the consistent nature of the violations across multiple sampling dates and the significant degree of exceedance, strongly contradicted this assertion. A functional and adequately maintained ETP would not consistently produce effluent with such high levels of pollutants. The Respondent's internal monitoring reports, while presented, were inconsistent and lacked the independent verification and comprehensive scope of the EPA

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,200,000/- (Rupees One Million Two Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 240,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
