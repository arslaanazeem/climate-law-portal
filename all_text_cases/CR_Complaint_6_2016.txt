**JUDGMENT**
This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 6/2016, initiated by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent glass manufacturing facility located in Lahore. The complaint seeks punitive and remedial action against the Respondent for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The proceedings commenced with the filing of the complaint on 15.03.2016, following several notices and inspections by the EPA.
The Complainant alleges that the Respondent's industrial operations, specifically the emissions from its glass melting furnaces and allied processes, are in persistent violation of the prescribed NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions. It is contended that despite repeated warnings and notices issued by the EPA, the Respondent has failed to install and/or properly operate adequate air pollution control devices, leading to the discharge of particulate matter, Sulphur Oxides (SOx), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) into the ambient air at levels significantly exceeding the permissible limits. Specifically, the complaint details that monitoring reports conducted by the EPA's accredited laboratories on 15.11.2015 and 20.01.2016 revealed exceedances in Particulate Matter (PM) by approximately 150-200% and SOx by 80-100% from the main stack, against the NEQS limits. Furthermore, the Complainant asserts that the Respondent has failed to submit regular and compliant environmental monitoring reports as stipulated under the conditions of its Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) approval, thereby obstructing the EPA's oversight functions. The cumulative effect of these alleged violations is presented as a serious threat to public health and the local environment, causing air pollution and potential respiratory ailments in the surrounding communities.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a series of documents and expert testimonies. Key among these were:
1.  **Environmental Monitoring Reports (EMRs):** Two detailed reports dated 15.11.2015 and 20.01.2016, generated by the EPA's certified laboratory, clearly indicating the exceedance of NEQS for Particulate Matter and SOx from the Respondent's primary emission stack. These reports included raw data, analytical methods used, and comparisons with NEQS.
2.  **Inspection Reports:** Multiple site inspection reports by EPA officials, dated 01.11.2015, 10.12.2015, and 05.02.2016, documenting visible emissions, lack of adequate pollution control measures, and non-compliance with previous directives.
3.  **Show Cause Notices and Compliance Notices:** Copies of formal notices issued to M/s Ghani Glass Limited on 20.11.2015 and 25.01.2016, detailing the violations and requiring immediate remedial actions, along with the Respondent's evasive or unsatisfactory replies.
4.  **Testimony of EPA Officials:** The Assistant Director and the relevant Environmental Officer appeared before the Tribunal, explaining the monitoring procedures, the findings, and the Respondent's historical non-compliance.
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel and technical representatives, contested the allegations. Their defense primarily rested on the following points:
1.  **Methodology Dispute:** They questioned the accuracy and methodology
of the EPA's monitoring reports, suggesting that the equipment used might not have been properly calibrated or that the sampling points chosen were not representative of the overall emissions. They argued that their internal monitoring, though not submitted regularly, generally showed compliance or only minor fluctuations.
2.  **Operational Constraints:** The Respondent submitted that glass manufacturing is an energy-intensive process with inherent challenges in maintaining absolutely constant emission levels, especially during start-up, shut-down, or furnace maintenance cycles. They contended that minor exceedances could be attributed to such operational exigencies and should not be construed as deliberate or persistent non-compliance.
3.  **Ongoing Efforts and Investment:** The Respondent's counsel highlighted that M/s Ghani Glass Limited is a responsible corporate entity that has invested significantly in environmental management. They claimed to be in the process of upgrading their existing Electrostatic Precipitators (ESPs) and installing additional Bag Filters to further reduce particulate matter and gaseous emissions. They presented purchase orders and installation schedules as evidence of their commitment, requesting reasonable time to complete these upgrades.
4.  **No Direct Harm Proven:** While acknowledging the theoretical risks of air pollution, the Respondent argued that the Complainant had failed to provide concrete evidence of direct, quantifiable harm to public health or specific environmental damage attributable solely to their operations. They suggested that the general ambient air quality in Lahore is influenced by multiple sources, and isolating their contribution was difficult.
5.  **Procedural Lapses:** The Respondent also argued that some notices issued by the EPA were either not received in a timely manner or did not provide sufficient time for a comprehensive response, thereby hindering their ability to engage constructively with the EPA.
**Tribunal's Analysis and Findings:**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the documentary evidence and testimonies on record.
Firstly, regarding the Respondent's challenge to the EPA's monitoring methodology, the Tribunal notes that the Environmental Monitoring Reports (EMRs) were generated by the EPA's certified laboratory, which operates under established protocols and standards. The Complainant provided details of the analytical methods used, which are consistent with international best practices and the requirements of PEPA, 1997. While the Respondent raised general doubts, they failed to produce any credible counter-monitoring reports from an equally accredited laboratory that would contradict the EPA's findings. The mere assertion of internal compliance without submitting verifiable, regular reports cannot override the documented evidence presented by the EPA. The Tribunal finds the EPA's monitoring reports to be reliable and conclusive evidence of exceedances.
Secondly, the argument of operational constraints, while understood in the context of industrial operations, does not absolve an industrial unit from its responsibility to comply with NEQS. PEPA, 1997, and the NEQS are designed to protect public health and the environment, and continuous exceedances, even if termed "minor" by the Respondent, contribute cumulatively to air pollution. The reported exceedances of 150-200% for PM and 80-100% for SOx are significant and cannot be dismissed as mere operational fluctuations. It is the responsibility of the industry to design and operate its pollution control systems to meet the standards consistently, accounting for operational variations.
Thirdly, the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's claims of ongoing efforts and investment in upgrading pollution control devices. However, these efforts appear to have been initiated or accelerated primarily after the EPA's repeated notices and the filing of this complaint. While a positive step, these future plans do not negate the fact of past and ongoing non-compliance for which the complaint was filed. The law requires immediate compliance, and the alleged "in-progress" status of upgrades does not excuse the violations that have already occurred and continue to occur.
Fourthly, the contention that no direct harm has been proven is not a valid defense against violations of NEQS. The very purpose of setting environmental quality standards is to prevent harm. Exceeding these standards inherently implies a risk to public health and the environment, and the Complainant is not required to prove specific, individual cases of illness or damage to establish a violation. The cumulative impact of industrial emissions on ambient air quality in a densely populated area like Lahore is a matter of serious concern.
Finally, concerning procedural lapses, the Complainant provided copies of formal notices with proof of dispatch or acknowledgment. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent was duly informed of the violations and given ample opportunity to respond and rectify the situation. Any alleged delays in internal processing by the Respondent do not invalidate the notices issued by the EPA.
Based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has been in persistent violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards for industrial gaseous emissions, particularly concerning Particulate Matter and Sulphur Oxides, and has failed to submit regular and compliant environmental monitoring reports as required under PEPA, 1997, and its IEE approval conditions. The Respondent's defense, while noted, does not sufficiently rebut the concrete evidence of non-compliance.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal hereby issues the following order:
1.  **Declaration of Violation:** M/s Ghani Glass Limited is found to be in violation of Section 11(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, read with the National Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2001, for persistent exceedance of NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions and failure to submit timely and compliant environmental monitoring reports.
2.  **Cessation of Violation and Remedial Action:** The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, is directed to immediately cease all operations that result in the discharge of emissions exceeding the prescribed NEQS. Specifically, the Respondent shall:
    a.  **Upgrade and Install Pollution Control Devices:** Within **ninety (90) days** from the date of this judgment, complete the installation and operationalization of all necessary air pollution control devices (including, but not limited to, upgraded ESPs and new Bag Filters, as per their submitted plans and as approved by EPA) to ensure that all emissions from its facility consistently meet the NEQS for Particulate Matter, Sulphur Oxides, Nitrogen Oxides, and any other relevant parameters.
    b.  **Submit Compliance Plan:** Within **fifteen (15) days** from the date of this judgment, submit a detailed, time-bound compliance plan to the EPA, outlining the steps, technologies, and timelines for achieving full NEQS compliance. This plan must be approved by the EPA.
    c.  **Regular Monitoring Reports:** Commence submitting monthly environmental monitoring reports, duly certified by an EPA-accredited laboratory, to the EPA, demonstrating continuous compliance with NEQS. The first such report shall be due **thirty (30) days** after the expiry of the ninety-day period mentioned in sub-clause (a) above, and thereafter on the 10th of each succeeding month.
3.  **Financial Penalty:** Pursuant to Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, a penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** is hereby imposed on M/s Ghani Glass Limited for the proven violations. This penalty shall be paid to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this judgment.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **25 May, 2022**.
