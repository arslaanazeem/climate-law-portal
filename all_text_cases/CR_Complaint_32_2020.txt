This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), alleging contravention of various provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The complaint, bearing No. 32/2020, was lodged on 15.05.2020, asserting that the Respondent's industrial operations were causing environmental pollution in excess of the prescribed standards, thereby posing a threat to public health and the environment.
The core allegations brought forth by the Complainant are that the Respondent, an industrial establishment engaged in manufacturing, has been discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into the municipal sewerage system/Lahore Canal, which ultimately empties into natural water bodies. Specifically, the Complainant alleged that multiple inspections conducted by EPA teams revealed that the Respondent's Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP), though reportedly installed, was either non-functional, improperly operated, or insufficient to treat the volume and characteristics of the industrial wastewater generated. Laboratory analysis reports of samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points consistently indicated concentrations of various pollutants, including Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels, significantly exceeding the limits stipulated in the NEQS for Industrial Effluents. Furthermore, the Complainant contended that despite repeated warnings, issuance of environmental protection orders, and show-cause notices, the Respondent failed to take effective remedial measures to bring its discharges into compliance with environmental standards, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental regulations.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included certified copies of inspection reports dated 10.03.2020, 01.04.2020, and 25.04.2020, prepared by EPA field teams, detailing the observations of discharge points and the collection of effluent samples. Crucially, the Complainant submitted three separate laboratory analysis reports, bearing reference numbers EPA/Lab/2020/112, 145, and 189, issued by the EPA's accredited laboratory. These reports unequivocally showed that the BOD levels in the Respondent's effluent ranged from 150-220 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 80 mg/L), COD levels from 350-480 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L), and TSS levels from 250-320 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 200 mg/L). The pH levels were also found to be outside the permissible range of 6-9. Photographs of the discharge points depicting visible signs of pollution were also tendered. Furthermore, copies of a show-cause notice dated 05.05.2020 and the Respondent's unsatisfactory reply dated 12.05.2020 were submitted, demonstrating the Respondent's awareness of the violations and its failure to provide a convincing explanation or a concrete plan for immediate compliance. The Complainant also produced the testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, an Environmental Analyst with EPA, who confirmed the sampling methodology, chain of custody, and the accuracy of the laboratory reports under oath.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, vehemently denied the allegations. Through its authorized representative, Mr. Bilal Hassan, the Respondent contended that it operates a state-of-the-art Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) which is fully functional and designed to meet NEQS. It was argued that any exceedance, if at all, might be attributed to temporary operational glitches or unusual process variations, which are promptly addressed. The Respondent submitted internal monitoring reports for the months of March and April 2020, which, *per contra*, showed compliance with NEQS on several occasions. It was further argued that
the sampling methodology employed by the EPA teams was flawed, as samples were not collected jointly in the presence of the Respondent's representatives, thus questioning the chain of custody and the representativeness of the samples. Mr. Hassan further contended that the EPA had failed to consider the significant investments made by M/s Packages Limited in environmental protection infrastructure, including continuous efforts to optimize their ETP and explore advanced treatment technologies.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,100,000/- (Rupees One Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 220,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
