This matter came before the Punjab Environmental Tribunal, Lahore, for adjudication on a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent") concerning alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations. The Tribunal, seized of the jurisdiction conferred upon it by the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"), proceeded to hear the parties and examine the evidence presented.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, brought forth serious allegations against the Respondent, a prominent textile manufacturing facility. It was contended that the Respondent was engaged in the discharge of industrial effluent directly into the public sewerage system, and subsequently into a natural water channel, without adequate treatment, thereby contravening the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) as prescribed under the Act. Specifically, the complaint detailed multiple instances of non-compliance, including excessive levels of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and other hazardous parameters in the discharged wastewater, significantly exceeding the permissible limits. The Complainant further alleged that despite repeated warnings, issuance of show-cause notices, and opportunities for rectification, the Respondent had failed to install or effectively operate a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) capable of bringing its discharge within NEQS parameters, thus posing a grave threat to public health and the environment.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included a series of inspection reports, dated 15.05.2023, 08.07.2023, and 20.09.2023, conducted by EPA officials, which consistently noted the absence of a fully operational ETP and the direct discharge of untreated effluent. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the certified EPA Laboratory, corresponding to samples collected during these inspections. These reports unequivocally demonstrated the exceedance of NEQS for various pollutants, including a COD level of 650 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L, BOD of 280 mg/L against 80 mg/L, and TSS of 320 mg/L against 200 mg/L, among others. Photographic evidence depicting the discharge point and the visibly polluted water channel was also tendered. Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 01.06.2023 and 15.08.2023, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were placed on record, indicating a persistent pattern of non-compliance. The Assistant Director, EPA, also appeared as a witness, providing testimony regarding the inspections, sampling procedures, and the Respondent's failure to take corrective measures.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its legal counsel, denied the allegations. It was argued that the samples taken by the EPA were not representative, or that the sampling methodology was flawed. The Respondent claimed to have an existing ETP, which it asserted was undergoing maintenance or an upgrade during the periods of inspection, and that any non-compliance was temporary and unintentional. The Respondent also presented internal reports, which it claimed demonstrated efforts towards environmental compliance, although these reports lacked independent verification and did not directly contradict the EPA's laboratory findings for the specific dates of violation. The Respondent's representative emphasized the company's significant contribution to the national economy and employment, urging a lenient view.
The legal framework governing this complaint is primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Specifically, the Complainant invoked Section 11,
the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, which prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS), or where applicable, the environmental standards established by the Agency under clause (g) of sub-section (1) of Section 6 of the Act. Furthermore, Section 16 of the Act empowers this Tribunal to hear and adjudicate upon complaints regarding violations of the Act and the rules and regulations made thereunder. Section 17 of the Act prescribes penalties for contravention of Section 11, stipulating that whoever contravenes or fails to comply with the provisions of Section 11 shall be punishable with a fine which may extend to one million rupees, and in case of a continuing contravention or failure, with an additional fine which may extend to one hundred thousand rupees for every day during which such contravention or failure continues.
Having carefully considered the submissions of both parties, the evidence on record, and the relevant provisions of law, this Tribunal finds the Complainant's case to be substantially proven. The evidence presented by the EPA, including multiple inspection reports, certified laboratory analysis reports, and photographic evidence, consistently demonstrates that the Respondent was indeed discharging industrial effluent exceeding the NEQS parameters on several occasions between May and September 2023. The significant exceedance levels for COD, BOD, and TSS are particularly alarming and indicative of a severe lack of effective treatment. The Complainant's witness testimony further corroborated these findings, establishing a clear and persistent pattern of non-compliance.
The Respondent's defense, primarily relying on claims of unrepresentative sampling, flawed methodology, or ongoing ETP maintenance/upgrade, remained unsubstantiated. No credible evidence was presented to rebut the certified EPA laboratory reports or to demonstrate that the ETP, if existing, was operational and effective during the periods of violation. The internal reports submitted by the Respondent lacked independent verification and did not negate the specific violations proven by the Complainant's evidence. While the Respondent's contribution to the economy is acknowledged, it cannot serve as an excuse for environmental degradation or non-compliance with statutory obligations. The paramount public interest in a clean and healthy environment, as enshrined in the Act, cannot be compromised.
Therefore, this Tribunal concludes that M/s Nishat Mills Limited has contravened the provisions of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards, thereby posing a significant threat to public health and the environment. The Respondent's failure to take timely and effective corrective measures despite repeated warnings and show-cause notices demonstrates a disregard for environmental regulations.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **05 April, 2023**.
