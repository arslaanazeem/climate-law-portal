**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 34/2024, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"). The complaint was initiated under Section 17 read with Section 20 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleging persistent violations of environmental laws and National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal took cognizance of the matter on 15.03.2024 and notices were issued to the Respondent, who appeared through their legal counsel and submitted a written reply. The proceedings involved detailed examination of documentary evidence and oral arguments from both parties.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, Mr. Adnan Ali, Assistant Director, EPA, Lahore, alleged that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, an industrial unit engaged in chemical manufacturing, located in Kala Shah Kaku, District Sheikhupura, has been discharging industrial effluent containing various pollutants directly into the adjacent drain without adequate treatment, thereby causing severe water pollution and posing a significant threat to public health and the environment. Specifically, it was alleged that during routine inspections conducted by EPA officials on various dates, including 05.01.2024, 28.01.2024, and 15.02.2024, samples of the Respondent's industrial effluent were collected from the discharge point. Subsequent laboratory analysis of these samples revealed that critical parameters such as Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels consistently exceeded the prescribed NEQS for industrial effluents. Furthermore, the Complainant contended that the Respondent had failed to install and operate an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) despite repeated verbal and written warnings, including a Show Cause Notice issued on 20.02.2024, demanding compliance with environmental standards and the terms and conditions of their No Objection Certificate (NOC) for establishment and operation. The Complainant sought imposition of penalties as stipulated under PEPA, 1997, and a directive for immediate cessation of pollution and installation of a functional ETP.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive set of
inspection reports, laboratory analysis reports detailing the exceedance of NEQS, photographs of the discharge point, copies of the Show Cause Notice, and correspondence exchanged with the Respondent. These documents clearly demonstrated a pattern of non-compliance and a failure by the Respondent to take adequate measures to mitigate the environmental damage being caused.
In response, the Respondent, through its learned counsel, Mr. Zahid Khan, while acknowledging the existence of their industrial unit and the nature of their operations, largely denied the persistent and willful violations alleged by the EPA. The Respondent contended that they are a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection and have made significant investments in environmental management. They argued that the effluent samples collected by the EPA might not be representative, questioning the methodology of sample collection and preservation, and the chain of custody. Furthermore, it was submitted that the Respondent had initiated steps to upgrade their existing effluent treatment facilities and had engaged consultants for this purpose. They presented some invoices and work orders as evidence of their ongoing efforts. It was also argued that some fluctuations in effluent quality are unavoidable in complex chemical manufacturing processes and that the EPA's approach was overly punitive rather than supportive of industrial operations. They sought dismissal of the complaint, or at least a lenient view, considering their economic contributions and ongoing efforts towards compliance.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both parties and meticulously examined the documentary and oral evidence presented. The inspection reports submitted by the Complainant are consistent and depict a clear picture of direct discharge of untreated or inadequately treated effluent. The laboratory analysis reports, prepared by an accredited laboratory, consistently show that the levels of BOD, COD, TSS, and pH in the Respondent's effluent samples significantly exceeded the NEQS on multiple occasions. The Respondent's challenge to the sample collection methodology was general in nature and lacked specific evidence to discredit the official procedures followed by the EPA, which are presumed to be regular unless proven otherwise. The photographic evidence corroborated the EPA's claims regarding the discharge point.
While the Respondent claimed to
have initiated steps to upgrade their existing effluent treatment facilities and presented some invoices and work orders as evidence of their ongoing efforts, the Tribunal finds that these prospective actions, however commendable, do not absolve the Respondent of the proven past and continuing violations of environmental laws. The duty to comply with NEQS and ensure proper treatment of industrial effluent is immediate and continuous. The evidence presented by the Respondent regarding future upgrades does not negate the consistent findings of the EPA's inspections and laboratory analyses, which unequivocally demonstrate that the Respondent has been discharging effluent in excess of the prescribed NEQS for an extended period, thereby causing environmental degradation. The Respondent's general assertions regarding the complexity of chemical manufacturing processes and the EPA's approach being overly punitive are unpersuasive in the face of concrete evidence of non-compliance.
The Tribunal, therefore, concludes that the Complainant, Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, has successfully established that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited has persistently violated Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards. Such actions pose a serious threat to public health and the environment, necessitating a firm response from this Tribunal in accordance with the law.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 300,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
