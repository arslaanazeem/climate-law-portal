**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 37/2024, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The complaint seeks punitive action and remedial measures against the Respondent for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents. The proceedings commenced with the filing of the complaint on 12th March 2024, followed by the issuance of notices to the Respondent, who subsequently appeared through their legal counsel and submitted a written reply.
The Complainant's allegations center on the Respondent's consistent failure to adequately treat its industrial effluent before discharge into the public sewerage system, leading to environmental pollution. Specifically, it was alleged that during routine inspections conducted on 15th January 2024 and 28th February 2024, samples of the Respondent's treated effluent were collected from their discharge point. Laboratory analysis of these samples, performed by the EPA's accredited laboratory, revealed significant exceedances of the NEQS for various parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. The Complainant further asserted that despite multiple show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, including one dated 1st March 2024, demanding immediate rectification and submission of a compliance plan, the Respondent failed to demonstrate substantial progress in resolving the non-compliance issues. The Complainant argued that this persistent disregard for environmental regulations constitutes a grave offense under PEPA, 1997, endangering public health and the aquatic ecosystem.
In support of its claims, the Complainant presented detailed inspection reports dated 15th January 2024 and 28th February 2024, duly signed by EPA officials. Crucially, the analytical reports from the EPA Environmental Monitoring Laboratory, bearing reference numbers EPA/EML/LHR/2024/01-123 and EPA/EML/LHR/2024/02-178, were submitted as primary evidence. These reports conclusively showed that the COD levels were 450 mg/L and 480 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L), BOD5 levels were 200 mg/L and 210 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 80 mg/L), and TSS levels were 250 mg/L and 265 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 200 mg/L), respectively. The pH levels, while generally within range, showed slight fluctuations towards the acidic side on one occasion. The Complainant also furnished copies of the show-cause notices and the Respondent's inadequate responses, which primarily cited "technical issues" and "ongoing upgrades" without providing concrete timelines or verifiable evidence of improvement. The Respondent, through its counsel, acknowledged the sampling but contested the severity of the violations, claiming that their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) was operational and that temporary malfunctions might have occurred due to power fluctuations or maintenance activities. They presented a general maintenance log and invoices for certain chemical purchases but failed to provide specific records of ETP performance, calibration reports, or independent third-party verification of their treatment efficiency during the period in question. No counter-analysis reports from an independent accredited laboratory were produced to challenge the EPA's findings.
The legal framework governing this complaint is primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Specifically, Section 11 of the Act prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the NEQS. Section 12 mandates that no industrial unit shall commence operation unless an Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) or Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) has been filed and approved by the EPA, and conditions related to environmental protection are complied with. Section 17 prescribes penalties for contravention of the Act, Rules, Regulations, or Orders made thereunder.
**Analysis and Findings:**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the pleadings, evidence, and arguments presented by both parties. It is evident from the analytical reports submitted by the Complainant, bearing reference numbers EPA/EML/LHR/2024/01-123 and EPA/EML/LHR/2024/02-178, that the Respondent's industrial effluent consistently exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), and Total Suspended Solids (TSS) on two separate occasions within a short span of time. These reports constitute primary and credible evidence, emanating from an accredited laboratory of the Environmental Protection Agency.
The Respondent's defense, primarily citing "technical issues," "power fluctuations," and "ongoing upgrades," without providing specific, verifiable data, performance logs, or independent third-party analysis to counter the EPA's findings, is found to be unsubstantiated and insufficient to rebut the strong evidence presented by the Complainant. While temporary malfunctions can occur, the
consistency of the violations across two separate sampling dates indicates a more systemic issue rather than isolated temporary malfunctions. It is the absolute responsibility of an industrial unit to ensure continuous compliance with environmental standards, and the onus was on the Respondent to provide concrete evidence of effective ETP operation or credible counter-analysis, which they failed to do. The general maintenance logs and invoices for chemical purchases, without corresponding performance data or calibration reports, are insufficient to demonstrate due diligence or effective treatment.
Therefore, this Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established, beyond reasonable doubt, that M/s Nishat Mills Limited has violated Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) on 15th January 2024 and 28th February 2024. The persistent nature of the non-compliance, despite the issuance of show-cause notices, further underscores the Respondent's failure to uphold its environmental obligations. Such flagrant disregard for environmental regulations cannot be condoned, as it poses a significant threat to public health and the ecological balance.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **06 July, 2023**.
