This judgment pertains to a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent") concerning alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The complaint seeks the imposition of penalties and directions for compliance with environmental laws and standards.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of packaging materials, was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system, which eventually flows into the River Ravi, thereby causing water pollution. Furthermore, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations, specifically emissions from its boiler stacks, exceeded the prescribed NEQS for air quality. The EPA contended that despite repeated inspections, issuance of show-cause notices, and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent failed to adequately address these environmental concerns, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental regulations. The specific allegations included the discharge of effluent with Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), and Total Suspended Solids (TSS) levels consistently above the NEQS, and stack emissions exceeding permissible limits for Particulate Matter (PM) and Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx).
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports dated 15.03.2019, 28.06.2019, and 10.10.2019, conducted by EPA officials at the Respondent's premises. These reports included photographic evidence and details of samples collected from the Respondent's effluent discharge points and boiler stacks. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the certified EPA Laboratory, which unequivocally indicated that the collected effluent samples consistently exceeded the NEQS for COD (e.g., 450 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), BOD (e.g., 280 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), and TSS (e.g., 250 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L). Similarly, the stack emission analysis reports showed exceedances for PM (e.g., 300 mg/Nm³ against a standard of 200 mg/Nm³) and NOx. The Complainant also produced copies of show-cause notices issued on 20.04.2019 and 05.08.2019, along with subsequent reminders, to which the Respondent's replies were deemed unsatisfactory or indicative of insufficient corrective action. An expert witness from the EPA testified to the accuracy of the sampling and analytical procedures and the environmental impact of such non-compliance.
The Respondent, represented by its legal counsel, denied the allegations, asserting that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental stewardship. While acknowledging certain initial challenges in meeting all NEQS, the Respondent contended that it had undertaken substantial measures to upgrade its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices. They presented their own internal monitoring reports, which, while showing some fluctuations, generally claimed compliance or near-compliance in later periods. The Respondent's technical expert argued that the EPA's sampling methodology might have been flawed on certain occasions, or that the samples were not representative of the overall discharge due to operational variations. They also submitted receipts and contracts for the procurement of new
filtration membranes, an advanced aeration system for its ETP, and a bag filter for its boiler stack emissions. They also provided a timeline for the complete overhaul and commissioning of these upgraded systems, asserting that full compliance would be achieved within a few months. The Respondent further argued that the EPA had not adequately considered these proactive steps and that the complaints were based on data from a period when upgrades were still underway or recently completed, hence not fully reflective of their current environmental performance. They requested a re-evaluation by the EPA and a stay on any punitive action, emphasizing their significant investment in environmental infrastructure.
**Analysis and Findings of the Tribunal:**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the pleadings, documentary evidence, expert testimonies, and arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent.
Firstly, regarding the Complainant's evidence, the inspection reports, photographic evidence, and particularly the laboratory analysis reports from the certified EPA Laboratory, carry significant weight. The consistency of exceedances across multiple parameters (COD, BOD, TSS for effluent; PM, NOx for emissions) and over different inspection dates (15.03.2019, 28.06.2019, and 10.10.2019) strongly substantiates the allegations of persistent non-compliance. The expert witness from the EPA further corroborated the accuracy of the sampling and analytical procedures, leaving little room for doubt regarding the veracity of the Complainant's findings. The show-cause notices and subsequent reminders, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, demonstrate that the EPA provided ample opportunity for the Respondent to rectify the situation before resorting to legal action.
Secondly, while the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's assertions of being a responsible corporate entity and its efforts to upgrade its environmental infrastructure, these efforts appear to have been reactive rather than proactive, undertaken after repeated violations were identified by the EPA. The Respondent's
internal monitoring reports, which were not independently verified by a third-party certified laboratory and showed "near-compliance" or fluctuations, cannot supersede the consistent and independently verified findings of the EPA Laboratory. The Tribunal finds the Respondent's argument regarding flawed sampling methodology unconvincing, as no concrete evidence was presented to discredit the EPA's established and certified procedures, which were further supported by expert testimony.
While the Respondent's commitment to upgrading its environmental infrastructure is noted and encouraged, these investments appear to have been prompted by, rather than pre-empted, the EPA's enforcement actions. The fact that upgrades were "still underway" or "recently completed" at the time of the complaints and subsequent inspections indicates that the Respondent was operating in violation of environmental laws for a significant period. Environmental compliance is a continuous obligation, not a future aspiration. The PEPA, 1997, and NEQS are not merely aspirational targets but mandatory standards that industrial units must adhere to at all times. The Respondent's failure to adequately address the identified issues despite repeated notices demonstrates a degree of negligence in upholding its environmental responsibilities during the period in question. The environmental impact of discharging untreated or inadequately treated effluent into public waterways and emitting pollutants into the air cannot be understated, posing significant risks to public health and the ecosystem, including the River Ravi.
Therefore, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established, through compelling documentary evidence and expert testimony, that M/s Packages Limited was indeed in persistent violation of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards concerning both effluent discharge and stack emissions for the period covered by the complaint. The Respondent's arguments, while indicating a future intent to comply, do not absolve it of liability for the past and ongoing contraventions.
**ORDER**
In light of the foregoing analysis and findings, this Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Declaration of Violation:** M/s Packages Limited (the Respondent) is hereby found guilty of violating Section 12, read with Section 11, of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for both industrial effluent discharge and industrial gaseous emissions.
2.  **Imposition of Penalty:**
    *   For the persistent contraventions documented by the Environmental Protection Agency, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, is hereby directed to pay an environmental penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million Only)**, which is the maximum initial fine permissible under Section 17(1) of the PEPA, 1997, for the first contravention. This penalty shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
    *   In addition to the above, if the Respondent fails to achieve full compliance with all applicable NEQS for effluent discharge and stack emissions within ninety (90) days from the date of this order, an additional fine of **PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand Only)** shall be imposed for each day the contravention continues thereafter, as provided under Section 17(1) of the PEPA, 1997.
3.  **Directions for Compliance:**
    *   The Respondent shall immediately take all necessary measures to ensure full and continuous compliance with all prescribed NEQS for industrial effluent discharge and stack emissions.
    *   The Respondent shall submit a comprehensive compliance plan, detailing the steps taken and to be taken, including timelines for the full operationalization and optimization of its upgraded Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
    *   The Respondent shall submit monthly self-monitoring reports, verified by a third-party certified laboratory, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, for a period of twelve (12) months, commencing from the first month following the ninety (90) day compliance period mentioned in clause 2(b) above. These reports shall demonstrate continuous adherence to NEQS.
4.  **EPA Oversight:** The Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, is directed to conduct regular and unannounced inspections of the Respondent's premises to monitor compliance with this order and the NEQS. The EPA shall submit quarterly compliance reports to this Tribunal for the next one year.
5.  **Costs:** The Respondent shall bear the costs of these proceedings.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
