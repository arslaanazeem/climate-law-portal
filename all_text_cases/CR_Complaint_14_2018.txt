This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The complaint seeks punitive action and imposition of penalties against the Respondent for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent into the environment, thereby causing pollution and posing a threat to public health and ecological integrity. The Tribunal, constituted under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), is tasked with adjudicating such matters and ensuring compliance with environmental standards.
The Complainant's case rests upon a series of allegations asserting the Respondent's failure to comply with environmental laws. It is alleged that the Respondent, operating a substantial textile dyeing and finishing unit, has consistently discharged industrial effluent that fails to meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial and municipal effluents. Specifically, the EPA conducted multiple inspections of the Respondent's premises. An inspection on March 15, 2017, revealed the direct discharge of dark-colored, foul-smelling effluent into an open drain leading to a local water body, without adequate treatment. Samples collected during this inspection, laboratory analysis report No. EPA/LAB/2017/03-123 dated March 28, 2017, showed alarmingly high levels of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) at 850 mg/L (NEQS limit: 150 mg/L), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD) at 320 mg/L (NEQS limit: 80 mg/L), and Total Suspended Solids (TSS) at 450 mg/L (NEQS limit: 200 mg/L). Following this, a Show Cause Notice bearing No. EPA/LH/ENV/2017/04-001 was issued to the Respondent on April 10, 2017, demanding an explanation and immediate remedial action. Subsequent inspections on July 20, 2017, and October 5, 2017, along with corresponding laboratory reports (No. EPA/LAB/2017/07-289 and No. EPA/LAB/2017/10-415), indicated a persistent non-compliance, with effluent quality still significantly exceeding NEQS despite the prior notice. The Complainant further alleged that the Respondent had failed to install an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) or operate existing facilities adequately, as evidenced by the continuous discharge of sub-standard effluent, thereby violating Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and the terms of their environmental approval.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive set of documents including the aforementioned inspection reports, duly attested laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, copies of the Show Cause Notices issued to the Respondent, and the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies to these notices. The Complainant also submitted photographic and videographic evidence depicting the nature of the effluent being discharged from the Respondent’s premises directly into the common drainage system. The EPA's Technical Officer, Mr. Ahmed Khan, who conducted the inspections and collected samples, appeared as a witness and affirmed the accuracy of the reports and the procedure followed for sample collection and analysis. He testified that standard protocols were adhered to, ensuring the integrity and representativeness of the samples.
The Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. They contended that they are a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection and have made substantial investments in environmental management. They argued that an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) was indeed operational at their facility. However, they admitted to occasional operational challenges and technical glitches in the ETP, which they claimed were temporary and promptly addressed. The Respondent also questioned the methodology and calibration of the EPA's laboratory equipment, suggesting that the analysis results might be inaccurate or not representative of their average discharge. They submitted documentation indicating purchase orders for ETP upgrades and maintenance records for the existing plant. The Respondent’s Environmental Manager, Mr. Bilal Hassan, appeared as a witness, explaining the operational complexities of managing an ETP in a large textile unit and highlighting their ongoing efforts to optimize its
operations and highlighting their ongoing efforts to optimize its performance and achieve full compliance with NEQS. They requested leniency, emphasizing their commitment to rectifying any shortcomings and their significant contribution to the national economy.
The Tribunal has meticulously considered the arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the documentary and oral evidence adduced by each party. It is undisputed that the Respondent operates a large-scale textile manufacturing unit which generates industrial effluent. The core issue before this Tribunal is whether the effluent discharged by the Respondent consistently meets the NEQS as mandated by law.
The Complainant's evidence, particularly the multiple laboratory analysis reports from an accredited EPA laboratory, consistently demonstrates that the Respondent's effluent quality significantly exceeded the prescribed NEQS limits for COD, BOD, and TSS on several occasions. The reports from March, July, and October 2017 all show alarmingly high levels of pollutants, far beyond the permissible standards. These reports are corroborated by the testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, the EPA Technical Officer, who detailed the inspection process, sample collection methodology, and chain of custody, affirming that standard protocols were strictly followed to ensure the integrity and representativeness of the samples. The photographic and videographic evidence further supports the Complainant’s assertion of direct discharge of sub-standard effluent into the common drainage system.
On the other hand, the Respondent's defense, while acknowledging the existence of an ETP and asserting efforts towards its optimization, fundamentally fails to rebut the concrete evidence of non-compliance. The admission of "occasional operational challenges and technical glitches" effectively concedes that the ETP was not consistently performing to the required standards. Environmental standards, particularly the NEQS, are not aspirational targets but mandatory limits that must be met continuously. An ETP that only functions intermittently or experiences frequent "challenges" does not fulfill the legal obligation to treat effluent to the prescribed standards. Furthermore, the Respondent's general questioning of the EPA's laboratory methodology, without providing any specific counter-analysis from an independent accredited laboratory or demonstrating procedural flaws in the EPA's testing, remains an unsubstantiated claim. While the submission of purchase orders for ETP upgrades and maintenance records indicates an intention to improve, these documents do not retrospectively nullify the documented instances of past and ongoing non-compliance. The legal obligation under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, is to *not discharge* effluent in excess of NEQS, regardless of the efforts being made to achieve compliance. The continuous discharge of sub-standard effluent, as proven by the Complainant, constitutes a clear violation of this fundamental environmental provision.
Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, explicitly states: "No person shall discharge or emit or allow to be discharged or emitted any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards." The evidence overwhelmingly establishes that M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited has repeatedly violated this provision. The persistent nature of the non-compliance, despite multiple Show Cause Notices and opportunities to rectify the situation, demonstrates a serious disregard for environmental protection laws and the potential harm posed to public health and the environment.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal holds M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited responsible for violating Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
Therefore, it is hereby ordered as follows:
1.  **PENALTY:** A penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million Only)** is imposed on M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited for the continuous discharge of untreated and sub-standard industrial effluent in violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and Section 11 of PEPA, 1997. Additionally, a daily fine of **PKR 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand Only)** shall be imposed for each day the violation continued from the date of the last inspection (October 5, 2017) until the date of this judgment, to be calculated and submitted by the Complainant within 15 days.
2.  **IMMEDIATE COMPLIANCE:** The Respondent is directed to ensure that its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) operates effectively and consistently, achieving full compliance with all parameters of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial and municipal effluents, with immediate effect.
3.  **COMPLIANCE PLAN:** The Respondent shall submit a detailed and verifiable compliance plan, including specific timelines for any necessary upgrades, operational adjustments, or new installations to achieve and maintain NEQS compliance, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within thirty (30) days from the date of this judgment. This plan must be approved by the EPA.
4.  **MONITORING AND REPORTING:** The Respondent shall submit monthly self-monitoring reports, duly verified by an independent accredited laboratory, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, for a period of one (1) year, demonstrating continuous compliance with NEQS. The EPA is authorized to conduct unannounced inspections and collect samples at any time to verify compliance.
5.  **COSTS:** The Respondent shall bear the costs of these proceedings.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,200,000/- (Rupees One Million Two Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 240,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **25 April, 2023**.
