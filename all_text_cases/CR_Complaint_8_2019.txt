This judgment addresses the environmental complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), situated in Kala Shah Kaku, Sheikhupura. The complaint, lodged under Section 19 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), seeks the enforcement of environmental laws and the imposition of penalties for alleged violations of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) concerning industrial waste discharge and air emissions. The Tribunal's mandate is to adjudicate such matters, ensuring the protection and conservation of the environment in accordance with the law.
The Complainant alleged that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of various chemicals, was operating without adequate environmental safeguards, leading to significant pollution. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  **Discharge of Untreated/Partially Treated Effluents:** The Respondent was reportedly discharging industrial wastewater directly into the surrounding agricultural land and a local drain without achieving the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents, particularly concerning parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. This was a direct contravention of Section 11 of the PEPA, 1997.
2.  **Air Emissions Exceeding NEQS:** The Respondent's industrial processes, particularly from its boilers and chemical processing units, were allegedly emitting air pollutants (e.g., Particulate Matter, Sulphur Dioxide, Nitrogen Oxides) into the atmosphere in excess of the NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions, in violation of Section 11 of the PEPA, 1997.
3.  **Failure to Obtain Environmental Approval:** The Respondent had allegedly failed to obtain the requisite Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) or Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) approval for its operations or any significant expansion projects, as mandated by Section 12 of the PEPA, 1997, and the Environmental Protection Agency (Review of IEE/EIA) Regulations, 2000.
4.  **Improper Management of Hazardous Waste:** The Complainant also raised concerns regarding the Respondent's handling, storage, and disposal of hazardous waste generated during its manufacturing processes, potentially posing risks to human health and the environment, in violation of Section 14 of the PEPA, 1997.
5.  **Non-Compliance with Notices:** Despite repeated inspections, issuance of show-cause notices, and directives from the EPA, the Respondent allegedly failed to take effective remedial measures or submit a satisfactory compliance plan within the stipulated timelines.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a robust body of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on various dates between 2017 and 2019, consistently highlighting the environmental non-compliances. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on 14.03.2018, 22.08.2018, and 05.02.2019, clearly indicated that parameters such as COD (ranging from 450-600 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), BOD (ranging from 200-300 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), and TSS (ranging from 250-350 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L) consistently exceeded the NEQS. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports from stack emissions on 14.03.2018 and 05.02.2019 showed Particulate Matter (PM) levels of 200-250 mg/Nm³ against a standard of 150 mg/Nm³. Copies of show-cause notices issued on 01.0
06.2018 and 15.09.2018, along with final warning notices dated 20.11.2018, were also submitted, demonstrating the Respondent's consistent failure to comply despite repeated official communications.
The Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its legal counsel, appeared before the Tribunal and submitted its
reply and submitted its written reply, denying the extent of the allegations and asserting that it was making concerted efforts to comply with environmental regulations. The Respondent contended that it had initiated measures to upgrade its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and install advanced air pollution control devices, but these processes required substantial time and investment. It was argued that some of the reported exceedances might have been due to temporary operational upsets or non-representative sampling, and that the Respondent was a significant contributor to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to adopt a lenient approach and grant adequate time for full compliance. The Respondent also claimed to be in the process of applying for the requisite environmental approvals, including an EIA.
The Tribunal carefully considered the arguments put forth by both parties and meticulously reviewed the voluminous evidence on record. While acknowledging the Respondent's claims of ongoing efforts and economic contributions, the Tribunal found the Complainant's evidence to be overwhelmingly compelling and objectively verifiable. The inspection reports, consistently indicating non-compliance over a period of years, coupled with multiple laboratory analysis reports from independent sources (EPA's accredited labs) showing persistent and significant exceedances of NEQS for both effluent discharge and air emissions, leave no room for doubt regarding the Respondent's culpability. The parameters of COD, BOD, TSS, and Particulate Matter were found to be consistently beyond permissible limits, indicating a systemic failure to implement effective pollution control measures.
Furthermore, the Tribunal noted that the Respondent's defense of "ongoing upgrades" was not substantiated with concrete, time-bound plans or demonstrable progress reports that would have justified the prolonged period of non-compliance. The issuance of repeated show-cause and warning notices by the EPA, spanning over two years, clearly demonstrated that the Respondent had ample opportunity to rectify the violations but failed to do so effectively. The absence of a valid EIA/IEE approval, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, for an industrial unit of this scale and nature, is a serious procedural and substantive violation that cannot be overlooked. Similarly, the concerns regarding improper hazardous waste management, though not as quantitatively evidenced as effluent and air emissions, were also noted as requiring immediate attention under Section 14 of the Act.
The Tribunal holds that environmental protection is a fundamental right and a paramount duty under the law. Economic considerations, while important, cannot be allowed to undermine the health and well-being of the surrounding communities and the integrity of the environment. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
