**JUDGMENT**
**Introduction**
This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit, for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The complaint, bearing No. 15/2017, was lodged with this Tribunal on [Insert a plausible date, e.g., 10.05.2017], seeking punitive action and remedial measures against the Respondent for causing environmental pollution. The Tribunal, constituted under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, has jurisdiction to hear and decide complaints pertaining to contraventions of the Act and its rules.
**Allegations**
The Complainant, through its Assistant Director, alleged that the Respondent's textile manufacturing facility located at [Insert a plausible location, e.g., Ferozepur Road, Lahore] was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into a public drain, ultimately contaminating nearby water bodies and agricultural land. It was further alleged that the Respondent's air emissions from its boiler and other processes were exceeding the prescribed NEQS, contributing to air pollution in the vicinity. Specifically, the complaint detailed that during a routine inspection conducted on [Insert a plausible date, e.g., 15.01.2017], EPA officials observed visible signs of pollution and collected samples of industrial effluent and stack emissions. Subsequent laboratory analysis of these samples, conducted by the certified laboratory of EPA, revealed that parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels in the effluent, and particulate matter (PM) and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) in the stack emissions, were significantly above the limits stipulated in the NEQS.
Following these findings, the EPA issued a Show Cause Notice to the Respondent on [Insert a plausible date, e.g., 01.02.2017], requiring an explanation for the non-compliance and demanding immediate corrective measures. Dissatisfied with the Respondent's response, which reportedly failed to demonstrate concrete steps towards compliance, the EPA issued an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, on [Insert a plausible date, e.g., 20.02.2017], directing the Respondent to cease the discharge of untreated effluent and to install appropriate pollution control equipment within a specified timeframe. Subsequent follow-up inspections on [Insert a plausible date, e.g., 10.04.2017] revealed continued non-compliance with the EPO, leading the Complainant to file the present complaint before this Tribunal under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, seeking imposition of penalties and directions for compliance.
**Evidence**
The Complainant presented a comprehensive set of evidence to substantiate its allegations. This included:
1.  **Inspection Report dated 15.01.2017:** Detailing the observations made by EPA officials at the Respondent's premises, including photographs of the effluent discharge point and visible emissions.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Report No. [Insert a plausible number, e.g., EPA/LAB/2017/005] dated 25.01.2017:** This crucial document provided the scientific basis for the allegations, clearly indicating the exceedance of NEQS for various parameters in both effluent and air emissions.
3.  **Show Cause Notice dated 01.02.2017:** A copy of the notice issued to the Respondent.
4.  **Respondent's Reply to Show Cause Notice dated 08.02.2017:** Submitted by the Respondent, acknowledging some issues but largely attributing them to operational challenges and promising future steps without concrete action plans.
5.  **Environmental Protection Order (EPO) dated 20.02.2017:** Issued under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, directing specific remedial actions within a stipulated period.
6.  **Follow-up Inspection Report dated 10.04.2017:** Confirming the Respondent's continued failure to comply with the EPO.
7.  **Witness Testimony:** The Assistant Director, EPA, and the laboratory analyst who conducted the tests, were examined and cross-examined, affirming the veracity of the reports and procedures followed.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, through its legal counsel, denied the allegations of
discharging untreated effluent and exceeding air emission standards. They contended that the EPA's sampling methodology was flawed and that the laboratory analysis reports were not entirely reliable, lacking proper chain of custody documentation in some instances. The Respondent further submitted that they had already initiated steps to upgrade their existing Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and install advanced air pollution control devices, but these processes required significant time and investment. They presented purchase orders for new equipment and a timeline for their installation and commissioning, arguing that they were committed to environmental compliance and should be granted reasonable time to implement these upgrades. They also highlighted their significant contribution to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to consider the economic implications of any drastic punitive action.
**Arguments of Counsel**
Learned counsel for the Complainant reiterated the persistent nature of the violations, emphasizing that despite multiple notices and an explicit Environmental Protection Order, the Respondent had failed to demonstrate tangible compliance. He argued that the Respondent's assurances of future action were insufficient in the face of ongoing environmental degradation and posed a serious threat to public health and the ecosystem. He stressed the legal mandate of the EPA and the Tribunal to enforce PEPA, 1997, and the NEQS rigorously, asserting that leniency would undermine the very purpose of environmental protection laws. He referred to Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, which mandates compliance with NEQS, and Section 16, which empowers the EPA to issue EPOs, arguing that the Respondent's continued non-compliance with the EPO constituted a clear violation punishable under Section 17(2) of the Act. He sought the maximum permissible penalties and strict directions for immediate compliance.
On the other hand, learned counsel for the Respondent vehemently denied wilful non-compliance. He maintained that the Respondent was a responsible corporate entity genuinely committed to environmental stewardship, as evidenced by their ongoing investment in new pollution control technologies. He challenged the Complainant's evidence, particularly the reliability of the laboratory reports, suggesting potential procedural lapses. He pleaded for a pragmatic approach, requesting the Tribunal to consider the practical difficulties faced by industries in upgrading their infrastructure and to grant a realistic timeframe for implementing the proposed remedial measures. He argued that imposing severe penalties without sufficient opportunity for compliance could stifle industrial growth and result in job losses, which would be detrimental to the economy. He urged the Tribunal to issue an order that balances environmental protection with sustainable industrial operations, focusing on corrective actions rather than solely punitive measures.
**Analysis and Findings of the Tribunal**
This Tribunal has carefully considered the pleadings, the evidence adduced by both parties, and the arguments presented by their respective counsels. The primary issue before this Tribunal is whether the Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, has contravened the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and the NEQS, and whether such contraventions warrant punitive action and remedial directions.
Upon a meticulous review of the evidence, the Tribunal finds the Complainant's case to be well-substantiated. The **Inspection Report dated 15.01.2017** provides clear eyewitness accounts and photographic evidence of visible pollution. More critically, the **Laboratory Analysis Report No. EPA/LAB/2017/005 dated 25.01.2017** stands as a robust scientific testament to the Respondent's non-compliance. The report, prepared by a certified EPA laboratory, meticulously details the exceedance of NEQS for critical parameters in both effluent and air emissions. The testimony of the laboratory analyst, who confirmed the testing procedures and findings under oath, further strengthens the credibility of this evidence. The Respondent's attempt to discredit the laboratory reports on grounds of alleged procedural flaws was not supported by any concrete counter-evidence or expert testimony. Vague assertions of "lack of proper chain of custody" are insufficient to rebut specific scientific findings.
Furthermore, the sequence of events – the issuance of a **Show Cause Notice dated 01.02.2017**, the Respondent's unsatisfactory **Reply dated 08.02.2017**, and the subsequent **Environmental Protection Order (EPO) dated 20.02.2017** – clearly demonstrates that the EPA followed due process before escalating the matter. The **Follow-up Inspection Report dated 10.04.2017**, confirming continued non-compliance with the EPO, is a crucial piece of evidence indicating the Respondent's failure to take the directed remedial actions within the stipulated timeframe. While the Respondent presented purchase orders and a timeline for future upgrades, these are prospective measures and do not negate the fact of past and ongoing non-compliance at the time the complaint was filed and subsequent inspections were conducted. The commitment to future action, while laudable, does not absolve the Respondent of their responsibility for existing violations and the environmental damage already caused.
The Tribunal notes that Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, clearly mandates that no person shall discharge or emit waste, effluent, or air pollution in an amount, concentration, or level exceeding the NEQS. The evidence unequivocally proves that the Respondent has been in contravention of this fundamental requirement. Moreover, their failure to comply with the directions contained in the EPO issued under Section 16 constitutes a serious offense under Section 17(2) of PEPA, 1997. The arguments regarding economic impact, while relevant in a broader policy context, cannot override the statutory duty to protect the environment and public health. Industries must incorporate environmental compliance costs as an integral part of their operational expenses.
Based on the overwhelming evidence presented by the Complainant and the Respondent's inability to effectively rebut the core allegations, this Tribunal finds that M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited has indeed violated the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards, both by discharging
untreated effluent and emitting air pollutants in excess of the prescribed limits. The Respondent's arguments regarding procedural flaws in sampling and analysis are found to be without merit, as the Complainant provided sufficient evidence, including witness testimony, to establish the integrity of their procedures. While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's stated commitment to future upgrades and their economic contribution, these factors do not excuse past and ongoing violations that directly impact the environment and public health. Environmental compliance is not an optional add-on but a fundamental prerequisite for industrial operation. The prolonged non-compliance, particularly after the issuance of an Environmental Protection Order, demonstrates a disregard for environmental regulations that this Tribunal cannot overlook.
**Final Order and Penalty**
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal hereby declares M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, the Respondent, GUILTY of violating Section 11 and Section 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and consequently, liable for punishment under Section 17(2) of the said Act.
Therefore, this Tribunal ORDERS as follows:
1.  **Monetary Penalty:** The Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, is hereby directed to pay a monetary fine of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million Only)** for the initial contravention of the Act and non-compliance with the Environmental Protection Order.
2.  **Continuing Contravention Fine:** In addition to the above, the Respondent is directed to pay an additional fine of **PKR 75,000/- (Rupees Seventy-Five Thousand Only) for each day** the contravention continued, commencing from the date of the Environmental Protection Order (February 20, 2017) until the date of this judgment. The total amount for this continuing fine shall be calculated and deposited within thirty (30) days from the date of this judgment.
3.  **Cessation of Pollution:** The Respondent is immediately directed to cease the discharge of any untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into any public drain or water body, and to halt the emission of air pollutants exceeding the NEQS.
4.  **Installation and Upgradation of Pollution Control Systems:** The Respondent shall, within a period of **ninety (90) days** from the date of this judgment, ensure the full installation, operationalization, and optimization of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and all necessary air pollution control devices to meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) in all respects.
5.  **Compliance Report and Monitoring:**
    *   Within **seven (7) days** of this judgment, the Respondent shall submit a detailed action plan, including a revised timeline for the completion of all remedial measures, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore.
    *   Upon completion of the upgrades, the Respondent shall submit a comprehensive compliance report, duly verified by an independent, EPA-certified environmental consultant, confirming adherence to NEQS.
    *   The EPA is directed to conduct regular monitoring and inspections of the Respondent's facility for a period of at least one year following the submission of the compliance report, to ensure sustained compliance.
6.  **Costs:** The Respondent shall bear the costs of these proceedings, assessed at **PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand Only)**, payable to the Complainant.
7.  **Consequences of Non-Compliance:** Failure to comply with any part of this order shall expose the Respondent to further legal action, including enhanced penalties and closure of operations, as per the provisions of PEPA, 1997.
All fines and costs shall be deposited in the designated account of the Environmental Protection Agency, Government of Punjab, within the stipulated timeframes.
