This judgment addresses Complaint No. 24/2017 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, acting on behalf of the Provincial Agency, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"). The complaint seeks the imposition of penalties and remedial measures against the Respondent for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and improper hazardous waste management.
The Complainant asserted that regular monitoring and inspections of the Respondent's industrial facility, located in Kala Shah Kaku, revealed consistent and severe non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluent discharge into the public sewerage system and ultimately into the River Ravi. It was alleged that the Respondent's effluent treatment plant (ETP), despite its existence, was either non-functional, inefficient, or deliberately bypassed, leading to the discharge of wastewater containing pollutants such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals (e.g., Chromium, Lead) significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS limits. Furthermore, the complaint detailed instances of improper storage and disposal of hazardous chemical waste, posing a direct threat to soil, groundwater, and public health, in contravention of the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003. The Complainant emphasized that despite repeated warnings, show-cause notices, and opportunities for rectification, the Respondent failed to bring its operations into compliance, necessitating the initiation of legal proceedings before this Tribunal under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997).
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant submitted detailed inspection reports (Exh. P-1 to P-5), sampling protocols, and laboratory analysis reports (Exh. P-6 to P-10) conducted by EPA-certified laboratories, clearly indicating the NEQS exceedances over multiple monitoring periods between 2015 and 2017. Photographs (Exh. P-11 to P-15) depicting the discharge points and hazardous waste storage areas were also presented. Witness testimony from EPA field officers (PW-1, PW-2) corroborated the findings of the reports, attesting to the Respondent's persistent non-compliance and lack of effective remedial action. The Complainant also produced copies of the show-cause notices (Exh. P-16 to P-18) issued to the Respondent, along with the unsatisfactory responses received.
In response, the Respondent, through its learned counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It was contended that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental stewardship and has invested substantially in its ETP. The Respondent argued that any reported exceedances were either isolated incidents, attributable to unforeseen operational upsets, or due to flawed sampling and analytical methodologies employed by the EPA. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports (Exh. D-1 to D-5) and a report from a private environmental consultant (Exh. D-6), which purported to show compliance or minor deviations. The Respondent's technical manager (DW-1) testified that the ETP was fully operational and regularly maintained, attributing any issues to the complex nature of chemical industrial processes and external factors. It was further argued that the hazardous waste was stored temporarily in designated areas awaiting collection by an authorized waste management company, and no improper disposal had occurred.
The primary legal framework governing this complaint is the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), particularly:
*   **Section 11:** Prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste into the environment in an amount, concentration, or level that exceeds the NEQS.
*   **Section 16:** Prescribes penalties for violations of the Act, including fines and imprisonment.
*   **Section 17:** Emp
owers the Tribunal to issue environmental protection orders, direct remedial measures, and impose penalties.
*   **Section 20:** Confers jurisdiction upon the Environmental Tribunal to hear and decide complaints regarding violations of the Act and rules made thereunder, and to impose penalties and issue directions.
*   **Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003:** Specifically governs the management, handling, storage, and disposal of hazardous substances and waste, ensuring their proper treatment to prevent environmental contamination and health risks.
Having carefully considered the submissions of the learned counsel for both parties, meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence, and weighed the oral testimonies, this Tribunal proceeds to render its findings.
The core of the Complainant's case rests upon consistent and independently verified data demonstrating severe exceedances of NEQS for effluent discharge and documented instances of improper hazardous waste management. The laboratory analysis reports (Exh. P-6 to P-10) from EPA-certified laboratories, spanning multiple monitoring periods, consistently show pollutant levels (COD, BOD, TSS, heavy metals) significantly above the prescribed limits. These reports are corroborated by detailed inspection reports (Exh. P-1 to P-5) and photographic evidence (Exh. P-11 to P-15) which visually depict the non-compliant discharge and storage practices. The testimonies of EPA field officers (PW-1, PW-2) further strengthen the Complainant's position, attesting to the persistent nature of the violations and the Respondent's failure to adequately address them despite repeated warnings. The issuance of show-cause notices (Exh. P-16 to P-18) and the Respondent's unsatisfactory responses underscore the prolonged nature of the non-compliance.
Conversely, the Respondent's defense primarily relied on the assertion of environmental commitment, the existence of an ETP, and claims of flawed sampling/analytical methodologies or isolated operational upsets. While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's investment in an ETP, its mere existence does not equate to effective operation and compliance. The internal monitoring reports (Exh. D-1 to D-5) and the report from a private environmental consultant (Exh. D-6), presented by the Respondent, do not sufficiently counter the robust and consistent evidence provided by the Complainant. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent failed to provide concrete evidence to substantiate its claims of flawed methodologies, nor did it adequately explain the persistent and severe nature of the exceedances as "isolated incidents" or "unforeseen operational upsets." A responsible industrial entity is expected to maintain its ETP in optimal working condition to ensure continuous compliance, and temporary storage of hazardous waste must adhere strictly to the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003, regarding containment, labeling, and segregation, which the Complainant's evidence suggests was not consistently met.
The weight of the evidence overwhelmingly supports the Complainant's assertions. The consistent data from independent, certified sources, coupled with photographic evidence and witness testimonies
irrefutably establish that the Respondent has been in consistent and severe violation of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards. Furthermore, the evidence clearly demonstrates non-compliance with the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003, regarding the proper management and disposal of hazardous waste. The Respondent's arguments, while acknowledging the existence of an ETP, failed to provide a credible explanation for the persistent exceedances and improper waste handling, nor did they effectively rebut the scientific evidence and eyewitness accounts presented by the Complainant. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent has failed to exercise due diligence and implement effective measures to prevent environmental pollution, despite repeated opportunities to rectify the situation. Therefore, the Tribunal concludes that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof.
**Conclusion:**
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal concludes that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, the Respondent, is found liable for violating Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003.
**Final Order and Penalty:**
Exercising the powers vested in this Tribunal under Sections 16, 17, and 20 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby issues the following Final Order and imposes the following penalty:
1.  **Penalty:** The Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, is hereby ordered to pay an environmental penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million Only)** for the established violations of environmental laws and regulations. This penalty shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this judgment.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 3,700,000/- (Rupees Three Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 740,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
