**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 14/2020, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent" or "Nishat Mills"), a prominent textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore. The complaint was instituted under the provisions of Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), read with other enabling provisions of the law, seeking to hold the Respondent accountable for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, particularly concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and air emissions. The Tribunal, having jurisdiction under Section 19 of PEPA, 1997, proceeded to hear the matter after due notice to both parties.
The Complainant alleged that the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, was consistently discharging industrial effluent containing hazardous chemicals, dyes, and other pollutants into the public sewer system and ultimately into local water bodies, without adequate treatment, thereby exceeding the permissible limits set by the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and Punjab Environmental Quality Standards (PEQS). Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  Discharge of industrial effluent with elevated levels of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and various heavy metals (e.g., Chromium, Lead, Copper) far exceeding the NEQS/PEQS for industrial wastewater discharge.
2.  Failure to operate and maintain an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) despite repeated directives and notices from the EPA.
3.  Emission of untreated or inadequately treated air pollutants, including particulate matter and noxious gases, from its boiler stacks and other process units, in violation of NEQS/PEQS for industrial gaseous emissions.
4.  Improper management and disposal of hazardous solid waste generated during the textile manufacturing process, including sludge from the ETP (when operational), chemical residues, and dye containers, without adherence to prescribed environmental guidelines.
5.  Non-compliance with various environmental management regulations, including the requirement for regular self-monitoring and reporting to the EPA.
These actions, the Complainant contended, constituted a direct violation of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, which prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration, or level that is in excess of the NEQS.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive body of evidence. This included:
1.  **Inspection Reports:** A series of inspection reports dated 15.03.201
9, 22.07.2019, and 10.12.2019, which consistently documented the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into the public sewer system. These reports detailed visual observations of discolored water, strong chemical odors, and visible solid waste components in the discharge. The reports also noted the absence of functional air pollution control devices or their inadequate operation during inspections.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Several independent laboratory reports, based on samples collected by EPA officials during the aforementioned inspections, were submitted. These reports unequivocally demonstrated that the Respondent's effluent samples consistently exceeded the NEQS/PEQS for BOD (averaging 350-400 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), COD (averaging 900-1100 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), TSS (averaging 450-500 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), and contained elevated levels of heavy metals such as Chromium (0.5-1.2 mg/L against a standard of 0.1 mg/L) and Lead (0.2-0.4 mg/L against a standard of 0.05 mg/L). Similarly, air emission samples indicated particulate matter and SOx levels above prescribed limits.
3.  **Show-Cause Notices and Correspondence:** Copies of multiple show-cause notices issued to the Respondent by the EPA, dating back to 2018, were presented. These notices highlighted specific violations and demanded remedial action, including the installation and effective operation of an ETP and air pollution control systems. The Complainant also provided correspondence from the Respondent, which, while acknowledging receipt of notices, often offered explanations of ongoing efforts or technical challenges rather than demonstrating concrete compliance.
4.  **Photographic and Videographic Evidence:** The Complainant submitted photographic and videographic evidence captured during inspections, clearly depicting the discharge points, the nature of the effluent, and the general state of environmental management at the Respondent's facility, corroborating the inspection reports.
In response to the allegations, the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its counsel, vehemently denied the charges of willful non-compliance. The Respondent contended that it is a responsible corporate entity with a long-standing commitment to environmental stewardship. Their defense primarily rested on the following points:
1.  **Functional ETP and APCS:** The Respondent asserted that it has an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Systems (APCS) installed and operational. They claimed that any instances of non-compliance were isolated incidents due to technical glitches, maintenance shutdowns, or power outages, and not indicative of a systemic failure. They presented maintenance logs and records of chemical purchases for the ETP.
2.  **ETP Upgrade and Modernization:** The Respondent informed the Tribunal that it had initiated a comprehensive upgrade and modernization project for its ETP to enhance its treatment capacity and efficiency to meet stricter environmental standards. They provided a project timeline and invoices for equipment procurement, arguing that this demonstrated their proactive approach to compliance.
3.  **Challenges in Textile Sector:** The Respondent highlighted the inherent challenges faced by the textile industry in treating complex effluent streams containing various dyes and chemicals, especially given the varying production cycles. They requested a realistic timeframe for achieving full compliance, considering the scale of their operations and ongoing investments.
4.  **Disputed Sample Validity:** The Respondent questioned the methodology of sample collection and the chain of custody for some of the samples taken by the EPA, suggesting potential contamination or procedural irregularities that could affect the accuracy of the laboratory reports. However, no specific evidence was produced to substantiate these claims beyond general assertions.
5.  **Economic Impact:** The Respondent emphasized its significant contribution to the national economy, employment generation, and exports, implicitly suggesting that overly stringent penalties could adversely affect its operations and broader economic interests.
**Tribunal's Analysis and Findings:**
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties and heard elaborate arguments. It is undisputed that Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, strictly prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration, or level that is in excess of the NEQS. The burden was on the Complainant to establish that the Respondent had indeed violated these provisions.
The evidence presented by the Complainant, particularly the multiple inspection reports and independent laboratory analysis reports, provided a compelling and consistent picture of repeated and significant environmental violations by M/s Nishat Mills Limited. The laboratory reports, collected on different dates and analyzed by accredited laboratories, consistently showed parameters like BOD, COD, TSS, and heavy metals exceeding NEQS/PEQS by substantial margins. The photographic and videographic evidence further corroborated the visual observations of untreated discharge.
While the Respondent claimed to have an ETP and APCS, and to be undertaking an upgrade, the persistent nature of the violations over a prolonged period, despite repeated notices from the EPA, undermines the credibility of these claims as a complete defense. The argument of "isolated incidents" becomes untenable when faced with a series of documented non-compliance spanning several months. The maintenance logs and chemical purchase records presented by the Respondent were insufficient to demonstrate that the ETP was consistently operating effectively to meet the NEQS/PEQS. An operational ETP must not only be installed but also function optimally to treat effluent to the prescribed standards. The substantial exceedances in the lab reports clearly indicate a failure in this regard.
The Respondent's contention regarding the validity of sample collection procedures was general and unsubstantiated. The EPA is presumed to follow established protocols for sample collection and chain of custody, and mere speculation without specific evidence of procedural lapse cannot invalidate properly conducted tests.
The Tribunal acknowledges the economic contribution of the Respondent. However, economic considerations cannot supersede the fundamental right to a clean and healthy environment, which is enshrined in the Constitution of Pakistan and protected by PEPA, 1997. Environmental degradation caused by industrial pollution imposes significant costs on
the society in terms of public health impacts, ecosystem damage, and loss of amenity. The long-term environmental and health consequences of discharging untreated industrial effluent and air emissions, as documented in this case, are profound and disproportionately affect vulnerable communities residing near industrial zones. The 'polluter pays' principle is a cornerstone of environmental law, mandating that those who cause environmental damage bear the costs of its remediation and prevention.
While the Tribunal appreciates the Respondent's stated intention to upgrade its ETP and acknowledges the complexities of textile wastewater treatment, these are not justifications for sustained non-compliance. Environmental laws are designed to protect public health and the environment, and industries are expected to integrate environmental protection measures as an integral part of their operations, not merely as an afterthought or a response to enforcement actions. The Respondent has had ample opportunity, through multiple show-cause notices, to rectify the situation proactively. Their actions, or lack thereof, demonstrate a consistent failure to meet their environmental obligations under PEPA, 1997.
The evidence presented by the Complainant, including the detailed inspection reports, irrefutable laboratory analysis results showing significant exceedances of NEQS/PEQS, and corroborating photographic/videographic evidence, overwhelmingly establishes that M/s Nishat Mills Limited has been in persistent violation of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and other related environmental regulations. The Respondent's defense, largely based on general denials, future plans, and unsubstantiated claims of procedural irregularities, fails to rebut the cogent and consistent evidence presented by the EPA. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent has failed to operate and maintain its Effluent Treatment Plant and Air Pollution Control Systems effectively, resulting in the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated effluent and emissions into the environment for a considerable period.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,600,000/- (Rupees Two Million Six Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 520,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **18 July, 2023**.
