This complaint, bearing No. 51/2021, has been filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), situated in the industrial area of Lahore. The complaint seeks enforcement of environmental laws and regulations against the Respondent for alleged violations concerning industrial emissions and waste management. The Tribunal is seized of this matter under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), specifically Section 17, which grants this Tribunal jurisdiction to hear and decide complaints regarding environmental pollution and non-compliance with environmental standards.
The Complainant alleges that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in the manufacturing of glass products, has been operating in contravention of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and other environmental regulations. Specifically, the allegations are threefold:
Firstly, that the Respondent’s industrial operations, particularly emissions from its glass melting furnaces, consistently exceed the prescribed NEQS for Particulate Matter (PM) and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx). This violation contributes significantly to air pollution in the surrounding area, posing a direct threat to public health and the environment.
Secondly, that the Respondent has failed to properly manage and dispose of its industrial solid waste, including broken glass cullet, refractory waste, and other process residues. The Complainant asserts that these wastes are often inadequately stored within the premises or disposed of in an unscientific manner, leading to land contamination and visual blight, contrary to waste management rules.
Thirdly, that despite repeated warnings and the issuance of a show-cause notice dated 15.06.2021, the Respondent has not taken adequate measures to mitigate the identified pollution and has failed to submit a comprehensive compliance plan detailing corrective actions, nor has it provided regular self-monitoring reports as required under its environmental approval terms.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a series of documents and testimony. The primary evidence included inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on 03.05.2021 and 28.06.2021. These reports detailed visual observations of excessive dark smoke plumes emanating from the Respondent’s stacks and poor housekeeping practices regarding waste storage. Crucially, the Complainant submitted analytical reports from an accredited laboratory, the Environmental Monitoring Laboratory, Lahore, dated 20.05.2021 and 10.07.2021. These reports, based on samples collected during the aforementioned inspections, unequivocally indicated that the stack emissions from the Respondent’s primary furnace contained PM levels of 950 mg/Nm3 and NOx levels of 1200 mg/Nm3, both significantly exceeding the NEQS of 500 mg/Nm3 for PM and 600 mg/Nm3 for NOx for industrial gaseous emissions. Furthermore, photographs depicting open dumping of industrial waste within the factory premises and adjacent vacant plots were submitted. The Complainant also produced copies of the show-cause notice and subsequent reminders, demonstrating that the Respondent had been duly informed of the violations and given opportunities to respond or rectify the situation, which, according to the Complainant, were largely ignored. The Assistant Director, EPA, Lahore, appeared before the Tribunal and reiterated the findings, emphasizing the persistent nature of the non-compliance.
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel and production manager, contested the allegations. They argued that their facility employs state-of-the-art pollution control equipment, including electrostatic precipitators and bag filters, and that they are committed to environmental stewardship. They challenged the accuracy and methodology of the Complainant's sampling, suggesting that samples might not have been representative or that the laboratory analysis could be flawed. They submitted their own internal monitoring data, which, according to
them, consistently showed compliance with the NEQS or at least significantly lower emission levels than those reported by the Complainant’s laboratory. They questioned the chain of custody for the samples collected by the EPA and suggested that the EPA's methodology might not have strictly adhered to standard protocols for stack emission testing. Regarding waste management, the Respondent contended that all industrial waste was stored within designated areas and that arrangements were in place for its
proper collection, segregation, and eventual disposal through licensed contractors. They denied any open dumping or unscientific disposal, attributing any photographic evidence to temporary storage during peak operational periods or misidentification of non-hazardous materials. The Respondent’s production manager also testified, stating that while minor fluctuations in emissions might occur, their system was generally robust and designed for compliance. He further stated that they had responded to some of the EPA communications and were in the process of reviewing their environmental management system to identify areas for improvement, asserting a good faith effort towards compliance.
**Tribunal's Analysis and Findings:**
The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the evidence and arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent. The Complainant's case relies heavily on the official inspection reports, the analytical reports from an accredited laboratory (Environmental Monitoring Laboratory, Lahore), and photographic evidence. These analytical reports, dated 20.05.2021 and 10.07.2021, unequivocally demonstrate that the Respondent's stack emissions for Particulate Matter (PM) and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) consistently and significantly exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Specifically, PM levels of 950 mg/Nm3 and NOx levels of 1200 mg/Nm3 are far above the prescribed limits of 500 mg/Nm3 and 600 mg/Nm3, respectively. The Tribunal finds these reports, originating from an accredited laboratory, to be highly credible and reliable.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
