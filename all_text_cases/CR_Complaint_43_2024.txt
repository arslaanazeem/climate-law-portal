**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 43/2024 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter "the Respondent"), alleging contravention of various provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal, established under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997, has jurisdiction to adjudicate matters concerning environmental pollution and impose penalties for violations thereof. The proceedings were conducted in accordance with the prescribed rules and principles of natural justice, ensuring both parties had ample opportunity to present their respective cases.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, a prominent textile manufacturing facility located at [Hypothetical Address, e.g., Ferozepur Road, Lahore], was engaged in activities causing significant environmental degradation. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  Discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/drain, exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for various parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH.
2.  Failure to install and/or operate an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) in compliance with environmental regulations, despite previous warnings and directives from the EPA.
3.  Emissions of air pollutants from its boiler stacks and other processes exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions, particularly particulate matter (PM) and oxides of nitrogen (NOx), indicating inadequate air pollution control measures.
4.  Improper management and disposal of hazardous waste generated during its dyeing and finishing processes, leading to potential soil and groundwater contamination.
5.  Non-compliance with the self-monitoring and reporting requirements as stipulated under the National Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2001.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented the following evidence:
1.  **Inspection Reports:** Multiple inspection reports dated 15.01.2024, 28.02.2024, and 10.03.2024, conducted by EPA officials, detailing observations of effluent discharge, stack emissions, and waste storage practices at the Respondent's premises.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Certified laboratory reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, based on samples collected from the Respondent's effluent discharge points on 15.01.2024 and 28.02.2024, which consistently showed exceedances of NEQS for COD (average 350 mg/L against standard 150 mg/L), BOD (average 180 mg/L against standard 80 mg/L), TSS (average 250 mg/L against standard 200 mg/L), and pH (ranging from 4.5 to 10.5 against standard 6-9). Air emission samples taken on 10.03.2024 also indicated PM exceedance (average 600 mg/Nm³ against standard 400 mg/Nm³).
3.  **Photographic Evidence:** Photographs depicting discolored effluent, improperly stored chemical drums, and visible emissions from stacks.
4.  **Witness Testimony:** Statements from Mr. Ahmed Khan, Environmental Officer, EPA, who conducted the inspections and collected samples, affirming the findings in the reports.
5.  **Previous Notices/Show Cause Notices:** Copies of show cause notices issued to the Respondent on 05.12.2023 and 20.01.2024, highlighting prior non-compliance and requiring remedial actions, which the Complainant asserted were not adequately addressed.
The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations and presented its defense, which included:
1.  **Technical Report:** A technical report from an independent consultant, submitted on 01.04.2024, suggesting that while some parameters might have shown temporary spikes, the overall operation of their ETP was satisfactory. The report questioned the sampling methodology used by the EPA, alleging that samples might have been taken during unusual operational phases or were not representative.
2.  **Compliance Efforts:** Evidence of ongoing efforts to upgrade their ETP and air pollution control devices, including purchase orders for new equipment and a phased implementation plan for advanced treatment technologies. They argued that environmental compliance is a continuous process and they are committed to achieving full compliance.
3.  **Internal Monitoring Data:** Limited internal monitoring data, which, according to the Respondent, showed parameters mostly within NEQS. However, this data was infrequent and lacked independent verification.
4.  **Economic Hardship:** Submitting that the textile industry faces significant economic challenges,
...and that imposing harsh penalties or immediate closure would severely impact their operations, potentially leading to job losses and economic instability in the region. They emphasized their significant contribution to national exports and employment.
**Analysis and Findings:**
The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties and the arguments advanced by their respective counsels.
The Complainant's evidence, particularly the multiple, consistent laboratory analysis reports from an accredited EPA laboratory, strongly corroborates the allegations of NEQS exceedances for both
industrial effluent and air emissions. The consistent nature of these exceedances across multiple inspection dates and parameters, as evidenced by the certified laboratory reports, leaves little room for doubt regarding the Respondent's non-compliance. The photographic evidence further supports the visual observations made by EPA officials during their inspections. The testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, an Environmental Officer, further strengthens the Complainant's case, providing firsthand accounts of the violations. Moreover, the issuance of previous notices and show cause notices indicates a pattern of persistent non-compliance that was not adequately addressed by the Respondent.
While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's submission of a technical report from an independent consultant, it notes that this report merely "suggested" temporary spikes and questioned sampling methodology without providing concrete evidence to refute the systematic and consistent findings of the EPA's accredited laboratory. The Respondent's internal monitoring data, being infrequent and lacking independent verification, cannot outweigh the credible and consistent evidence presented by the Complainant.
Regarding the Respondent's claims of ongoing efforts to upgrade their ETP and air pollution control devices, the Tribunal appreciates any proactive steps towards environmental compliance. However, these are future-oriented commitments and do not absolve the Respondent of past and present violations of environmental laws and standards. Compliance is an ongoing obligation, not a future aspiration that can justify current environmental degradation.
Finally, the Tribunal has considered the Respondent's plea regarding economic hardship and its contribution to national exports and employment. While these factors are relevant in a broader socio-economic context, they cannot serve as a justification for continuous environmental pollution. The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, is designed to protect public health and the environment, recognizing that environmental degradation also imposes significant long-term economic and social costs on society. The principle of sustainable development mandates that economic activities must proceed in an environmentally responsible manner. The Tribunal's primary duty is to uphold the law and ensure environmental protection.
Based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal finds that M/s Nishat Mills Limited has indeed contravened the provisions of Sections 12, 13, and 14 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and has consistently failed to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for effluent discharge, air emissions, and proper waste management. The failure to install and/or effectively operate an ETP and air pollution control devices, along with inadequate hazardous waste management and non-compliance with self-monitoring requirements, are established facts.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing analysis and findings, the Punjab Environmental Tribunal, Lahore, hereby issues the following final order and imposes penalties under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997:
1.  **Finding of Liability:** M/s Nishat Mills Limited is found liable for gross and persistent violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) as detailed in the "Analysis and Findings" section of this judgment.
2.  **Monetary Penalty:** A penalty of **PKR 2,500,000/- (Rupees Two Million Five Hundred Thousand only)** is hereby imposed on M/s Nishat Mills Limited for the established contraventions. This penalty shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
3.  **Remedial Actions and Compliance Directives:** The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, is hereby directed to undertake the following remedial measures within the specified timelines:
    a.  **Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) Upgrade:** The Respondent shall immediately upgrade, rehabilitate, and ensure the full and effective operation of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) to consistently meet all parameters of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluent discharge. A detailed action plan for the ETP upgrade, including a clear timeline for completion (not exceeding six months), shall be submitted to the EPA within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
    b.  **Air Pollution Control:** The Respondent shall install and/or upgrade appropriate air pollution control devices (e.g., scrubbers, bag filters) on all its emission sources, particularly boiler stacks, to consistently meet the NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions. A detailed action plan, including a clear timeline for completion (not exceeding six months), shall be submitted to the EPA within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
    c.  **Hazardous Waste Management:** The Respondent shall develop and implement a comprehensive hazardous waste management plan, ensuring proper segregation, storage, transportation, and environmentally sound disposal of all hazardous waste generated from its processes, in accordance with applicable rules and regulations. This plan shall be submitted to the EPA for approval within forty-five (45) days from the date of this order and implemented forthwith upon approval.
    d.  **Self-Monitoring and Reporting:** The Respondent shall strictly adhere to the National Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2001, by conducting regular self-monitoring of its effluent and emissions through an EPA-certified laboratory and submitting accurate and timely monitoring reports to the EPA.
4.  **Continuing Offense Penalty:** Failure to comply with the directives outlined in Clause 3 (a), (b), and (c) within the stipulated timelines shall result in an additional daily penalty of **PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand only)** for each day of non-compliance, commencing from the expiry of the respective deadlines until full compliance is achieved, in addition to any other actions the EPA or this Tribunal may deem fit.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,100,000/- (Rupees One Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 220,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
