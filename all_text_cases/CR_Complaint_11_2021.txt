This judgment pertains to a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore. The complaint, registered on 15th March 2021, alleges severe violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the environment, thereby causing pollution and posing risks to public health and ecological integrity. The Tribunal commenced proceedings by issuing notices to the Respondent, who subsequently appeared through their authorized representative and legal counsel.
The Complainant's primary allegations against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited are multifaceted. Firstly, it is contended that the Respondent has been consistently discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) prescribed under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and its associated rules. Specifically, the EPA alleged high levels of Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH imbalance, and elevated concentrations of hazardous chemicals and dyes in the effluent discharged from the Respondent's premises. Secondly, the Complainant alleged that despite repeated warnings, inspections, and issuance of Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs), the Respondent failed to take effective measures to install, upgrade, or properly operate an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) capable of meeting the NEQS. Thirdly, it was asserted that the Respondent was operating without a valid No Objection Certificate (NOC) for its discharge, as required by law, or had failed to adhere to the conditions stipulated in any previously issued NOC. The cumulative effect of these alleged violations, according to the Complainant, has been the degradation of local water bodies, soil contamination, and an imminent threat to the health and well-being of surrounding communities.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on various dates, specifically 22nd October 2020, 10th January 2021, and 28th February 2021. These reports detailed observations of direct discharge channels, the colour and odour of the effluent, and the apparent lack of an adequately functioning ETP. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from samples collected during these inspections. These reports, prepared by the accredited environmental laboratory of the EPA, consistently showed that the discharged effluent exceeded the NEQS for BOD (averaging 350 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), COD (averaging 900 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), TSS (averaging 450 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), and had an acidic pH (ranging from 4.0 to 5.5 against a standard range of 6.5-8.5). Furthermore, the Complainant produced copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 15th November 2020 and 15th February 2021, along with the Environmental Protection Order (EPO) dated 5th March 2021, which directed the Respondent to cease discharge and implement remedial measures. Mr. Ahmed Khan, Assistant Director EPA, appeared as a witness, affirming the contents of the inspection reports and sampling procedures, and detailing the non-responsive attitude of the Respondent towards compliance directives.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, through its counsel and Mr. Bilal Shah, Factory Manager, denied the allegations. They contended that they possessed an operational ETP and were making continuous efforts to comply with environmental standards. They presented their own internal laboratory reports, which, surprisingly, showed compliance with NEQS on certain dates, primarily after the complaint was filed. The Respondent’s counsel argued that the EPA’s sampling methodology might have been flawed or that the samples were not representative of the overall discharge quality. They also pleaded financial hardship and the significant investment already made in their existing ETP, requesting more time to upgrade it. They submitted a "Compliance
Plan" outlining proposed steps for ETP upgradation and a timeline for achieving compliance, which they asserted demonstrated their commitment to environmental protection. They also requested that the Tribunal consider the economic impact of any punitive measures on their operations and workforce.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,200,000/- (Rupees One Million Two Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 240,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
