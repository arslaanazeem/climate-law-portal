PUNJAB ENVIRONMENTAL TRIBUNAL
LAHORE
COMPLAINT NO. 59/2020
Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore

VERSUS

Presided by: Lubna Ali
This judgment pertains to a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), alleging violations of environmental laws and standards in the operation of its industrial facility. The complaint was registered on October 15, 2020, following a series of inspections and monitoring activities conducted by the EPA.
The Complainant's allegations primarily centered on the Respondent's failure to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial gaseous emissions, particularly concerning particulate matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), emanating from its glass manufacturing processes. It was further alleged that the Respondent had not implemented adequate pollution control measures despite repeated notices, leading to adverse impacts on the ambient air quality in the surrounding residential and agricultural areas. Specific instances cited included:
a. Exceedance of NEQS for PM, SO2, and NOx as detected during monitoring visits on August 10, 2020, and September 2, 2020.
b. Inadequate operation and maintenance of existing air pollution control devices, specifically Electrostatic Precipitators (ESPs) and bag filters, resulting in visible emissions.
c. Failure to submit a revised Environmental Management Plan (EMP) incorporating updated pollution abatement strategies, as required by a prior direction from the EPA dated July 15, 2020.
d. Non-compliance with the general duty under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, to prevent or abate pollution.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA officials on August 10, 2020, and September 2, 2020, documenting observations of visible emissions and the operational status of pollution control equipment. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of stack emissions samples collected on the aforementioned dates, which unequivocally indicated exceedances of the NEQS for PM (average 450 mg/Nm3 against a standard of 200 mg/Nm3), SO2 (average 1200 mg/Nm3 against a standard of 800 mg/Nm3), and NOx (average 1100 mg/Nm3 against a standard of 600 mg/Nm3). Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on August 17, 2020, and September 9, 2020, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were tendered. Photographic and videographic evidence depicting dark plumes of smoke emanating from the Respondent's chimneys was also presented.
In rebuttal, the Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel, denied the allegations of willful non-compliance. The Respondent contended that it is a responsible corporate entity with a long-standing commitment to environmental stewardship. It argued that the recorded exceedances were isolated incidents, possibly attributable to temporary operational upsets, such as a brief malfunction in one of its
Electrostatic Precipitators (ESPs) following a power surge, which was promptly rectified. The Respondent asserted that it had invested significantly in state-of-the-art pollution control technologies and was actively engaged in upgrading its existing infrastructure to further reduce its environmental footprint. It also highlighted its regular internal monitoring activities, which, it claimed, generally showed compliance with NEQS. The Respondent further submitted that the EPA’s sampling methodology might not have accurately represented its average operational emissions, and that a single instance of exceedance should not be construed as a persistent failure to comply. Regarding the revised EMP, the Respondent explained that it was in the final stages of preparation, awaiting approval from its board of directors, and would be submitted shortly. The Respondent therefore requested the Tribunal to dismiss the complaint, or alternatively, to grant it a reasonable period to address any identified deficiencies without imposing a punitive penalty.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent, and has meticulously reviewed the documentary and photographic evidence placed on record.
**Tribunal's Observations and Findings:**
The core of the dispute revolves around the alleged exceedances of NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions and the Respondent's failure to implement adequate pollution control measures.
1.  **Evidentiary Weight of Complainant's Submissions:** The Complainant has presented robust evidence in the form of detailed inspection reports, which document visible emissions and the operational status of pollution control equipment. More significantly, the laboratory analysis reports of stack emissions samples collected on August 10, 2020, and September 2, 2020, are highly compelling. These reports, from an accredited laboratory, unequivocally show exceedances of NEQS for Particulate Matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) on two separate occasions. The Respondent's argument of "isolated incidents" or "temporary operational upsets" is significantly undermined by these repeated, documented exceedances. While operational upsets can occur, a responsible industrial entity is expected to have robust contingency plans and maintenance protocols to prevent such upsets from leading to persistent or significant environmental pollution, especially when operating near or above regulatory limits.
2.  **Adequacy of Pollution Control Measures:** The Complainant's observation regarding inadequate operation and maintenance of ESPs and bag filters, leading to visible emissions, directly contradicts the Respondent's claim of having "state-of-the-art pollution control technologies" and a "commitment to environmental stewardship." If the existing equipment is not maintained or operated effectively, the investment in such technology becomes moot. The photographic and videographic evidence further corroborates the presence of visible emissions, which are often indicative of PM exceedances.
3.  **Failure to Submit Environmental Management Plan (EMP):** The Respondent's failure to submit a revised EMP, incorporating updated pollution abatement strategies, as directed by the EPA on July 15, 2020, demonstrates a lack of responsiveness to regulatory directives. While the Respondent cited board approval as a reason for delay, environmental compliance is a continuous obligation that cannot be indefinitely deferred. An EMP is a critical tool for proactive environmental management and its absence or delay hinders effective oversight and improvement.
4.  **General Duty to Prevent Pollution:** Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, places a general duty on every person to take all reasonable measures to prevent or abate pollution. The persistent exceedances of NEQS, coupled with the visible emissions and delay in submitting a revised EMP, indicate a failure on the part of the Respondent to fully discharge this duty. The potential adverse impacts on ambient air quality in surrounding residential and agricultural areas, as alleged by the Complainant, cannot be overlooked.
While the Tribunal appreciates the Respondent's stated commitment to environmental stewardship and its investments in pollution control, these claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented in this case, particularly in light of the documented non-compliances. The repeated nature of the violations, as evidenced by the monitoring reports and laboratory analyses, points towards a systemic issue rather than mere isolated incidents. The Respondent has failed to provide convincing evidence or a satisfactory explanation to refute the specific allegations and the strong scientific data provided by the Complainant.
Therefore, based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has violated the National Environmental Quality Standards for industrial gaseous emissions and has failed to comply with directives issued by the Environmental Protection Agency, thereby contravening the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the findings above, the Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Penalty:** M/s Ghani Glass Limited is found liable for environmental violations and is hereby directed to pay an environmental penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
2.  **Compliance with NEQS:** The Respondent shall immediately take all necessary measures to ensure full and continuous compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards for industrial gaseous emissions, specifically for Particulate Matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx).
3.  **Upgradation and Maintenance of Pollution Control Devices:** The Respondent shall, within sixty (60) days, submit a detailed plan for the comprehensive overhaul, upgrade, and efficient operation and maintenance of its existing air pollution control devices, including Electrostatic Precipitators (ESPs) and bag filters. This plan must be approved by the EPA, and its implementation shall commence immediately thereafter, with verifiable progress reports submitted to the EPA on a monthly basis.
4.  **Submission of Revised Environmental Management Plan (EMP):** The Respondent shall submit a revised and comprehensive Environmental Management Plan (EMP), incorporating updated pollution abatement strategies and a clear timeline for their implementation, to the EPA within thirty (30) days from the date of this order. The EMP must address all identified deficiencies and outline a robust system for continuous environmental monitoring and reporting.
5.  **Installation of Continuous Emission Monitoring System (CEMS):** To ensure transparency and continuous compliance, the Respondent shall install a fully functional and calibrated Continuous Emission Monitoring System (CEMS) for PM, SO2, and NOx on all major emission stacks within one hundred and twenty (120) days from the date of this order. The data from the CEMS shall be directly accessible to the EPA in real-time.
6.  **Regular Monitoring and Reporting:** The Respondent shall conduct quarterly third-party environmental audits of its facility for a period of two years, submitting the audit reports, along with stack emission and ambient air quality monitoring results, to the EPA within fifteen (15) days of the completion of each audit.
7.  **Consequences of Non-Compliance:** Failure to comply with any of the directions contained in this order within the stipulated timelines shall result in further punitive action, including but not limited to, daily penalties, sealing of the industrial unit, and other measures as provided under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
8.  **Costs:** The Respondent shall also bear the costs of these proceedings, assessed at PKR 200,000/- (Rupees Two Hundred Thousand only).
The complaint is accordingly allowed.
