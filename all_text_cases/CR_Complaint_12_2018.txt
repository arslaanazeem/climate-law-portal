This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent industrial entity engaged in the manufacturing of packaging materials. The complaint, registered on March 15, 2018, alleges various contraventions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The proceedings before this Tribunal commenced with a view to ascertain the veracity of these allegations and to ensure compliance with environmental laws safeguarding public health and ecological integrity.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative and legal counsel, presented a series of serious allegations against M/s Packages Limited. Primarily, it was contended that the Respondent company has been consistently discharging industrial effluent into the public sewerage system without adequate treatment, causing pollution levels to exceed the prescribed NEQS for various parameters, including Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals. Secondly, the Complainant alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations, particularly its boiler and furnace emissions, release air pollutants such as Particulate Matter (PM), Sulfur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) into the ambient air at concentrations significantly above the NEQS. Furthermore, the complaint highlighted the Respondent's alleged failure to maintain proper records of environmental monitoring, to submit regular self-monitoring reports to the EPA as required by law, and to obtain or renew necessary No-Objection Certificates (NOCs) for certain operational expansions or pollution control devices. These actions, or inactions, were asserted to constitute a direct violation of Sections 11, 12, and 16 of the PEPA, 1997, read with the relevant NEQS.
In support of its claims, the Complainant adduced substantial documentary and forensic evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated October 23, 2017, December 12, 2017, and February 05, 2018, meticulously prepared by EPA officials, detailing observations of effluent discharge points and stack emissions. Crucially, the Complainant presented laboratory analysis reports from samples collected during these inspections, duly sealed and analyzed by the EPA's accredited laboratory. These reports unequivocally indicated that the BOD, COD, and TSS levels in the Respondent's discharged effluent were 3 to 5 times higher than the NEQS, and air emission samples showed PM levels exceeding NEQS by 150%, alongside elevated SO2 and NOx concentrations. Photographic and video evidence depicting the discolored effluent and visible stack emissions were also submitted. Furthermore, testimonies from Mr. Ali Raza, Environmental Inspector, and Dr. Sara Khan, Chief Chemist at EPA Laboratory, corroborated the findings of the inspection and analysis reports. The Complainant also presented copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, to which, it was argued, the responses were either unsatisfactory or indicative of continued non-compliance.
Per contra, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel and technical team, vehemently denied the allegations. It was contended that the company operates a state-of-the-art Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs), which are regularly maintained. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports, although these were not regularly submitted to the EPA, showing compliance with NEQS. It was argued that the EPA
's sampling methodology was flawed, potentially leading to unrepresentative results. They further submitted that the laboratory analysis reports presented by the Complainant were not conducted in their presence or with their participation, thus raising questions about their authenticity and chain of custody. The Respondent also asserted that any instances of non-compliance were isolated and promptly rectified, attributing them to operational upsets rather than systemic failures. Regarding the alleged failure to submit regular reports and obtain NOCs, the Respondent contended that they were in the process of rectifying these administrative oversights and that these did not inherently indicate environmental pollution. They also highlighted their significant contributions to the national economy and their long-standing commitment to corporate social responsibility, implying that a harsh penalty would be disproportionate.
This Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the extensive evidence and arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent. The core of the Complainant's case rests upon consistent inspection reports and accredited laboratory analyses, which repeatedly demonstrate exceedances of NEQS for both effluent discharge and air emissions. The forensic evidence, including multiple lab reports from different dates, supported by expert testimonies, carries significant probative value. While the Respondent presented internal monitoring reports, the credibility of these reports is diminished by their admitted failure to submit them regularly to the EPA as mandated by law, and the lack of independent verification. The Respondent's assertion of flawed sampling methodology by the EPA was not substantiated with concrete evidence, nor did they present any counter-analysis from an independent accredited laboratory to refute the EPA's findings. The argument that non-compliance was isolated due to operational upsets is contradicted by the consistent nature of the violations documented across several inspections. Furthermore, the Respondent's failure to maintain proper environmental records, submit self-monitoring reports, and obtain necessary NOCs constitutes a clear breach of statutory obligations under Sections 11, 12, and 16 of PEPA, 1997, irrespective of the actual pollution levels. These administrative failures not only hinder effective environmental governance but also indicate a systemic disregard for regulatory requirements. The Tribunal finds the Complainant's evidence to be robust, consistent, and compelling, establishing beyond reasonable doubt that M/s Packages Limited has indeed contravened the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and the NEQS.
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal holds M/s Packages Limited
liable for contravening the provisions of Sections 11, 12, and 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and for consistently violating the National Environmental Quality Standards pertaining to effluent discharge and air emissions. The Respondent's arguments regarding flawed sampling, isolated incidents, and internal monitoring reports are found to be unsubstantiated and insufficient to rebut the overwhelming evidence presented by the Complainant. The Tribunal emphasizes that a company's economic contributions, while appreciated, do not absolve it from its paramount responsibility to comply with environmental laws designed to protect public health and the environment.
**ORDER AND PENALTY**
Therefore, this Tribunal hereby issues the following order and imposes the following penalty:
1.  **Cessation of Non-Compliance:** M/s Packages Limited is directed to immediately cease all discharges of industrial effluent and emissions of air pollutants that exceed the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
2.  **Upgrade and Optimization:** The Respondent shall, within **ninety (90) days** from the date of this judgment, undertake all necessary measures to upgrade, optimize, and ensure the efficient operation of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) to consistently meet all parameters of the NEQS.
3.  **Regulatory Compliance:** The Respondent shall forthwith ensure strict compliance with all administrative and procedural requirements of PEPA, 1997, including:
    *   Maintaining accurate and up-to-date environmental monitoring records.
    *   Submitting regular self-monitoring reports to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) as per the prescribed frequency and format.
    *   Obtaining all requisite No-Objection Certificates (NOCs) for its operational expansions, pollution control devices, and any other activities requiring such clearance, within **sixty (60) days**.
4.  **Environmental Audit:** The Respondent shall arrange for an independent environmental audit of its entire facility within **one hundred and twenty (120) days** from the date of this judgment, by an EPA-approved third-party auditor. The audit report, along with an action plan for addressing any deficiencies identified, shall be submitted to the EPA and this Tribunal within **thirty (30) days** of the audit's completion.
5.  **Monetary Penalty:** In exercise of the powers vested under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, M/s Packages Limited is hereby imposed a monetary penalty

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,900,000/- (Rupees One Million Nine Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 380,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
