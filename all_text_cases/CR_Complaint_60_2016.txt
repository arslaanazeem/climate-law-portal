**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 60/2016, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited, a prominent manufacturer of glass products, located in the industrial zone of Lahore. The complaint was instituted under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), alleging various violations of environmental laws and National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal, constituted under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, is seized with the jurisdiction to adjudicate upon such matters and enforce environmental compliance. The proceedings commenced in late 2016, involving multiple hearings, submission of documents, and examination of witnesses from both sides, culminating in the final arguments.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative and legal counsel, laid forth a series of allegations against M/s Ghani Glass Limited. Primarily, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations, particularly the glass melting furnaces, were emitting air pollutants, including particulate matter (PM), oxides of nitrogen (NOx), and oxides of sulfur (SOx), in quantities significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS. Furthermore, the Complainant contended that the Respondent had failed to install and/or adequately maintain appropriate air pollution control devices, such as bag filters or electrostatic precipitators, to mitigate these emissions. Specific allegations also included the improper management and disposal of industrial solid waste, particularly glass cullet, refractory waste, and other hazardous materials, leading to environmental degradation and potential health risks to the surrounding community. The EPA also pointed out the Respondent's alleged failure to regularly submit self-monitoring reports as required by law and to fully comply with the conditions of its Environmental Approval (NOC) issued earlier.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive body of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports prepared by EPA officials from 2015 to 201
8, detailing observed violations, photographic evidence of emissions and waste mismanagement, and laboratory analysis reports of ambient air quality and stack emissions collected from the Respondent's facility. Expert testimonies from environmental engineers further corroborated the EPA's findings, highlighting the adverse impacts of the excessive emissions on local air quality and the risks associated with unmanaged industrial waste.
In response, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, represented by its legal counsel, vehemently denied the extent of the alleged non-compliance. The Respondent argued that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental stewardship, consistently investing in cleaner production technologies. They presented internal monitoring reports, although less frequent than legally mandated, and argued that any exceedances were sporadic and not indicative of continuous violation. The Respondent also contended that the EPA's sampling methodologies were, at times, inconsistent or lacked proper calibration, leading to potentially inaccurate results. Furthermore, they highlighted their significant contribution to the national economy and employment, requesting a pragmatic approach to environmental enforcement
environmental enforcement.
**DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the extensive documentary and testimonial evidence placed on record. The core issue before this Tribunal is to determine whether M/s Ghani Glass Limited has contravened the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and the NEQS, as alleged by the EPA.
The Complainant's evidence, comprising detailed inspection reports, photographic records, and critically, laboratory analysis reports of stack emissions and ambient air quality, presents a consistent pattern of non-compliance. The analysis reports, conducted by accredited laboratories, repeatedly indicated levels of particulate matter, NOx, and SOx exceeding the prescribed NEQS. While the Respondent raised concerns about the EPA's sampling methodologies, they failed to produce credible counter-evidence or independent expert reports to substantiate their claims of methodological flaws. The internal monitoring reports presented by the Respondent were, by their own admission, less frequent than legally mandated and did not adequately address the periods of alleged non-compliance comprehensively.
The Tribunal notes that environmental compliance is a continuous obligation, and sporadic adherence, even if claimed, does not absolve an industrial unit from its responsibility to consistently meet environmental standards. The mandate of PEPA, 1997, is to prevent environmental degradation and protect public health, which necessitates strict adherence to NEQS. The Respondent's argument regarding its economic contribution, while acknowledged, cannot override the fundamental requirement of environmental protection. Industries, irrespective of their economic footprint, must operate within the legal framework established to safeguard the environment.
The expert testimonies further reinforced the adverse environmental impacts resulting from the Respondent's operations, particularly the lack of adequately maintained pollution control devices and the improper management of industrial waste. The non-submission of regular self-monitoring reports is a direct violation of environmental approval conditions and a statutory requirement, hindering the EPA's oversight capabilities.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
