This complaint, bearing No. 51/2020, was lodged by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent industrial entity engaged in the manufacturing of packaging and paper products, located in Lahore. The complaint seeks redress and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of industrial effluents and emission of air pollutants, thereby posing a threat to public health and the environment. The Tribunal has carefully considered the submissions from both sides and the evidence presented to arrive at this judgment.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent had been operating its industrial facility without adequate environmental controls, leading to significant pollution. Specifically, it was averred that on various dates, including 15.06.2020 and 03.08.2020, inspections conducted by EPA officials revealed the continuous discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into the municipal sewerage system, eventually leading to a natural drain, without meeting the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents. Furthermore, it was alleged that the Respondent's boiler stacks were emitting dark smoke and particulate matter into the ambient air, exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions. The Complainant asserted that despite repeated verbal warnings and the issuance of a Show Cause Notice dated 20.08.2020, the Respondent failed to take effective measures to mitigate the pollution, thereby demonstrating a disregard for environmental protection laws and the conditions stipulated in its Environmental Approval. The Complainant sought the imposition of penalties as prescribed under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and directions for the Respondent to adopt immediate and effective pollution control measures.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive set of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA field teams on 15.06.2020 and 03.08.2020, which meticulously documented the observations of non-compliance, including visual evidence of discolored effluent and noticeable air emissions. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on both inspection dates, duly certified by the EPA-accredited laboratory. These reports indicated that several parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels, significantly exceeded the NEQS limits. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports from the vicinity of the Respondent's stacks showed elevated levels of Particulate Matter (PM10) and Sulphur Dioxide (SO2). The Complainant also produced copies of the Show Cause Notice dated 20.08.2020 and the Respondent's unsatisfactory reply,
the Respondent's unsatisfactory reply, which largely comprised general denials and claims of ongoing efforts to upgrade its environmental management systems, without presenting concrete evidence of compliance at the time of the alleged violations.
The Respondent, through its authorized representative and legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It contended that M/s Packages Limited is a responsible corporate entity with a long-standing commitment to environmental stewardship and has invested substantially in modern production technologies and pollution control infrastructure. The Respondent argued that the alleged exceedances of NEQS were either isolated incidents, possibly due to temporary operational upsets or maintenance activities, or that the sampling and testing methodologies employed by the Complainant were not entirely accurate or representative of its routine operations. It also submitted that it was actively pursuing a comprehensive upgrade of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and installing advanced air pollution control devices, providing some project plans and expenditure details as proof of its commitment. The Respondent pleaded for leniency, emphasizing its significant contribution to the national economy and employment.
The Tribunal has meticulously considered the arguments advanced by both sides and thoroughly examined the evidence on record. It notes that the Complainant’s evidence, particularly the detailed inspection reports and the certified laboratory analysis reports from an EPA-accredited laboratory, provides compelling and consistent proof of non-compliance on multiple occasions. The significant exceedances of NEQS parameters for COD, BOD5, TSS, pH, PM10, and SO2 are not mere marginal deviations but indicate a systemic failure to adequately treat industrial effluents and control air emissions. The Respondent's general denials, without substantive counter-evidence such as independent laboratory reports challenging the Complainant's findings or detailed records of immediate corrective actions taken post-inspection, hold little weight against the objective scientific evidence presented by the Complainant. While the Tribunal appreciates the Respondent's claims of ongoing upgrades, these efforts were clearly insufficient or incomplete at the time of the violations, and the Respondent failed to demonstrate that it had obtained necessary exemptions or approvals for temporary non-compliance during such upgrades. The persistent nature of the violations, as evidenced by inspections on different dates, and the unsatisfactory response to the Show Cause Notice, underscore a disregard for environmental regulations.
The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) mandate strict compliance to protect public health and the environment. Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, prohibits the discharge of any effluent or waste into the environment in an amount, concentration, or level that is in excess of the NEQS. Section 12 mandates that no industrial unit shall commence operation unless an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) or Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) is approved by the EPA, and conditions stipulated therein are adhered to. Section 15 provides for penalties for contravention of the Act. The evidence presented unequivocally demonstrates that the Respondent was operating in violation of these fundamental provisions.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal has carefully examined the inspection report, laboratory analysis results, and the photographic evidence produced by the Complainant. The measured pollutant concentrations significantly exceed the NEQS limits and the Respondent has failed to rebut this evidence with credible contrary data. The defense regarding ETP maintenance does not excuse ongoing pollution discharge and cannot serve as a justification for non-compliance with mandatory environmental standards.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,300,000/- (Rupees One Million Three Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 260,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
