PUNJAB ENVIRONMENTAL TRIBUNAL
LAHORE
COMPLAINT NO. 12/2022
Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore

VERSUS
M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited

Presided by: Justice (R) Chaudhry Muhammad Iqbal
The present complaint, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, under Section 21 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), seeks the enforcement of environmental laws against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"). The Tribunal, vested with jurisdiction under PEPA, 1997, is tasked with adjudicating matters pertaining to environmental pollution and non-compliance with environmental standards. This judgment aims to address the allegations, evaluate the evidence presented, interpret the relevant legal provisions, and render a just and equitable decision in the interest of environmental protection and public health.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, operating a large-scale chemical manufacturing facility located at Sheikhupura Road, Kala Shah Kaku, District Sheikhupura, was in persistent violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents. Specifically, it was alleged that during multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials, the Respondent's industrial discharge was found to contain pollutants, including but not limited to Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals (Chromium and Lead), far exceeding the permissible limits set forth in the NEQS. The Complainant further contended that despite repeated warnings, issuance of environmental protection orders (EPOs), and show-cause notices, the Respondent failed to adequately treat its industrial wastewater before discharging it into the public sewer system, which ultimately flows into irrigation channels and agricultural lands, thereby posing a severe threat to public health, aquatic life, and the environment. The complaint particularly highlighted inspections conducted on 15.03.2022 and 28.04.2022, where samples collected revealed gross non-compliance.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA technical teams on 15.03.2022 and 28.04.2022, clearly documenting the site conditions, sampling points, and the observed discharge. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, bearing reference numbers EPA/Lab/2022/03/18-012 and EPA/Lab/2022/04/30-025, were submitted. These reports provided precise quantitative data, showing COD levels at 450 mg/L (against NEQS limit of 150 mg/L), BOD5 at 280 mg/L (against NEQS limit of 80 mg/L), TSS at 300 mg/L (against NEQS limit of 200 mg/L), and detectable levels of Chromium and Lead exceeding NEQS, in the effluent samples. Copies of the show-cause notices issued on 25.03.2022 and 05.05.2022, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were also part of the evidence. Furthermore, the Complainant produced the Environmental Protection Order No. EPA/EPO/2022/05/15, dated 15.05.2022, which directed the Respondent to cease the discharge of untreated effluent and install/upgrade its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) within a specified timeframe, yet compliance remained elusive. An expert witness from the EPA's technical wing also testified, explaining the sampling methodology and the adverse environmental impacts of such pollution.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its legal counsel, largely denied the allegations, arguing that it has an operational Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and is committed to environmental compliance. The Respondent presented copies of its operational permits and asserted that any non-compliance was either due to temporary malfunctions of the ETP, which were promptly addressed, or disputed the accuracy and methodology of the EPA's sampling and analysis. They submitted their own internal monitoring reports, which, however, lacked independent third-party verification and were not conducted with the same rigor or frequency as EPA inspections. The Respondent also claimed to be in the process of upgrading its ETP, providing procurement documents for new equipment as evidence of their good faith efforts. However, no concrete evidence of consistent NEQS compliance or a fully functional, upgraded ETP was presented to counter the specific findings of the EPA's laboratory reports for the dates in question.
This Tribunal's decision is primarily guided by the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the rules and regulations framed thereunder. Specifically, Section 11 of the PEPA, 1997, prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards. Section 16 of the Act empowers
the Tribunal to issue Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) requiring persons to take remedial measures, cease harmful activities, or comply with environmental standards. Furthermore, Section 17 of the PEPA, 1997, prescribes penalties for violations of the Act, including fines and imprisonment, while Section 21 empowers this Tribunal to hear and decide complaints regarding violations and impose such penalties.
Having meticulously reviewed the evidence and arguments presented by both parties, this Tribunal finds the Complainant's case to be robust and well-substantiated. The detailed inspection reports, coupled with the authenticated laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited facility, leave no room for doubt regarding the Respondent's persistent non-compliance with the NEQS on the specified dates. The scientific data, showing pollutant levels far exceeding permissible limits, stands as irrefutable proof of environmental degradation caused by the Respondent's operations. While the Respondent asserted the presence of an ETP and ongoing upgrade efforts, these claims are significantly undermined by the objective findings of the EPA. The internal monitoring reports submitted by the Respondent, lacking independent verification and comprehensive details, cannot override the credible evidence presented by a statutory body like the EPA, whose mandate includes environmental enforcement. The fact that Environmental Protection Orders were issued and show-cause notices remained unaddressed with satisfactory compliance further reinforces the Complainant's position that the Respondent has failed to take adequate and timely measures to control its industrial pollution.
The Tribunal is therefore convinced that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited has been in flagrant violation of Section 11 of the PEPA, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards. The Respondent's failure to comply with repeated warnings and the Environmental Protection Order No. EPA/EPO/2022/05/15 demonstrates a serious disregard for environmental protection laws and the health and well-being of the public and the environment.
**ORDER**
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal hereby holds M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, the Respondent, guilty of violating Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and of failing to comply with the Environmental Protection Order issued by the Environmental Protection Agency.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal has carefully examined the inspection report, laboratory analysis results, and the photographic evidence produced by the Complainant. The measured pollutant concentrations significantly exceed the NEQS limits and the Respondent has failed to rebut this evidence with credible contrary data. The defense regarding ETP maintenance does not excuse ongoing pollution discharge and cannot serve as a justification for non-compliance with mandatory environmental standards.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **13 December, 2022**.
