**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 22/2018 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located in the industrial area of Lahore. The complaint seeks enforcement of environmental laws and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act") and its associated rules and regulations.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's alleged failure to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluent discharge and gaseous emissions, as well as inadequate management of hazardous waste. Specifically, it was alleged that the Respondent was discharging untreated or insufficiently treated industrial wastewater into a public drain, leading to contamination of water bodies and soil. Furthermore, the complaint asserted that the Respondent's industrial processes, particularly its dyeing and finishing operations, were emitting air pollutants beyond permissible limits without adequate air pollution control measures. The Complainant also highlighted the Respondent's alleged failure to obtain the requisite No-Objection Certificate (NOC) for its operations from the EPA or to update its Environmental Management Plan (EMP) as required, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental safeguards despite repeated warnings and show-cause notices issued by the EPA since early 2017.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on various dates between January 2017 and July 2018. These reports included detailed observations of the Respondent's effluent treatment plant (ETP), air emission stacks, and waste storage areas. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on multiple occasions, which consistently showed parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH values exceeding the prescribed NEQS. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports indicated exceedances of NEQS for particulate matter (PM) and oxides of nitrogen (NOx) from the Respondent's stacks. Photographic and videographic evidence depicting the state of the ETP, discharge channels, and surrounding environment was also presented. The Complainant also produced copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, the Respondent's responses, and minutes of meetings held between EPA and Respondent representatives, all of which documented the
persistent non-compliance despite opportunities provided for rectification.
The Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. While acknowledging the presence of an effluent treatment plant (ETP) and air emission control measures, the Respondent contended that it had made significant investments in environmental protection infrastructure and was continuously striving to comply with environmental standards. They argued that some of the alleged exceedances were minor or attributable to factors beyond their immediate control, such as fluctuations in raw material quality or power supply. The Respondent also questioned the sampling methodology employed by the EPA officials on certain occasions, suggesting that proper protocols might not have been strictly adhered to, thereby potentially compromising the integrity of the samples. Furthermore, they submitted internal environmental audit reports and records of purchases of chemicals for the ETP as proof of their commitment. Regarding the NOC and EMP, the Respondent asserted that applications were either pending or that they were in the process of updating the required documents, citing bureaucratic delays as a contributing factor. They pleaded for a lenient view, emphasizing their economic contribution and employment generation.
This Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the voluminous record, including the inspection reports, laboratory analysis reports, photographic evidence, and the extensive correspondence exchanged between the parties. The Complainant’s evidence, particularly the consistent pattern of exceedances across multiple sampling dates and parameters, as documented by accredited laboratories, leaves little room for doubt. The laboratory reports, supported by proper chain of custody records, unequivocally demonstrate that the Respondent’s industrial effluent and gaseous emissions have repeatedly violated the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) prescribed under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industries) Rules, 2001. The photographic and videographic evidence further corroborates the observations of the EPA inspection teams regarding the inadequacy of the ETP and visible emissions.
The Respondent’
s arguments regarding sampling methodology, while noted, are not persuasive enough to discredit the comprehensive evidence presented by the Complainant. The laboratory reports were generated by accredited facilities, adhering to established protocols, and were accompanied by proper chain of custody documentation. The alleged minor exceedances, when viewed in the aggregate and across multiple parameters and dates, reveal a systemic failure to consistently meet environmental standards, rather than isolated or negligible

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,000,000/- (Rupees Two Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 400,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
