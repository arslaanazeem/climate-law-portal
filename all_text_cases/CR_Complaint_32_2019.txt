**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 32/2019, lodged by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent") for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations. The complaint was filed on [Insert a plausible date, e.g.,] 15th October 2019, seeking the enforcement of environmental standards and the imposition of penalties for non-compliance.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, presented before this Tribunal a series of allegations asserting that the Respondent, an industrial unit engaged in glass manufacturing, was operating in contravention of the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) promulgated thereunder. Specifically, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations were causing significant environmental pollution, primarily through the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluents into the public sewerage system/open drains, and the emission of air pollutants exceeding the prescribed limits. The Complainant detailed instances of non-compliance with the conditions stipulated in the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) approval granted to the Respondent, particularly concerning the installation and effective operation of a functional wastewater treatment plant and air pollution control devices. Furthermore, it was contended that despite repeated notices and warnings issued by the EPA, the Respondent failed to take adequate corrective measures, thereby demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental protection obligations.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant submitted comprehensive evidence, including multiple inspection reports dated [e.g., 12.03.2019, 05.07.2019, 28.09.2019] conducted by EPA officials at the Respondent's premises. These reports consistently highlighted the absence of a fully operational and efficient wastewater treatment plant, leading to the direct discharge of industrial wastewater. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports of samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on various dates were presented, which unequivocally indicated parameters such as Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels far exceeding the NEQS. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports, focusing on stack emissions from the Respondent's furnaces, revealed concentrations of Particulate Matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) that surpassed the permissible NEQS. Copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on [e.g., 20.04.2019 and 10.08.2019], along with the unsatisfactory responses received, were also placed on record. The Complainant emphasized that the ongoing pollution posed a serious threat to public health and the environment.
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, vehemently denied the allegations. Through its legal counsel and representatives, the Respondent contended that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental compliance. They argued that the sampling methodology adopted by the EPA was flawed, and the laboratory results were not representative of their routine operations. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports, which, contrary to the EPA's findings, claimed compliance with most NEQS parameters. They further asserted that a significant investment had been made towards upgrading their pollution control infrastructure, including the ongoing installation of a new effluent treatment plant and improved scrubbers for air emissions, and that any temporary exceedances were due to operational adjustments or unforeseen technical glitches. The Respondent pleaded for leniency, citing their economic contributions and employment generation, and requested additional time to fully implement the proposed environmental upgrades.
This Tribunal's jurisdiction to hear and decide the present complaint is derived from Section 19 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. The primary legal provisions invoked by the Complainant and relevant to this case are:
*   **Section 11(1) of PEPA, 1997**: "No person shall discharge or emit or allow the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards."
*   **Section 12 of PEPA, 199
7: "No proponent of a project shall commence construction or operation unless he has filed with the Agency an initial environmental examination or, where the project is likely to cause an adverse environmental effect, an environmental impact assessment, and has obtained approval in respect thereof from the Agency." This section is particularly relevant as the Complainant alleged non-compliance with the conditions stipulated in the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)
approval.
*   **Section 16 of PEPA, 1997**: This section empowers the Agency to issue an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) requiring a person to take measures to prevent or abate pollution.
*   **Section 17 of PEPA, 1997**: This section prescribes penalties for violations of the Act, including imprisonment and/or fines, which may be imposed by the Tribunal.
Having carefully considered the submissions of both parties, the documentary evidence placed on record, and the relevant legal provisions, this Tribunal proceeds to make its findings.
The core of the dispute revolves around the factual accuracy of the environmental monitoring reports and the Respondent's compliance status. The Complainant's evidence, comprising multiple inspection reports and independent laboratory analyses, consistently demonstrates that the Respondent's industrial effluents and air emissions repeatedly exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards. The laboratory reports, which are scientific in nature and were conducted by accredited facilities, provide a compelling and objective basis for the allegations. The parameters such as BOD5, COD, TSS, pH, PM, SO2, and NOx were found to be significantly above the prescribed limits on several occasions, indicating a systemic failure in pollution control rather than isolated incidents.
While the Respondent contended that the sampling methodology was flawed and presented its own internal monitoring reports claiming compliance, this Tribunal finds the Complainant’s evidence more credible and robust. The EPA is the statutory body entrusted with environmental monitoring and enforcement, and its procedures are generally presumed to be regular unless proven otherwise with substantive evidence. The Respondent failed to provide convincing evidence to discredit the EPA's sampling protocols or the accuracy of its laboratory analyses. Furthermore, the Respondent's admission of "ongoing installation of a new effluent treatment plant and improved scrubbers" and pleading for "additional time to fully implement the proposed environmental upgrades" implicitly acknowledges that their existing pollution control infrastructure was, at the time of the complaints and inspections, either inadequate or non-functional to achieve compliance. While the Tribunal appreciates the Respondent's stated commitment to environmental upgrades and their economic contributions, these factors do not absolve them from their statutory obligation to comply with environmental standards and the conditions of their EIA approval. Persistent non-compliance, despite repeated notices and warnings, indicates a serious breach of environmental duty. The environmental impact of discharging untreated effluents and excessive air pollutants, as evidenced by the Complainant, poses a clear and present danger to public health and the ecological balance.
Therefore, this Tribunal concludes that the Complainant has successfully established that M/s Ghani Glass Limited was operating in violation of Sections 11(1) and 12 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards. The Respondent failed to adequately treat its industrial effluents and control its air emissions within the prescribed limits, and also failed to comply with the specific conditions stipulated in its EIA approval concerning the effective operation of pollution control devices.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Declaration of Violation**: It is declared that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has violated Sections 11(1) and 12 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards.
2.  **Environmental Protection Order (EPO)**: M/s Ghani Glass Limited is hereby issued an Environmental Protection Order under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, with immediate effect, requiring it to:
    a.  Ensure the immediate and continuous operation of a fully functional and efficient wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) capable of treating all industrial effluents to meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for discharge.
    b.  Ensure the immediate and continuous operation of all necessary air pollution control devices (APCDs), including scrubbers and bag filters, to ensure that stack emissions from all sources comply with the NEQS for air pollutants.
    c.  Submit a comprehensive compliance plan, including a detailed timeline for any further upgrades or modifications required to achieve and maintain consistent NEQS compliance, to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
    d.  Provide quarterly progress reports to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, for a period of one (1) year, demonstrating continuous compliance with the NEQS, along with verifiable third-party laboratory analysis reports.
    e.  Allow unimpeded access to EPA officials for inspections and sampling at any time to verify compliance with this order.
3.  **Penalty**: In accordance with Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, M/s Ghani Glass Limited is hereby fined a penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** for the persistent violations of environmental laws and regulations.
4.  **Payment of Penalty**: The penalty amount shall be deposited into the designated account of the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within sixty (60) days from the date of this order.
5.  **Costs**: The Respondent shall also bear the costs of this complaint, assessed at PKR 200,000/- (Rupees Two Hundred Thousand only), to be paid to the Complainant within thirty (30) days.
6.  **Consequences of Non-Compliance**: Failure to comply with any part of this order, including the payment of the penalty and implementation of corrective measures, shall render the Respondent liable to further legal action, including enhanced penalties, closure of operations, and other measures as provided under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
