This complaint, bearing No. 51/2016, was lodged by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent textile manufacturing unit located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The Complainant sought the intervention of this Tribunal under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleging various environmental violations and seeking appropriate penalties and remedial directions against the Respondent.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolved around the Respondent's alleged failure to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents and gaseous emissions. It was contended that M/s Nishat Mills Limited, being a large-scale textile dyeing and finishing facility, was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial wastewater directly into the public sewerage system and ultimately into surface water bodies, thereby causing significant water pollution. Specifically, the complaint detailed that routine inspections conducted by EPA officials on multiple occasions, starting from late 2015 and continuing through 2016, revealed consistent exceedance of NEQS parameters such as Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels in the effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points. Furthermore, the Complainant alleged that the Respondent's air emission sources, particularly its boiler and generator exhausts, were not equipped with adequate air pollution control devices (APCDs) or were operating inefficiently, leading to the emission of particulate matter, Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) above the prescribed NEQS. The complaint also highlighted the Respondent's failure to obtain the requisite Environmental Approval (NOC) for its operations or to submit regular self-monitoring reports as mandated by law. It was asserted that despite repeated notices, warnings, and opportunities for compliance provided by the EPA, the Respondent had failed to take effective measures to mitigate the environmental damage, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental laws and regulations.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple environmental inspection reports dated 15.12.2015, 28.03.2016, and 12.07.2016, prepared by EPA field teams, detailing the observations of non-compliance. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, corresponding to the effluent and air emission samples collected during these inspections, were submitted. These reports consistently indicated that the measured parameters significantly exceeded the NEQS. Photographs and videographic evidence depicting the colour and nature of the discharged effluent, along with the visible emissions from stacks, were also presented. Official correspondence, including show-cause notices issued under Section 16(1) of PEPA, 1997, dated 20.01.2016 and 05.05.2016
, and the Respondent's replies thereto, were also placed on record.
In response to the complaint and the comprehensive evidence presented by the EPA, the Respondent, through its learned counsel, vehemently contested the allegations. The Respondent primarily argued that it had invested substantially in establishing and maintaining an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and various Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) to manage its industrial discharges and emissions. It was asserted that their operations were generally compliant with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and that any reported exceedances were either isolated incidents, attributable to sampling methodology discrepancies, or minor deviations that were promptly addressed. The Respondent also submitted that they were actively engaged in a continuous improvement process for their environmental infrastructure and had initiated steps to apply for or update the necessary environmental approvals, including the NOC, and to streamline their self-monitoring reporting mechanisms. They emphasized their corporate responsibility and their significant contribution to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to adopt a pragmatic and lenient approach.
The Tribunal meticulously examined the voluminous record, including the detailed inspection reports, the laboratory analysis reports, photographic and videographic evidence, and the extensive correspondence exchanged between the parties. Arguments from both the learned counsel for the Complainant and the Respondent were heard at length, and due consideration was given to all submissions.
Upon careful consideration, the Tribunal found the evidence presented by the Complainant to be overwhelmingly convincing and largely unrebutted by substantive counter-evidence from the Respondent. The consistent findings of exceedance of NEQS parameters in multiple effluent and air emission samples, collected on different dates and corroborated by detailed inspection reports, unequivocally established the Respondent's failure to comply with environmental standards.
The Respondent's arguments regarding substantial investments in environmental infrastructure, while noted, failed to adequately explain or justify the consistent and documented breaches of NEQS. The purpose of such infrastructure is to *achieve* compliance, not merely to exist. The laboratory reports, being from an accredited EPA laboratory, carried significant probative value and were not effectively challenged on their scientific methodology or accuracy. Furthermore, the contention of isolated incidents or sampling discrepancies was belied by the pattern of non-compliance observed across multiple inspections over several months. The Tribunal notes that while the Respondent expressed an intent to apply for necessary environmental approvals and streamline reporting, the fact remains that these were either absent or deficient at the time the complaint was lodged and during the period of alleged violations. Corporate responsibility and economic contribution, while important facets of a business, cannot be invoked as an exemption from statutory environmental obligations, especially when the potential for harm to public health and the environment is evident.
The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, particularly Sections 11, 12, 13, 16, and 17, places a clear obligation on industrial units to prevent or control pollution and to comply with the NEQS. Section 16 prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in excess of the NEQS. The persistent nature of the violations, despite repeated warnings and notices from the EPA, demonstrates a serious lapse on the part of the Respondent in fulfilling its environmental duties. This Tribunal is mandated not only to penalize violations but also to ensure future compliance and deter similar conduct by others. The evidence clearly establishes that M/s Nishat Mills Limited has contravened the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and the rules and regulations framed thereunder, causing environmental degradation.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the foregoing findings, and exercising the powers vested in this Tribunal under Section 19(1) read with Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, it is hereby ordered as follows:
1.  **Declaration of Guilt:** M/s Nishat Mills Limited (the Respondent) is hereby found guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents and gaseous emissions, and for operating without the requisite environmental approvals/NOCs, in contravention of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,000,000/- (Rupees Two Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 400,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **15 February, 2023**.
