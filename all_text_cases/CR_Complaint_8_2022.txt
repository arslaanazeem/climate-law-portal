This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 8/2022, instituted by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent industrial entity engaged in glass manufacturing. The complaint seeks redress for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of industrial effluents and emission of air pollutants exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS), thereby posing a significant threat to public health and the environment. The Tribunal, presided
by Mr. Justice (R) Ali Baqar Najafi, meticulously reviewed the submissions, evidence, and arguments presented by both parties over a series of hearings conducted between March 2022 and October 2023.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, presented detailed inspection reports, laboratory analyses of effluent samples, and air quality monitoring data. These reports, prepared by accredited laboratories, consistently indicated that the Respondent's industrial discharges and emissions exceeded the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for various parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and particulate matter (PM2.5/PM10) in stack emissions. The EPA contended that despite multiple notices and warnings issued to the Respondent regarding these violations, satisfactory and sustained compliance had not been achieved, thereby necessitating the present action under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997).
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, represented by its legal counsel, acknowledged the scale of its operations and the inherent challenges in managing industrial waste. They argued that significant investments had been made in installing and upgrading pollution control technologies, including an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air scrubbers. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring data and expert opinions, contending that compliance was an ongoing process, and any recorded exceedances were either temporary, attributable to maintenance issues, or within acceptable margins of error, or that the EPA's sampling methodology was at times flawed. They further highlighted their economic contribution to the region and their commitment to corporate social responsibility.
The primary issues for determination by this Tribunal were: (i) whether the Respondent had violated the provisions of the PEPA, 1997, and the NEQS; (ii) the extent and gravity of such violations; and (iii) the appropriate measures and penalties to be imposed in light of the established facts.
Upon careful consideration of all documentary evidence, including the inspection reports, laboratory analyses, expert testimonies, and legal arguments advanced by both sides, the Tribunal finds that the evidence presented by the Complainant overwhelmingly demonstrates a pattern of non-compliance with the NEQS. The laboratory reports, duly authenticated and cross-verified, consistently indicate that the Respondent’s industrial effluents and air emissions have, on multiple occasions, exceeded the permissible limits. While the Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent’s investments in pollution control infrastructure, the evidence suggests that the operational efficiency, maintenance, and overall effectiveness of these systems have been inadequate to ensure sustained adherence to environmental standards. The repeated nature of the violations, despite prior notices and opportunities for rectification, underscores a failure to implement a robust and effective environmental management system
system. The Respondent's arguments regarding temporary exceedances or alleged flaws in EPA's sampling methodology, while noted, do not sufficiently explain or justify the consistent pattern of violations across multiple parameters and sampling dates. The onus was on the Respondent to demonstrate sustained compliance, which it has failed to do. Therefore, the Tribunal concludes that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has indeed violated the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards.
The Tribunal further notes that the repeated nature of these violations, despite prior warnings and opportunities for corrective action, indicates a serious and persistent disregard for environmental regulations. Such conduct poses an unacceptable risk to public health and the environment, undermining the very purpose of environmental protection laws.
Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, empowers the Tribunal to impose penalties, including fines, and direct the offender to take such measures as may be necessary to prevent or abate pollution. Given the gravity and persistent nature of the violations, and the potential harm posed to public health and the environment, the Tribunal deems it necessary to impose a penalty and issue specific directions for immediate and sustained compliance.
**NOW, THEREFORE, IT IS HEREBY ORDERED AS FOLLOWS:**
1.  The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, is found liable for violating the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, as established above.
2.  The Respondent is directed to immediately undertake a comprehensive audit and upgrade of its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air emission control systems (including air scrubbers and particulate matter control devices) to ensure sustained compliance with all prescribed NEQS. A detailed action plan for these upgrades, including specific timelines for implementation, shall be submitted to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this judgment for approval.
3.  The Respondent shall implement a robust internal environmental monitoring system, including real-time monitoring capabilities for critical parameters where feasible, and submit monthly compliance reports, duly verified by an independent accredited laboratory, to the EPA for a period of **two (2) years** from the date of this judgment.
4.  The Respondent is hereby imposed a penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** for the violations established. This amount shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within **sixty (60) days** from the date of this judgment.
5.  Failure to comply with any of the directions contained in paragraphs 2 and 3 of this Order, or any recurrence of NEQS violations after the implementation of the directed upgrades, shall render the Respondent liable to a further daily penalty of **PKR 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand only)** for each day of non-compliance or continued violation, in addition to any other legal action that the EPA may initiate under the PEPA, 1997.
6.  The EPA, Lahore, shall conduct unannounced inspections and monitoring of the Respondent's premises at regular intervals to ensure strict adherence to this judgment and the NEQS.
7.  The complaint is accordingly disposed of. There shall be no order as to costs.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,300,000/- (Rupees One Million Three Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 260,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
