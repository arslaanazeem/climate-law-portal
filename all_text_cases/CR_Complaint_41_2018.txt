This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), alleging contraventions of various provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The complaint, bearing No. 41/2018, was initiated following multiple inspections and monitoring activities conducted by the EPA at the Respondent's industrial facility located in Lahore.
The Complainant's primary allegations against M/s Packages Limited are multifaceted. Firstly, it is contended that the Respondent has been discharging industrial effluent into the sewerage system/open drain without adequate treatment, resulting in the exceedance of the prescribed NEQS for various parameters, including but not limited to Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. Specific dates of sampling and analysis reports, annexed with the complaint, indicate persistent non-compliance. Secondly, the Complainant alleges that the Respondent failed to effectively operate and maintain its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP), leading to the aforementioned violations. Thirdly, it is asserted that the Respondent did not consistently provide self-monitoring reports as required under environmental regulations, thereby obstructing the EPA's oversight functions. Fourthly, it was alleged that despite repeated warnings and notices issued by the EPA, the Respondent failed to take substantive and timely corrective measures to achieve compliance, demonstrating a disregard for environmental protection laws.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of documents and testimonies. Key evidence included inspection reports dated 15.03.2018, 22.05.2018, and 01.08.2018, detailing observations of effluent discharge and the state of the ETP. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's certified laboratory, corresponding to samples collected on 22.05.2018 and 01.08.2018, were submitted. These reports consistently showed concentrations of pollutants significantly exceeding the NEQS. For instance, the COD levels were reported at 450 mg/L and 520 mg/L, against the NEQS limit of 150 mg/L, and BOD5 levels at 280 mg/L and 310 mg/L, against the NEQS limit of 80 mg/L. Copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 05.06.2018 and 15.08.2018, along with their unsatisfactory replies, were also placed on record. The Assistant Director, EPA, also appeared as a witness, reiterating the findings of the inspection teams and the continuous nature of the violations.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel, denied the allegations. They contended that their ETP was operational and they were making continuous efforts to comply with environmental standards. The Respondent submitted that the samples taken by the EPA might not be representative, or that the analytical methods used could be subject to error. They also presented a plan for upgrading their ETP, arguing that the process was underway and required time for completion. While acknowledging some initial challenges, they maintained that they were committed to environmental stewardship and had invested substantial resources in their ETP. They also produced some internal monitoring reports, which, however, did not fully align with
the Complainant's reports.
The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties, including the detailed inspection reports, laboratory analysis results, show-cause notices, and the testimonies. It is an established principle that environmental compliance is not merely an aspiration but a legal obligation. The core of the Complainant's case rests upon scientific evidence from a certified laboratory, which consistently recorded pollutant
concentrations far exceeding the permissible NEQS limits. The Respondent’s assertion that samples might not be representative or analytical methods erroneous is not supported by any credible counter-evidence. The EPA’s laboratory is a certified facility, and its reports carry significant weight, especially when consistently showing violations over several months. Furthermore, while the Tribunal commends the Respondent’s stated commitment to environmental stewardship and their plan for upgrading the ETP, such plans do not absolve them of liability for past and ongoing non-compliance. The law requires immediate and continuous adherence to environmental standards, not merely an intention to comply at some future date. The fact that repeated show-cause notices and warnings were issued, yet the violations persisted, indicates a serious lapse in the Respondent's environmental management system and a failure to take timely corrective actions. The internal monitoring reports submitted by the Respondent, which contradict the EPA’s findings, lack independent verification and cannot override the robust evidence presented by the Complainant.
The Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established, beyond reasonable doubt, that M/s Packages Limited has been discharging industrial effluent in contravention of Section 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and has consistently failed to meet the National Environmental Quality Standards, thereby violating the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and its allied rules and regulations. The persistent nature of these violations, despite repeated notices and opportunities to rectify, demonstrates a clear breach of environmental obligations.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,300,000/- (Rupees One Million Three Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 260,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
