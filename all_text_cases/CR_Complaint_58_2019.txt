This judgment addresses the environmental complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent manufacturer of glass products, located at 23-KM, Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The complaint, registered as No. 58/2019, was initiated following multiple inspections and monitoring activities carried out by the EPA, which allegedly revealed significant environmental non-compliance by the Respondent with the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act") and its associated regulations. The Tribunal has carefully considered the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's failure to adequately manage and treat its industrial emissions and effluents, leading to a contravention of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Specifically, the EPA alleged that the Respondent's industrial processes, particularly the glass manufacturing operations, were discharging untreated or inadequately treated gaseous emissions into the ambient air and industrial wastewater into the public drainage system and ultimately into agricultural fields, thereby causing air and water pollution. The Complainant asserted that regular monitoring conducted by EPA teams consistently showed exceedances of NEQS for various parameters in both air emissions (such as Particulate Matter (PM), Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), and Sulphur Oxides (SOx)) and wastewater effluents (including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH). Furthermore, the Complainant contended that despite repeated issuance of show-cause notices, environmental protection orders, and opportunities for remedial action, the Respondent failed to implement effective pollution control measures, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental regulations and public health. The Complainant sought a declaration of the Respondent's non-compliance, imposition of penalties, and a directive for immediate remedial actions.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated between January 2019 and June 2021, detailing observations of black smoke discharge from chimneys, foul-smelling effluent discharge, and general poor housekeeping practices at the Respondent's facility. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of samples collected from the Respondent's air emission stacks and wastewater discharge points on various dates (e.g., March 12, 2019; August 28, 2019; April 05, 2020; January 15, 2021). These reports, prepared by accredited EPA laboratories, unequivocally showed pollutant concentrations significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS limits. For instance, PM levels were found to be up to 800 mg/Nm3 against a standard of 150 mg/Nm3, and COD levels in wastewater were recorded as high as 650 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L. The Complainant also produced copies of show-cause notices issued under Section 16 of the Act, dated April 25, 2019, and September 10, 2020, and subsequent Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) issued on November 15, 2019, and March 20, 2021, which directed the Respondent to cease discharge of untreated pollutants and install/upgrade pollution control devices. The Respondent, in its defense
, submitted that it had undertaken several measures to improve its environmental performance. The Respondent denied the persistent nature of the alleged non-compliance, arguing that while some exceedances might have occurred due to operational anomalies or during maintenance periods, the company was generally committed to environmental stewardship. It further contended that it had already initiated the process of upgrading its existing Bag Filter system for air emissions and was in the advanced stages of
installing a new Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) to address wastewater issues. The Respondent provided purchase orders and installation contracts as proof of these initiatives, asserting that these were significant capital investments demonstrating their commitment to environmental responsibility. They argued that environmental compliance is an ongoing process and that minor deviations, particularly during periods of equipment upgrade or unforeseen operational challenges, should not be construed as deliberate defiance. Furthermore, the Respondent questioned the methodology and calibration of some of the EPA's monitoring equipment, suggesting potential inaccuracies in the reported pollutant levels. They requested additional time to complete the ongoing upgrades and demonstrate full compliance, emphasizing their contribution to the national economy and employment.
The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the evidence and arguments presented by both the Complainant and the Respondent. While acknowledging the Respondent's claims regarding ongoing efforts to upgrade its pollution control infrastructure, the overwhelming evidence presented by the Complainant, particularly the consistent and numerous laboratory analysis reports from accredited EPA laboratories, unequivocally demonstrates a prolonged and significant pattern of non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards. The repeated exceedances of NEQS for Particulate Matter, NOx, SOx in air emissions, and COD, BOD, TSS, and pH in wastewater effluents, as documented over several years, cannot be dismissed as mere "operational anomalies" or minor deviations. The pollutant levels recorded were often several times higher than the prescribed limits, indicating a fundamental failure in the Respondent's existing pollution control systems.
The Tribunal notes that the EPA provided ample opportunities for the Respondent to rectify the situation through multiple show-cause notices and Environmental Protection Orders. Despite these directives, the Respondent's actions, while perhaps indicative of a future intent to comply, did not result in actual compliance within the stipulated periods, leading to persistent environmental degradation. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal has carefully examined the inspection report, laboratory analysis results, and the photographic evidence produced by the Complainant. The measured pollutant concentrations significantly exceed the NEQS limits and the Respondent has failed to rebut this evidence with credible contrary data. The defense regarding ETP maintenance does not excuse ongoing pollution discharge and cannot serve as a justification for non-compliance with mandatory environmental standards.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
