**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 46/2021, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), invoking the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"). The complaint seeks remedial action and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, an industrial entity involved in the manufacturing of packaging materials, paper, and board, was operating its facility located in Lahore in persistent violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluent discharge and air emissions. Specifically, it was contended that the Respondent's effluent treatment plant (ETP) was either non-functional, inadequate, or improperly maintained, leading to the discharge of untreated or partially treated wastewater into the sewerage system/drain, exceeding the prescribed NEQS for various parameters including but not limited to BOD5, COD, TSS, and pH. Furthermore, it was alleged that the Respondent's industrial processes, particularly its boiler operations and other combustion sources, were emitting air pollutants (such as particulate matter, SOx, and NOx) in concentrations higher than the permissible limits set by the NEQS, without adequate air pollution control devices (APCDs) or proper operational protocols. The Complainant further asserted that despite multiple inspections, issuance of show-cause notices, and directives for compliance, the Respondent failed to take effective remedial measures, thereby causing environmental degradation and posing a threat to public health and the ecosystem.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports, dating from May 2020 to March 2021, conducted by EPA officials. These reports detailed observations of the Respondent's operational practices, including the visible condition of the ETP and stacks. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on three separate occasions (June 15, 2020; October 28, 2020; and February 10, 2021) from the Respondent's discharge points. These reports, prepared by an accredited laboratory, consistently indicated that parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and oil & grease significantly exceeded the NEQS limits. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports, based on stack emissions sampling conducted on November 05, 2020, and March 02, 2021, were presented, showing exceedances for particulate matter (PM) and sulfur dioxide (SO2) from the Respondent's boiler stacks. Copies of show-cause notices issued on July 01, 2020, and December 15, 2020, and a final compliance directive dated April 05, 2021, were also placed on record, demonstrating the EPA's efforts to secure compliance prior to filing the complaint. The Complainant also produced the testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, Environmental Inspector, who elaborated on the inspection findings and sampling procedures.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel and environmental manager, denied the allegations of persistent non-compliance. The Respondent contended that it is a responsible corporate entity with a long-standing commitment to environmental stewardship and has invested significantly in environmental infrastructure, including a functional ETP and APCDs. They argued that any reported exceedances were either isolated incidents, attributable to temporary operational upsets, or resulted from flawed sampling methodologies employed by the Complainant. The Respondent submitted its own internal monitoring reports, which, for most periods, showed compliance with NEQS. They also presented evidence of recent upgrades to their ETP and installation of bag filters on their boiler stacks, along with a schedule for further improvements. An Environmental Management Plan (EMP) document, outlining their commitment to achieving and maintaining NEQS, was also submitted. The Respondent further argued that the EPA's sampling points might not have been representative of the final treated discharge and that the laboratory analysis reports lacked proper chain of custody documentation for some samples. Mr. Zeeshan Ali, the Respondent's Environmental Manager, testified, detailing the operational parameters of their environmental control systems and the
challenges faced in maintaining consistent compliance due to the scale and complexity of their manufacturing processes. He further stated that the Respondent had initiated a comprehensive upgrade program for its ETP in late 2020 and installed advanced particulate removal systems for its boilers by early 2021, demonstrating a proactive approach towards environmental responsibility. The Respondent also questioned the methodology of EPA's sampling, suggesting potential inconsistencies in
the collection and preservation techniques, which could have led to inaccurate results, thereby rendering the Complainant's evidence unreliable.
This Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both parties and meticulously reviewed the documentary and oral evidence placed on record. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,200,000/- (Rupees One Million Two Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 240,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
