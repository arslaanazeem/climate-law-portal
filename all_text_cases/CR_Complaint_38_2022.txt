This judgment addresses Complaint No. 38/2022, lodged by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent textile manufacturing entity located in Lahore. The complaint was filed under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"), alleging violations of environmental standards and regulations pertaining to industrial operations. This Tribunal, constituted under the Act, possesses the requisite jurisdiction to adjudicate upon matters concerning environmental pollution and non-compliance with environmental laws within its territorial and subject-matter purview.
The Complainant's primary allegations stem from an inspection conducted by EPA officials at the Respondent's manufacturing facility on 15th July 2022, following receipt of public grievances regarding foul smell and discharge of discolored water into the nearby drain. During the inspection, it was observed that the Respondent's industrial effluent treatment plant (ETP) was allegedly not operating at its optimal capacity, leading to the discharge of partially treated or untreated industrial wastewater into the public sewerage system, which eventually drains into a natural water body. Samples of the effluent were collected from the discharge point and subjected to laboratory analysis. The Complainant further alleged that the analysis reports indicated several parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH, to be in excess of the permissible limits stipulated by the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents, as enshrined in the Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2000. Additionally, the Complainant contended that the Respondent had failed to regularly submit self-monitoring reports as required by law, and had not taken adequate measures to abate the nuisance caused by the discharge, thereby violating Section 11 of the Act.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a detailed inspection report dated 18th July 2022, prepared by the inspection team, outlining the on-site observations and findings. Crucially, the analytical reports from the EPA-accredited laboratory, certified on 25th July 2022, were submitted, clearly indicating the exceedance of NEQS for the collected effluent samples. Specifically, the COD levels were found to be 350 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L, BOD levels at 180 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 80 mg/L, and TSS at 280 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 200 mg/L. Photographs depicting the discolored discharge were also tendered as evidence. Furthermore, an affidavit from Mr. Ahmed Khan, Assistant Director (Technical) of EPA, who was part of the inspection team, was presented, corroborating the findings and observations. The Complainant also highlighted the absence of recent self-monitoring reports from the Respondent's file, a statutory requirement for industries.
The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It was argued that the Respondent operates a state-of-the-art ETP, which is regularly maintained and upgraded. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports for the period preceding and following the EPA inspection, which purportedly showed compliance with NEQS. It was contended that the EPA's sampling methodology might have been flawed, or the samples could have been contaminated or improperly handled, leading to erroneous results. The Respondent also submitted proof of recent investments in ETP upgrades and presented a certificate from an independent environmental consultant attesting to the general operational efficiency of their ETP. While acknowledging a possible temporary operational glitch on the specific date of inspection due to an unforeseen power fluctuation or chemical imbalance, the Respondent argued that such an isolated incident should not lead to a finding of persistent non-compliance. They also submitted a few belated self-monitoring reports, explaining the delay as an administrative oversight.
The legal framework governing this matter is primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Section 11 of the Act prohibits any person from discharging or emitting any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount, concentration, or level which is in excess of the NEQS, or where applicable, the standards established by the Provincial Environmental Protection Agency. Section 17 prescribes penalties for violations of Section 11, stating that whoever contravenes the provisions of Section 11 shall be punishable with a fine which may extend to one million rupees, and in case of a continuing contravention, with a further fine which may extend to one hundred thousand rupees for every day during which such contravention continues. Furthermore, the Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2000, mandate industries to monitor their effluents and emissions and submit regular reports to the EPA, making non-submission a violation of regulatory requirements.
Upon careful consideration of the pleadings, documentary evidence, and arguments presented by both parties, this Tribunal finds the allegations of the Complainant largely substantiated. The core of the evidence lies in
the accredited laboratory analysis reports, which unequivocally demonstrate that the Respondent's industrial effluent, sampled on 15th July 2022, significantly exceeded the permissible limits set by the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for several critical parameters. The Complainant's detailed inspection report, corroborated by photographic evidence and the affidavit of Mr. Ahmed Khan, further reinforces the findings of environmental degradation and non-compliance.
While the Respondent contended that its ETP is generally state-of-the-art and presented internal monitoring reports, this Tribunal finds such reports less credible when directly contradicted by independent, EPA-accredited laboratory results. The Respondent's assertion of a "possible temporary operational glitch" on the date of inspection, coupled with an admission of "administrative oversight" for delayed self-monitoring reports, implicitly acknowledges a lapse in environmental stewardship, even if an attempt is made to downplay its severity or duration. Environmental compliance, particularly for large industrial entities, is a continuous responsibility, not merely a sporadic achievement. A temporary glitch leading to such severe NEQS exceedances points to either systemic issues in ETP maintenance, inadequate operational protocols, or a failure to implement robust contingency measures. The argument regarding flawed sampling methodology was not substantiated with concrete evidence that could discredit the EPA's established procedures. The failure to regularly submit self-monitoring reports, as mandated by the Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2000, constitutes a clear violation of regulatory requirements, hindering the EPA's ability to effectively oversee and ensure compliance.
Therefore, this Tribunal holds that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof, establishing that M/s Nishat Mills Limited was in contravention of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging effluent in excess of the NEQS, and also violated the Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2000, by failing to submit regular self-monitoring reports. The public grievances regarding foul smell and discolored water provide further contextual evidence of the adverse impact of the Respondent's operations on the local environment.
**ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Finding of Guilt:** The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, is found guilty of violating Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2000.
2.  **Penalty for NEQS Violation:** For the contravention of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the Respondent is hereby directed to pay a fine of **PKR 750,000/- (Rupees Seven Hundred Fifty Thousand Only)**.
3.  **Penalty for Non-Submission of Reports:** For the failure to regularly submit self-monitoring reports as required by the Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2000, the Respondent is hereby directed to pay a fine of **PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand Only)**.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,600,000/- (Rupees One Million Six Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 320,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
