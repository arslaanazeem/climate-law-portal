This matter came before the Punjab Environmental Tribunal, Lahore, pursuant to a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter "the Respondent"), a company engaged in the manufacturing of various chemical products. The complaint seeks punitive action and remedial measures under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter "the Act"), for alleged violations of environmental standards and regulations. The Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction to hear and decide complaints regarding environmental pollution and non-compliance with environmental laws under Section 21 of the Act.
It is alleged by the Complainant that the Respondent has been consistently operating in contravention of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and other provisions of the Act and its subordinate rules. Specifically, the complaint details several instances of non-compliance: (a) Discharge of industrial effluent containing pollutants such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals (e.g., mercury, lead) significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS limits, as evidenced by multiple sample analyses conducted by EPA-accredited laboratories. (b) Improper disposal of hazardous solid waste, including chemical sludge, in an uncontrolled manner within the factory premises and adjacent areas, posing a direct threat to soil and groundwater quality. (c) Failure to install and effectively operate a functional primary and secondary effluent treatment plant capable of achieving NEQS compliance. (d) Non-submission of mandatory self-monitoring reports and environmental audit statements as required under the Pakistan Environmental Protection (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2001. (e) Operating certain expanded units without obtaining the requisite Environmental Approval (NOC) under Section 12 of the Act, thereby proceeding without an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) or Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) where applicable. Show-cause notices were duly issued to the Respondent, followed by personal hearings, yet the alleged violations persisted.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented the following evidence:
1.  **Site Inspection Reports:** Multiple reports from EPA inspection teams dated 15.03.2021, 28.05.2021, and 12.08.2021, detailing observations of effluent discharge points, waste disposal practices, and the apparent inadequacy of the existing treatment facilities. These reports included photographic evidence.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Certified laboratory reports from the Punjab Environmental Quality Standards Laboratory, attached as Annexures 'A' to 'C' of the complaint, corresponding to effluent samples collected on the aforementioned inspection dates. These reports unequivocally indicated levels of COD, BOD, TSS, and specific heavy metals far exceeding the NEQS. For instance, the sample collected on 12.08.2021 showed COD at 450 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L, and mercury at 0.05 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 0.01 mg/L.
3.  **Correspondence Record:** Copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 20.04.2021 and 05.07.2021, along with the Respondent's replies, which, in the Complainant's view, failed to adequately address the non-compliance or demonstrate concrete steps towards rectification.
4.  **Affidavits:** Sworn affidavits from the EPA inspection officers attesting to the authenticity of the reports and the findings.
The Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its legal counsel, contested the allegations. The Respondent's defense primarily rested on the following points: (a) Assertions that the samples were not collected in strict adherence to established protocols, potentially leading to inaccurate results. (b) Claims of ongoing efforts to upgrade the effluent treatment plant, with significant capital investment already committed, and that the observed non-compliance was temporary during the upgrade phase. (c) Presentation of internal monitoring reports, which, contradictorily, sometimes showed compliance or marginally exceeded limits, though these were not from EPA-accredited laboratories. (d) Arguments that the hazardous waste disposal was being managed by a third-party contractor, absolving the Respondent of direct liability for potential malpractices by the contractor. (e) Denial of operating any
expanded units without the requisite Environmental Approval (NOC), asserting that all operations were either within approved parameters or temporary in nature pending formalization.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both parties and meticulously reviewed the evidence presented.
**Tribunal's Findings:**
The Complainant, through the production of multiple site inspection reports, certified laboratory analysis reports from an accredited laboratory, and official correspondence, has established a compelling case of persistent environmental non-compliance by the Respondent. The laboratory reports, in particular, provide objective and scientific evidence of effluent discharge exceeding NEQS limits for critical parameters such as COD, BOD, TSS, and heavy metals. The consistency of these findings across several sampling dates strengthens the Complainant's assertions. The photographic evidence further corroborates the observations regarding improper waste disposal and inadequate treatment facilities.
The Respondent's defense, while acknowledging some operational challenges, largely failed to rebut the substantive evidence presented by the EPA.
(a) The argument regarding alleged sampling protocol deviations was unsubstantiated. The Complainant's witnesses, through their affidavits, confirmed adherence to standard sampling procedures, and the Respondent did not present any credible evidence to prove otherwise. Moreover, the significant exceedances reported suggest a systemic issue rather than a mere sampling anomaly.
(b) While the Tribunal appreciates efforts towards upgrading the effluent treatment plant, such ongoing efforts do not absolve the Respondent of liability for past or current non-compliance. Environmental protection is a continuous obligation, and a functional, NEQS-compliant treatment facility should be operational at all times. The upgrade process, if it impacts compliance, should have been managed with interim measures or a temporary cessation of operations until full compliance could be assured.
(c) The internal monitoring reports submitted by the Respondent, not being from EPA-accredited laboratories, carry less probative value compared to the certified reports from the Punjab Environmental Quality Standards Laboratory. Furthermore, even some of these internal reports occasionally indicated marginal exceedances, implicitly supporting the Complainant's overall contention.
(d) The responsibility for the proper management and disposal of hazardous waste ultimately rests with the generator of the waste, in this case, the Respondent. Delegating this task to a third-party contractor does not absolve the Respondent of its legal obligations under the Act to ensure environmentally sound disposal. The evidence of uncontrolled waste disposal within the premises and adjacent areas points to a failure in oversight and management.
(e) The denial of operating expanded units without NOC was contradicted by the EPA's inspection findings, which indicated operational activities in such units. The requirement for an Environmental Approval (NOC) under Section 12 of the Act is mandatory for new projects or expansions and is a fundamental aspect of preventive environmental management.
Based on the totality of the evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully proven that M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited has been operating in consistent violation of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and its subordinate rules and regulations, including the National Environmental Quality Standards. The violations are serious and pose a significant threat to public health and the environment.
**ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Penalty:** M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited is hereby directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** for the proven violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards. This penalty shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
2.  **Cessation of Non-Compliant Discharge:** The Respondent shall immediately cease the discharge of any industrial effluent that does not meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
3.  **Effluent Treatment Plant Upgrade and Operation:** The Respondent shall, within **ninety (90) days** from the date of this order, complete the upgrade, installation, and ensure the effective and continuous operation of a primary and secondary effluent treatment plant fully capable of treating all industrial effluent to achieve consistent compliance with the NEQS. The Respondent shall submit weekly compliance reports, including certified laboratory analysis results from an EPA-accredited laboratory, to the EPA for a period of six months post-commissioning.
4.  **Hazardous Waste Management:** The Respondent shall, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this order, develop and implement a comprehensive Hazardous Waste Management Plan, approved by the EPA, for the proper collection, storage, treatment, and disposal of all hazardous solid waste in an environmentally sound manner, in strict accordance with the Pakistan Environmental Protection (Hazardous Substances) Rules, 2003, and other relevant regulations. All existing improperly disposed waste shall be remediated and disposed of properly within sixty (60) days.
5.  **Environmental Approvals (NOCs):** The Respondent shall, within **sixty (60) days** from the date of this order, apply for and obtain all necessary Environmental Approvals (NOCs) under Section 12 of the Act for any expanded or unapproved units currently in operation. Operations of any unapproved units shall be suspended until such NOCs are obtained, unless an interim arrangement is approved by the EPA.
6.  **Reporting Compliance:** The Respondent shall strictly adhere to the requirements of the Pakistan Environmental Protection (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2001, and regularly submit all mandatory self-monitoring reports and environmental audit statements to the EPA.
7.  **Monitoring and Inspection:** The EPA shall conduct regular monitoring and inspections of the Respondent's premises to ensure strict compliance with this order and the provisions of the Act.
8.  **Consequences of Non-Compliance:** Failure to comply with any part of this order may result in further punitive action, including but not limited to, additional fines, closure of operations, or other measures deemed appropriate under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
This order is issued under Sections 16, 17, 20, and 21 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,600,000/- (Rupees One Million Six Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 320,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
