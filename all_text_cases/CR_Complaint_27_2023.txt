**Presided by:Justice (R) Chaudhry Muhammad Iqbal
This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), an industrial unit engaged in chemical manufacturing. The complaint, registered on 15th May 2023, alleges multiple violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter "NEQS"), posing a significant threat to public health and the environment within the jurisdiction of Lahore.
The Complainant's case rests on several grave allegations. Firstly, it is contended that the Respondent has been operating its chemical manufacturing facility without obtaining the mandatory Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) or Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) approval as required under Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, thereby commencing operations without a proper environmental assessment. Secondly, the Respondent is accused of discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into a nearby natural water channel, and subsequently into agricultural land, resulting in severe water and soil pollution. Laboratory analyses conducted by the EPA, attached with the complaint, purportedly demonstrate that the discharged effluent consistently exceeds the prescribed NEQS for various parameters, including Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals such as Mercury and Chromium. Thirdly, the complaint highlights significant air pollution caused by the Respondent's industrial processes, alleging emissions of particulate matter, Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) that far exceed the NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions, impacting ambient air quality and the health of local communities. Furthermore, the Complainant alleges improper management and disposal of hazardous waste generated by the Respondent's operations, in contravention of the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003. Lastly, it is asserted that despite repeated inspections, issuance of show-cause notices, and Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, the Respondent has failed to take effective remedial measures or demonstrate any tangible commitment towards environmental compliance, exhibiting a persistent disregard for environmental laws and regulatory directives.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant submitted a comprehensive set of documentary and testimonial evidence. This included detailed inspection reports dated 10th February 2023, 15th March 2023, and 20th April 2023, prepared by EPA field teams, which documented observations of direct effluent discharge, visible smoke emissions, and inadequate waste storage facilities. Crucially,
laboratory reports, duly signed by certified analysts, confirming the presence of pollutants in the discharged effluent and air emissions far exceeding the NEQS. Furthermore, copies of the show-cause notices and EPOs, along with proof of their service upon the Respondent, were submitted, demonstrating the persistent failure of the Respondent to comply with regulatory directives. Testimonial evidence included statements from EPA officials involved in the inspections, corroborating the findings and detailing the Respondent's non-cooperation.
The Respondent, represented by its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. In its written reply and oral submissions, the Respondent contended that it operates a modern chemical plant equipped with an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices, claiming to be in compliance with environmental standards. It was argued that the EPA's reports were either flawed, based on unrepresentative samples, or misinterpreted. The Respondent further asserted that it had applied for the necessary environmental approvals and was in the process of upgrading its facilities to meet any perceived deficiencies. Regarding hazardous waste, the Respondent claimed to have an agreement with a licensed waste management company for its disposal. However, when pressed for documentary evidence to substantiate these claims, such as valid IEE/EIA approval certificates, operational permits, recent ETP performance records, air emission monitoring reports from accredited third parties, or detailed hazardous waste manifests and agreements, the Respondent largely failed to produce convincing or complete documentation. The few documents submitted were either outdated, incomplete, or lacked independent verification.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties. It found the Complainant's evidence, particularly the detailed inspection reports, the laboratory analyses from certified sources, and the consistent record of show-cause notices and EPOs, to be robust, consistent, and highly credible. The repeated findings of NEQS exceedances across multiple parameters and media (water, air, waste) left little room for doubt regarding the Respondent's non-compliance. The Tribunal noted that the Respondent's claims of operating an ETP and air pollution control devices, while verbally asserted, were not adequately supported by verifiable, up-to-date performance data or independent audits. The absence of a valid IEE/EIA approval, a fundamental requirement under Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, was a particularly grave finding, indicating that the Respondent had commenced or continued operations without the requisite environmental clearance. The alleged application for approval, without actual grant, does not absolve the Respondent of its legal obligations. Furthermore, the Respondent's failure to provide satisfactory documentation regarding its hazardous waste management plan and actual disposal records further weakened its defense. The Tribunal concluded that the Respondent had indeed exhibited a persistent and deliberate disregard for environmental laws and regulatory directives, causing significant environmental degradation and posing risks to public health.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the overwhelming evidence and the findings of persistent non-compliance, this Tribunal hereby finds M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, the Respondent, guilty of violating multiple provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards.
Therefore, it is ordered as follows:
1.  **Cessation of Non-Compliant Operations:** The Respondent is immediately directed to cease all operations that involve the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated effluent, uncontrolled air emissions exceeding NEQS, and improper disposal of hazardous waste.
2.  **Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) & Approval:** The Respondent shall forthwith initiate and complete the process for obtaining a comprehensive Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) and secure the necessary environmental approval under Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, within a strict period of **ninety (90) days** from the date of this order. Operations without this approval shall remain suspended.
3.  **Upgradation and Installation of Pollution Control Systems:** The Respondent shall, within **one hundred and twenty (120) days** from the date of this order, upgrade its existing Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and install/upgrade appropriate Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) to ensure that all discharged effluent and emitted gases consistently meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Detailed plans for these upgrades, along with a timeline for implementation, must be submitted to the EPA within thirty (30) days.
4.  **Hazardous Waste Management Plan:** The Respondent shall submit a comprehensive Hazardous Waste Management Plan, in strict compliance with the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003, to the EPA within **sixty (60) days**, detailing generation, storage, treatment, and disposal protocols, and implement the same immediately upon approval.
5.  **Environmental Monitoring and Reporting:** The Respondent shall install continuous online monitoring systems for its effluent discharge and air emissions and submit monthly environmental compliance reports, duly verified by an EPA-accredited third-party laboratory, to the Environmental Protection Agency for a period of two (2) years.
6.  **Financial Penalty:** Pursuant to Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, is hereby imposed a cumulative penalty of **PKR 15,000,000/- (Rupees Fifteen Million only)** for the multiple and persistent violations of environmental laws. This penalty shall be paid to the Environmental Protection Agency within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this order.
7.  **Default Clause:** In the event of non-compliance with any part of this order, the Environmental Protection Agency is authorized to take further stringent actions, including but not limited to the sealing of the industrial unit, imposition of further daily fines as stipulated under PEPA, 1997, and initiation of criminal proceedings against the management of the Respondent company.
8.  **Costs:** The Respondent shall bear all costs of this complaint.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,100,000/- (Rupees One Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 220,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
