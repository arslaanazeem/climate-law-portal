This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 20/2017, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited, a prominent manufacturer of glass products located in Lahore (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"). The complaint was initiated following repeated observations and findings of non-compliance with environmental standards, primarily concerning industrial emissions and effluent discharge. The Tribunal was constituted under Section 20 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), to adjudicate matters relating to environmental pollution and enforcement of environmental laws.
The Complainant asserted that the Respondent's industrial operations were in persistent violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial gaseous emissions and liquid effluents, as promulgated under the PEPA, 1997. Specifically, the EPA alleged that M/s Ghani Glass Limited was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial wastewater directly into the municipal sewerage system, and emitting gaseous pollutants, including oxides of nitrogen (NOx), oxides of sulphur (SOx), and particulate matter (PM), from its furnace stacks into the ambient air, significantly exceeding the permissible NEQS limits. These alleged violations, according to the Complainant, posed a serious threat to public health and the environment, causing air pollution, water contamination, and potential harm to local ecosystems and communities residing in the vicinity of the industrial unit. The EPA further claimed that despite multiple warnings, notices, and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent had failed to take adequate measures to control pollution from its operations, thereby necessitating the filing of the present complaint before this Tribunal.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 15.03.2016, 22.08.2016, and 05.01.2017, documenting the EPA teams' visits to the Respondent's facility. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory were submitted, demonstrating that samples of gaseous emissions collected from the Respondent's stacks consistently showed NOx, SOx, and PM levels significantly above the NEQS. Similarly, analysis of liquid effluent samples collected from the discharge points revealed elevated levels of Total Suspended Solids (TSS), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), and pH values outside the prescribed NEQS range. The Complainant also produced copies of Show Cause Notice No. EPA/LH/2016/543 dated 15.04.2016 and Final Warning Notice No. EPA/LH/2016/98
7 dated 20.09.2016, both clearly outlining the violations and demanding corrective actions within specified timelines. The Complainant contended that despite these repeated official communications and warnings, the Respondent had failed to take adequate measures to control pollution from its operations, thereby demonstrating a willful disregard for environmental protection laws and regulations and necessitating the filing of the present complaint before this Tribunal.
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. They argued that their industrial
operations adhered to all applicable environmental standards. They claimed that their existing pollution control measures, including an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and bag filters for gaseous emissions, were fully functional and regularly maintained. The Respondent's counsel questioned the accuracy and methodology of the EPA's sampling and analysis, suggesting potential procedural lapses or calibration issues with the EPA's equipment. They further contended that the environmental samples might not have been representative of their routine operations, or that external factors beyond their control could have influenced the readings. M/s Ghani Glass Limited also highlighted its significant contribution to the national economy, employment generation, and corporate social responsibility initiatives, implying that a harsh penalty could adversely affect its operations and stakeholders. They asserted that they had always been proactive in addressing environmental concerns and had invested substantially in upgrading their machinery and pollution control infrastructure. The Respondent requested the Tribunal to dismiss the complaint, or alternatively, to grant them a reasonable timeframe to address any proven discrepancies, rather than imposing immediate punitive measures.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence and heard detailed arguments from both sides. It observed that the Complainant had presented consistent and well-documented evidence, including multiple inspection reports and accredited laboratory analysis results, which unequivocally demonstrated the Respondent's non-compliance with NEQS. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal has carefully examined the inspection report, laboratory analysis results, and the photographic evidence produced by the Complainant. The measured pollutant concentrations significantly exceed the NEQS limits and the Respondent has failed to rebut this evidence with credible contrary data. The defense regarding ETP maintenance does not excuse ongoing pollution discharge and cannot serve as a justification for non-compliance with mandatory environmental standards.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

This Tribunal is vested with jurisdiction under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997 to try environmental offences committed within its territorial limits. Having found the Respondent guilty of violating NEQS standards, the Tribunal is empowered under Section 17(1) to impose financial penalties, direct remedial measures, and order contribution to the Provincial Sustainable Development Fund (PSDF) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
