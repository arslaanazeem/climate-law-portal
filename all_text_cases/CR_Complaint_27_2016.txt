This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 27/2016, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited, hereinafter referred to as the Respondent. The complaint was lodged on 15th March 2016, alleging significant environmental pollution caused by the Respondent's industrial operations, specifically the discharge of untreated industrial effluent into public waterways and the emission of air pollutants exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal commenced proceedings in accordance with the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and the rules framed thereunder, aiming to ensure environmental compliance and uphold public health and ecological integrity.
The complaint detailed a series of grave allegations against M/s Nishat Mills Limited. It was contended that the Respondent's textile manufacturing facility, located in the vicinity of Lahore, was operating without an adequately functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs). Specifically, the Complainant asserted that the Respondent was directly discharging industrial wastewater containing hazardous chemicals, dyes, and other pollutants into the nearby Hudiara Drain, which eventually merges with the River Ravi, without prior treatment. Furthermore, the complaint highlighted concerns regarding the emission of smoke, particulate matter, and other gaseous pollutants from the factory's stacks, which were observed to be dark and dense, indicating a clear violation of air quality standards. The EPA had issued multiple warning notices and show-cause notices to the Respondent prior to filing the complaint, including a notice dated 20th January 2016, requiring immediate compliance with NEQS, which the Respondent allegedly failed to address effectively. These actions, or rather inactions, were deemed to be in contravention of Sections 11, 12, and 16 of the PEPA, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2001.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a robust body of evidence. Key among these were multiple inspection reports compiled by EPA officials on various dates between October 2015 and February 2016, which consistently noted the absence of a fully operational ETP and visible signs of water and air pollution. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points on 10th December 2015 and 5th February 2016 were submitted. These reports, prepared by the EPA's accredited laboratory, unequivocally demonstrated that parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels far exceeded the permissible NEQS limits. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports indicated elevated levels of particulate matter (PM10 and PM2.5) and sulfur dioxide (SO2) in the ambient air surrounding the factory. The Complainant produced the testimony of Mr. Zulfiqar Ali, Environmental Inspector, who personally conducted inspections and collected samples, affirming the findings. Copies of the warning and show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, along with their unsatisfactory replies, were
also submitted as documentary evidence, demonstrating the Respondent's consistent failure to comply despite repeated official communications.
In response, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through their legal counsel Mr. Ahmed Khan, vehemently denied the allegations. The Respondent contended that they had indeed installed an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control mechanisms, albeit acknowledging that these might have been undergoing maintenance or upgrades at the time of some inspections. They argued that the EPA's sampling methodology was flawed and that the laboratory results were not entirely reflective of their routine operational discharges. Furthermore, the Respondent submitted an environmental management plan (EMP) outlining future upgrades and commitment to environmental protection, asserting that they were a responsible corporate entity contributing significantly to the national economy and employment, and any non-compliance was unintentional and temporary. They also requested leniency, citing the substantial investment already made in environmental infrastructure and the economic challenges faced by the textile sector.
The Tribunal meticulously examined the evidence presented by both parties. It noted the consistent and corroborated nature of the Complainant's evidence, particularly the multiple inspection reports and the independent laboratory analyses of effluent and air samples. The scientific data, demonstrating significant exceedances of NEQS for key parameters, remained largely unchallenged in its veracity, despite the Respondent's general assertions of flawed methodology. While acknowledging the Respondent's claims of ongoing maintenance and future plans, the Tribunal found no concrete evidence to suggest that the ETP and APCDs were consistently operational and effective in bringing discharges within NEQS limits during the period covered by the complaint. The unsatisfactory replies to warning notices further underscored a pattern of non-compliance. The Tribunal emphasized that environmental protection is not merely a matter of intent but of demonstrable compliance with statutory standards. Sections 11, 12, and 16 of the PEPA, 1997, mandate strict adherence to NEQS and prohibit the discharge of untreated effluent or emissions of pollutants exceeding prescribed limits. The evidence on record overwhelmingly established that M/s Nishat Mills Limited
was indeed in contravention of Sections 11, 12, and 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) during the period under complaint. The Respondent’s operations demonstrably resulted in the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and the emission of air pollutants far exceeding the prescribed limits, thereby posing a significant threat to public health and the environment.
Therefore, having carefully considered all the evidence and arguments presented, this Tribunal finds M/s Nishat Mills Limited guilty of the charges leveled against it by the Environmental Protection Agency.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the findings, this Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Cessation of Non-Compliance:** M/s Nishat Mills Limited shall immediately cease and desist from discharging any industrial effluent or emitting any air pollutants that do not conform to the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
2.  **Operational ETP and APCDs:** The Respondent shall, within **ninety (90) days** from the date of this judgment, ensure the installation, full operationalization, and consistent maintenance of a robust and effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and appropriate Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) capable of bringing all discharges and emissions into strict compliance with NEQS. The Respondent shall submit a detailed compliance plan, including a timeline for implementation and verifiable milestones, to the Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days.
3.  **Monitoring and Reporting:** The Respondent shall establish a rigorous self-monitoring program, regularly test its effluent and emissions, and submit quarterly compliance reports, duly verified by an EPA-accredited laboratory, to the Environmental Protection Agency for a period of two (2) years. The EPA shall retain the right to conduct unannounced inspections and independent sampling.
4.  **Environmental Audit:** The Respondent shall commission an independent environmental audit of its entire facility within six (6) months, the report of which shall be submitted to the EPA and this Tribunal.
5.  **Penalty:** For the proven contraventions of Sections 11, 12, and 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, M/s Nishat Mills Limited is hereby imposed a penalty of **Rupees One Million (PKR 1,000,000/-)**. This amount shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days from the date of this judgment.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,000,000/- (Rupees Two Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 400,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **09 October, 2023**.
