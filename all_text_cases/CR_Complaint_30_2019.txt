This judgment addresses Complaint No. 30/2019, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore. The complaint seeks a determination of environmental violations committed by the Respondent and the imposition of penalties under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"). The Tribunal, having heard arguments from both sides and meticulously reviewed the submitted evidence, proceeds to render its decision.
The Complainant's allegations against M/s Nishat Mills Limited primarily revolve around persistent non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents and air emissions, as well as operating without a valid No Objection Certificate (NOC) for its operations. Specifically, the EPA alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations were discharging untreated or inadequately treated wastewater directly into the nearby public drain, which eventually flows into the River Ravi. Sampling conducted by the EPA revealed concentrations of various pollutants, including Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and heavy metals (such as Chromium and Lead), significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS. Furthermore, the complaint highlighted concerns regarding visible particulate matter emissions from the Respondent's stacks, indicative of inadequate air pollution control measures, again in violation of NEQS for air quality. The EPA also contended that despite multiple notices and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent failed to take effective remedial actions and had not renewed its Environmental Approval/NOC as required under Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, thereby operating without due environmental authorization.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive set of documentary evidence. This included an initial inspection report dated 15.03.2019, detailing observations of effluent discharge and visible smoke plumes. Crucially, a laboratory analysis report from the EPA-certified laboratory, dated 25.03.2019, was submitted, which conclusively showed that samples of the Respondent's effluent collected from its discharge point exceeded the NEQS for BOD (250 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), COD (450 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), TSS (300 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), and also indicated elevated levels of Chromium. Subsequent follow-up inspection reports dated 10.05.2019 and 01.07.2019 indicated continued non-compliance. Copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 05.04.2019 and 15.06.2019, demanding an explanation for the violations and corrective measures, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies, were also part of the record. The Complainant also produced evidence of
the Respondent's failure to obtain or renew its Environmental Approval/No Objection Certificate (NOC) as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, despite repeated reminders and opportunities to regularize its operations.
**Respondent's Defense:**
M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its learned counsel, contested the EPA's allegations, arguing that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection. The Respondent asserted that it had already initiated steps to upgrade its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and install advanced air pollution control equipment, and that these measures required significant capital investment and time. It was contended that the EPA's sampling might have been unrepresentative or that the observed exceedances were temporary aberrations that did not reflect the Respondent's overall commitment to compliance. Regarding the
renewal of the Environmental Approval/NOC, the Respondent submitted that while a previous approval had expired, the application for renewal was in process, and delays were often attributable to bureaucratic procedures rather than a deliberate disregard for environmental regulations. Furthermore, the Respondent emphasized its significant contribution to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to consider the potential adverse economic impact of harsh penalties on its operations and workforce. It was argued that a more constructive approach involving a compliance schedule and monitoring, rather than punitive measures, would be more beneficial for achieving long-term environmental sustainability.
**Findings and Analysis:**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the documentary evidence presented.
Regarding the allegations of non-compliance with NEQS for industrial effluents, the Complainant has presented compelling and irrefutable evidence. The laboratory analysis report dated 25.03.2019, from an EPA-certified laboratory, conclusively demonstrates that the Respondent's effluent discharge significantly exceeded the prescribed NEQS for BOD, COD, TSS, and Chromium. These findings are not isolated; subsequent inspection reports on 10.05.2019 and 01.07.2019 indicate a persistent pattern of non-compliance, suggesting that the exceedances were not merely temporary aberrations as contended by the Respondent. While the Respondent's efforts to upgrade its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) are commendable, the fact remains that at the time of the inspections and complaint, its operations were in clear violation of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, which mandates adherence to NEQS. Intent to comply in the future does not absolve a party of past and ongoing violations.
Similarly, concerns regarding air emissions, though primarily based on visual observations of visible particulate matter, are indicative of inadequate pollution control measures. While specific quantitative data for air emissions comparable to effluent analysis was not provided, the persistent visible emissions, as noted in multiple inspection reports, suggest a failure to implement proper air pollution control mechanisms in accordance with NEPA, 1997, and associated NEQS for air quality.
Crucially, the Respondent's operation without a valid Environmental Approval/No Objection Certificate (NOC) constitutes a fundamental breach of Section 12 of PEPA, 1997. This section unequivocally requires that no proponent of a project shall commence construction or operation unless an environmental approval has been granted by the Agency. The Respondent's admission that its previous approval had expired, coupled with the Complainant's evidence of repeated reminders and the lack of a current valid NOC, confirms this violation. While the Respondent claims the renewal application is in process, this does not legalize operations conducted without the requisite approval. The requirement for an NOC is a proactive measure designed to ensure environmental safeguards are in place *before* operations commence or continue, and its absence represents a serious lapse in regulatory compliance.
The Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established the allegations of persistent non-compliance with National Environmental Quality Standards for industrial effluents and air emissions, and operating without a valid Environmental Approval/No Objection Certificate, in violation of Sections 11 and 12 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. The Respondent's defense, while highlighting future intentions and economic considerations, does not sufficiently rebut the concrete evidence of past and ongoing environmental infractions. The public interest in a clean and healthy environment, protected by law, must take precedence.
**Final Order and Penalty:**
In light of the foregoing findings, this Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Declaration of Guilt:** M/s Nishat Mills Limited is found to be in violation of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, for persistent non-compliance with National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents and air emissions, and in violation of Section 12 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, for operating without a valid Environmental Approval/No Objection Certificate (NOC).
2.  **Cessation of Violations:** M/s Nishat Mills Limited is immediately directed to cease all discharges of industrial effluents that do not comply with the prescribed NEQS and to implement effective measures to control air emissions to meet the NEQS.
3.  **Obtainment of NOC:** M/s Nishat Mills Limited shall, within sixty (60) days from the date of this order, obtain a valid Environmental Approval/No Objection Certificate from the Environmental Protection Agency for its operations.
4.  **Imposition of Penalty:** Pursuant to Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, a monetary penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand Only)** is hereby imposed on M/s Nishat Mills Limited for the aforementioned violations. This penalty shall be paid to the Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.
5.  **Daily Fine for Continuing Violation:** In addition to the lump sum penalty, should M/s Nishat Mills Limited fail to comply with the NEQS for effluents and air emissions, or fail to obtain the requisite NOC within the stipulated timeframes mentioned above, a further penalty of **PKR 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand Only)** per day shall be imposed for each day the violation continues, until full compliance is achieved.
6.  **Compliance Report:** M/s Nishat Mills Limited shall submit a comprehensive compliance report to the Environmental Protection Agency and this Tribunal within seventy-five (75) days from the date of this order, detailing the steps taken to achieve compliance with NEQS and the status of its NOC application.
7.  **Costs:** The Respondent shall also bear the costs of these proceedings, assessed at **PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand Only)**, payable to the Complainant within thirty (30) days.
Failure to comply with any part of this order may result in further legal action, including but not limited to, closure of operations under Section 16 of the PEPA, 1997.
