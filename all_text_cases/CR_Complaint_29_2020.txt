This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 29/2020, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, on October 15, 2020, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a manufacturing facility located in the industrial zone of Lahore. The Complainant, acting under the mandate of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter "PEPA, 1997"), initiated these proceedings alleging significant environmental non-compliance by the Respondent, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and excessive air emissions, thereby causing pollution and posing a threat to public health and the environment in contravention of national environmental quality standards and statutory obligations.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around two major violations. Firstly, it is contended that the Respondent's industrial facility has been consistently discharging process wastewater directly into the municipal sewerage system and ultimately into local water bodies without adequate treatment, resulting in pollutant levels far exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents. Specific parameters cited in the complaint include elevated levels of Total Suspended Solids (TSS), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), and pH outside the permissible range. Secondly, the complaint asserts that the Respondent's furnace operations and stack emissions release particulate matter (PM), Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), and Sulphur Oxides (SOx) into the ambient air at concentrations significantly above the NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions, contributing to air pollution in the vicinity. The Complainant further alleged that despite multiple warning notices and opportunities for rectification issued by the EPA since early 2020, the Respondent failed to implement effective pollution control measures, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental regulations and the well-being of the surrounding community.
In support of its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included three detailed inspection reports dated February 10, 2020, April 28, 2020, and August 15, 2020, prepared by EPA field teams, documenting observations of dark-colored effluent discharge and visible smoke plumes from the Respondent's stacks. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, confirming that samples of industrial effluent collected from the Respondent's discharge points on March 5, 2020, and July 20, 2020, showed TSS levels up to 300 mg/L (NEQS: 200 mg/L), COD up to 450 mg/L (NEQS: 250 mg/L), and BOD up to 180 mg/L (NEQS: 80 mg/L), with pH values ranging from 4.5 to 9.5. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports from May 10, 2020, indicated PM levels from the main stack at 350 mg/Nm³ (NEQS: 150 mg/Nm³ for existing industries). The Complainant also produced copies of the warning notices issued to the Respondent on March 15, 2020, and July 30, 2020, and the subsequent show-cause notice dated September 10, 2020, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies. Oral testimonies from EPA inspectors corroborated the findings of the reports. The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, while not denying the existence of the facility or its operations, contested the
veracity and extent of the alleged violations, primarily arguing that their facility was in the process of implementing upgrades to its environmental control systems and that some of the reported exceedances were either transient or attributable to factors beyond their immediate control. The Respondent claimed to have initiated a feasibility study for a new Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and proposed an action plan for improving air emission controls, albeit without concrete timelines or demonstrable progress at the time of the complaint. They further suggested that the EPA's sampling methodologies might not have fully captured the operational
variability of their processes. They also questioned the representativeness of the samples taken, suggesting they might not reflect average operating conditions. However, the Respondent failed to provide any independent laboratory reports or technical data to counter the specific findings of the EPA's accredited laboratory. Their defence largely relied on assurances of future compliance and ongoing internal assessments, without presenting concrete evidence of immediate or effective remedial actions taken prior to or during the proceedings.
This Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments presented by both parties and meticulously reviewed the documentary and oral evidence on record. The Complainant, through the production of multiple inspection reports, accredited laboratory analysis reports, and copies of official communications including warning and show-cause notices, has established a compelling case of consistent and significant environmental non-compliance by M/s Ghani Glass Limited. The laboratory results unequivocally demonstrate that both the industrial effluent discharged and the air emissions from the Respondent's facility consistently exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for various critical parameters, as prescribed under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2001. The Respondent's failure to adequately respond to repeated warnings and its inability to present concrete evidence of effective pollution control measures or a credible, time-bound action plan for achieving compliance prior to the initiation of these proceedings further strengthens the Complainant's assertions. The Respondent's defence, primarily based on general claims of ongoing upgrades and questioning sampling methodologies without corroborating evidence, is found to be unsubstantiated and insufficient to rebut the robust evidence presented by the EPA.
It is evident that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has been operating in contravention of Sections 11, 12, and 16 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, which prohibit the discharge of effluent or emission of air pollutants in excess of the NEQS, and mandate the adoption of pollution control measures. Such persistent violations not only endanger public health and safety but also cause irreversible damage to the environment, including water bodies and ambient air quality, in the vicinity of the Respondent's operations. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent has demonstrated a clear disregard for its environmental responsibilities and statutory obligations.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
