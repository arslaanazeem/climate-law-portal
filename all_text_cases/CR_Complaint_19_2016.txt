This judgment addresses the environmental complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent industrial enterprise engaged in the manufacturing of paper, board, and packaging materials. The complaint, registered on 15th March 2016, arises from alleged non-compliance with the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal's jurisdiction is invoked under Section 21 of the PEPA, 1997, to adjudicate upon environmental violations and impose penalties where necessary, thereby safeguarding public health and the environment.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's purported failure to adequately treat its industrial effluent and control its air emissions, resulting in environmental degradation. Specifically, it is contended that multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials revealed consistent discharge of untreated or inadequately treated wastewater into the municipal sewerage system, subsequently flowing into natural water bodies. Laboratory analyses of samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points indicated levels of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals (specifically lead and chromium) significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial effluents. Furthermore, the complaint asserts that the Respondent's boiler stacks emitted particulate matter, Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) above the permissible NEQS limits, contributing to air pollution in the vicinity. The Complainant also highlighted deficiencies in the Respondent's hazardous waste management practices, particularly concerning the storage and disposal of sludge generated from the wastewater treatment plant and used oils, which were allegedly not handled in accordance with the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003. Despite repeated notices and opportunities for corrective action issued by the EPA, the Respondent allegedly failed to implement effective measures to bring its operations into compliance.
In support of its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included a series of inspection reports dated 10th November 2015, 28th December 2015, and 15th February 2016, detailing the observations of EPA field teams. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, bearing reference numbers EPA/LAB/2015/11/057, EPA/LAB/2015/12/098, and EPA/LAB/2016/02/012, were submitted, which unequivocally demonstrated the exceedance of NEQS parameters for both wastewater and air emissions. Photographic evidence depicting the colour and turbidity of the effluent being discharged, as well as improperly stored hazardous waste containers, was also tendered. The testimony of Mr. Asif Mehmood, Environmental Inspector, who conducted the site inspections and collected samples, corroborated the findings in the reports. On the other hand, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel, Mr. Zahid Khan, contested the allegations. They presented their own internal monitoring reports, which, *per contra*, claimed compliance with NEQS. They argued that the EPA's sampling methodology was flawed, that the analytical results were not representative of their
operations. They further contended that their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air emission control systems were fully functional and that any exceedances noted by the EPA were transient or attributable to external factors beyond their control. The Respondent also submitted that they had initiated measures to improve their hazardous waste management in line with regulatory requirements and were committed to environmental stewardship. They requested the Tribunal to dismiss the complaint, asserting that
they were a responsible corporate entity with a long-standing commitment to sustainable practices and had already invested substantial resources in environmental protection infrastructure. They emphasized their significant contribution to the national economy and employment, urging the Tribunal to consider these factors while adjudicating.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties. It noted the consistent nature of the EPA's inspection reports and the corroborating laboratory analyses. The EPA's sampling methodology and analytical procedures were found to be in accordance with established protocols, and the reports were duly signed by accredited personnel. While the Respondent presented internal monitoring reports claiming compliance, these reports lacked the independent verification and consistent documentation seen in the EPA's submissions. The Tribunal found the Respondent's claims of flawed sampling and transient exceedances to be unsubstantiated by concrete evidence. The photographic evidence, though circumstantial, supported the EPA's observations regarding effluent quality and waste storage. The testimony of the Environmental Inspector was clear and consistent. The PEPA, 1997, and NEQS are stringent legal instruments designed to protect the environment and public health, and industrial entities are mandated to operate within their prescribed limits. The Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's economic contributions but stresses that economic activity cannot be permitted at the expense of environmental degradation and public well-being.
**FINDINGS OF THE TRIBUNAL:**
Based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established that M/s Packages Limited has been in violation of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards. Specifically, the Respondent failed to adequately treat its industrial effluent, resulting in the discharge of wastewater exceeding NEQS for BOD, COD, TSS, lead, and chromium. Furthermore, its air emissions of particulate matter, SO2, and NOx were found to be above permissible limits. The Tribunal also finds deficiencies in the Respondent's hazardous waste management practices, particularly concerning sludge and used oils, which were not handled in accordance with the Hazardous Substances Rules, 2003. The Respondent’s arguments regarding flawed methodology and transient exceedances are hereby dismissed due to insufficient credible evidence.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the aforementioned findings and the serious nature of the environmental violations, the Tribunal hereby issues the following final order and penalty under Section 21 and Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997:
1.  **Cessation of Violations:** The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, is forthwith directed to cease all discharges of industrial effluent and air emissions that exceed the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 3,100,000/- (Rupees Three Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 620,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
