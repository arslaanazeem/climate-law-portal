This judgment addresses Complaint No. 27/2018, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The complaint, lodged on October 15, 2018, seeks enforcement of environmental laws and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal, having heard the parties and meticulously reviewed the submitted record, proceeds to render its decision.
The allegations leveled by the Complainant against M/s Nishat Mills Limited primarily pertain to the discharge of industrial effluent that fails to conform to the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards. Specifically, the Complainant asserted that during a routine inspection conducted on September 25, 2018, by an EPA team, effluent samples were collected from the Respondent's discharge point. Subsequent laboratory analysis of these samples revealed that several key parameters, including Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH, significantly exceeded the permissible limits set forth in the NEQS for Municipal and Industrial Effluents. The Complainant further alleged that despite previous warnings and a Show Cause Notice issued on October 05, 2018, the Respondent failed to take adequate measures to treat its effluent, thereby continuously polluting the environment and posing a risk to public health and aquatic ecosystems. It was also contended that the Respondent had not consistently submitted its Self-Monitoring and Reporting Tool (SMART) reports as required under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industry) Rules, 2017, indicative of a systemic failure in environmental compliance.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive set of evidence. This included the EPA site inspection report dated September 25, 2018, which documented the conditions observed at the Respondent's premises, including the discharge point. Crucially, the official laboratory analysis report (Lab Reference No. EPA/LHR/1018/005) from the EPA's accredited laboratory, dated October 12, 2018, was submitted. This report clearly indicated BOD levels of 350 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 80 mg/L), COD levels of 800 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 150 mg/L), TSS of 220 mg/L (against a NEQS limit of 200 mg/L), and a pH value of 10.5 (against a NEQS range of
6.5-8.5). This stark disparity between the observed values and the statutory limits formed the cornerstone of the Complainant's case, demonstrating a clear breach of the National Environmental Quality Standards and, consequently, Section 11(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. They contended that they operate a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) designed to treat industrial wastewater in accordance with environmental standards. While acknowledging the possibility of occasional deviations, they argued that the samples collected might not have been representative of their typical discharge or that the sampling methodology employed by the EPA was flawed. They further submitted that they are committed to environmental stewardship and have made
significant investments in upgrading their ETP over the years. They presented records of their ETP's operational logs, maintenance schedules, and invoices for chemicals used in the treatment process, asserting that these demonstrate a genuine effort towards compliance. They also highlighted the complexity of treating textile effluent, which can vary in composition depending on the dyes and chemicals used in different production batches. While admitting to some delays in submitting SMART reports, they attributed this to administrative oversight rather than a deliberate attempt to conceal data, assuring the Tribunal of their intention to rectify this promptly. The Respondent's counsel requested the Tribunal to consider their overall track record, their contributions to the national economy, and their willingness to cooperate with environmental authorities in finding a pragmatic solution, rather than imposing a harsh penalty based on a single instance of alleged non-compliance. They also submitted an undertaking to improve their monitoring and reporting mechanisms and to ensure that their ETP operates optimally at all times.
**Tribunal's Analysis and Findings:**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent, along with the documentary evidence placed on record. The core of the Complainant's case rests squarely on the laboratory analysis report, which unequivocally demonstrates the exceedance of NEQS limits for critical parameters. The EPA's inspection report corroborates the context in which the samples were collected. **V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 2,000,000/- (Rupees Two Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 400,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
