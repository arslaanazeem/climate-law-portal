This judgment addresses the environmental complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore. The complaint seeks enforcement of environmental laws and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997). The Tribunal has heard arguments from both sides and meticulously reviewed the evidence presented to arrive at its decision.
The Complainant's case rests on a series of inspections and subsequent laboratory analyses conducted at the Respondent's facility. It is alleged that the Respondent has been consistently discharging industrial effluent containing pollutants far exceeding the permissible limits set by the NEQS. Specifically, an inspection carried out on 15th January 2020 by an EPA team revealed direct discharge of untreated or inadequately treated wastewater into the sewerage system, which eventually flows into natural water bodies. Samples of the effluent were collected and sent to the EPA's accredited laboratory for analysis. The laboratory report, dated 28th January 2020, indicated alarmingly high levels of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH values outside the prescribed range. For instance, the COD was reported at 650 mg/L against the NEQS limit of 150 mg/L, BOD5 at 280 mg/L against 80 mg/L, and TSS at 320 mg/L against 200 mg/L. Furthermore, the pH was recorded at 10.5, exceeding the upper limit of 9.0. Following these findings, a show-cause notice was issued to the Respondent on 5th February 2020, demanding an explanation and a compliance plan. Dissatisfied with the Respondent's response and subsequent follow-up inspections indicating continued non-compliance, the Complainant filed the present complaint before this Tribunal, seeking appropriate legal action under PEPA, 1997.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant submitted a comprehensive set of documents, including the initial inspection report dated 15th January 2020, the laboratory analysis report bearing reference No. EPA/Lab/2020/01-234 dated 28th January 2020, copies of the show-cause notice dated 5th February 2020, and the Respondent's reply dated 15th February 2020. Additionally, photographs depicting the discharge point and the colour of the effluent were presented. The Complainant also produced the testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, the inspecting officer, who confirmed the observations made during the inspection and the sampling procedure. Mr. Bilal Hussain, the laboratory analyst, testified regarding the methodology used for analysis and the accuracy of the results. The Complainant also presented a brief history of prior informal warnings issued to the Respondent regarding environmental concerns, though no formal complaints had been lodged previously.
The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations of willful non-compliance. While acknowledging the presence of an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) at its facility, the Respondent contended that the reported exceedances were anomalous and possibly due to a temporary
malfunction in the Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) or an unexpected surge in production during the period of inspection. The Respondent submitted that its ETP is generally functional and designed to meet the NEQS. It presented maintenance records of the ETP, indicating regular servicing and repairs. Furthermore, the Respondent argued that a single instance of exceedance, especially if attributable to an isolated incident, should not lead to severe penalties. It also produced its internal monitoring reports, although these were not from an EPA-accredited laboratory, suggesting that on most other occasions, the effluent quality was within acceptable parameters. The Respondent's counsel emphasized the company's significant contribution to the national economy and its commitment to environmental stewardship, requesting the Tribunal to consider a lenient view and allow time for any necessary upgrades or rectifications. They also highlighted ongoing efforts to modernize the ETP, including plans for implementing advanced tertiary treatment technologies, for which proposals were already under review. They sought an opportunity to demonstrate compliance rather than face immediate punitive measures.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments and evidence presented by both parties. The Complainant has successfully established, through credible and verifiable evidence, that on 15th January 2020, the Respondent's facility discharged effluent containing pollutants significantly exceeding the NEQS. The inspection report, corroborated by the testimony of the inspecting officer, clearly details the observation of discharge. The laboratory analysis report, from an accredited
laboratory analysis report, from an accredited laboratory of the Complainant, stands as compelling and irrefutable evidence of the Respondent's non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) on the specified date. The significant exceedances in Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH values are not minor deviations but indicate a substantial failure to adequately treat industrial effluent before discharge.
The Tribunal acknowledges the Respondent's assertion regarding a temporary malfunction or an unexpected surge in production. However, the Respondent failed to provide concrete and verifiable evidence to substantiate these claims, such as detailed malfunction reports, immediate repair logs demonstrating the anomaly was promptly resolved, or production

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
