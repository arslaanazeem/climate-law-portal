This judgment addresses Complaint No. 35/2017 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited, a prominent industrial entity located in Lahore (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"). The complaint invokes the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 21 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"), alleging contraventions of various provisions of the Act and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The core of the dispute revolves around the Respondent's alleged failure to manage its industrial discharges in an environmentally sound manner, thereby causing pollution.
The Complainant's primary allegations against the Respondent are two-fold: firstly, that the Respondent has been discharging industrial effluent into the municipal sewerage system/receiving water bodies without adequate treatment, resulting in pollutant concentrations far exceeding the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS); and secondly, that the Respondent
has failed to obtain requisite environmental approvals (such as an Environmental Impact Assessment or Initial Environmental Examination where applicable) for its operations or significant expansions, and has also been observed to be emitting air pollutants that potentially exceed prescribed NEQS for ambient air quality and industrial emissions. The Complainant submitted various inspection reports, laboratory analyses of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points, and photographic evidence to substantiate these claims. These reports consistently indicated high levels of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and other parameters significantly above the permissible limits.
The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, vehemently denied the allegations in its written statement. It contended that it operates a state-of-the-art effluent treatment plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices, and is committed to environmental compliance. The Respondent asserted that the samples collected by the EPA were either not representative, improperly collected, or analyzed using flawed methodologies. It further argued that it possessed all necessary environmental approvals and was regularly monitored by internal and external environmental consultants. The Respondent also presented its own laboratory reports, internal audit reports, and records of ETP operations to demonstrate compliance.
During the proceedings, both parties presented their respective witnesses and documentary evidence. The Complainant produced the EPA officials who conducted inspections and collected samples, along with the laboratory analysts who processed them. These witnesses were cross-examined by the Respondent's counsel. Similarly, the Respondent presented its environmental manager, technical experts, and ETP operators, who were
cross-examined by the Complainant's counsel regarding the functionality of their ETP, the validity of their internal reports, and the consistency of their operational practices with environmental standards. The Tribunal meticulously reviewed all documentary evidence, including the EPA's inspection reports, effluent analysis results, photographs, and the Respondent's counter-reports, ETP logbooks, and environmental audit findings. Expert opinions were also considered where relevant, particularly concerning the methodologies of sampling and analysis.
**Analysis of Evidence and Findings:**
The Tribunal carefully considered the arguments put forth by both parties. Regarding the Complainant's allegations of effluent discharge exceeding NEQS, the EPA's laboratory reports consistently demonstrated high levels of pollutants such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), and Total Suspended Solids (TSS). While the Respondent challenged the sampling methodology and representativeness, the Complainant's witnesses provided detailed accounts of the collection process, adhering to established protocols. The Tribunal found the EPA's evidence, supported by multiple inspections and analyses over a period, to be credible and compelling. The Respondent's own reports, while attempting to show compliance, often presented data that was either inconsistent with the EPA's findings or did not fully address the periods of alleged non-compliance. The Tribunal noted that even minor discrepancies in sampling could not account for the significant and repeated exceedances reported by the EPA. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
