This judgment addresses Complaint No. 31/2023 filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located on Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The complaint seeks punitive action and remedial orders against the Respondent for alleged persistent violations of environmental laws and standards, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and emission of untreated gaseous pollutants into the environment, thereby causing significant water and air pollution. The Tribunal, having heard the learned counsel for both parties, examined the evidence adduced, and considered the relevant legal provisions, proceeds to render its decision.
The Complainant's primary allegations are that the Respondent has been operating its textile manufacturing facility without adequately treating its industrial wastewater and gaseous emissions, in flagrant disregard of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA 1997). Specifically, it is alleged that:
a. The Respondent consistently discharges industrial effluent, containing high levels of chemical oxygen demand (COD), biological oxygen demand (BOD), total suspended solids (
TSS), and other hazardous substances, directly into the nearby drain/water body without proper treatment, thereby contaminating surface water and groundwater resources.
b. Multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials on [specific dates, e.g., 10.03.2023, 25.05.2023, and 15.08.2023] revealed that the Respondent's Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) was either non-functional, inadequate, or bypassed, leading to the discharge of effluent significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS for various parameters.
c. Laboratory analyses of effluent samples collected by EPA consistently showed values far above the permissible limits for COD, BOD, TSS, pH, and heavy metals, indicating severe pollution.
d. The Respondent's industrial operations also involve the emission of untreated gaseous pollutants, including particulate matter, sulfur dioxide (SO2), and nitrogen oxides (NOx), from its stacks, contributing to ambient air pollution, in violation of NEQS for air emissions.
e. Despite repeated notices, warnings, and opportunities for compliance issued by the EPA under Sections 16 and 17 of PEPA 1997, the Respondent has failed to take effective measures to rectify the violations, demonstrating a persistent and wilful disregard for environmental regulations.
The Complainant, therefore, prayed for the Tribunal to impose a substantial penalty on the Respondent, direct the immediate installation and operationalization of an adequate and fully functional ETP and air pollution control devices (APCDs) in compliance with NEQS, and order the Respondent to pay compensation for environmental damage caused.
The Respondent, through its learned counsel, vehemently denied the allegations, contending that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection. It was argued that:
a. The Respondent has invested substantially in environmental infrastructure, including an ETP and APCDs, which are generally operational and maintained.
b. Any instances of non-compliance, if proven, were isolated incidents resulting from technical glitches, power outages, or unforeseen operational challenges, rather than a deliberate intent to violate environmental laws.
c. The sampling and testing methodology employed by the EPA was challenged, suggesting potential procedural irregularities or contamination, which could have led to inaccurate results.
d. The Respondent presented internal monitoring reports and certificates from third-party consultants, asserting compliance with NEQS for most parameters, most of the time.
e. It was further submitted that the Respondent is actively undertaking measures to upgrade its existing environmental infrastructure and has submitted an environmental management plan to the EPA, which demonstrates its commitment to achieving full compliance.
The Tribunal carefully considered the arguments advanced by both sides and meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence presented. The evidence primarily consisted of:
1.  Inspection reports filed by EPA officials detailing their observations during site visits, often accompanied by photographic evidence.
2.  Laboratory analysis reports of effluent and emission samples collected by EPA, indicating the levels of various pollutants and their comparison with NEQS.
3.  Copies of show-cause notices, environmental protection orders, and other correspondence exchanged between the EPA and the Respondent.
4.  The Respondent's internal monitoring data, technical specifications of its ETP and APCDs, and correspondence related to its proposed upgrades.
**Findings of Fact and Law:**
Upon a thorough examination of the record, the Tribunal finds that the EPA has presented compelling evidence demonstrating consistent and significant violations of environmental laws by the Respondent. The inspection reports clearly indicate instances where the ETP was either non-operational or bypassed, leading to the discharge of untreated effluent. The laboratory reports, critically, provide objective and scientific evidence of pollutant levels in the Respondent's discharges far exceeding the NEQS. While the Respondent attempted to challenge the sampling methodology and presented its own monitoring data, the consistency and frequency of the EPA's non-compliance findings, corroborated by multiple independent tests, outweigh the Respondent's claims of isolated incidents or flawed sampling.
The Tribunal notes that PEPA 1997, particularly Sections 11, 12, 13, 15, and 16, places a statutory obligation on industrial units to comply with the NEQS. Section 16 specifically empowers the EPA to issue environmental protection orders and initiate legal proceedings for non-compliance. The persistent nature of the violations, despite repeated warnings and notices, demonstrates a clear failure on the part of M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited to meet its environmental responsibilities under PEPA 1997 and the applicable NEQS. The Respondent's efforts towards upgrades, while commendable, do not absolve it of past and ongoing non-compliance. The environmental degradation caused by the discharge of untreated effluent and emissions into the environment poses a serious threat to public health and ecological balance, warranting immediate and stringent action.
**ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal hereby orders as follows:
1.  **PENALTY:** A penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** is imposed on M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited for persistent violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards. This penalty shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order. Failure to comply shall result in further legal action, including recovery as arrears of land revenue.
2.  **IMMEDIATE COMPLIANCE:** M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited is directed to immediately cease the discharge of any untreated industrial effluent and the emission of untreated gaseous pollutants. The Respondent shall ensure that its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) are fully functional, adequately maintained, and consistently operate in strict compliance with all applicable National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
3.  **SUBMISSION OF COMPLIANCE PLAN:** Within fifteen (15) days from the date of this order, the Respondent shall submit a detailed, time-bound **Environmental Compliance Action Plan** to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, outlining specific measures, timelines, and financial commitments for upgrading and ensuring the consistent operational efficiency of its ETP and APCDs to meet NEQS. This plan must be approved by the EPA.
4.  **THIRD-PARTY ENVIRONMENTAL AUDIT:** The Respondent shall commission an independent, EPA-approved third-party environmental audit of its entire facility, focusing on effluent and emission parameters, within sixty (60) days from the date of this order. The audit report, along with recommendations, shall be submitted to both the EPA and this Tribunal. The cost of this audit shall be borne by the Respondent.
5.  **MONITORING AND REPORTING:** The Respondent shall install continuous online monitoring systems for key effluent and emission parameters, as prescribed by the EPA, within ninety (90) days. Data from these systems shall be made accessible to the EPA in real-time. Until such systems are installed, the Respondent shall submit weekly compliance reports, including laboratory analyses from EPA-approved laboratories, to the EPA.
6.  **ENVIRONMENTAL PERFORMANCE BOND:** The Respondent shall furnish an **Environmental Performance Bond** of **PKR 10,000,000/- (Rupees Ten Million only)** to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days. This bond shall be forfeited

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,500,000/- (Rupees One Million Five Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 300,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
