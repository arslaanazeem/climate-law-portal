This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore, concerning alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations. The complaint, registered on 15.03.2022, seeks the enforcement of environmental standards, imposition of penalties, and remedial measures against the Respondent for discharging industrial effluent exceeding prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, Mr. Ahsan Ali, Assistant Director, EPA, alleged that the Respondent's textile dyeing and finishing unit was discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent directly into the public sewerage system, which eventually contaminates surface and ground water resources, thereby causing significant environmental degradation and posing a threat to public health. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  Violation of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA"), by discharging effluent containing pollutants in an amount or concentration exceeding the NEQS.
2.  Failure to operate and maintain an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) despite repeated notices and directives from the EPA.
3.  Non-compliance with the conditions of its Environmental Approval/NOC (if any) or operating without a valid approval under Section 12 of PEPA.
4.  Causing environmental pollution detrimental to ambient water quality, aquatic life, and human health.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of documents and expert testimonies. These included:
1.  **Inspection Reports:** Multiple inspection reports dated 18.09.2021, 05.11.2021, and 28.01.2022, indicating observations of dark-colored effluent discharge from the Respondent's premises.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Three separate laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, bearing reference numbers EPA/Lab/2021/09-014, EPA/Lab/2021/11-032, and EPA/Lab/2022/01-087, corresponding to the dates of inspection. These reports consistently showed that samples of the Respondent's discharged effluent exceeded the NEQS for various critical parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. For instance, the report dated 28.01.2022 indicated COD levels of 450 mg/L (NEQS: 150 mg/L), BOD5 of 180 mg/L (NEQS: 80 mg/L), and TSS of 220 mg/L (NEQS: 200 mg/L).
3.  **Show-Cause Notices:** Copies of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent on 25.10.2021 and 15.02.2022, requiring explanation for the non-compliance and immediate remedial measures, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies.
4.  **Expert Testimony:** Testimony from Dr. Faiza Khan, a Senior Environmental Analyst at EPA, who explained the methodology of sampling, the reliability of the laboratory tests, and the environmental impact of the observed NEQS exceedances.
In response, the Respondent, represented by its legal counsel, Mr. Zahid Hussain, argued that it was a responsible corporate entity with a commitment to environmental protection. Their defense primarily rested on the following points:
1.  **Existence of ETP:** The Respondent claimed to have an operational Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and presented architectural drawings and invoices for its installation in 2019.
2.  **Ongoing Upgradation:** They submitted that the ETP was undergoing a continuous upgradation process to meet evolving standards and that recent fluctuations in effluent quality were temporary, attributed to technical glitches and maintenance work.
3.  **Financial Constraints:** The Respondent pleaded financial constraints, citing the economic downturn and the high cost of advanced treatment technologies, which had delayed the full optimization of their ETP.
4.  **Sampling Irregularities:** While not outright denying the results, they subtly questioned the sampling methodology, suggesting that samples might have been taken during abnormal operational phases or that composite sampling over 24 hours would have yielded different results. However, no concrete evidence was provided to discredit the EPA's sampling protocols, which were conducted in accordance with standard procedures.
5.  **Commitment to Compliance:** The Respondent assured the Tribunal of its full commitment to
ensure compliance with environmental laws and regulations, and sought an opportunity to rectify any shortcomings identified.
**ANALYSIS AND DELIBERATION**
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments presented by both parties and meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence and expert testimonies on record.
The Complainant, the Environmental Protection Agency, has presented a compelling case, substantiated by concrete and consistent evidence. The multiple inspection
reports, consistently showing observations of dark-colored effluent discharge, coupled with the highly corroborative laboratory analysis reports, leave no doubt that the Respondent's industrial effluent has repeatedly exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for critical parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. These violations were not isolated

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,600,000/- (Rupees One Million Six Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 320,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
