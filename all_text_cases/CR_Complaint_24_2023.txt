**Judgment**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 24/2023, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent" or "the Mill"), concerning alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations. The Tribunal was constituted under Section 20 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter "the Act"), to adjudicate matters related to environmental pollution.
**Introduction**
The present complaint was lodged on September 15, 2023, following multiple inspections and laboratory analyses conducted by the Complainant at the Respondent's textile manufacturing facility located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The EPA, in its capacity as the primary regulatory body responsible for environmental protection in the province, initiated these proceedings after observing persistent non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents. The core issue revolves around the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated wastewater into the municipal sewerage system, eventually impacting local water bodies. The Tribunal assumed jurisdiction under Section 17 of the Act to hear and determine this complaint, aiming to enforce environmental standards and ensure sustainable industrial practices.
**Allegations**
The Complainant's primary allegations against M/s Nishat Mills Limited are as follows:
1.  **Discharge of Untreated/Sub-Standard Effluent**: That the Respondent has been consistently discharging industrial effluent from its textile dyeing and finishing processes without adequate treatment, or with treatment that fails to meet the prescribed NEQS for industrial effluents, into the public sewerage system.
2.  **Violation of NEQS**: Specifically, that laboratory analyses of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points consistently show parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and colour exceeding the limits stipulated in the NEQS, as notified under the Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
3.  **Failure to Operate and Maintain Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP)**: That despite having an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) on paper, the Respondent either fails to operate it effectively, or the ETP is inadequate in design or capacity to treat the volume and characteristics of the effluent generated by the Mill, leading to chronic non-compliance.
4.  **Non-Compliance with Environmental Management Plan (EMP)**: That the Respondent has failed to adhere to the Environmental Management Plan (EMP) outlined in its approved Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) or Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) report, particularly regarding wastewater management.
5.  **Causing Environmental Degradation**: That the continuous discharge of sub-standard effluent contributes to the pollution of surface and groundwater resources, posing risks to public health and aquatic ecosystems.
**Evidence**
The Complainant presented the following evidence:
1.  **Inspection Reports**: Multiple inspection reports dated July 10, 2023, August 3, 2023, and August 25, 2023, detailing observations of the Respondent's ETP operation, discharge points, and general environmental management practices. These reports consistently noted signs of inadequate treatment and non-compliance.
2.  **Effluent Sample Collection and Analysis Reports**: Certified laboratory analysis reports from the EPA-accredited laboratory for samples collected on July 10, 2023, August 3, 2023, and August 25, 2023. These reports conclusively showed that the discharged effluent exceeded the NEQS limits for COD (ranging from 350-480 mg/L against a limit of 200 mg/L), BOD5 (ranging from 180-250 mg/L against a limit of 80 mg/L), TSS (ranging from 220-300 mg/L against a limit of 200 mg/L), and exhibited significant colouration (above permissible standards).
3.  **Photographic and Videographic Evidence**: Visual records depicting the colour and turbidity of the discharged effluent
, and the impact of such discharge on the receiving environment.
**Respondent's Defence**
The Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, through its learned counsel, vehemently contested the allegations. It was argued that the Mill operates a state-of-the-art Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and is committed to environmental compliance. The Respondent contended that:
1.  **ETP Functionality**: The ETP is fully functional and regularly maintained, with records of maintenance logs and operational parameters.
2.  **Sampling Irregularities**: The samples collected by the Complainant might not be representative, or the sampling methodology employed could be flawed, leading to inaccurate results. It was suggested that samples might have been taken during process upsets or from points not indicative of the final treated effluent.
3.  **Internal Monitoring**: The Respondent presented internal monitoring reports, claiming that their treated effluent generally meets NEQS, attributing any occasional exceedances to unforeseen operational variations or peak production loads.
4.  **Commitment to Environment**: The Respondent reiterated its commitment to environmental protection and continuous improvement, highlighting past investments in environmental infrastructure.
**Proceedings and Arguments**
The Tribunal meticulously heard arguments from both sides over several sessions. The Complainant's counsel presented a compelling case, relying heavily on the consistency of the laboratory reports and the observations made during multiple inspections. It was emphasized that the repeated nature of the violations, despite prior warnings and opportunities for corrective action, pointed towards a systemic failure in the Respondent's environmental management practices.
The Respondent's counsel cross-examined the Complainant's witnesses regarding the chain of custody for samples, laboratory accreditation, and the interpretation of NEQS parameters. While acknowledging the importance of environmental compliance, the Respondent sought to cast doubt on the conclusiveness of the EPA's evidence and presented their internal records to counter the allegations.
The Tribunal also considered the socio-economic implications of any potential order while prioritizing the paramount importance of environmental protection and public health.
**Findings of the Tribunal**
After a thorough review of the evidence, the arguments presented, and relevant legal provisions, the Tribunal makes the following findings:
1.  **Reliability of Complainant's Evidence**: The inspection reports, coupled with the certified laboratory analysis reports from an EPA-accredited laboratory, provide robust and consistent evidence. The multiple samples collected on different dates, all showing significant exceedances of NEQS, negate the Respondent's claim of occasional excursions or sampling irregularities. The parameters like COD, BOD5, TSS, and colour were consistently found to be beyond permissible limits, indicating a persistent problem.
2.  **Failure to Meet NEQS**: The Tribunal finds that the Respondent has consistently failed to treat its industrial effluent to meet the National Environmental Quality Standards as prescribed under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. The evidence unequivocally demonstrates violations of Section 11 of the Act.
3.  **Ineffective ETP Operation**: The Respondent's assertion of a fully functional ETP is directly contradicted by the analytical results. While an ETP may exist, its operation is either inadequate, inefficient, or it is not designed to handle the characteristics and volume of the effluent generated, leading to chronic non-compliance.
4.  **Environmental Degradation**: The continuous discharge of sub-standard effluent by the Respondent, as proven by the evidence, contributes directly to environmental degradation, polluting water bodies and posing risks to public health and aquatic life.
5.  **Lack of Diligence**: Despite repeated inspections and warnings, the Respondent failed to take effective and sustainable corrective measures to ensure compliance, demonstrating a lack of diligence in fulfilling its environmental obligations.
**Conclusion**
Based on the overwhelming and consistent evidence presented by the Complainant, the Tribunal concludes that M/s Nishat Mills Limited is in clear and persistent violation of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards for industrial effluents. The allegations made by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, stand proven beyond reasonable doubt. Environmental compliance is not merely a regulatory burden but a fundamental responsibility, and industries must operate within the framework of laws designed to protect public health and the environment.
**Order and Penalty**
In light of the foregoing findings, and in exercise of the powers vested under Section 17(3) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the Tribunal hereby issues the following order and imposes the following penalty:
1.  **Conviction**: M/s Nishat Mills Limited is hereby found guilty of violating Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, for consistently discharging industrial effluent in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards.
2.  **Fine**: A penalty of **PKR 2,500,000/- (Rupees Two Million Five Hundred Thousand only)** is imposed on M/s Nishat Mills Limited
, and shall be deposited into the account of the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within **sixty (60) days** from the date of this order.
3.  **Mandatory Corrective Measures**: M/s Nishat Mills Limited is hereby directed to forthwith take all necessary and effective steps to ensure that its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) operates at its optimal capacity and is adequately upgraded, if required, to consistently meet all parameters of the National Environmental Quality Standards for industrial effluents.
4.  **Submission of Compliance Plan**: The Respondent shall submit a detailed and comprehensive **Compliance Plan** to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this order. This plan must outline specific technical and operational measures, including any proposed upgrades, operational protocols, and a clear timeline for achieving and sustaining full compliance with NEQS. The EPA shall review and approve this plan.
5.  **Regular Monitoring and Reporting**: For a period of **one (1) year** following the approval of the Compliance Plan, the Respondent shall conduct weekly internal monitoring of its treated effluent for all NEQS parameters and submit certified laboratory reports to the EPA on a monthly basis. The EPA is further directed to conduct unannounced inspections and collect independent samples at least twice a month for the first six months to verify compliance and provide its findings to the Tribunal if non-compliance persists.
6.  **Consequences of Further Non-Compliance**: Any future failure by M/s Nishat Mills Limited to comply with the NEQS, or any part of this order, shall result in more stringent penalties, which may include enhanced fines, immediate closure of the industrial unit, and other coercive measures as provided under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
7.  **Costs**: The Respondent shall bear the costs of these proceedings.
The Complaint No. 24/2023 stands disposed of in these terms.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal has carefully examined the inspection report, laboratory analysis results, and the photographic evidence produced by the Complainant. The measured pollutant concentrations significantly exceed the NEQS limits and the Respondent has failed to rebut this evidence with credible contrary data. The defense regarding ETP maintenance does not excuse ongoing pollution discharge and cannot serve as a justification for non-compliance with mandatory environmental standards.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,900,000/- (Rupees One Million Nine Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 380,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
