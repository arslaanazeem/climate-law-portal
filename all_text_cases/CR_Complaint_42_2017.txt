This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent industrial enterprise engaged in the manufacturing of packaging materials, paper, and allied products, situated in Lahore. The complaint, registered on 15th May 2017, alleges serious violations of environmental laws and standards, posing a potential threat to public health and the environment.
The Complainant asserted that the Respondent's industrial unit was operating in contravention of the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). Specifically, the allegations included the discharge of industrial effluent containing pollutants far exceeding the prescribed NEQS without adequate treatment, and the emission of air pollutants, particularly particulate matter and gaseous emissions from its operational stacks, also in excess of the NEQS. The EPA further alleged that despite previous warnings and notices, the Respondent had failed to install or effectively operate appropriate pollution control devices, thereby causing environmental degradation and public nuisance to the surrounding community. The Complainant sought a finding of guilt against the Respondent, imposition of penalties as stipulated by law, and directives for immediate compliance with environmental regulations.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on various dates, including 15th March 2017 and 28th April 2017. These reports meticulously documented observations of dark-colored effluent being discharged from the Respondent's premises into a nearby drain, and visible smoke emissions from multiple stacks. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on these dates, which indicated alarmingly high levels of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and certain heavy metals, all significantly exceeding the NEQS for industrial effluents. Similarly, air quality monitoring reports, based on samples taken from the Respondent's stacks, revealed concentrations of particulate matter (PM10 and PM2.5), Sulfur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) that surpassed the prescribed NEQS for industrial air emissions. Expert testimony from EPA chemists and environmental engineers corroborated these findings, explaining the methodologies used for sampling and analysis, and the environmental implications of the observed exceedances. The Complainant also presented photographic evidence showing the visible pollution and inadequacies in the Respondent'
's pollution control mechanisms and treatment facilities.
The Respondent, represented by its learned counsel, Mr. Asif Mehmood, vehemently contested the allegations. While acknowledging its industrial operations and the generation of effluent and emissions, the Respondent asserted that it had invested substantially in an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs). It was argued that the facility was generally compliant with environmental standards, and any reported exceedances were either isolated incidents, temporary aberrations during maintenance periods, or a result of potentially unrepresentative sampling methodologies employed by the Complainant. The Respondent presented its own internal environmental audit reports and operational logs, along with a statement from
its environmental manager, Mr. Kamran Ali, who detailed the operational parameters of their Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs), emphasizing their effectiveness when properly maintained. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
