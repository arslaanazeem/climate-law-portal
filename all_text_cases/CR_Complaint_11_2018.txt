This judgment addresses a complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited, hereinafter referred to as the Respondent, alleging violations of the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The complaint, registered on 15th May 2018, seeks the imposition of penalties and directives for compliance, citing the Respondent's failure to adhere to prescribed environmental standards in its industrial operations located at [Fictional Location, e.g., Manga Mandi Road, Lahore]. The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the submissions, evidence, and arguments presented by both parties to arrive at a just and lawful determination.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's industrial activities, specifically the manufacturing of glass products, which are asserted to be causing significant environmental pollution. It is alleged that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has been discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluents and emitting gaseous pollutants from its factory premises into the environment, exceeding the permissible limits set by the NEQS. The specific violations highlighted include:
a.   **Air Pollution:** Emission of particulate matter (PM), oxides of nitrogen (NOx), and sulphur dioxide (SO2) from its furnace stacks substantially above the NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions. The Complainant contends that the existing air pollution control devices, if any, are either inefficient or improperly maintained.
b.   **Water Pollution:** Discharge of industrial wastewater containing elevated levels of Total Suspended Solids (TSS), chemical oxygen demand (COD), and having an abnormal pH and high temperature, thereby contravening the NEQS for industrial effluents. This discharge is reported to be affecting nearby agricultural lands and water bodies.
c.   **Failure to Comply with Environmental Protection Order (EPO):** The Complainant asserts that prior warnings and an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) issued under Section 16 of the Act on 1st March 2018, requiring the Respondent to install and operate adequate pollution control equipment and submit a compliance plan, were largely ignored or inadequately addressed.
d.   **Lack of Proper Environmental Management:** General negligence in adopting environmentally sound practices and a consistent failure to monitor and report its emissions and effluents as per regulatory requirements.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive body of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports prepared by EPA officials dated 15th January 2018, 20th February 2018, and 10th May 2018, detailing observations of dark smoke plumes emanating from factory stacks and direct discharge of untreated wastewater. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from an accredited laboratory, confirming that samples collected from the Respondent's emission stacks and effluent discharge points consistently showed parameters
exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for both air emissions and industrial effluents by significant margins. The Complainant also produced copies of the Environmental Protection Order (EPO) dated 1st March 2018, and subsequent reminders, demonstrating the Respondent's consistent failure to comply with regulatory directives.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations, asserting that it is a responsible corporate entity committed to environmental protection. The Respondent contended that it has invested substantially in pollution control measures, including the installation of an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices such as bag filters and scrubbers. They argued that any occasional exceedances were minor, temporary, and attributable to unforeseen operational fluctuations or maintenance activities, rather than a deliberate disregard for environmental laws. The Respondent submitted internal monitoring reports, which, contrary to the Complainant's findings, purported to show compliance with NEQS in most instances. They also challenged the methodology and chain of custody of the samples collected by the EPA, suggesting that the results presented by the Complainant might be inaccurate or unrepresentative. Furthermore, the Respondent claimed that it had initiated steps to upgrade its existing pollution control infrastructure and had engaged environmental consultants to prepare a detailed compliance action plan, which was in the final stages of submission. They requested the Tribunal to consider their ongoing efforts and provide a reasonable timeframe for achieving full compliance, rather than imposing harsh penalties.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments, documentary evidence, and laboratory reports submitted by both parties. It is evident from the multiple inspection reports and the accredited laboratory analysis presented by the Complainant that the Respondent's industrial operations have, on several occasions, resulted in the discharge of effluents and emission of gases exceeding the prescribed NEQS. The consistency of these violations across different dates and parameters, as evidenced by the Complainant's reports, undermines the Respondent's claim of minor or temporary exceedances. While the Respondent asserted the existence of pollution control devices, the laboratory results conclusively demonstrate their inadequacy or inefficient operation to meet the statutory standards. The challenge to the EPA's sampling methodology was unsubstantiated by concrete evidence, and the Tribunal finds the Complainant's evidence, particularly the accredited laboratory reports, to be credible and reliable.
Moreover, the Tribunal notes with concern the Respondent's failure to adequately respond to the Environmental Protection Order (EPO) issued under Section 16 of the Act. The purpose of an EPO is to provide an opportunity for compliance, and the Respondent's lack of effective action following its issuance reinforces the Complainant's assertion of non-compliance. While the Respondent's stated commitment to upgrading its facilities and preparing a compliance plan is acknowledged, these are prospective actions and do not negate the established fact of past and ongoing violations. The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, particularly Sections 11 and 13, explicitly prohibits the discharge or emission of pollutants in excess of the NEQS, and Section 16 empowers the EPA to issue orders for compliance. Section 17 of the Act prescribes penalties for contravention or failure to comply with these provisions or any EPO.
Based on the preponderance of evidence, the Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully established that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has violated the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards. The Respondent's conduct amounts to a contravention of Section 11 (Prohibition of certain discharges or emissions) and Section 13 (Handling of hazardous substances) of the Act, and a failure to comply with the Environmental Protection Order issued under Section 16 of the Act.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Punjab Environmental Tribunal, Lahore, hereby orders as follows:
1.  **Declaration of Violation:** M/s Ghani Glass Limited is found guilty of violating the provisions of Section 11 and Section 13 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and of failing to comply with the Environmental Protection Order issued under Section 16 of the Act.
2.  **Imposition of Fine:** Pursuant to Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, M/s Ghani Glass Limited is hereby imposed a fine of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million Only)** for the established contraventions and failures to comply. This fine shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Government of Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this order.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **23 March, 2023**.
