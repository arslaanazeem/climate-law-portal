This judgment addresses Complaint No. 2/2024, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent industrial entity engaged in the manufacturing of packaging materials, paper, and board products. The complaint invokes the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 19 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleging various violations of environmental laws and regulations. The Tribunal is seized with the responsibility to determine whether the Respondent has contravened environmental standards and, if so, to impose appropriate penalties and remedial measures.
The Complainant's allegations primarily revolve around the Respondent's alleged failure to comply with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents and gaseous emissions, as prescribed under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and its associated rules and regulations. Specifically, it was alleged that during routine inspections conducted by EPA officials on various dates, including 15.11.2023, 28.12.2023, and 10.01.2024, the Respondent's industrial operations were found to be discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/surface drains. Laboratory analysis of samples collected on these dates reportedly indicated levels of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and certain heavy metals (e.g., Chromium and Lead) significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS limits. Furthermore, the complaint detailed concerns regarding air emissions from the Respondent's boiler house and power generation units, with preliminary monitoring suggesting exceedances of NEQS for Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx) and Particulate Matter (PM) on 28.12.2023. The Complainant asserted that despite repeated verbal and written warnings, including Environmental Protection Orders (EPOs) issued under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997, the Respondent had failed to take adequate corrective measures, thereby causing environmental degradation and posing a threat to public health and the ecosystem.
In support of its allegations, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA field teams on 15.11.2023, 28.12.2023, and 10.01.2024, meticulously documenting the observations of non-compliance. Crucially, the Complainant submitted certified laboratory analysis reports from the EPA-accredited laboratory, confirming the exceedance of NEQS for effluent parameters (BOD 350 mg/L, COD 800 mg/L, TSS 450 mg/L, Chromium 1.5 mg/L, Lead 0.8 mg/L against NEQS of BOD 80 mg/L, COD 150 mg/L, TSS 200 mg/L, Chromium 1.0 mg/L, Lead 0.5 mg/L, respectively) and air emissions (NOx 900 mg/Nm3, PM 250 mg/Nm3 against NEQS of NOx 600 mg/Nm3, PM 150 mg/Nm3, respectively). Photographic and videographic evidence depicting the discharge points and air emission stacks were also tendered. Testimonies were recorded from Mr. Ahmed Khan, Assistant Director, EPA, and Mr. Bilal Shah, Environmental Analyst, who conducted the inspections and analyses. On its part, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, vehemently denied the allegations, asserting that it operates an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices (APCDs) and is committed to environmental compliance. The Respondent presented its internal monitoring reports, which, surprisingly, showed compliance with NEQS. They argued that the EPA's sampling methodology might have been flawed or that the samples were not representative of their treated discharge. They also submitted records of recent upgrades to their ETP and APCDs, along with a commitment to conduct a third-party environmental audit. However, the Respondent failed to produce any independent third-party validated reports to counter the specific findings of the EPA's accredited laboratory.
The legal framework governing this complaint is primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. Section 11 of
the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste, or the air pollutant, or noise in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) or, where applicable, the standards established under sub-section (1) of Section 11. Section 16 empowers the Agency to issue an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) requiring a person to take such measures as specified in the order to mitigate or prevent environmental damage. Section 19 vests this Tribunal with the jurisdiction to hear complaints against violations of the Act and its rules. Crucially, Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, prescribes penalties for contravention of Section 11 or any condition of an EPO, stipulating a fine which may extend to one million rupees, and in case of a continuing contravention, an additional fine which may extend to one hundred thousand rupees for every day during which such contravention continues.
Having considered the submissions from both parties and meticulously reviewed the documentary and testimonial evidence presented, this Tribunal finds the Complainant's assertions to be well-substantiated. The core of the Complainant's case rests upon the detailed inspection reports and, more significantly, the certified laboratory analysis reports from an EPA-accredited laboratory. These reports unequivocally demonstrate that the Respondent's industrial effluent and air emissions consistently exceeded the prescribed NEQS on multiple occasions. The levels of BOD, COD, TSS, Chromium, Lead, NOx, and PM were found to be significantly higher than the permissible limits, indicating a substantial failure in the Respondent's environmental management systems. The photographic and videographic evidence further corroborated the observations of the EPA field teams regarding the discharge points and emission stacks. The testimonies of Mr. Ahmed Khan and Mr. Bilal Shah provided direct evidence of the inspection procedures and analytical findings, affirming the credibility and reliability of the Complainant's evidence.
Conversely, the Respondent's defense, while asserting a commitment to environmental compliance and the operation of an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices (APCDs), failed to effectively counter the specific and robust evidence presented by the Complainant. The Respondent's internal monitoring reports, showing compliance, stand in stark contrast to the independent, accredited laboratory results obtained by the EPA. Without independent third-party validation, the Respondent's internal reports carry less probative value compared to the findings of an accredited regulatory laboratory. The Respondent's arguments regarding flawed sampling methodology or non-representative samples were unsubstantiated by any credible counter-evidence. The mere assertion of having an ETP and APCDs is insufficient; the critical requirement is that these systems must operate effectively to ensure compliance with NEQS. The fact that EPOs were issued and, according to the Complainant, not adequately complied with, further strengthens the case for persistent non-compliance. The Tribunal places significant weight on the objective, scientific evidence provided by the EPA's accredited laboratory, which clearly demonstrates a breach of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and a failure to adhere to the conditions of the EPOs issued under Section 16. The environmental degradation caused by the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated effluent and excessive air emissions poses a serious threat to public health and the surrounding ecosystem, warranting immediate and decisive action.
Therefore, this Tribunal finds the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, guilty of contravening the provisions of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and failing to comply with the Environmental Protection Orders issued under Section 16 of the said Act.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **15 March, 2023**.
