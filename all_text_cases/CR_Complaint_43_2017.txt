PUNJAB ENVIRONMENTAL TRIBUNAL
LAHORE
COMPLAINT NO. 43/2017
Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore

VERSUS
M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited

Presided by: Justice (R) Nasir Saeed Sheikh
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 43/2017, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited. The complaint, registered on 15th May 2017, alleges persistent non-compliance with environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of untreated industrial effluent and air emissions exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal, established under Section 22 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), has jurisdiction to adjudicate matters related to environmental pollution and impose penalties for contraventions of environmental laws. The core issue before this Tribunal is to determine whether the Respondent has violated the provisions of PEPA, 1997, and its allied rules and regulations, and if so, to issue appropriate orders for compliance and impose penalties.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, an industrial unit engaged in textile manufacturing located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore, has been operating without adequate environmental safeguards. Specifically, the allegations included:
a) Discharge of untreated or partially treated industrial effluent directly into the Lahore Branch Canal and the municipal sewerage system, containing pollutants far exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial effluents.
b) Emission of air pollutants (particulate matter, oxides of nitrogen, sulfur dioxide) from its boiler stacks and process units without proper treatment or control mechanisms, leading to ambient air quality deterioration in the surrounding areas.
c) Failure to obtain the requisite No Objection Certificates (NOCs) from the EPA for its industrial operations, particularly for new expansions or modifications, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, and the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)/Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) Regulations, 2000.
d) Non-installation and non-operation of an effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) in accordance with the industry best practices and NEQS.
e) Persistent disregard for notices and warnings issued by the EPA for non-compliance, demonstrating a lack of commitment to environmental protection.
The Complainant contended that these actions constitute a clear violation of Sections 11 and 12 of PEPA, 1997, and warrant immediate intervention by this Tribunal.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of documents and expert testimonies. These included:
i. Multiple inspection reports conducted by EPA teams on various dates, specifically 12th January 2017, 8th March 2017, and 25th April 2017, detailing observations of dark-colored effluent discharge and visible smoke emissions.
ii. Laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on 12th January 2017 and 8th March 2017, which showed alarming levels of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) at 850 mg/L (NEQS: 150 mg/L), Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD5) at 320 mg/L (NEQS: 80 mg/L), Total Suspended Solids (TSS) at 450 mg/L (NEQS: 200 mg/L), and pH values outside the permissible range (NEQS: 6-9).
iii. Air emission monitoring reports from stack sampling conducted on 25th April 2017, indicating particulate matter (PM) concentrations of 650 mg/Nm³ (NEQS: 400 mg/Nm³ for existing units) and SOx levels exceeding standards.
iv. Copies of show-cause
notices issued to the Respondent on 20th January 2017, 15th March 2017, and 30th April 2017, demanding explanations for the observed non-compliances, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies or, in some instances, complete lack of response.
v. Expert testimony from an environmental chemist,
who confirmed the inaccuracies in the Respondent's self-monitoring reports and the severe environmental degradation caused by the discharge of untreated effluent and uncontrolled air emissions. This expert also highlighted the potential long-term health impacts on the local population and damage to aquatic ecosystems.
The Respondent, M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited, represented by its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. Their defense primarily rested on the following points:
a) That the industrial unit was already undergoing a phased upgrade of its environmental infrastructure, including the installation of a new, advanced Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and improved Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs). They claimed that the work was in progress and would be completed within a reasonable timeframe.
b) That the samples collected by the EPA were not representative or were taken during unusual operational conditions, leading to skewed results. They presented their own internal monitoring reports, which, according to them, showed compliance with NEQS in certain parameters or at least a trend towards compliance.
c) That the delay in obtaining NOCs was due to procedural complexities and not a deliberate defiance of regulations. They argued that applications for NOCs were pending with the EPA.
d) That the economic viability of the unit would be severely impacted by immediate and stringent compliance orders, potentially leading to job losses and adverse economic consequences. They requested leniency and a longer grace period for compliance.
e) They also contended that the EPA had not provided adequate technical guidance or support for compliance, and that some of the NEQS were overly stringent for the textile sector.
To support their defense, the Respondent presented:
i. Procurement documents and contracts for the new ETP and APCDs, indicating an ongoing investment in environmental infrastructure.
ii. Internal laboratory reports, albeit with less frequency and detail than EPA reports, attempting to show partial compliance.
iii. Correspondence with EPA regarding NOC applications.
iv. Testimonies from their technical staff, explaining the complexities of treating textile effluent and controlling air emissions.
The Tribunal carefully considered the arguments, evidence, and testimonies presented by both parties. It is an established principle that environmental protection is a fundamental right and a constitutional obligation. The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, provides a robust legal framework to ensure sustainable development and prevent pollution. Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste in an amount, concentration, or level that exceeds the NEQS. Section 12 mandates obtaining an NOC for new projects or modifications to existing projects that are likely to cause an adverse environmental effect.
Upon thorough examination of the evidence, the Tribunal finds the Complainant's case to be substantially proven. The multiple inspection reports, corroborated by independent laboratory analyses of effluent and air emission samples, consistently demonstrate a clear and persistent violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards. The levels of COD, BOD5, TSS, and PM significantly exceeded the permissible limits, indicating a severe failure to adequately treat industrial waste before discharge and emission. The pH values outside the prescribed range are particularly concerning, posing direct threats to aquatic life and soil quality.
While the Respondent's claims of initiating upgrades are noted, these efforts appear to be reactive and insufficient given the long history of non-compliance and repeated warnings from the EPA. The internal monitoring reports presented by the Respondent were inconsistent with the independent analyses and lacked the transparency and rigor required for credible environmental compliance verification. The argument regarding the economic impact, while relevant in some contexts, cannot override the fundamental duty to protect public health and the environment. Environmental externalities must be internalized, and the cost of pollution prevention is an integral part of doing business. Furthermore, the failure to obtain requisite NOCs for operations and expansions, as mandated by Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, is a clear procedural and substantive violation.
The Tribunal concludes that M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited has indeed violated Sections 11 and 12 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and its allied rules and regulations, specifically by:
1. Discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent exceeding NEQS into public waterways and sewerage systems.
2. Emitting air pollutants exceeding NEQS without proper control mechanisms.
3. Failing to obtain the necessary No Objection Certificates (NOCs) for its operations and expansions.
4. Demonstrating persistent disregard for environmental notices and warnings.
The cumulative effect of these violations has resulted in significant environmental degradation and poses a serious threat to public health and ecological balance in the surrounding areas.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal hereby issues the following order and imposes penalties:
1.  **Cessation of Violations:** M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited is hereby directed to immediately cease the discharge of any untreated or partially treated industrial effluent that exceeds the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and to halt all air emissions that violate the NEQS.
2.  **Installation and Operation of Environmental Infrastructure:** The Respondent shall, within **ninety (90) days** from the date of this judgment, install and ensure the full operationalization of a fully functional and effective Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and appropriate Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) that consistently meet the NEQS for all relevant parameters. The design and operational parameters of these systems shall be submitted to the EPA for approval within **thirty (30) days**.
3.  **Obtaining NOCs:** The Respondent is directed to immediately complete all formalities and obtain the necessary No Objection Certificates (NOCs) from the Environmental Protection Agency for all its operational units and expansions, as required under Section 12 of PEPA, 1997, within **sixty (60) days**.
4.  **Environmental Monitoring and Reporting:** The Respondent shall implement a robust self-monitoring program, including regular sampling and laboratory analysis of its effluent and air emissions by an EPA-certified laboratory. Reports of such monitoring shall be submitted to the EPA on a monthly basis for a period of one year, commencing from the date of full compliance with NEQS.
5.  **Environmental Management Plan:** The Respondent shall submit a comprehensive Environmental Management Plan (EMP) to the EPA within **forty-five (45) days**, outlining measures for sustainable environmental practices, waste management, and emergency response.
6.  **Monetary Penalty:** In accordance with Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, a penalty of **PKR 2,000,000/- (Rupees Two Million Only)** is hereby imposed on M/s Sapphire Fibres Limited for the persistent violations of environmental laws and regulations. This amount shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within **thirty (30) days** from the date of this judgment.
7.  **Daily Penalty for Non-Compliance:** Failure to comply with any part of this order, particularly the timelines stipulated in clauses 2 and 3, shall result in an additional daily penalty of **PKR 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand Only)** for each day of continued non-compliance, until full compliance is achieved. The EPA is authorized to calculate and recover this daily penalty.
8.  **Closure Order:** In the event of continued and deliberate non-compliance with this order, particularly after the expiry of the ninety (90) day period for ETP/APCD operationalization, the Tribunal reserves the right to issue an order for the temporary or permanent closure of the Respondent's industrial unit, as per the provisions of PEPA, 1997.
9.  **Costs:** The Respondent shall bear the costs of these proceedings, assessed at PKR 100,000/- (Rupees One Hundred Thousand Only), to be paid to the Complainant within thirty (30) days.
The Registrar of the Tribunal is directed to ensure strict compliance with this order and to report any instances of non-compliance to the Tribunal for further necessary action.
