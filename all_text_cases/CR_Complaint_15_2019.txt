**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 15/2019, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent chemical manufacturing facility located in Kala Shah Kaku Industrial Estate, Sheikhupura. The complaint invokes the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), seeking an Environmental Protection Order and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of environmental laws and National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The proceedings commenced following persistent reports and direct observations by EPA officials regarding significant environmental degradation attributed to the Respondent's industrial operations.
The Complainant, through its diligent field inspections and laboratory analyses, alleged that the Respondent has been consistently discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system and ultimately into local water bodies, thereby exceeding the permissible limits set by the NEQS for various parameters, including but not limited to Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and heavy metals. Furthermore, the complaint detailed allegations of uncontrolled release of hazardous air pollutants, particularly sulfur dioxide (SO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), and particulate matter, from the Respondent's stacks, which similarly contravened the NEQS for ambient air quality and industrial emissions. The EPA also highlighted the Respondent's alleged failure to properly manage and dispose of hazardous waste generated during its manufacturing processes, leading to environmental contamination and potential risks to public health and biodiversity. Specific instances of non-compliance, including the failure to install and effectively operate a functional effluent treatment plant (ETP) and air pollution control devices (APCDs) commensurate with the scale and nature of its operations, despite repeated warnings and show-cause notices issued by the EPA, formed the crux of the allegations.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included multiple inspection reports dated 12.03.2019, 28.05.2019, and 15.08.2019, detailing on-site observations of effluent discharge points, stack emissions, and waste storage practices. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, corresponding to samples collected during these inspections, were submitted. These reports unequivocally demonstrated exceedances of NEQS for effluent discharge (e.g., COD values ranging from 450-600 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L; TSS values between 300-450 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L; pH outside the 6-9 range) and air emissions (e.g., SO2 concentrations significantly above permissible limits). Furthermore, copies of show-cause notices issued to M/s Ittehad Chemicals
Limited on various dates, along with their acknowledgments, were also presented, demonstrating the Respondent's consistent disregard for regulatory directives. The Complainant also submitted expert affidavits from environmental scientists attesting to the adverse impacts of such untreated discharges and emissions on the local ecosystem and public health.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited, through its legal counsel, initially contested the factual accuracy of some inspection reports and the methodology of sample collection. However, during subsequent hearings, the Respondent largely failed to present compelling counter-evidence or independent laboratory reports that could credibly refute the Complainant's findings. While the Respondent acknowledged the existence of an effluent treatment plant and air pollution control devices, it admitted to operational challenges and occasional breaches of NEQS, attributing them to technical glitches and maintenance issues. The Respondent also presented a vague commitment to upgrade its facilities but did not provide a concrete timeline or detailed plan of action during the proceedings, nor did it demonstrate significant progress in rectifying the identified violations despite ample opportunity. The Tribunal noted the Respondent's failure to adequately respond to the repeated show-cause notices and to implement effective measures to bring its operations into compliance.
Having meticulously reviewed the pleadings, documentary evidence, and arguments presented by both parties, this Tribunal finds that the Complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof. The evidence, particularly the consistent exceedances of NEQS as documented in multiple laboratory analysis reports over several months, coupled with the Respondent's admitted operational deficiencies and failure to demonstrate effective remedial action, unequivocally establishes a pattern
of persistent environmental non-compliance by M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited. The Respondent's arguments regarding technical glitches and maintenance issues, while acknowledged, do not absolve it of its statutory duty to comply with environmental laws and NEQS. The repeated nature of the violations, despite ample warnings and opportunities for rectification, indicates a systemic failure to prioritize environmental protection and adhere to regulatory directives. Such conduct is detrimental to public health, local ecosystems, and undermines the very spirit of environmental governance established by PEPA, 1997.
Therefore, this Tribunal finds M/s Ittehad Chemicals Limited guilty of violating Sections 11, 12, and 13 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act,

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

The evidence presented by the Punjab EPA is comprehensive, consisting of original inspection reports signed by authorized officers, certified laboratory analyses, and photographic documentation of pollution discharge. This Tribunal finds that the Complainant has discharged its evidentiary burden and the pollution violations are proven. The Respondent's argument that samples were not collected in its presence is not supported by the record and is accordingly rejected.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **26 September, 2023**.
