This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited, an industrial entity operating in the manufacturing sector. The Punjab Environmental Tribunal is seized with jurisdiction to adjudicate this matter under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"). The complaint alleges serious violations of environmental laws and regulations by the respondent, particularly concerning the discharge of industrial effluents and emissions, posing a significant threat to public health and the environment.
The complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that M/s Ghani Glass Limited, situated in the industrial area of Lahore, was operating without adequate environmental safeguards. Specifically, the allegations included: (a) persistent discharge of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the public sewerage system/nearby water bodies, exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for various parameters such as Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH; (b) emission of air pollutants, including Particulate Matter, Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), from its industrial processes, which similarly exceeded the prescribed NEQS for air emissions; (c) failure to install and effectively operate a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and appropriate Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs); and (d) non-compliance with previous warning notices and directions issued by the EPA for rectifying these environmental violations. The complainant asserted that these acts constituted a direct contravention of Section 11 of the Act and the NEQS promulgated thereunder, warranting punitive action and remedial measures.
To substantiate its claims, the complainant presented a series of documents and testimonies. Key evidence included multiple site inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on various dates between January 2020 and September 2020, which consistently highlighted operational deficiencies in the respondent's environmental management systems. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected from the respondent’s discharge points on 15.02.2020, 10.05.2020, and 28.08.2020, and air emission samples taken on 01.03.2020 and 15.07.2020, were submitted. These reports, prepared by the EPA's accredited laboratory, conclusively demonstrated that the pollutant levels in both the discharged effluent and stack emissions significantly exceeded the NEQS. For instance, BOD levels were found to be 350 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 80 mg/L, and TSS at 450 mg/L against a NEQS limit of 200 mg/L for effluents, while Particulate Matter in air emissions was recorded at 650 mg/Nm3 against a NEQS limit of 200 mg/Nm3. The complainant also produced copies of show-cause notices issued to the respondent on 05.03.
2020, 05.07.2020, and 20.09.2020, which had instructed the respondent to rectify the identified violations and submit a compliance report, failing which legal proceedings would be initiated. The complainant emphasized that despite these repeated warnings, the respondent had shown a consistent disregard for environmental regulations, necessitating the present complaint before the Tribunal.
The respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It was contended that the company is a responsible industrial entity committed to sustainable practices and has invested significantly in environmental protection measures. The respondent argued that the samples collected by the EPA were not representative, or that the sampling methodology was flawed, leading to inaccurate laboratory results. They further submitted that an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs) were indeed installed and operational, albeit acknowledging that minor operational adjustments and upgrades were sometimes necessary due to the complex nature of their manufacturing processes. The respondent’s counsel presented internal maintenance records and purchase orders for chemicals used in the ETP as evidence of their efforts. They also claimed to have initiated a project for further upgrading their ETP and APCDs, providing a tentative timeline for completion. While admitting receipt of some warning notices, the respondent asserted that they had responded to these notices, outlining their ongoing efforts and challenges. They sought dismissal of the complaint, or in the alternative, a lenient view and a reasonable timeframe to achieve full compliance, without imposing punitive penalties.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties. The complainant's evidence, particularly the multiple site inspection reports and the consistent pattern of high pollutant levels in the EPA's accredited laboratory analysis reports, established a strong *prima facie* case of non-compliance. The detailed laboratory results, indicating significant exceedances of NEQS for critical parameters in both effluent and air emissions, could not be dismissed lightly. While the respondent claimed flaws in sampling, no concrete evidence or counter-analysis from an independent accredited laboratory was presented to challenge the EPA's findings. The internal maintenance records and purchase orders submitted by the respondent, while indicating some level of environmental effort, did not negate the objective findings of the EPA's lab reports, which demonstrated actual pollutant discharge exceeding the prescribed standards. The Tribunal noted that the mere presence of an ETP or APCDs is insufficient; their effective operation to meet NEQS is the statutory requirement under Section 11 of the Act. The respondent's admission of "minor operational adjustments and upgrades" and the initiation of a "project for further upgrading" implicitly acknowledged existing deficiencies in their environmental management systems. The repeated warning notices issued by the EPA, coupled with the respondent's failure to achieve compliance within the stipulated periods, further underscored the persistent nature of the violations.
Upon careful consideration of all material facts and legal arguments, the Tribunal finds that the complainant has successfully discharged its burden of proof. The evidence conclusively demonstrates that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has persistently discharged industrial effluents and emitted air pollutants exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards, thereby contravening the provisions of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997. The respondent's arguments, while acknowledging some effort, fail to rebut the objective evidence of non-compliance. The Tribunal holds that the respondent is culpable for the environmental degradation caused by its operations and for failing to comply with the directions of the Environmental Protection Agency. This persistent non-compliance poses an undeniable threat to public health and the environment, warranting stringent action under the Act.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Punjab Environmental Tribunal, exercising its powers under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act,

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,400,000/- (Rupees One Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 280,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **25 December, 2022**.
