**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 61/2020, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent textile manufacturing unit located in Lahore. The complaint seeks enforcement of environmental laws and imposition of penalties for alleged violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties to arrive at a just and lawful determination.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent has been consistently discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into a municipal sewerage system, which eventually flows into natural water bodies, causing significant water pollution. Furthermore, the complaint detailed allegations of excessive air emissions from the Respondent's boiler stacks, exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions. The EPA contended that despite multiple inspections, issuance of show-cause notices, and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent failed to take effective measures to control the pollution emanating from its industrial operations. Specifically, the EPA pointed to the Respondent's alleged failure to properly operate and maintain its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and Air Pollution Control (APC) devices, leading to the contravention of environmental standards and posing a threat to public health and the environment.
To substantiate its allegations, the Complainant presented several pieces of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports dated 15.05.2020, 02.07.2020, and 20.08.2020, documenting observations of dark-colored effluent discharge and visible particulate matter emissions. Crucially, the EPA submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on 15.05.2020 and 20.08.2020, which indicated various parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels far exceeding the NEQS. Similarly, air emission monitoring reports from 02.07.2020 showed particulate matter (PM) and Sulfur Dioxide (SO2) concentrations above the permissible limits. The Complainant also furnished copies of show-cause notices issued on 25.05.2020 and 10.09.2020, along with the Respondent's replies, which, according to the EPA, either acknowledged deficiencies or provided inadequate assurances of compliance without concrete action. The testimony of Mr. Ahmed Khan, Assistant Director (Technical) EPA, further elaborated on the inspection findings and the technical aspects of the violations.
In response, M/s Nishat Mills Limited vehemently denied the allegations of willful non-compliance. The Respondent argued that it is a responsible corporate entity with a commitment to environmental stewardship and has invested substantially in pollution control infrastructure, including a state-of-the-art ETP and APC systems. The Respondent’s counsel challenged the EPA's sampling methodology, alleging that the samples might not have been representative or taken in accordance with established protocols. They also questioned the veracity and calibration status of the EPA's laboratory equipment and the qualifications of the personnel conducting the analyses. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports, which, contrary to the EPA's findings, claimed compliance with NEQS. Furthermore, the Respondent submitted evidence of recent upgrades to its ETP and APC systems, along with a schedule for continuous improvement and maintenance. Mr. Raza Ali
, Environmental Manager for the Respondent, testified regarding the company's substantial investments in environmental infrastructure, detailing the operational procedures of their ETP and APC systems, and highlighting their internal monitoring protocols. He further elaborated on the recent upgrades, including the installation of advanced filtration units and improved scrubber technology, asserting that these measures had brought the facility into full compliance with NEQS. He challenged the EPA’s sampling and analytical procedures, suggesting potential deviations from standard practices that could lead to inaccurate results and, therefore, unreliable findings.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments and evidence presented by both sides. While the Respondent’
's assertions regarding its investments in environmental infrastructure and its commitment to environmental stewardship are noted, the Tribunal finds that the weight of evidence presented by the Complainant, the Environmental Protection Agency, convincingly establishes a pattern of non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards during the period under review. The detailed inspection reports, coupled with consistent laboratory analysis results from multiple effluent and air emission samples, unequivocally demonstrate that the Respondent’s discharges and emissions repeatedly exceeded the permissible limits for various critical parameters.
The Respondent's challenges to the EPA's sampling methodology and laboratory procedures, while raising pertinent questions about scientific rigor, were not substantiated with sufficient credible evidence to invalidate the consistent findings of the regulatory body. The Tribunal places significant reliance on the EPA's independent and repeated findings, corroborated by expert testimony, which paint a clear picture of environmental degradation attributable to the Respondent's operations. The fact that the Respondent presented evidence of *recent upgrades* and a *schedule for continuous improvement* implicitly acknowledges that full compliance was not consistently achieved prior to these measures and during the periods when the violations were documented. The Tribunal concludes that, despite its stated commitments and investments, M/s Nishat Mills Limited failed to operate and maintain its pollution control systems effectively

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,100,000/- (Rupees One Million One Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 220,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
