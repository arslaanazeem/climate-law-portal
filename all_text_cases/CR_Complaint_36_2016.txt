This judgment addresses Complaint No. 36/2016, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"). The complaint pertains to alleged contraventions of various provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997") and the National Environmental Quality Standards (hereinafter referred to as "NEQS"). The Tribunal, constituted under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, is seized of the matter to adjudicate upon the environmental violations and render a just decision.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent, a glass manufacturing facility located in an industrial zone within the jurisdiction of Lahore, has been operating without adequately treating its industrial effluent and controlling its air emissions, thereby causing significant environmental pollution. Specifically, it was alleged that the Respondent's discharge of wastewater into the public drainage system and emissions from its stacks consistently exceeded the prescribed NEQS. The Complainant further contended that despite repeated notices and opportunities for compliance, the Respondent failed to take effective measures to abate the pollution, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental regulations. The allegations included failure to install and effectively operate an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and appropriate air pollution control devices, leading to the release of pollutants such as suspended solids, heavy metals, and particulate matter into the environment, adversely affecting public health and ecological balance.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of documents and expert testimonies. These included multiple inspection reports dated 15.03.2015, 22.07.2015, and 05.01.2016, conducted by EPA officials, which documented the visual observation of discolored effluent discharge and visible smoke emissions. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of samples collected from the Respondent's effluent discharge point on 18.03.2015, 25.07.2015, and 08.01.2016, and air emission samples from its
stacks on corresponding dates. These reports consistently indicated pollutant levels significantly exceeding the permissible NEQS for parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and heavy metals in effluent, and particulate matter (PM10), Sulfur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) in air emissions. The Complainant also submitted
the series of show-cause notices issued to the Respondent, dated 01.04.2015, 01.08.2015, and 15.01.2016, along with the Respondent’s inadequate or non-existent responses, which demonstrated a clear lack of commitment to rectify the identified violations. The Complainant sought a declaration that the Respondent was in violation of PEPA, 1997, and NEQS, an order for the Respondent to cease its polluting activities, install appropriate pollution control equipment, and a suitable penalty as prescribed by law.
In response, the Respondent, through its learned counsel, vehemently denied the allegations. It was contended that the Respondent is a responsible corporate entity that adheres to all applicable environmental laws and standards. The Respondent argued that the EPA's inspection and sampling procedures were flawed, lacking proper chain of custody and scientific rigor, thereby rendering the laboratory analysis reports unreliable. It was further submitted that the Respondent had already initiated steps to upgrade its existing ETP and air pollution control systems, and that any alleged exceedances were temporary or attributable to factors beyond its immediate control, such as power fluctuations or maintenance activities. The Respondent also claimed that it had invested substantial capital in environmental management and was committed to achieving full compliance, requesting further time to implement necessary improvements. No independent expert reports or counter-analysis of samples were presented by the Respondent to refute the Complainant's technical evidence.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the evidence presented by both parties, including the inspection reports, laboratory analysis results, show-cause notices, and the Respondent's replies. The core issue for determination was whether the Respondent had indeed violated the provisions of PEPA, 1997, particularly Section 11 (Prohibition of discharge or emission of pollutants in excess of NEQS) and Section 16 (Handling of hazardous substances), and the NEQS.
The Tribunal found that the Complainant had presented consistent and cogent evidence in the form of multiple inspection reports and laboratory analyses, conducted over an extended period, which unequivocally demonstrated that the Respondent's industrial effluent and air emissions repeatedly exceeded the prescribed NEQS. The sampling dates and the corresponding laboratory reports showed a persistent pattern of non-compliance, rather than isolated incidents. The chain of custody documents for the samples were found to be in order and the laboratory reports were issued by a duly accredited environmental laboratory, making them admissible and reliable evidence. The Respondent’s general denial and unsubstantiated claims of flawed procedures, without presenting any concrete counter-evidence or independent verification, were found to be insufficient to rebut the strong technical evidence presented by the EPA.
Furthermore, the repeated issuance of show-cause notices and the Respondent's failure to provide satisfactory responses or demonstrate concrete, measurable progress towards compliance, highlighted a deliberate disregard for environmental regulations. While the Respondent claimed to be in the process of upgrading its systems, no tangible evidence of such upgrades, timelines, or financial commitments was provided to the Tribunal that would justify the continued pollution. The Tribunal emphasized that environmental compliance is not merely a matter of intent but requires demonstrable action and adherence to standards. The adverse impact on public health and the environment due to the release of untreated effluent and uncontrolled emissions cannot be overlooked.
**FINDINGS:**
Based on the foregoing analysis, the Tribunal makes the following findings:
1.  The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, has consistently discharged industrial effluent and emitted air pollutants exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for various parameters, including COD, BOD, TSS, pH, heavy metals, PM10, SO2, and NOx.
2.  The Respondent has failed to install and/or effectively operate adequate Effluent Treatment Plants (ETPs) and air pollution control devices, as required under the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997).
3.  The Respondent has failed to respond adequately to repeated compliance and show-cause notices issued by the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, and has not taken effective measures to abate the pollution despite ample opportunities.
4.  The actions and inactions of the Respondent constitute a clear violation of Section 11 and other relevant provisions of PEPA, 1997.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY:**
In light of the proven environmental violations and the persistent non-compliance by M/s Ghani Glass Limited, the Tribunal hereby issues the following final order and penalty:
1.  **Declaration of Violation:** M/s Ghani Glass Limited is hereby declared to be in violation of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, and the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS).
2.  **Cessation of Pollution:** The Respondent is directed to immediately cease all discharges of untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent and uncontrolled air emissions that exceed the prescribed NEQS.
3.  **Installation/Upgradation of Pollution Control Systems:** The Respondent shall, within a period of **six (6) months** from the date of this order, install and ensure the effective operation of a fully functional and compliant Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and appropriate Air Pollution Control (APC) devices. These systems must be capable of consistently achieving and maintaining the NEQS for all relevant parameters.
4.  **Submission of Compliance Plan:** Within **thirty (30) days** of this order, the Respondent shall submit a detailed compliance plan to the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, outlining the specific measures, technologies, and timelines for the installation/upgradation of the ETP and APC devices. This plan shall be subject to review and approval by the EPA.
5.  **Regular Monitoring and Reporting:** Upon completion of the installation/upgradation, the Respondent shall conduct regular self-monitoring of its effluent and emissions, submitting quarterly compliance reports, duly verified by an EPA-accredited laboratory, to the EPA for a period of two years. The EPA shall also conduct unannounced inspections and sampling to verify compliance.
6.  **Monetary Penalty:** In exercise of the powers conferred under Section 17(1) of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, the Tribunal imposes a penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** on M/s Ghani Glass Limited for the persistent violations and environmental damage caused. This amount shall be deposited with the Environmental Protection Agency, Lahore, within **sixty (60) days** of the date of this order.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,200,000/- (Rupees One Million Two Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 240,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **07 June, 2023**.
