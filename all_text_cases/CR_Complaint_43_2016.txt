This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited, a prominent glass manufacturing facility located in the industrial zone of Lahore. The complaint, registered under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"), alleges significant violations of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) and non-compliance with environmental regulations, thereby posing a threat to public health and the surrounding environment. The Tribunal assumed cognizance of the matter to ensure the enforcement of environmental laws and to adjudicate upon the alleged infractions.
The allegations leveled by the Complainant assert that M/s Ghani Glass Limited has been operating its manufacturing facility without adequate pollution control measures, leading to excessive emissions and discharges. Specifically, the EPA maintained that during multiple monitoring visits and inspections conducted between late 2015 and mid-2016, the Respondent's industrial processes, particularly the glass melting furnaces, were found to be emitting gaseous pollutants, including particulate matter (PM), Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx), and Sulphur Dioxide (SOx), far exceeding the prescribed NEQS for industrial gaseous emissions. Furthermore, the complaint highlighted the absence of a fully functional and efficiently operating Continuous Emission Monitoring System (CEMS), as mandated for industries of such scale and nature. The EPA also pointed out deficiencies in the management
of solid and hazardous waste, alleging improper storage and disposal practices that could contaminate soil and groundwater. The Complainant further contended that despite repeated notices and warnings issued under Section 16 of the Act, the Respondent failed to take adequate corrective measures, demonstrating a persistent disregard for environmental safeguards.
In response, M/s Ghani Glass Limited vehemently denied the charges, asserting that it has consistently striven to operate in an environmentally responsible manner. The Respondent argued that it had invested significantly in pollution control equipment, including electrostatic precipitators and bag filters, and that any exceedances recorded were either sporadic, due to temporary operational upsets, or attributable to flaws in the EPA's monitoring methodology. They presented evidence of their own internal monitoring data, environmental management plans, and efforts towards obtaining ISO 14001 certification. The Respondent also contended that the installation of a fully functional CEMS was underway and faced technical delays, not an intentional omission. Furthermore, they highlighted their contributions to the local economy and corporate social responsibility initiatives.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence presented by both parties, including the EPA's
detailed monitoring reports, laboratory analyses of emission samples, photographic evidence of alleged deficiencies, and the series of warning notices issued to the Respondent. The Tribunal also considered the Respondent's internal monitoring data, technical specifications of their pollution control equipment, progress reports on the CEMS installation, and their environmental management plans. Expert opinions were sought where necessary to reconcile conflicting technical data and assess the efficacy of the installed pollution control measures.
Upon a comprehensive evaluation, the Tribunal finds that the evidence overwhelmingly supports the Complainant's assertions regarding sustained non-compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards. The EPA's monitoring data, collected through standardized procedures and corroborated by independent laboratory analyses, consistently demonstrated that emissions of particulate matter, Oxides of Nitrogen, and Sulphur Dioxide from M/s Ghani Glass Limited's facility repeatedly exceeded the prescribed NEQS during the periods in question. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,600,000/- (Rupees One Million Six Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 320,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
