This judgment addresses the complaint filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), pursuant to the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"). The complaint, registered as Complaint No. 19/2017, alleges serious violations of environmental standards and regulations by the Respondent, specifically concerning the discharge of industrial effluent and emission of air pollutants.
The Complainant's case rests on the assertion that the Respondent, an industrial enterprise engaged in manufacturing activities, has been operating in a manner detrimental to the environment and public health. It was specifically alleged that M/s Packages Limited was discharging industrial effluent that did not meet the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) into the municipal sewerage system, which eventually leads to public water bodies. Furthermore, the complaint detailed allegations of excessive air emissions from the Respondent's industrial processes, particularly from its boilers and processing units, contributing to air pollution beyond permissible NEQS limits. The EPA contended that despite repeated communications and issuance of Show Cause Notices, the Respondent failed to take adequate corrective measures, including the installation or proper functioning of a robust Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and appropriate Air Pollution Control Devices (APCDs). These actions, or lack thereof, were stated to constitute a direct contravention of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, which prohibits the discharge or emission of any effluent or waste or air pollutant in an amount, concentration, or level that exceeds the NEQS, and Section 16, which prescribes penalties for such contraventions.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports conducted by EPA officials on various dates between 2016 and 2017. These reports consistently highlighted observations of dark-colored effluent, foul smell, and visible particulate matter emissions from the Respondent's premises. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports of effluent samples collected on 12th April 2017 and 28th June 2017, which unequivocally indicated significant exceedances of NEQS for several key parameters, including Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH levels. Similarly, air emission samples taken on 15th May 2017 revealed particulate matter concentrations and Sulfur Dioxide (SO2) levels well above the prescribed NEQS for industrial emissions. Copies of Show Cause Notices dated 20th May 2017 and 10th July 2017, along with the unsatisfactory responses received from the Respondent, were also placed on record. The Complainant's witnesses, comprising EPA technical staff, testified to the accuracy of these reports and the persistent non-compliance by the Respondent.
In response, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, vehemently denied the allegations. Through its legal counsel, the Respondent argued that it is a responsible corporate entity with a long-standing commitment to environmental stewardship. It was submitted that the company had installed an ETP and APCDs, and that efforts were continuously being made to optimize their performance. The Respondent contended that the sampling methods employed by the EPA were flawed, and the laboratory results were not entirely reliable or representative of their routine operations. They presented internal monitoring reports, albeit less frequent and detailed than the EPA's, suggesting compliance on certain parameters. The Respondent also highlighted its substantial investment in environmental infrastructure and its ongoing plans for further upgrades. It was further
submitted that the company had been cooperating with the EPA and had sought to address any deficiencies identified, albeit within practical and financial constraints. They requested the Tribunal to consider their efforts, investments, and the potential economic impact of a stringent penalty, urging for a more lenient approach that would allow them to continue operations while progressively enhancing their environmental performance. The Respondent also questioned the frequency and methodology of the EPA's sampling, suggesting that isolated instances of non-compliance might have been unfairly highlighted, rather than a systemic failure. They requested an opportunity to present a detailed action plan for environmental improvements and sought a grace period to implement the same.
The Tribunal has carefully considered the arguments advanced by both the Complainant and the Respondent, meticulously examining the documentary evidence and the testimonies of the witnesses. It is evident from the record that the Complainant, the EPA, has presented substantial and credible evidence in support of its allegations. The series of inspection reports, consistently noting environmental degradation, coupled with the detailed laboratory analysis reports of effluent and air emission samples, provide a compelling case. The samples collected on 12th April 2017, 28th June 2017 (effluent), and 15th May 2017 (air emissions) clearly demonstrate exceedances of the NEQS for various critical parameters. These reports, prepared by official environmental regulatory bodies, carry significant weight.
While the Respondent has asserted its commitment to environmental stewardship and highlighted its investments, these claims, unfortunately, are not sufficiently substantiated by evidence to counter the specific and quantified violations presented by the EPA. The internal monitoring reports presented by the Respondent were neither comprehensive nor frequent enough to negate the findings of the EPA's official analyses. Furthermore, the argument regarding flawed sampling methods or unreliable laboratory results remained largely unsubstantiated, lacking specific technical counter-evidence. The repeated issuance of Show Cause Notices and the Respondent’s unsatisfactory responses also indicate a persistent pattern of non-compliance despite regulatory interventions.
The Tribunal finds that the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, has indeed contravened the provisions of Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, by discharging industrial effluent and emitting air pollutants in amounts, concentrations, and levels exceeding the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). Such actions pose a significant threat to public health and the environment, undermining the very purpose of environmental protection legislation. The Tribunal also notes that the Respondent's failure to take adequate and timely corrective measures, despite repeated notices, constitutes a serious dereliction of its environmental responsibilities.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

The jurisdiction of this Tribunal is established under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997. The complaint has been filed in the prescribed manner and the Respondent has been afforded full opportunity to be heard. Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997 empowers this Tribunal to impose a penalty of up to PKR 1,000,000 for a first violation, with an additional PKR 100,000 per day for each day the violation continues.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,700,000/- (Rupees One Million Seven Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 340,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
