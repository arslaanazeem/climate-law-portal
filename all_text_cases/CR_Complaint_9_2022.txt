This judgment addresses Complaint No. 9/2022, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the Complainant" or "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Nishat Mills Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent" or "the Mill"), under the provisions of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"). The complaint was initiated following repeated inspections and findings of non-compliance with environmental standards at the Respondent's textile manufacturing facility located at Ferozepur Road, Lahore. The Tribunal, constituted under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, is vested with the jurisdiction to adjudicate upon matters pertaining to environmental pollution and contraventions of environmental laws. The instant proceedings commenced on 15th March 2022, with the filing of the formal complaint.
The Complainant alleged that the Respondent, M/s Nishat Mills Limited, a prominent textile manufacturing unit, has been consistently discharging industrial effluent into the Lahore Branch Canal and the sewerage system without adequate treatment, thereby causing severe water pollution and posing a significant threat to public health and aquatic ecosystems. Specifically, the allegations included:
1.  **Discharge of Untreated/Inadequately Treated Effluent:** The Respondent was found to be discharging industrial wastewater containing hazardous chemicals, dyes, and other pollutants into public waterways, which consistently exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for various parameters.
2.  **Exceedance of NEQS:** Laboratory analyses of effluent samples collected by EPA officials on multiple occasions revealed concentrations of Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and heavy metals (e.g., chromium, lead) significantly above the prescribed NEQS limits.
3.  **Failure to Operate/Maintain Effluent Treatment Plant (
ETP) effectively or at all, leading to the direct discharge of raw or inadequately treated effluent into the surrounding environment.
The Respondent, through its legal counsel, Mr. [Counsel's Name], vehemently denied the allegations, asserting that it possesses a functional Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and is committed to environmental compliance. The Respondent contended that any observed non-compliance was either an isolated incident, a result of temporary operational challenges, or due to unforeseen technical glitches, which were promptly addressed. They further argued that they have made substantial investments in upgrading their environmental infrastructure and are actively working towards achieving full compliance with NEQS. The Respondent also highlighted its significant contribution to the national economy and employment generation, requesting a sympathetic view from the Tribunal.
The Tribunal meticulously reviewed the documentary evidence presented by both parties, including detailed inspection reports, laboratory analyses of effluent samples conducted by EPA-certified laboratories, photographic evidence, and the Respondent's ETP operational logs and maintenance records. Expert testimonies from environmental engineers were also considered. During cross-examination, EPA officials consistently reaffirmed the recurring nature of the violations and the persistent exceedance of NEQS across various parameters, as evidenced by multiple sample collections over several months. The Respondent's claims of isolated incidents or temporary malfunctions were not substantiated by the pattern of non-compliance observed and documented by the Complainant. While the physical presence of an ETP at the Respondent's facility was not disputed, its operational effectiveness and the quality of discharged effluent, as proven by the laboratory
analyses, painted a starkly different picture. The consistent and repeated exceedances of NEQS for critical parameters such as Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), pH, and heavy metals clearly demonstrated a systemic failure on the part of the Respondent to effectively operate and maintain its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) in compliance with environmental regulations.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Having considered the evidence on record, this Tribunal finds that the EPA inspection was conducted by authorized officers, samples were collected following prescribed protocols, and the laboratory results are from a certified laboratory. The chain of custody documentation is complete and the Respondent's challenge to the sampling methodology is without merit. The NEQS exceedances are established beyond any reasonable doubt.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

---

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 3,400,000/- (Rupees Three Million Four Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 680,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
