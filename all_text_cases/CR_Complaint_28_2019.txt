This judgment pertains to Complaint No. 28/2019, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "the EPA" or "the Complainant"), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"). The complaint invokes the jurisdiction of this Tribunal under Section 19 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (hereinafter referred to as "PEPA, 1997"), alleging various violations of environmental laws and regulations by the Respondent concerning the discharge of industrial effluent from its manufacturing facility located in Lahore. The Tribunal is seized with the matter to adjudicate upon the alleged contraventions and determine appropriate remedies, including penalties, as provided under the relevant statutes.
The Complainant, through its authorized representative, alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations were in persistent violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluents, particularly concerning the discharge of untreated or inadequately treated wastewater into public sewers and ultimately into natural water bodies. Specifically, the allegations included:
a. Discharge of industrial effluent containing parameters such as Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD5), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and heavy metals (e.g., Lead, Chromium) in concentrations significantly exceeding the prescribed NEQS.
b. Failure to operate and maintain its Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) effectively, leading to consistent non-compliance despite repeated warnings and notices issued by the EPA.
c. Non-compliance with the conditions stipulated in its Environmental Approval (EIA/IEE approval) regarding the regular monitoring and reporting of effluent quality.
d. Causing environmental pollution detrimental to public health and the ecosystem, in contravention of Section 11 of PEPA, 1997.
The Complainant submitted that multiple inspections and sampling exercises conducted by EPA officials consistently revealed the Respondent's non-compliance, which necessitated the filing of the present complaint after the Respondent failed to take satisfactory corrective measures within the stipulated timeframes provided in various show-cause notices.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of documents and testimonies. These included:
i. **Inspection Reports:** Dated 15.03.2018, 22.08.2018, and 10.01.2019, detailing observations of the Respondent's ETP operations and effluent discharge points.
ii. **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Issued by the EPA-certified laboratory, confirming the exceedance of NEQS for BOD5 (up to 300 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), COD (up to 800 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), TSS (up to 250 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), and detectable levels of heavy metals, from samples collected on 15.03.2018, 22.08.2018, and 10.01.2019.
iii. **Show-Cause Notices and Warning Letters:** Copies of notices issued to the Respondent on 01.04.2018, 05.09.2018, and 20.01.2019, outlining the violations and demanding remedial action, along with the Respondent's unsatisfactory replies.
iv. **Testimony of Mr. Adnan Khan:** Assistant Director (Technical), EPA, who personally conducted some of the inspections and attested to the persistent non-compliance and the validity of the laboratory reports.
The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its legal counsel and representatives, contested the allegations. They presented the following evidence and arguments:
i. **ETP Operational Records:** Submitting records indicating regular operation and maintenance of their ETP, along with significant capital investment made in 2017 for upgrading the plant.
ii. **Internal Monitoring Reports:** Presenting their own internal monitoring data, which, in some instances, showed compliance or near-compliance, challenging the EPA's sampling methodology or the representativeness of the EPA's samples.
iii. **Expert Opinion:** An affidavit from an environmental consultant, Engr. Dr. Fatima Zahra, who opined that industrial processes inherently produce complex effluents and that the Respondent was making continuous efforts
to achieve full compliance, facing inherent technical challenges in treating complex industrial wastewater streams. She further suggested that the EPA's sampling might not always represent the average discharge quality and could be susceptible to variability.
iv. **Commitment to Environmental Stewardship:** The Respondent emphasized its corporate social responsibility initiatives and its long-standing commitment to environmental protection, citing significant financial allocations for environmental management and upgrades to its
ETP, including significant investments in tertiary treatment modules and advanced monitoring equipment in late 2018. They further argued that the intermittent nature of EPA's sampling might not accurately reflect the overall operational efficiency of their ETP, which typically operates within acceptable parameters for most of the time. **IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

This Tribunal observes that the Respondent has been served with an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) under Section 16 of PEPA, 1997 and was granted adequate time to achieve compliance. The persistence of NEQS violations after expiry of the EPO compliance period demonstrates willful non-compliance and disentitles the Respondent from any lenient treatment. The defense of temporary malfunction is rejected as an afterthought unsupported by contemporaneous records.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 creates an absolute prohibition against the discharge of pollutants in excess of NEQS. This prohibition is reinforced by the principle of strict environmental liability recognized in the jurisprudence of the superior courts of Pakistan. Non-compliance with an EPO issued under Section 16 aggravates the culpability of the Respondent and justifies the imposition of an enhanced penalty.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
