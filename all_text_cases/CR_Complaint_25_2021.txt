This complaint, filed on 15th March 2021, by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, under Section 17 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), seeks enforcement against M/s Packages Limited, a prominent manufacturing facility located in Kot Lakhpat, Lahore. The crux of the matter revolves around alleged persistent non-compliance by the Respondent with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial effluent discharge and improper management of hazardous waste, thereby causing environmental degradation and public nuisance. The Tribunal was called upon to adjudicate whether the Respondent had indeed violated environmental laws and, if so, to impose appropriate penalties and remedial directives.
The Complainant averred that M/s Packages Limited, being an industrial undertaking involved in the manufacturing of paper, packaging, and related products, was under a statutory obligation to treat its industrial wastewater and manage its solid and hazardous waste in accordance with the standards prescribed under the PEPA, 1997, and its subsidiary rules and regulations, particularly the NEQS. It was specifically alleged that despite repeated warnings and notices issued by the EPA, the Respondent continued to discharge industrial effluent containing pollutants far exceeding the permissible limits into the public sewerage system, which eventually flows into the River Ravi. Furthermore, the Complainant contended that the Respondent failed to properly segregate, store, and dispose of its hazardous waste, posing a significant risk to soil and groundwater contamination. The Complainant cited multiple inspection reports and laboratory analyses conducted between October 2020 and February 2021, indicating consistent violations of NEQS for parameters such as Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. Specifically, an inspection on 12th January 2021 revealed BOD levels of 350 mg/L against the NEQS limit of 80 mg/L, and COD levels of 800 mg/L against the NEQS limit of 150 mg/L. The Complainant prayed for the imposition of maximum penalties as provided under the Act and a mandatory directive for the Respondent to immediately comply with all environmental standards.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of documents, including:
1.  **Inspection Reports:** Dated 20th October 2020, 12th January 2021, and 25th February 2021, detailing observations of effluent discharge points and waste storage areas.
2.  **Laboratory Analysis Reports:** Issued by the EPA-certified laboratory, confirming the exceedance of NEQS parameters for samples collected on the aforementioned dates. For instance, the report dated 15th January 2021, pertaining to the sample from 12th January 2021, showed BOD at 350 mg/L, COD at 800 mg/L, TSS at 250 mg/L, and pH at 4.5.
3.  **Show Cause Notices and Warning Letters:** Issued by the EPA to the Respondent on 5th November 2020 and 1st February 2021, highlighting non-compliance and requiring corrective action.
4.  **Photographic Evidence:** Depicting the discharge of colored effluent and improperly stored waste drums at the Respondent's premises.
The Complainant also produced Mr. Adnan Khan, Environmental Inspector, as a witness, who testified to the accuracy of the inspection reports and
the procedures followed during sample collection, ensuring chain of custody and proper methodology. He further reiterated that despite repeated communications and issuance of notices, significant improvements in the effluent quality were not observed during subsequent inspections.
The Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, represented by its counsel, Mr. Zahid Hussain, vehemently denied the allegations of persistent non-compliance. The Respondent's counsel argued that M/s Packages Limited is a responsible corporate entity with an operational Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) and a dedicated team for environmental management. He contended that the company is continuously investing in upgrades and maintenance of its ETP to meet evolving environmental standards. The Respondent challenged the accuracy and representativeness of some of the samples taken by the EPA, suggesting that samples might have been collected during abnormal process conditions or without proper notification, thus not reflecting the typical discharge quality. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports, which, it claimed, showed compliance or near-compliance on several occasions. Furthermore, it was argued that the discharge into the public sewerage system, while a point of concern, eventually undergoes dilution and further treatment by municipal facilities, thereby mitigating direct impact on the River Ravi. Regarding hazardous waste, the Respondent claimed to have designated storage areas and established contracts with EPA-approved waste management contractors for proper disposal, attributing any observed discrepancies to temporary logistical issues rather than a systemic failure. The Respondent sought leniency, emphasizing its significant economic contribution, employment generation, and ongoing commitment to achieving full compliance, requesting a reasonable timeframe for any required upgrades or corrective actions.
The Tribunal carefully considered the evidence, arguments, and testimonies presented by both parties. It noted the consistency and frequency of the Complainant's inspection reports and laboratory analyses, which repeatedly indicated exceedances of NEQS for key parameters over a significant period from October 2020 to February 2021. The photographic evidence further corroborated the observations of colored effluent discharge and improperly stored waste drums at the Respondent's premises. While acknowledging the Respondent's claims of having an ETP and making efforts towards environmental management, the Tribunal found that the *actual performance* of the ETP, as evidenced by the EPA's independent monitoring and certified laboratory results, was clearly inadequate to meet the prescribed standards. The Respondent's internal monitoring reports, though presented, did not sufficiently counter the consistent pattern of non-compliance established by the EPA's independent and certified laboratory results. The argument
that samples might have been collected during abnormal process conditions or without proper notification was not substantiated with credible evidence. The Tribunal notes that EPA's sampling procedures are typically governed by established protocols, and the burden to prove procedural irregularities lay with the Respondent, which it failed to discharge effectively. Furthermore, the contention that discharge into the public sewerage system undergoes dilution and municipal treatment, thereby mitigating direct impact, does not absolve the Respondent of its primary responsibility to meet the NEQS at the point of discharge. The "polluter pays" principle dictates that the source of pollution must internalize the costs of environmental damage and remediation, rather than relying on subsequent dilution or public infrastructure to manage its untreated waste. The Tribunal also finds the Respondent's explanations for improper hazardous waste management, attributing them to "temporary logistical issues," to be insufficient given the photographic evidence and the potential severe risks posed by such practices to soil and groundwater.

**IV. TRIBUNAL'S ANALYSIS**

Upon examination of all evidence produced before this Tribunal, it is evident that the Respondent has been operating its facility in continuous violation of applicable NEQS standards. The defense arguments do not address the substance of the documented pollution. This Tribunal holds that strict liability applies under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, and proof of intent or negligence is not required for the imposition of penalty.

**V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK**

Section 11 of PEPA, 1997 imposes strict liability upon any person who discharges or emits, or allows the discharge or emission of, any effluent, waste, air pollutant or noise in excess of NEQS. The Respondent's industrial activity falls squarely within this prohibition. Proof of intent is not required; the act of pollution itself attracts penal consequences under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,000,000/- (Rupees One Million only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 200,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
