This judgment addresses Complaint No. 22/2021, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), Lahore, against M/s Packages Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent manufacturing entity located in Lahore. The complaint alleges significant violations of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997) and its associated National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS). The Tribunal, constituted under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, and vested with the jurisdiction to hear and decide environmental complaints, commenced proceedings on 15.03.2021, following the submission of the complaint and preliminary scrutiny. Notices were duly issued to the Respondent, who subsequently appeared through their legal counsel and filed a detailed reply.
The gravamen of the allegations brought forth by the Complainant is that the Respondent, operating a large-scale paper and packaging manufacturing facility, has been consistently discharging untreated or inadequately treated industrial effluent into the Lahore Branch Canal, a public water body. The Complainant asserts that routine monitoring and inspection activities conducted by EPA officials on various dates, specifically on 12.01.2021, 05.02.2021, and 28.02.2021, revealed that the Respondent's effluent discharge exceeded the prescribed National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for several critical parameters, including Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and pH. Furthermore, the Complainant alleges that the Respondent failed to maintain proper records of its effluent treatment plant (ETP) operations, did not adequately upgrade its ETP despite previous warnings, and was found to be managing hazardous waste (sludge from paper manufacturing) in an environmentally unsound manner, potentially contaminating soil and groundwater, in violation of Section 11 and 13 of PEPA, 1997, and related NEQS Rules. The Complainant sought imposition of penalties as per Section 17 of PEPA, 1997, and a directive for immediate compliance.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a comprehensive dossier of evidence. This included detailed inspection reports prepared by EPA field teams on the aforementioned dates, complete with photographic evidence documenting the alleged discharge points and the state of the ETP. Crucially, laboratory analysis reports from the certified EPA laboratory were submitted, which contained the results of effluent samples collected from the Respondent's discharge points. These reports consistently indicated values significantly exceeding the NEQS for BOD (up to 300 mg/L against a standard of 80 mg/L), COD (up to 750 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), and TSS (up to 250 mg/L against a standard of 200 mg/L), with pH levels occasionally falling outside the permissible range of 6-9. The Complainant also produced copies of previous warning notices issued to the Respondent, dating back to 2019, highlighting non-compliance issues and the Respondent's failure to adequately respond or implement corrective measures. The Assistant Director, EPA, also appeared as a witness, reiterating the findings of the inspection reports and the criticality of preventing pollution of public water bodies.
In its defense, the Respondent, M/s Packages Limited, through its counsel, vehemently denied the allegations of willful non-compliance. The Respondent contended that it operates a state-of-the-art ETP and is committed to environmental stewardship. It argued that the samples collected by the Complainant might not have been representative, or that the sampling methodology itself was flawed. The Respondent presented its own internal monitoring reports and some third-party laboratory analyses, which, it claimed, demonstrated compliance with NEQS. It was further argued that any occasional exceedances were minor and attributable to unforeseen operational upsets, which were promptly rectified. The Respondent also submitted a detailed plan for upgrading its ETP, which was stated to be underway, and presented evidence of significant financial investment in environmental protection infrastructure. Regarding hazardous waste, the Respondent claimed to be in the process of acquiring a license for off-site disposal through an approved vendor and denied any direct environmental damage from temporary on-site storage. The Respondent's Environment Manager testified, asserting the company's continuous efforts towards achieving full compliance.
The legal framework governing this complaint is primarily the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997). Specifically, Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, prohibits the discharge or emission
of any effluent or waste or air pollutant or noise in an amount, concentration or level which is in excess of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) or, where applicable, the standards established under sub-section (1) of Section 12. Section 14 of PEPA, 1997, deals with the handling of hazardous substances, requiring licenses for their import, manufacture, transportation, treatment, storage, or disposal, and mandates adherence to prescribed rules and standards to prevent environmental pollution. Contravention of these provisions is an offense punishable under Section 17 of PEPA, 1997. Section 17 stipulates that whoever contravenes or fails to comply with any provision of this Act or any order or direction issued thereunder shall be punishable with a fine which may extend to one million rupees, and in case of a continuing contravention, with an additional fine which may extend to one hundred thousand rupees for every day during which such contravention continues. The Tribunal also has the power to direct the offender to take such measures as may be necessary to mitigate or nullify the damage caused, or to restore the environment.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,900,000/- (Rupees One Million Nine Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 380,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.
