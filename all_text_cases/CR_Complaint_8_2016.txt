**JUDGMENT**
This judgment addresses Complaint No. 8/2016, filed by the Assistant Director, Environmental Protection Agency (hereinafter referred to as "EPA"), Lahore, against M/s Ghani Glass Limited (hereinafter referred to as "the Respondent"), a prominent glass manufacturing facility located in Lahore. The complaint seeks redressal for alleged violations of environmental laws and regulations, specifically concerning the discharge of industrial emissions exceeding prescribed standards, thereby posing a significant threat to public health and the environment. The Tribunal has carefully considered the submissions, evidence, and legal arguments presented by both parties.
The Complainant, through its legal counsel, alleged that the Respondent's industrial operations were in persistent violation of the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial gaseous emissions, particularly concerning particulate matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx) emanating from its stack emissions. It was specifically contended that multiple inspections conducted by EPA officials between late 2015 and early 2016 consistently revealed exceedances of the NEQS. The complaint further stated that despite several warning notices and an Environmental Protection Order (EPO) issued under Section 20 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997 (PEPA, 1997), the Respondent failed to implement adequate pollution control measures or demonstrate sustainable compliance. The EPA sought a declaration of guilt, imposition of penalties, and a mandatory order for the Respondent to bring its operations into full compliance with the environmental laws.
To substantiate its claims, the Complainant presented a series of inspection reports, dating from October 2015 to March 2016, detailing observations of visible emissions and operational deficiencies in the Respondent's air pollution control equipment. Crucially, the Complainant submitted laboratory analysis reports from the EPA's accredited laboratory, which contained detailed readings of stack emissions. These reports consistently showed PM levels significantly above the NEQS limit of 150 mg/Nm³, with some readings exceeding 300 mg/Nm³. Similarly, SO2 and NOx levels were also reported to be in excess of the prescribed standards. Copies of warning notices, show-cause notices, and the Environmental
Protection Order (EPO) were also placed on record as documentary evidence.
The Respondent, M/s Ghani Glass Limited, through its legal representative, vehemently denied the allegations of persistent non-compliance. It was contended that the Respondent is a responsible corporate entity with a strong commitment to environmental stewardship. The Respondent argued that it had invested significantly in state-of-the-art pollution control technologies and was continuously working towards improving its environmental performance. While acknowledging certain operational challenges, the Respondent questioned the accuracy and methodology of some of the EPA's sampling and analysis reports, suggesting potential anomalies or inconsistencies. It further submitted that subsequent to the issuance of the EPO, it had initiated steps to upgrade its electrostatic precipitators (ESPs) and install additional scrubbers to further reduce emissions, and requested more time to demonstrate full compliance. The Respondent also presented a timeline of its planned remedial actions and technical reports from its own consultants purporting to show efforts towards compliance.
The Tribunal has meticulously reviewed the entire record, including the documentary evidence and the oral submissions made by both parties. It is an established principle that environmental protection is a fundamental right, and industrial entities bear a significant responsibility to operate within the prescribed environmental standards. The evidence presented by the Complainant, particularly the multiple, consistent laboratory analysis reports from an accredited EPA laboratory, unequivocally demonstrates that the Respondent's stack emissions, specifically particulate matter, Sulphur Dioxide, and Oxides of Nitrogen, consistently exceeded the National Environmental Quality Standards during the period under scrutiny. The repeated issuance of warning notices, show-cause notices, and ultimately an Environmental Protection Order under Section 21 of PEPA, 1997, clearly indicates that the EPA afforded the Respondent ample opportunity to rectify the violations. While the Respondent's assertions of ongoing efforts to upgrade pollution control measures are noted, these efforts, as presented, do not negate the fact of past and persistent non-compliance during the period leading up to and immediately following the EPO, which forms the basis of this complaint. The alleged anomalies in EPA's reports were
not substantiated with credible counter-evidence or expert analysis sufficient to discredit the consistent findings of the EPA's accredited laboratory. The burden of proof to demonstrate compliance or to effectively challenge the Complainant's technical evidence largely rests with the Respondent once a prima facie case of violation has been established, which the Complainant has successfully done. The Tribunal finds that the Respondent's arguments, while acknowledging some remedial efforts, do not absolve it from the documented and persistent breaches of environmental standards.
The Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, specifically Sections 11, 12, and 20, read with the National Environmental Quality Standards (Self-Monitoring and Reporting by Industries and other Segments of Environment) Rules, 2001, places a clear obligation on industrial units to operate within prescribed limits. Failure to do so constitutes an offense punishable under Section 17 of the PEPA, 1997. The persistent exceedance of NEQS, despite repeated warnings and an EPO, demonstrates a failure on the part of the Respondent to effectively manage its emissions and comply with environmental laws. Such a failure not only contravenes statutory obligations but also poses a direct threat to the right to a clean and healthy environment, a right recognized under the Constitution of Pakistan.
**FINAL ORDER AND PENALTY**
In light of the foregoing findings, the Tribunal declares as follows:
1.  **Declaration of Guilt:** M/s Ghani Glass Limited, the Respondent, is hereby found guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for industrial gaseous emissions, specifically concerning particulate matter (PM), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx), in contravention of Sections 11 and 20 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997.
2.  **Imposition of Penalty:** A penalty of **PKR 5,000,000/- (Rupees Five Million only)** is hereby imposed upon M/s Ghani Glass Limited for the persistent non-compliance and environmental violations. This penalty shall be paid to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within thirty (30) days from the date of this judgment.
3.  **Mandatory Compliance Order:** The Respondent is hereby directed to:
    *   Immediately take all necessary measures to bring its stack emissions into full and continuous compliance with the National Environmental Quality Standards (NEQS) for PM, SO2, and NOx.
    *   Submit a comprehensive Environmental Compliance Plan to the Environmental Protection Agency, Punjab, within sixty (60) days from the date of this judgment, detailing the specific technical upgrades, operational changes, and monitoring protocols it will implement to achieve and maintain NEQS compliance.
    *   Install and operationalize continuous emission monitoring systems (CEMS) for particulate matter, SO2, and NOx at its stack outlets, linked to the EPA's central monitoring system, within ninety (90) days from the date of this judgment.
    *   Submit monthly compliance reports, duly verified by an independent EPA-approved third-party environmental laboratory, to the EPA for a period of one year, commencing from the date of this judgment, demonstrating continuous adherence to NEQS.
4.  **Consequences of Non-Compliance:** Failure to comply with any part of this order, particularly the mandatory compliance directives, shall result in further legal action, including but not limited to, the imposition of additional daily penalties as per Section 17(2) of the PEPA, 1997, and potential closure of the industrial unit under Section 20(3) of the PEPA, 1997, without further notice.
5.  **Costs:** There shall be no order as to costs.
The file shall be consigned to the record after necessary completion.

**FINAL ORDER**

Having found the Respondent, the Respondent, guilty of violating the National Environmental Quality Standards under Section 11 of the Pakistan Environmental Protection Act, 1997, this Tribunal hereby passes the following order:

**1. Declaration of Guilt**

The Respondent, the Respondent, is hereby declared guilty of violating NEQS standards under Section 11 of PEPA, 1997, read with applicable NEQS Regulations (SRO 742(I)/2000).

**2. Penalty**

The Respondent is directed to pay a penalty of **PKR 1,800,000/- (Rupees One Million Eight Hundred Thousand only)** under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997, payable to the Punjab Environmental Protection Agency within thirty (30) days of this order.

**3. Environmental Charges (PSDF)**

The Respondent shall additionally deposit **PKR 360,000/-** into the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) under the Punjab Environmental Protection (Provincial Sustainable Development Fund) Rules, 2010.

**4. Compliance Directive**

The Respondent is directed to achieve full compliance with NEQS standards within sixty (60) days of this order and to submit documentary evidence of compliance to the Punjab EPA.

**5. Monthly Compliance Reports**

The Respondent shall submit monthly compliance reports to the Punjab EPA for a period of twelve (12) months from the date of this order.

**6. Daily Fine for Non-Compliance**

In the event of failure to comply within the stipulated period, the Respondent shall be liable to pay a daily fine of **PKR 100,000/-** per day of continued non-compliance under Section 17(1) of PEPA, 1997.

**7. EPA Monitoring**

The Punjab EPA is directed to conduct monthly inspection of the Respondent's facility for one (1) year and report findings to this Tribunal.

**8. Costs**

The Respondent shall bear costs assessed at **PKR 50,000/-**, payable to the Punjab EPA.

**9. Right of Appeal**

Any party aggrieved by this order may prefer an appeal before the Lahore High Court under Section 22 of PEPA, 1997 within thirty (30) days of receipt of this order.

Announced in open Tribunal this **13 March, 2023**.
